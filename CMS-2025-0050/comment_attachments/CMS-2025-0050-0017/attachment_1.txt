To whom it may concern: I appreciate ONC/ASTP’s and CMS’ developing this RFI and make
the following suggestions as these agencies and others support patient, provider and payment
adoption and engagement with digital health technologies: A. To support and bolster patient
engagement and trust, emphasize Health Literacy, Broadband Access and Patient Privacy and B.
Support use of consistent and standardized terminology across government agencies. Thank you
for considering these personal views.
A. To support and bolster patient engagement and trust, emphasize Health Literacy,
Broadband Access and Patient Privacy: The RFI requests input on potential facilitators and
barriers to bolstering patient and caregiver engagement with digital health tools. Supporting
access to broadband may be one important facilitator of consumer use of digital health tools
(however defined as discussed below). As a starting point, more than 20 million Americans may
lack access to high-speed Internet/broadband resources that are important to being able to use
digital health technologies. Those with lower incomes, older adults and those in rural areas
especially may lack access to high-speed Internet that is essential to being able to use many
digital health tools.1 The same areas and communities that lack access to good Internet services
also tend to be those with more limited health care access. These communities therefore perhaps
may be among those who most could benefit from effective and judiciously employed digital
health tools.2 Supporting access to broadband among other ‘health related social needs’ could be
one way CMS and others could bolster digital health adoption among patients and families.3
Leveraging cellphones and smartphones as platforms for digital health tools rather than
emphasizing computers also may be important as well; many users may chiefly rely on their
phones for Internet and other services and not necessarily own or frequently use computers or
other devices with Internet access.4 (That said, owning a smartphone and being able to
effectively access and use digital health tools such as apps are not necessarily the same).
Health literacy and digital health literacy also are important. Put simply, people will not use
digital health technologies or use them effectively and well if they do not understand on a basic

Ensuring digital health tools protect user privacy also is essential, particularly when technologies
are used to treat conditions such as mental health or substance use disorders that may result in
stigma and discrimination or are used by vulnerable populations such as children/adolescents,
those with behavioral health conditions and some older adults.7 Privacy violations, sometimes
high profile, due to company practices or conduct by outsiders (e.g., hacking) may make
consumers and providers reluctant to use digital health tools.
Users should fully understand how their data will be obtained, stored, used and shared. This
should include what happens to that information once a patient’s use ends and considerations as
to what may happen if an entity stops supporting the app/tool, goes out of business or is
purchased by/merges with another organization. Express informed consent to these uses should
be obtained from consumers. Compliance by apps and other digital health tools with HIPAA, 42
CFR Part 2 (Confidentiality of Substance Use Disorder Patient Records) and relevant state-level
requirements, including with respect to behavioral health privacy, should be expected. Long
terms of use on Web sites or embedded in apps do not fully inform patients about how data will
be collected and used and in and of themselves probably are not typically sufficient to educate
consumers and obtain informed consent.8 Using plain language summaries or having patients
view brief videos or graphics explaining privacy and confidentiality issues such as data sharing
may be more optimal approaches. Working with the Office for Civil Rights and others,

Beyond patient consent, there is the challenge of cybersecurity and deliberate or careless
compromise of data collected by these tools. As one author observes, “HL7 (Health Level Seven)
and FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) are key standards for exchanging
healthcare data. They ensure that different health information systems can communicate
effectively and share information seamlessly. However, both protocols have notable security
vulnerabilities.” Many examples exist of consumer privacy being compromised due to period or
fertility tracker apps, fitness apps, wearables, etc. Adherence to cybersecurity and related best
practices is essential as digital health apps are developed, distributed to patients and providers
and used over time.10
B. Support use of consistent and standardized terminology across government agencies: It
seems axiomatic that before ONC/ASTP, CMS or many others should carefully define the term
‘digital health’ Yet, many somewhat varying definitions of digital health are used by the federal
government and the private and nonprofit sectors. This may in turn contribute to lack of clarity
or inconsistency in regulatory approaches and consumer misunderstanding. For instance, does
the term digital health always include eHealth, mobile apps, wellness apps, etc.?
In this RFI, OSTP and CMS define digital health products “as digital tools that support
individual health needs.” This definition is not perfectly aligned with other definitions that could
emphasize uses such as wellness or population health needs. While statutes and regulations
sometimes may require use of terminology unique to an agency/Department or a particular
regulation or guidance, to the extent feasible the National Institutes of Health, Veterans
Administration, CMS, OSTP, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA), Substance Abuse and
Mental Health Services Administration, the Networking and Information Technology Research
and Development Program,11 the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) and
others should develop, standardize and try to use consistent terminology. Consistent terminology
will support CMS and ONC/ASTP in promoting user engagement with digital heath technology
products and spurring innovation and development.
ONC/ASTP, CMS and others should review existing definitions and consider developing a
glossary of key terms and related terms such as eHealth, digital technologies and artificial
intelligence, perhaps building on existing glossaries, that can be used across the federal
government and perhaps by others as well.12 (An alternative approach may be to simply stop

One definition worth considering is that within the World Health Organization’s Global Strategy
on Digital Health, which recently defined digital health as “‘[t]he field of knowledge and
practice associated with the development and use of digital technologies to improve health.
Digital health expands the concept of eHealth to include digital consumers, with a wider range of
smart-devices and connected equipment. It also encompasses other uses of digital technologies
for health such as the Internet of things, artificial intelligence, big data and robotics.’”14 In a
footnote, WHO notes that this includes the term “eHealth” and that the term digital health may
include “developing areas such as the use of advanced computing sciences (in the fields of ‘big
data’, genomics and artificial intelligence, for example).” After reviewing 95 definitions in the
scientific literature of digital health, one review concluded “we can infer that digital health is
about the proper use of technology for improving the health and wellbeing of people at
individual and population levels, as well as enhancing the care of patients through intelligent
processing of clinical and genetic data.”15 Related to the core term ‘digital health’ are subsidiary
terms such as digital public health16 and digital mental health technologies.17
In its recent Physician Fee Schedule (2025) rule, CMS defined digital mental health technology
as “in general [meant] to refer to software devices cleared, approved, or granted De Novo
authorization by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) that are intended to treat or alleviate a
mental health condition, in conjunction with ongoing behavioral health care treatment under a
behavioral health treatment plan of care, by generating and delivering a mental health treatment
intervention that has a demonstrable positive therapeutic impact on a patient's health.”18 CMS
also has used the term ‘digital cognitive behavioral therapy.’ CMS also has referenced the
Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration’s definition of digital therapeutics
as “health software intended to treat or alleviate a disease, disorder, condition, or injury by
generating and delivering a medical intervention that has a demonstrable positive therapeutic
impact on a patient’s health.”19

ONC/ASTP explains in its Strategic Plan that “Digital therapeutics are a subset of digital health
tools that deliver evidence-based therapeutic interventions to patients that are driven by high-
quality software programs.”20 However, ONC/ASTP’s glossary is fairly short and does not
expressly define the term ‘digital health.”21
FDA has defined digital health technology as: “A system that uses computing platforms,
connectivity, software, and/or sensors for health care and related uses. These technologies span a
wide range of uses, from applications in general wellness to applications as a medical device.
They include technologies intended for use as a medical product, in a medical product, or as an
adjunct to other medical products (devices, drugs, and biologics). They may also be used to
develop or study medical products.22 Elsewhere, FDA has more informally stated that: “Digital
health technologies use computing platforms, connectivity, software, and sensors for health care
and related uses.”23
As may be apparent, there exists a wide range of differing and overlapping terms that may
ultimately hinder consumer and health provider understanding and effective use and oversight of
digital health technologies.
