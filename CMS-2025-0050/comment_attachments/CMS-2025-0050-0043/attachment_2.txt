You Can’t Improve Healthcare
Access on a Broken Foundation: The
Truth About Pharmacy Deserts
We talk a lot about improving healthcare access these days. It fills
headlines, conference agendas, political talking points.
But if we’re being honest — you can’t improve healthcare access when
the very system designed to provide it is broken, corrupted, and getting
worse by the year.
Today, over 15.8 million Americans live in what we call "pharmacy
deserts" — areas where getting medication isn’t just inconvenient, it’s
almost impossible. But instead of asking how many pharmacy deserts
there are, it’s time to ask a better question:
How many more have appeared since we first named
the problem a decade ago?
The term "pharmacy desert" first entered the academic and public
conversation in 2014, when researchers at the University of Illinois called
out growing gaps in pharmacy access — gaps tied to poverty, race, and
healthcare inequality.
We named the problem. We studied the problem. We reported on the
problem.
And yet, from 2014 to today, more than 9,000 pharmacies have closed
across the U.S. Mostly in the places that needed them most: rural towns,
urban neighborhoods, low-income communities.
Published

Pharmacy deserts didn’t shrink. They exploded.
If you listen to excuses, you’ll hear talk about shrinking
margins, tightening budgets, economic realities. But
here’s the truth:
While patients lost their local pharmacies, PBMs and corporate
healthcare giants raked in record profits.
CVS Health (which owns CVS Pharmacy and Caremark PBM): 2023
Net Income $8.3 billion, nearly doubling from $4.2 billion in 2022
UnitedHealth Group (OptumRx PBM, UnitedHealthcare Insurance):
2023 Net Income $22.4 billion, up 11% from 2022
Cigna Group (Express Scripts PBM): 2023 Net Income $5.2 billion,
though down from $6.7 billion in 2022
There wasn’t a shortage of money. There was a redistribution — upward,
toward corporate conglomerates, and away from the small pharmacies,
patients, and communities they claimed to serve.
While corporations profited, families were left without access to basic
medications. While PBMs tightened their grip on pharmacy
reimbursements, small towns watched their last remaining lifeline vanish.
I Lived It.
There were times I had to choose between my own medications and what
my kids needed. I chose my kids — every time. Because when you live in a
pharmacy desert, healthcare isn’t a service. It’s a gamble.
People like to talk about healthcare access in abstract numbers and
percentages. But for millions of families, it's a brutal, everyday reality.
Driving hours for an antibiotic.
Waiting days for a life-saving medication that used to be down the
street.
Rationing pills because refills aren't reliably accessible.
Choosing between medicine, groceries, and school supplies.
This is what pharmacy deserts look like up close. And no amount of
buzzwords will fix that.
Measuring Deserts Doesn't Save Lives. Acting Does.
We’ve gotten good at measuring the social determinants of health. We
know who is struggling — which zip codes have higher poverty, which
neighborhoods have worse access, which towns are losing pharmacies the
fastest.
But what good are statistics if we don't act on them?
Published

For too many in healthcare, social determinants of health have become
news stories and conference talking points — drama to highlight, not
devastation to fix.
For the families being measured, it’s not academic. It’s not a case study.
It’s survival.
They don’t need another report telling them how sick or underserved they
are. They need real action — funding, protection, and policy that restores
access instead of just documenting its loss.
Compliance Isn’t Rational in a Desert.
We love to talk about medication adherence. About patients "complying"
with care plans.
But let’s be honest: There’s no rational adherence in a healthcare desert.
You can’t pick up a prescription from a pharmacy that no longer
exists.
You can’t manage a chronic illness if refilling life-saving medication
requires taking a day off work and driving 50 miles.
You can’t stay on therapy when you’re forced to choose between
buying food, paying rent, or affording medicine.
Nonadherence isn’t about irresponsibility. It’s about inaccessibility.
Until we fix the deserts, no amount of lectures about patient responsibility
will matter. Let's Call It What It Is.
Pharmacy deserts didn't appear out of nowhere. They were created by:
PBMs squeezing reimbursement to the bone.
Chain pharmacy closures prioritizing profit over people.
Government inaction that measured the problem but never
addressed it.
And every year we keep “talking” about pharmacy deserts while doing
nothing, more Americans lose access to the healthcare they need to
survive.
So, Here’s the Real Question:
If we’ve known about pharmacy deserts since 2014, If
we’ve watched them grow worse, If we’ve seen record
profits for the corporations who helped create them...
Why has nothing changed?
Is it because solving the problem doesn’t make anyone rich? Or because
too many powerful players benefit from keeping it broken? Published

Bottom Line:
You can’t fix healthcare deserts by studying them. You can’t fix pharmacy
deserts by measuring them. You can’t fix access by preaching buzzwords
while abandoning real communities.
If we truly want to improve healthcare access in America, We have to stop
measuring the cracks — and start rebuilding the foundation.
Until then, patients will keep paying the price — and corporations will
keep counting the profits.
The next time someone publishes a report about
“pharmacy deserts,” ask them one simple question:
How many more have appeared since we started
talking about it — and when are we going to stop
highlighting the pain — and actually fix it?
