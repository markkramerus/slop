The Human Cost of Healthcare
Money, Politics, and Healthcare: The Overlooked Human Cost
In the U.S., healthcare is a multi-billion-dollar industry—one that has
grown increasingly driven by business and political interests. Decisions
about who receives care, at what price, and under which conditions are
often determined in corporate boardrooms or by legislative bargaining,
with profits at the core. But in the race to cut costs and secure profits, one
crucial factor is repeatedly sidelined: the human cost. In an industry
fueled by dollars and decisions made far from patient rooms, it’s the
people on the ground who bear the real burden. After 25 years of
navigating this system, I know all too well the price paid by patients left to
bear the weight of rising premiums, out-of-network restrictions, and
inaccessible treatments.
We were promised affordable, accessible care through the Affordable
Care Act (ACA), yet for most people, the reality is anything but. Behind
every dollar saved, every contract terminated, and every policy adjusted,
real people are left to cope with the aftermath. For those of us juggling
jobs, caregiving, or managing chronic conditions, the ACA’s intentions feel
far removed from the daily struggles of navigating an often-hostile
system.
Concrete Realities: What We're Actually Facing
In theory, the ACA was meant to streamline and simplify access to
healthcare. But in practice, many people face a very different reality. While
the media often portrays the ACA as a success story of affordable care,
the truth on the ground tells another story. Premiums have climbed
steadily across much of the country, leaving families with monthly bills
that don’t feel affordable at all. Insurance networks, once seen as a lifeline,
are now tightening, meaning more people find their preferred doctors or Draft - saved
hospitals suddenly "out of network." Even when coverage is there, high

deductibles force many to pay thousands out-of-pocket before any real
benefits kick in. These challenges are not hypothetical; they’re the day-to-
day struggles of millions of families.
The Emotional Cost
The ACA’s impact isn’t just financial; it’s deeply personal. Each extra dollar
spent, each bureaucratic hurdle faced, takes a toll on those managing a
chronic illness. Families who thought the ACA would simplify their lives
often find themselves exhausted, demoralized, and anxious about next
month’s bills. It’s a system that often seems to prioritize savings over
people, leaving real families feeling unsupported and vulnerable.
A Need for Change
The ACA was a bold step toward reimagining healthcare in America, but
it's time to recognize that the current system still fails many of us. We
need adjustments that reflect not just economic efficiencies but also the
real needs of patients and families. Moving forward, healthcare reform
must focus on creating a system that doesn’t just promise care but
delivers it—consistently, affordably, and compassionately. Such reform
would especially benefit those most vulnerable: families, caregivers, and
those managing chronic conditions who are caught in the current
system’s gaps.
Living in the Gap: Real-Life Examples of the ACA’s Human Cost
The Affordable Care Act (ACA) aimed to bring healthcare to millions more
people. Despite the ACA’s mission, countless patients find themselves in a
costly game of tug-of-war between insurers and providers, often forced
to choose between keeping their trusted doctors or paying exorbitant out-
of-pocket costs. Here are some recent examples from around the country
that reveal how hospital and insurer disputes continue to make access to
affordable healthcare tenuous:
Vanderbilt University Medical Center and Blue Cross BlueShield of
TN and UnitedHealthcare: Many Tennesseans face the painful
choice between switching providers to keep trusted care or
incurring higher out-of-pocket costs. This choice is especially painful
for those with chronic conditions who depend on familiar healthcare
teams.
Huntsville Hospital Health System and United Healthcare: Alabama
families may lose access to their regular healthcare providers due to
unresolved contract negotiations, leading to potentially higher fees
and interrupted care.
WellSpan Health and UnitedHealthcare in Pennsylvania: For
Medicare patients, WellSpan’s negotiation breakdown threatens
their access to essential, long-standing care. The cost of losing
continuity for elderly or chronically ill patients is far more than
financial.
UF Health Flagler Hospital and Aetna in Florida: Patients now face
the decision of traveling further to access care within network or
paying more to stay local, creating an undue burden, particularly for

Why Patients Deserve Control—and How We Can Start
These stories expose a harsh truth: the current system puts profits,
contracts, and corporate interests ahead of what should be its true
purpose—taking care of people. When insurers and hospitals negotiate,
patients’ needs are often an afterthought. But we can’t wait for the system
to change without pushing for it ourselves. Here’s what we, as patients,
can do to start taking back control:
Understand Your Rights and Options: Every state has different rules
about network coverage and ongoing treatments. For example,
many states require coverage continuity if a patient is mid-
treatment. This is critical knowledge for someone with chronic
conditions that need stable, ongoing management.
Fight for Out-of-Network Coverage: If you find yourself losing in-
network access, you can appeal to your insurance for temporary out-
of-network rates. Knowing these rights can be the difference
between keeping and losing essential care continuity.
Request Continuity of Care Agreements: Many providers offer
temporary agreements that allow ongoing treatments to continue at
in-network rates while contracts are being negotiated. Knowing to
ask for these agreements can prevent major disruptions in care.
Explore Financial Assistance Programs: Hospitals and insurers
sometimes offer financial help for out-of-pocket costs tied to
contract disputes. For a family with limited resources, these
programs can bridge the gap and make a big difference.
Stay Informed: Hospitals and insurers aren’t always transparent
about their negotiations. By calling and requesting updates or
asking directly about provider status, we can push for more
transparency in these processes.
Advocate Through Patient Organizations: Groups like the National
Patient Advocate Foundation work to amplify patient voices in
policy. Joining these organizations or even following their updates
can provide a sense of agency and resources that make navigating
healthcare easier.
Review Insurance Options Annually: During open enrollment,
carefully check if there’s a risk your provider could go out of
network. Planning ahead can help families avoid some of these
conflicts and stay with the care teams they trust.

For those of us who have spent decades in this system, the question of
“when is enough, enough?” becomes pressing. How many times must we
fight to keep our providers or pay more for essential treatments before
the system listens? Healthcare is about more than balancing sheets or
winning political points; it’s about lives, relationships, and health journeys.
Patients are more than a line item in a budget or a checkbox in a policy—
each disrupted treatment and every unaddressed need carry a human
cost.
Each time a senior loses a trusted doctor because of new network
restrictions, or a family faces the impossible choice between paying rent
and affording medication, the toll deepens. Declining health. Lost trust.
Destabilized lives. This isn’t just a policy failure; it’s a human cost we can’t
keep paying.
A Call for Patient-Centered Change
The time has come for healthcare to truly serve its purpose: to care for
people. Real change will only happen when patients’ voices are placed
front and center, making sure that policies serve us—not just the
corporate or political bottom line. Reach out to local representatives.
Support advocacy groups pushing for patient-first policies. Share your
own healthcare story to raise awareness. Together, we can push for a
healthcare model that values people over profits.
Because when it comes to our health, our families, our lives—when is
enough, enough?
