Your Health Data Should Serve You,
Not Profits—Because Yes, It’s All
Patients, What You Should Know About Health Data Sharing and Why
Transparency Matters
In today’s healthcare system, one of the biggest promises is that our
health information will move smoothly from one doctor or hospital to
another, helping us receive better, more coordinated care. Networks like
the CommonWell Health Alliance and new initiatives like TEFCA (Trusted
Exchange Framework and Common Agreement) aim to make this vision a
reality. But recent developments in health data exchange—and a
troubling lack of transparency around data breaches—reveal why we need
a more patient-centered approach. Patients deserve more than just
promises; we deserve transparency and, ultimately, control over our own
health data.
The Evolving World of Health Information Networks and
QHINs
For years, networks like the CommonWell Health Alliance have been
building systems to securely connect healthcare providers and prioritize
patient needs. CommonWell, often regarded as a neutral player, focuses
on linking systems in ways that serve patients without ulterior motives.
Recently, CommonWell became a Qualified Health Information Network
(QHIN) under the new TEFCA framework, joining other major players like
Epic, Oracle Health (formerly Cerner), and Surescripts.
A QHIN, in simple terms, is a trusted, centralized hub that allows different
healthcare systems to connect and securely share patient data across state
lines and health networks. These networks act as "bridges" between
healthcare providers, ensuring that your health information can follow you

wherever you go for care. The TEFCA framework is designed to
standardize data exchange across these QHINs, potentially making it

easier for healthcare providers to access and share your health
information.
However, the entry of large, for-profit corporations into the QHIN space
raises critical questions about patient control. While CommonWell has
built a reputation for neutrality, some QHINs might prioritize business
interests, shifting the focus away from patient-centered care. Patients
must ask: Who really controls our data, and who benefits from it? This is
especially concerning as health data becomes a valuable asset not only
for care but also for marketing and corporate profit.
The Change Healthcare Data Breach: A Cautionary Tale
To understand why patient control over health data is so crucial, consider
the recent data breach at Change Healthcare, a major healthcare payment
processing company. On February 21, 2024, Change Healthcare
experienced a ransomware attack, compromising the sensitive health data
of at least 100 million Americans. Although no one at Change Healthcare
intended for this breach to occur, it happened. Yet, information about it
wasn’t widely publicized, and patients didn’t receive immediate
notification. Change Healthcare didn’t begin mailing notices to affected
individuals until late July—months after the initial incident. And even then,
patients were left without clear guidance on what this breach meant for
them or how they should protect themselves.
This delay and limited media coverage expose a serious gap in how
companies communicate with patients about data security. If we aren’t
informed about why breaches happen or what risks we face, we’re left in a
vulnerable position, unable to protect ourselves. When health companies
fail to communicate transparently, they not only leave us exposed but also
miss an opportunity to help us understand the importance of our own
data security.
Why Recent Healthcare Mergers Matter
Data breaches aren’t the only risk; mergers between healthcare
organizations are another concern. In recent years, we’ve seen a rapid
increase in healthcare mergers, with many health data exchanges,
technology companies, and health systems combining forces. These
mergers often promise seamless, integrated care, but the reality is more
complex. Merging large datasets from different organizations can create
new security risks, making it easier for hackers to find vulnerabilities. And
when things go wrong, it’s our privacy that’s at stake—not the companies’.
The effects of these mergers are real, and patients are often the ones who
suffer the consequences. With each merger, our data is moved,
integrated, and combined in ways we’re rarely informed about. When
something goes wrong, it’s our privacy and peace of mind that are
compromised. Patients shouldn’t have to bear the consequences of these
complex business moves without transparency or control over where our
information goes and how it’s used.
Why We Need to Be Proactive
As these shifts continue in health data sharing, it’s important for patients
to recognize that while these changes aim to improve data exchange, they
also increase security risks. Yet, after a breach, we are often left to protect
ourselves, with limited support or resources to understand what’s at risk

or how to respond. This makes it even more critical to stay vigilant and
involved, asking who has access to our data and how it’s being used.

What Can We Do?
Here are a few steps patients can take to safeguard their data and
advocate for better transparency:
Stay Informed: Learn about the companies and organizations that
hold your health data, especially if they’re involved in recent
mergers or acquisitions.
Ask Questions: Don’t hesitate to ask your healthcare providers or
health plans about their data protection measures. You have a right
to know how your data is managed and secured.
Advocate for Transparent Communication: Patients deserve
prompt, honest updates if their information is compromised. Insist
on transparency from the companies that hold your data.
Support Data Privacy Legislation: Stay informed about and support
legislation aimed at protecting patient data. The Health Insurance
Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) provides some
safeguards, but it’s worth pushing for updated protections as
technology advances.
Moving Forward: Why Patient Control Matters
These ongoing changes in health data sharing highlight why patients
need control over their data. Imagine being able to access your health
records whenever you want, choose who can see them, and understand
exactly how they’re used. When we have this level of control, we’re
empowered to make choices that align with our needs and values—not a
company’s bottom line. This isn’t just about convenience; it’s about
protecting our privacy, our peace of mind, and our right to make
decisions about our health.
We’re all patients at some point—whether it’s us, our children, our
parents, or someone we care deeply about. We deserve a system that
values transparency, respect, and the right to control our own health data.
Imagine a future where patients aren’t just passive participants but active
managers of their health information, empowered to protect themselves
and their loved ones.
As the landscape of health data sharing continues to evolve, staying
informed and advocating for our own privacy is more important than
ever. The future of healthcare should be one that empowers patients—not
just providers and corporations.
After all, we should be the ones writing the story of our own health—
because yes, it really is all about you!
