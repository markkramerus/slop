Challenges Faced by Individuals Managing Health Issues For themselves, aging parents, children and more. They are dealing with: 1.Emotional Stress 2.Physical Exhaustion 3.Administrative Load (the silent burden often overlooked) The KinKeeper is the person in their circle of friends and family (their Kith + Kin) who is managing all of this information for themselves or a loved one 
The KinKeepers’ Dilemma Care extends far beyond the four walls of traditional healthcare systems Healthcare is no longer tied to one provider or payer - telehealth, psychiatry, urgent care, non-traditional medicine and more 3 Care is multi-generational People are caring for themselves, spouses, children in addition to their aging parents Care circles transcend traditional nuclear family structures One-to-one “patient” solutions don’t reflect the modern family circles dealing with health issues 
The KinKeepers’ Common Need All of their people and all of their info, all in one place A place for information, not just data To coordinate care and share different information with different people the right information at the right place at the right time They Need to Share Information . . . and Share the Load

The Impact AARP reports 61% of US KinKeepers are currently employed. American Academy of Physicians reports those with chronic conditions spend 2 hours per day managing that condition and CNBC reports those caring for others spend nearly double that at 4.3 hours per day. Satisfaction Reduced Time Pressure Reduced Burnout Improved Health Outcomes The CDC reports 60% of Americans have at least one chronic condition. American Psychological Assoc notes 66% report one or more adverse mental health effects due to managing health issues, 30.7% report thoughts of suicide and 57% of family caregivers report high levels of stress, anxiety, and/or depression. 83% report time savings 88% report reduced stress 91% report more confidence An American Academy of Family Medicine study showed that improved health confidence led to lower use of hospital care. The CDC reports growing evidence that people with higher skills and confidence to become actively engaged in their healthcare have better health outcomes.
