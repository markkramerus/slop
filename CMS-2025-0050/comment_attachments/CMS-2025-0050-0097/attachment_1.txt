Tennr's Impact at Scale Amidst New RADV Audit Enhancements:
The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) is responsible for auditing Medicare
Advantage risk adjustment to ensure plans are paid correctly. The primary audit mechanism is
the RAD program. In a RADV audit, CMS selects a sample of enrollees from a given MA
contract and requires the MA Organization (MAO) to submit the medical records for those
beneficiaries to validate the diagnoses billed. Auditors verify each HCC-driving diagnosis in the
sample against the patient’s medical record documentation. If a diagnosis (e.g., diabetes with
complications) that the plan submitted to CMS is not actually supported by the record (for
example, the chart lacks evidence of that condition or proper documentation of management), it
is flagged as an unsupported diagnosis. CMS then recalculates the sample enrollees’ risk
scores with those unsupported codes removed, determining how much the plan was overpaid
for each sampled member. Historically, CMS would recover overpayments only for the sampled
beneficiaries, but not extrapolate to the full contract population. Recent CMS announcements
indicate a significant strengthening of RADV audits, dramatically increasing their pace and
scale. Starting with Payment Year 2018 audits, CMS will extrapolate sample audit findings to the

entire plan contract, meaning overpayments identified in a sample can lead to recoupment
across the entire contract's Part C payments. Furthermore, CMS plans to:
● Audit all eligible MA contracts annually.
● Review as many as 200 records per MA plan each year, a substantial increase from the
previous 35 records.
● Invest in enhanced technology and expanded human resources, including increasing its
medical coder team to approximately 2,000 by September 1, 2025, and utilizing
"enhanced technology" systems to streamline medical record review for unsupported
diagnoses.

a. What additional data would be most valuable if made available through CMS APIs?

health management and care navigation applications. Tennr's Intake and Qualifications
services, powered by RaeLM™, are designed to extract and structure this type of
complex, often unstructured, clinical information. This data is crucial for CMS's RADV

● Medicare Local Coverage Determinations (LCDs) and National Coverage

● Data Siloing and Fragmentation: Information often resides in disparate systems
(EHRs, payer systems, HIEs) with limited seamless exchange.
● Lack of Native Interoperability in Legacy Systems and Prohibitive API Costs: Many
healthcare ERPs/EMRs currently in use by providers either lack native interoperability
capabilities (APIs) or offer them at a prohibitively high cost. This perpetuates data siloing
and forces providers to rely on error-prone manual data entry as patients traverse from
one provider to the next.
● Lack of Granularity and Standardization: Even when data is available, it often lacks
the necessary granularity or adheres to inconsistent standards, making it difficult to
aggregate and utilize effectively. Tennr's RaeLM™ model is specifically trained to

data from unstructured sources, including transforming provider narrative descriptions of
patient diagnoses and symptoms into standardized ICD-10 codes with high accuracy.
This capability directly addresses a key obstacle for CMS in validating diagnoses from
diverse documentation during RADV audits.
● Lack of API Access to Current Medicare LCDs/NCDs: The absence of standardized,
machine-readable APIs for Medicare Local Coverage Determinations (LCDs) and
National Coverage Determinations (NCDs) forces manual interpretation and integration
by providers and technology vendors, creating a significant burden and delays in
medical necessity determinations.
● Technical Complexity of Integration: Integrating with numerous proprietary systems or
non-standard APIs is resource-intensive for developers.
● Privacy and Security Concerns: Navigating complex privacy regulations and ensuring
secure data exchange can be a significant barrier.
● Information Blocking Practices: As noted in TD-18, practices that hinder access,
exchange, or use of EHI remain a significant obstacle.
TD-9. Regarding certification of health IT:

a. What are the benefits of redefining certification to prioritize API-enabled capabilities
over software functionality?
● True Interoperability: Prioritizing API-enabled capabilities shifts the focus from merely
having data to ensure that data can flow seamlessly and be used by a diverse
ecosystem of applications. This moves beyond siloed functionalities within a single
software.
● Accelerated Innovation: By guaranteeing robust API access, certification would
unleash innovation by enabling third-party developers, like Tennr, to build specialized
applications that leverage health data without needing to deeply integrate with
proprietary EHR internals. This innovation can lead to better tools for MAOs to prepare
for RADV audits, and for CMS to use directly.
● Reduced Vendor Lock-in and Increased Competition: A strong emphasis on
standardized APIs allows healthcare organizations to choose best-of-breed solutions for
specific workflows, rather than being confined to a single vendor's ecosystem, promoting
market competition.
● Focus on Data Liquidity: The primary goal becomes ensuring the fluid and secure
movement of health information, rather than just the presence of certain features within a
software system. For CMS, this means a more reliable and accessible data stream for
audit purposes.
b. What would be the drawbacks?
● Potential for Neglecting Core Clinical Usability: An overemphasis on APIs could
inadvertently lead to less attention on the direct usability and efficiency of the core EHR's
clinical workflows for providers.
● Complexity of Comprehensive API Standards: Defining and maintaining API
standards that cover all aspects of a patient's chart (including unstructured data) is
technically challenging and requires continuous evolution.
● Implementation Burden for Legacy Systems: Older or less technologically advanced
health IT systems may face significant challenges and costs in re-architecting to meet
stringent API-centric certification requirements.
● Need for Robust Security and Privacy Controls: As more data becomes accessible
via APIs, the need for stringent security and privacy controls, and rigorous testing of
these, becomes even more critical.
c. How could ASTP/ONC revise health IT certification criteria to require APIs to
consistently support exchanging data from all aspects of the patient's chart (for example,
faxed records, free text, discrete data)?
● Mandate Data Transformation Capabilities: Require certified health IT to include
capabilities for transforming unstructured data (e.g., scanned documents, free text notes)
into structured, queryable formats accessible via APIs, where clinically appropriate and
technically feasible. This could involve leveraging advanced AI/ML models like Tennr's
RaeLM™ for intelligent data extraction and interpretation from diverse document types,

including the transformation of provider narrative descriptions of patient diagnoses and
symptoms into standardized ICD-10 codes. This capability is directly applicable to CMS's
need to validate diagnoses from diverse medical records during RADV audits.
TD-10. For EHR and other developers subject to the ONC Health IT Certification Program,
what further steps should ASTP/ONC consider to implement the 21st Century Cures Act's
API condition of certification (42 U.S.C. 300jj-11(c)(5)(D)(iv)) that requires a developer's
APIs to allow health information to be accessed, exchanged, and used without special
effort, including providing access to all data elements of a patient's electronic health
record to the extent permissible under applicable privacy laws?
To fully realize the "without special effort" mandate of the 21st Century Cures Act, ASTP/ONC
should consider these further steps:
● Concrete Definition of "Without Special Effort": Move beyond a conceptual definition
to provide specific, measurable criteria for API usability, developer experience, and data
access. This could include metrics for API response times, clear error handling, and
intuitive data models.
● Mandate Comprehensive EHI Access: Explicitly require APIs to expose all data
elements of a patient's electronic health record, including structured, unstructured (e.g.,
free text notes, scanned documents), and image data, to the extent permissible under
privacy laws. Tennr's Intake and Document Wrangling capabilities demonstrate the
technical feasibility and value of extracting actionable data from these diverse formats.
● Standardized Data Transformation Requirements: For unstructured data, establish
requirements or best practices for how this data should be transformed or referenced via
APIs to ensure usability by downstream applications. This could involve mandating the
use of specific FHIR resources for document references or encouraging the use of AI/ML
for extracting key concepts into structured fields.
● Enhanced API Documentation and Developer Tools: Require certified developers to
provide comprehensive, machine-readable, and human-readable API documentation,
including detailed data element definitions, example queries, and robust software
development kits (SDKs).

TD-16. What are the tradeoffs of maintaining point-to-point models vs. shared network
infrastructure?

The choice between point-to-point data exchange models and shared network infrastructure
presents significant tradeoffs in terms of scalability, efficiency, and the realization of a truly
interoperable healthcare ecosystem.
Point-to-Point Models:
● Tradeoffs:
○ Limited Scalability: Each new connection requires a separate integration effort,
leading to exponential complexity as the number of participants grows. This
creates a significant administrative and technical burden.
○ Data Siloing: Even with direct connections, data often remains fragmented
across multiple discrete links, making it difficult to achieve a comprehensive
patient view.
○ High Maintenance Overhead: Managing numerous individual connections, each
with its own specifications and updates, is resource-intensive.
○ Hindered Innovation: The effort required for individual integrations discourages
developers from building applications that need data from multiple sources.
○ Increased Information Blocking Potential: Without a centralized framework,
it's easier for entities to create technical or contractual barriers to data exchange.
● Current Rules Encouraging Scalable Network Participation? Current rules, while
pushing for interoperability, do not sufficiently disincentivize point-to-point models. The
lack of a universally adopted, enforced, and easy-to-implement shared infrastructure
means organizations often default to individual agreements, which are less scalable.
Shared Network Infrastructure (e.g., TEFCA, QHINs):
● Tradeoffs:
○ Enhanced Scalability: A single connection to a trusted network allows access to
a vast number of participants, significantly reducing individual integration efforts.
○ Comprehensive Data Access: Facilitates a more holistic view of patient data
across different providers and care settings, crucial for care coordination and
population health.
○ Reduced Burden: Lowers the technical and administrative burden on individual
organizations by centralizing exchange mechanisms and governance.
○ Fosters Innovation: Enables developers to build applications that can access
data from a wide range of sources through a single, standardized entry point.
○ Improved Data Quality and Trust: Common rules and technical requirements
within a shared infrastructure can lead to more consistent data quality and
increased trust among participants.
● Changes to Improve Alignment (for scalable network participation):
○ API Unification and Standardization: CMS and ASTP/ONC should
aggressively promote and enforce the adoption of unified, standards-based APIs
(e.g., FHIR) across all shared network infrastructures. This ensures consistency
regardless of the underlying network.

○ Reciprocal Access Requirements: Mandate reciprocal data access, where
participants in a network are obligated to both send and receive data, preventing
one-sided data hoarding.
○ Clear Governance and Trust Frameworks: Establish robust and transparent
governance models for shared networks, including clear rules of the road, dispute
resolution mechanisms, and security policies.
○ Incentives for Network Participation: Provide strong financial and non-financial
incentives for providers, payers, and technology vendors to join and actively
participate in designated shared network infrastructure.
○ Technical Assistance and Onboarding Support: Offer resources and support
to help organizations, especially smaller practices, easily connect to and utilize
shared network infrastructure.
Tennr's platform, with its ability to intelligently process and route information from various
sources, is ideally positioned to thrive within a shared network infrastructure. By reducing the
manual effort of data wrangling and intake, Tennr can help organizations maximize the value
derived from comprehensive data exchange across such networks.
TD-18. Information blocking:
Information blocking remains a significant impediment to achieving a truly interoperable and
patient-centric healthcare ecosystem. As a technology vendor focused on automating
healthcare operations through data, Tennr frequently encounters practices that constitute
information blocking, both through non-responsiveness and the provision of unusable data.
a. Could you, as a technology vendor, provide examples for the types of practices you
have experienced that may constitute information blocking. Please include both
situations of non-responsiveness as well as situations that may cause a failure or
unusable response?
● Situations of Non-Responsiveness:
○ Delayed or Ignored Requests for Information: Despite automated outreach via
Tennr's Communication Coordination product for missing patient information
(e.g., unsigned orders, missing insurance cards, complete referrals), some
external providers or facilities may consistently fail to respond within reasonable
timeframes or at all, directly delaying patient care and administrative processes.
○ Lack of Accessibility to Patient Portals/APIs: While theoretically available,
some patient portals or provider APIs are difficult to discover, lack clear
documentation, or have cumbersome onboarding processes for third-party
applications, effectively hindering access.
● Situations Causing Failure or Unusable Response:
○ Provision of Incomplete or Fragmented Data: Receiving only partial patient
records or fragmented documents (e.g., a multi-page fax where critical pages are
missing or out of order), which requires significant manual effort to piece together

and often results in insufficient information for Tennr's Referral Intake or Clinical
Qualification product to complete their automated processes.
○ Poor Quality or Unreadable Documents: Receiving scanned documents or
faxes that are illegible, skewed, or contain significant artifacts, making it difficult
even for advanced OCR and AI and machine learning models like Tennr's
RaeLM™ to extract data accurately. This necessitates manual review and
correction, negating the benefits of automation.
○ API Responses Lacking Granularity or Context: While an API might
technically exist, the data returned may be too high-level, lack necessary context,
or omit critical discrete data elements required for automated workflows (e.g.,
specific CPT codes, detailed diagnoses, or precise dates needed for
Qualifications).
○ Excessive Fees or Burdensome Agreements: Imposing exorbitant fees or
overly restrictive data use agreements for accessing EHI, even when technically
feasible, acts as a significant deterrent. This is particularly true for payer prior
authorization rules and requirements, which are often difficult to access
programmatically, lack standardization, and are siloed, leading to ambiguity and
hindering automated medical necessity determinations.
b. What additional policies could ASTP/ONC and CMS implement to further discourage
healthcare providers from engaging in information blocking practices?
● Clearer Definitions and Examples: Provide more granular definitions and concrete
examples of what constitutes information blocking across various scenarios, particularly
regarding data quality, completeness, and responsiveness.
● Stricter Enforcement and Escalating Penalties: Implement more robust enforcement
mechanisms and a tiered system of escalating penalties for confirmed information
blocking violations, ensuring consequences are significant enough to deter the practice.
● Proactive Audits and Monitoring: Conduct proactive audits of data exchange practices
and monitor API usage patterns to identify potential information blocking behaviors,
rather than relying solely on reactive complaints.
● Incentives for Proactive Data Sharing: Introduce positive incentives (e.g., financial
bonuses, public recognition) for providers who consistently demonstrate exemplary data
sharing practices and actively facilitate interoperability.
● Mandatory Transparency of Data Exchange Capabilities: Require providers and
health IT vendors to publicly attest to and describe their data exchange capabilities,
including supported APIs, data elements available, and typical response times for data
requests.
● Streamlined Complaint Process: Further simplify and publicize the information
blocking complaint process for patients, caregivers, and technology vendors, ensuring it
is accessible and responsive.
