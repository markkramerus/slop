Delivering Healthcare Abundance – Safety-Focused and Scalable.
Recommendation: CMS should consider establishing a Digital Health Sandbox and voluntary
“Safe Harbor” certification for non-diagnostic, safety-tested AI health agents.
Rather than traditional FDA-style approvals, this would be a CMS-led validation program to
review digital health products for efficacy, quality, and safety in improving outcomes. Hippocratic
AI strongly supports such a framework. We have demonstrated through a rigorous Real-World
Evaluation that generative AI agents can achieve safety parity with clinicians. In over 307,000
patient-agent interactions, our AI provided correct medical advice 99.38% of the time without
making a single error that could have caused severe patient harm. By engaging over 6,200 licensed
clinicians to test our AI across iterative improvements, we eliminated major safety concerns and
reached human-level accuracy. A CMS sandbox could allow developers to pilot similarly safety-
optimized AI tools on Medicare use cases (e.g. post-discharge follow-ups, care navigation,
preventative care education) under real-world conditions. High-performing solutions that emerge
from the sandbox would earn a “Safe Harbor” designation – giving providers and ACOs
confidence to adopt them without fear of regulatory uncertainty.
Criteria for this safe harbor might include: robust clinical testing (e.g. better than human accuracy,
no critical errors), transparency of algorithms, FHIR-based data exchange, and ongoing monitoring

for quality improvement. This voluntary pathway would spur developer interest (TD-1) by
providing clear guidelines and a stamp of credibility, and it would increase patient and provider
trust in AI-enabled digital health (PC-5a) by ensuring only validated, safety-first tools are
scaled.
(PC-5b & PC-6) Promoting Prevention and Equitable Access. To drive patient and
caregiver adoption of digital health (PC-5b), CMS should leverage these safe, user-friendly AI
agents and incentivize their use in preventive care programs. Hippocratic AI’s agents have proven
effective in motivational health coaching – for example, our multilingual AI “Ana” conducted
outreach calls that significantly boosted colorectal cancer screening rates among Spanish-speaking
Medicare patients. By speaking the patient’s preferred language and using empathetic, culturally-
tailored dialogue, the AI achieved FIT-test opt-in rates 2.6× higher for Spanish speakers than for
English speakers. Notably, many of these beneficiaries had limited prior experience with patient
portals or apps. The key to engagement was meeting patients where they are – via a simple phone
call in their own language – and building trust through empathy. The AI agent “Ana” explains the
importance of preventive screening, listens to patient concerns, and helps arrange at-home test kits,
mirroring the compassionate tone of a nurse. This illustrates features critical to making digital
health products accessible and easy to use for Medicare beneficiaries (PC-6): multi-language
support (14+ languages), voice-based interfaces, and human-like empathy. Unlike smartphone
apps that some seniors may struggle to navigate, a telephone-based AI companion can reach even
those with low digital literacy.
CMS could support pilot programs where such AI-driven preventive outreach is deployed at scale
for chronic disease management, prevention and screenings. By empowering individuals in their
health goals with friendly, always-available coaching, we can improve prevention and wellness
across diverse populations. Early results show tangible outcomes – e.g. our AI-driven wellness
calls for congestive heart failure patients saw 96% completion rates and a 4.6% reduction in 30-
day readmissions (internal data), indicating fewer complications when patients receive consistent
follow-up. Importantly, these agents also capture patient-reported outcomes like PHQ-9
depression screenings and Social Determinants of Health during conversations, feeding that data
back to care teams to inform comprehensive holistic care plans.
In short, safety-focused AI can be a force-multiplier for prevention and health equity, helping
achieve the “healthcare abundance” vision where every patient gets the guidance they need to
stay healthy.

(PR-1) Reducing Burden on Providers and Care Teams. Generative AI health agents
offer a promising solution to relieve overextended providers by handling routine patient outreach,
2

education, and data gathering. To encourage provider uptake of these tools, the safety and quality
must be assured (as addressed above) and workflow integration must be seamless. Current
obstacles (PR-1a) include provider concern about liability or accuracy of AI recommendations,
and lack of time to manage yet another IT tool. We address these by keeping clinicians “in the
loop” as needed. For instance, our AI agents focus strictly on non-diagnostic tasks (post-discharge
check-ins, Annual Wellness Visit intakes, preventive reminders) and generate a full transcript or
summary of each patient encounter for the provider’s review. If any red flags or urgent issues are
detected during an AI call, the system can escalate to a human nurse or physician in real-time (e.g.
warm transfer to a telehealth nurse). This ensures the provider remains informed and in control of
the patient’s care plan – the AI is an extender, not an independent decision-maker. Providers should
disclose to patients when a digital agent is assisting in their care and clarify the agent’s role and
limitations (PR-1b). For example, at call commencement our agent introduces itself as a virtual
assistant working with the care team, so the patient knows they are interacting with an AI.
Providers have a responsibility (PR-1c) to vet that the AI tool is appropriate for the patient (e.g.
only using agents that are CMS-vetted/Safe Harbor-certified for safety), and to monitor outcomes
(reviewing transcripts or alerts). In practice, however, the burden on providers is minimal – over
95% of our AI-guided patient calls run on “auto-pilot” without needing human intervention, and
the notes/transcripts are stored in the EHR for later reference rather than demanding immediate
physician time. By outsourcing routine but time-intensive tasks to AI – medication check-ins,
lifestyle coaching, scheduling follow-ups – clinicians can refocus on complex care. This can
meaningfully reduce burnout and administrative burden. One large health system noted that our
AI completed hundreds of post-discharge calls that staff would otherwise struggle to perform, yet
patients rated the experience 8.95/10 in satisfaction, comparable to human interactions. In
summary, clear governance (disclosure, oversight) and proven safety outcomes will give providers
confidence to delegate certain care tasks to AI, lightening their load while keeping care quality
high.
(PR-2 & PR-4) Integrating AI into Clinical Workflows. Key obstacles hindering
deployment of innovative digital applications in provider workflows (PR-2) are lack of
interoperability with clinical systems and unclear reimbursement models. To truly streamline
documentation or quality reporting via AI, the agents must plug into EHRs and other health IT
systems. We recommend CMS promote standards and policies that enable third-party digital
tools to perform administrative workflows (PR-4) – for example, open scheduling APIs so an
agent can book or modify appointments on a patient’s behalf, or SMART on FHIR integration that
allows an AI to auto-populate intake forms directly into the EHR. Today, many of these workflows
(scheduling, form intake) are locked behind proprietary portals or require manual staff entry. By
working with ONC to expand FHIR® API use cases to cover scheduling, referrals, and other
administrative data, CMS can unlock innovation. Our AI agents, for instance, could automatically
update a patient’s care plan or flag a quality measure gap in the EHR after a call – but this is
feasible only if vendor systems provide the hooks for write-back. We applaud CMS’s inclusion of
the Provider Access API and other new FHIR endpoints in recent interoperability rules, and
encourage continued focus on data liquidity. Dual-purpose investments that enable data reuse will
also mitigate provider burden (PR-2): when an AI gathers patient information (e.g. home blood
pressure readings or social needs), sending that data via FHIR to both the clinical team and a
quality reporting database means the work serves care delivery and reporting needs
simultaneously. CMS could further stimulate integration by funding pilot programs or
challenges for EHR vendors to develop “plug-and-play” API modules for common workflows
3

(appointments, forms, etc.), specifically to support digital health apps (including AI agents).
Removing technical barriers will invite a wave of innovative tools that make providers’ lives easier
by automating documentation and coordination tasks that currently sap clinical productivity.
(TD-2 & PC-9) Data as Fuel for Innovation – Expanding CMS APIs. Hippocratic AI
leverages both clinical and claims data to tailor our agents’ conversations to each patient. CMS’s
Blue Button 2.0 API has been a foundational resource by providing OAuth access to Medicare
claims (Parts A, B, D) for patient-facing apps. However, to truly unleash innovation (TD-2),
additional data should be made available via API beyond the current scope (PC-9). We recommend
CMS prioritize opening the following data through secure APIs: (a) Social risk and SDOH data
– for example, ICD-10 Z-codes for social needs or entries from health risk assessments. These help
an AI agent understand if a patient has transportation barriers, food insecurity, or other factors so
it can respond with appropriate resources (or escalate to a human social worker if needed). (b) Lab
results and vital signs – Medicare claims include procedural codes for tests but not the result
values. Having API access to key lab results (e.g. A1c, lipid levels from outpatient labs) and vital
signs recorded during visits would enable richer, more personalized coaching by digital tools. For
instance, an AI could congratulate a diabetic patient on an improved A1c or counsel them if a value
is trending poorly, using factual data pulled from claims/EHR. (c) Medication fill data including
OTC usage – Blue Button includes Part D prescription claims; additionally incorporating known
over-the-counter medications or supplements (perhaps via supplemental beneficiary surveys or
integrations) could help identify potential adherence issues or interactions. (d) Provider
information and appointment data – an API that exposes a beneficiary’s upcoming appointments,
or at least the ability to query their providers’ scheduling systems (with consent), would allow AI
assistants to help with appointment reminders and even rescheduling or finding open slots.
Currently, a patient’s interactions with scheduling are siloed per provider. A unified approach
could be for CMS to work with provider directories and Health Information Exchanges so that a
patient-authorized app can fetch their upcoming visits. In general, CMS should consider blending
claims data with selected clinical and patient-generated data in its API offerings – possibly
leveraging the USCDI data classes as a guide (TD-2b). Our agents also connect to electronic health
record systems via HL7® v2 feeds and FHIR® resources where available, but smaller providers
may not have enabled those. CMS has an opportunity to fill data gaps by expanding Blue Button.
Making more data available (with privacy safeguards) will not only spur developer creativity but
also directly empower patients – they benefit when their apps and AI services have the full picture
of their health (PC-9). We believe adding these data types to CMS APIs, along with timely claims
updates, will accelerate the next generation of highly effective digital health products.
(TD-4 & TD-5) Interoperability and Open Standards. We strongly agree that open,
standards-based APIs are preferable to proprietary systems (TD-4). Hippocratic AI’s platform is
built with interoperability at its core: our Polaris orchestration engine communicates via FHIR®
APIs(R4) to retrieve or update health records, and we conform to standards like HL7® v2 and C-
CDA when exchanging information with hospital systems. All data our agents generate (call notes,
patient-reported metrics, form fill etc.) can be output in standardized formats so it flows into
existing IT systems. We encourage CMS and ONC to continue policies that reward use of public
standards – for example, advancing the requirement that certified EHRs support bulk FHIR data
export (for population health) and SMART on FHIR apps. In our experience, some providers still
resort to custom CSV or flat-file exchanges because certain data (e.g. free-text notes or scanned
documents) isn’t exposed cleanly via API. Certification criteria could be tightened to ensure APIs
4

cover “all aspects of the patient’s chart,” including unstructured data (TD-9c) – perhaps through
emerging standards for text or image exchange.
We support the idea of a nationwide provider FHIR endpoint directory (TD-5) to ease
connections between systems. As a digital health vendor, finding and maintaining connections to
numerous provider EHR endpoints is cumbersome. A centralized directory maintained by a trusted
entity (e.g. ONC or a consortium) would help platforms like ours dynamically discover where to
send data or queries for a given provider or health organization. We believe users would fund such
a utility if it significantly reduces interface costs, though public funding to launch it would be
welcome given the public-good nature.
We also support the idea of a Universal Patient Identifier (UPI) (PC-14) which would be
transformative for Hippocratic AI’s generative agents and for CMS’s broader interoperability
agenda: by giving every Medicare beneficiary a single, privacy‑protected token that is recognized
across hospitals, post‑acute facilities, health plans, and community programs, the AI can reliably
“follow” the patient from one touch‑point to the next, carry forward the precise clinical context of
prior conversations, and reduce incorrect mismatches when names, addresses, or portal log‑ins
change. For Hippocratic AI, this means each engagement—whether a post‑discharge CHF
check‑in, a preventive cancer‑screening reminder, or a social‑needs assessment—can refer to the
correct longitudinal data, allowing the agent to personalize guidance, prevent duplicative outreach,
and maintain the high safety bar (99.38% accuracy) that our RWE‑LLM framework demands. For
CMS and its stakeholders, accurate patient matching via a UPI would sharply cut the $1.5 million
in annual denied‑claim costs per hospital and the $6 billion lost system‑wide to identification
errors, while raising record‑matching rates that can dip to as low as 50 percent when data are shared
across organizations. A UPI therefore underpins safer AI‑driven care navigation, more reliable
quality measurement, and smarter stewardship of taxpayer dollars—essential pillars of the digital
health strategy to Make America Healthy Again.
Finally, Hippocratic AI supports the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement
(TEFCA) network through our integration with EHR vendors. We view TEFCA’s nationwide data
exchange as a complement to FHIR APIs – enabling query/retrieval of clinical records across
different networks (TD-6) and through EHR integrations, we operate within the provider and
care team workflow. While still in nascent stages, TEFCA can fill gaps especially for smaller
providers who aren’t covered by existing networks. In sum, interoperability is critical to both
innovation and patient safety, and we applaud HHS efforts (like USCDI v3 expansion and recent
rules) that push the industry toward seamless, secure data exchange. We encourage CMS to
continue using levers like conditions of participation and payment incentives to drive adoption of
these open standards and continue to seek other innovations in industry to improve data liquidity.
(VB-1) Incentivizing Digital Health in Value-Based Care. Accountable Care
Organizations (ACO) and other APM (Alternative Payment Models) entities have strong
motivation to keep patients healthy and out of the hospital – a perfect alignment for preventive
digital health interventions. However, ACOs often operate on thin margins and may be hesitant to
invest in new technology without clear ROI. CMS can encourage APM participants to leverage
digital health tools by incorporating them into the value-based care incentive structure. For
example, CMS could launch a reimbursement pilot or bonus program for AI-augmented care
management activities. If an ACO uses a certified AI agent to conduct Annual Wellness Visit
5

(AWV) prep calls or chronic disease coaching, those touches could count as billable care
management under a temporary code or as in-kind quality improvement activities. Early evidence
from our deployments suggests significant payoff: one partnership saw a 4.6 percentage-point
reduction in CHF readmissions (contributing to shared savings) when post-discharge AI calls
were added to standard care, and another showed increased colorectal screening rates in
underserved groups, potentially preventing costly cancers down the line. By sharing these savings
or quality gains with ACOs upfront (through model adjustments), CMS would offset the initial
costs of adoption. Another incentive is providing credit in performance evaluations – e.g. if an ACO
can demonstrate that a high percentage of their patients engage with digital health coaching, it could
earn recognition or bonus points in Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) quality scoring.
Current obstacles (VB-1) include limited capital for technology and unclear guidelines on using
such tools (some fear AI might not be allowed or could risk compliance). The Safe Harbor
certification discussed earlier would alleviate compliance concerns by signaling CMS’s support for
these tools. In addition, CMS could facilitate bulk procurement or deployment of proven AI
solutions for smaller ACOs. Similar to how CMS has offered subsidized tools for care coordination
in the past, CMS could negotiate enterprise licenses for vetted AI agents and offer them to rural
ACOs who might not otherwise afford them. By lowering financial and regulatory barriers, CMS
will help APM entities embrace digital health solutions that prevent complications, engage
patients, and ultimately save money for Medicare – aligning with the goal of protecting taxpayer
dollars through smarter care.
(VB-2 & VB-3) Key Technologies for VBC Success. We believe several technologies and
capabilities are essential for value-based care organizations to thrive, many of which are enhanced
by generative AI (VB-2, VB-3):
● Proactive Care Navigation & Coaching: AI agents excel at patient engagement
between visits. In VBC, keeping patients on track – whether it’s medication adherence,
diet/exercise for chronic disease, or follow-up appointments – is crucial. Our platform’s
use of motivational interviewing techniques drives behavior change by eliciting patients’
own goals and barriers. Such personalized coaching can be delivered at scale via AI,
ensuring no patient falls through the cracks. This directly integrates patient engagement
and risk stratification – the AI can be programmed to focus on high-risk patients (recent
discharges, rising risk scores) for more frequent outreach (VB-2). It’s a patient
empowerment tool that complements clinical care (VB-3a: akin to a virtual care
manager).
● Care Planning and Coordination: AI can support dynamic care plans by recording
patients’ progress and new issues in real time. For example, after each AI call, a summary
can be sent to the care team. The care team can suggest followup and actions, which the
AI can communicate back to the patient. This ensures the plan is up-to-date without a nurse
manually calling. The AI can also follow standard advice guidelines to streamline cycles
(e.g., “Patient reports swelling in leg; advised to elevate and call if worsens; will follow up
in 24 hours to see if swelling persists). The AI essentially acts as an extra care team member
who is always available. It can also trigger patient event notifications – e.g. if a patient
indicates they visited the ER, the AI flags the ACO care coordinator immediately. These
functions complement existing EHR capabilities and could be counted as meeting care
coordination requirements in APMs (VB-3a, VB-3b).
6

● Data Integration and Quality Measurement: Value-based models require integrating
claims, clinical, and patient-reported data to get a full picture of outcomes. An AI agent
that interacts with patients can collect patient-generated health data (symptoms, home
readings, satisfaction scores) and feed it into the analytic pipeline. Moreover, by interfacing
with claims (as discussed with Blue Button) and EHR data, the AI can help normalize and
aggregate data for quality reporting. For instance, during an Annual Wellness Visit intake
call, our agent can administer standardized questionnaires (PHQ-9 for depression,
functional status, SDOH surveys) and output structured results that map to quality
measures. This reduces the burden on providers to manually enter data for HEDIS or Stars
measures – the AI has already gathered it. Having these data extraction and normalization
tasks automated (VB-3a) means ACO staff can focus on intervening on the insights rather
than chasing data.
● Patient Attribution and Outreach: AI tools can assist in the tedious work of patient
attribution and panel management by using algorithms to identify which patients are
aligned to which providers or programs and conducting outreach accordingly. For example,
in a complex ACO with multiple clinics, an AI can ensure that every attributed beneficiary
gets a “welcome to our care program” call and help schedule their first visit. This kind of
patient empowerment outreach helps engage patients in the ACO’s services early (VB-3a:
an empowerment tool and care navigation function).
In summary, artificial intelligence, when applied responsibly, can knit together population
health analytics with personalized patient communication (VB-2). The result is a set of
capabilities – from continuous care coaching to automated quality data collection – that
dramatically augment an ACO’s ability to manage health outcomes. CMS should encourage ACOs
to incorporate these AI-driven capabilities by recognizing them in program requirements or
offering flexibility to use them in lieu of more labor-intensive processes. Over time, as these
technologies mature, they will become integral to value-based care operations, enabling providers
to scale up care quality without scaling up cost.
(VB-8) Supplementing CEHRT: Flexibility for Innovation. We appreciate that current
CMS policy allows some flexibility in certified EHR technology (CEHRT) requirements for
APMs, and we encourage even more flexibility to integrate novel digital health tools (VB-8). For
instance, a hospital’s Conditions of Participation now require electronic patient event notifications,
but ACOs could benefit from extending that requirement such that ACO care partners also receive
those notifications (as the RFI notes). We recommend HHS consider broadening data-sharing
mandates to cover APM entities – e.g. when a beneficiary in an ACO is admitted to any hospital,
ensure the ACO’s care team is notified (perhaps via the QHIN/TEFCA network or other
intermediaries). This would empower our AI agents (or other tools) to react promptly, say by
calling the patient post-discharge no matter where they were hospitalized. Additionally, CMS
could allow substituting certain CEHRT functions with certified AI capabilities. If an APM can
demonstrate that a certified AI agent performs a function (for example, patient education or care
planning documentation) as effectively as a CEHRT module, that should “count” toward meeting
technology use requirements. In practice, this might mean creating an alternative compliance
pathway where non-EHR solutions that meet interoperability and safety criteria fulfill the tech
requirement. This would prevent stifling innovation – ACOs wouldn’t be forced to use only
traditional EHR features if a more innovative solution exists. Overall, aligning HHS policies to
support the use of cutting-edge digital health products in value-based models – through expanded
7

data exchange, safe harbors, and flexible tech standards – will better equip APM organizations to
succeed.
