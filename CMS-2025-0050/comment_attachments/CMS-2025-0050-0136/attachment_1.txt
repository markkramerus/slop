 Patient360 | (800) 537-4473 | info@patient360.com | patient360.com BACKGROUND: CMS’ vision for a patient-centric digital health ecosystem and successful value-based care environment requires a modernized and standards-based health technology framework. This framework must support effective, low-burden quality data collection, transmission, and reporting. Seamless exchange between providers, health IT systems, and vendors like EHRs and data registries is essential to achieving these objectives. OBSTACLES: Despite CEHRT requirements, many EHR vendors fail to deliver timely, usable, and affordable QRDA I files. This poses significant challenges for providers, ACOs, and registries like ours. Specifically: • Delayed software updates: Required software updates for updated quality measure specifications often aren’t released until Q4 of the performance year, leaving providers without actionable data for much of the year. • Excessive export fees: Many EHR vendors charge high or unexpected fees to generate QRDA I files, even though this is a certified capability. • Lack of readiness support: Practices are often left without sufficient support to implement the necessary updates in time for successful tracking and reporting. These issues are especially burdensome for small practices, which may lack the resources to overcome delayed access or high costs, increasing their risk of reporting failures. Recommendations for FURTHER STEPS: 1. Require Timely CEHRT Availability a. Modify and enforce ONC Health IT Certification criteria to require that CEHRTs make quality reporting-related software updates - including those for updated quality measure specifications - available by Q1 of the relevant payment programs’ performance year. b. Tie certification maintenance to demonstrated delivery of updates (e.g., X% of client base active on updated version by April 1 for that year’s measure specifications and other program requirements). 2. Establish Cost Transparency & Accountability a. Require EHR vendors to publish QRDA I-export pricing in a public and accessible format.

Develop a public dashboard comparing vendor timelines and fees for QRDA I availability. 3. Enforce Data Element Standardization as Part of the Transition to FHIR a. Certification should not only require data access via FHIR APIs but also ensure standardized placement of key quality measure data elements within FHIR resources. EHRs should be tested for this conformance. b. Consider a near-term certification strategy to map QRDA data structures to FHIR resources, facilitating hybrid approaches while the FHIR ecosystem matures. 4. Recognize Timing of CEHRT Updates as a Regulatory Lever a. CMS and ONC should recognize that while data access may exist in theory, delayed software availability renders that access functionally meaningless during critical portions of the performance year. b. Timely provider feedback during the performance year is essential for identifying gaps and making improvements. Delayed updates compromise opportunities for meaningful course correction. c. The timeliness of CEHRT updates should be treated as a certifiable dimension of readiness, on par with other conditions of certification. SUMMARY: Advancing digital health, interoperability, and real-time performance measurement feedback will require stronger oversight of CEHRT functionality, including timely software updates, transparent pricing, and standardized data exchange. We encourage CMS to use its authority under the 21st Century Cures Act to adopt policies that improve transparency, promote accountability, and ensure health IT vendors contribute meaningfully to a lower-burden, higher-impact quality ecosystem.
