Public Comment on CMS-0042-NC: Health Technology Ecosystem
Introduction
NeuroFlow appreciates the opportunity to respond to the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid
Services (CMS) and the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy/Office of the National
Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ASTP/ONC) Request for Information regarding
the Health Technology Ecosystem (CMS-0042-NC).
As a provider of behavioral health technology infrastructure with implementations across 200+
provider groups, health systems, payers, and government organizations including 140 VA
Medical Centers, NeuroFlow offers perspectives on how digital health technologies can better
support behavioral health integration within the healthcare ecosystem.
Digital Health Products for Medicare Beneficiaries (TD-1, PC-3)
To stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries and
caregivers, we recommend:
● Establish clear reimbursement pathways for digital behavioral health solutions that
demonstrate improved outcomes and reduced costs
● Develop evidence-based frameworks for evaluating digital health product effectiveness
that align with existing quality measurement approaches
● Integrate digital health education into Medicare enrollment and wellness visits to improve
digital literacy among beneficiaries
● Support providers with implementation resources and best practices for introducing
digital health tools to Medicare populations
Data Interoperability for Behavioral Health (PC-8, TD-13)
To improve behavioral health data interoperability within the broader health technology
infrastructure:
● Expand the U.S. Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) to include additional
standardized behavioral health assessment data elements beyond PHQ-9 and GAD-7.
Priority additions include substance use screenings (e.g., AUDIT-C, DAST-10), social
determinants of health (SDOH) assessments, trauma screenings (e.g., PCL-5), and
functional assessments that are critical for comprehensive behavioral health monitoring
and care coordination.
● Leverage existing health IT standards to enable longitudinal tracking of behavioral health
metrics while supporting domain-specific documentation needs.

● Provide clearer guidance for the exchange of behavioral health information in ways that
uphold enhanced privacy protections under 42 CFR Part 2.
● Incentivize bidirectional integration between claims and clinical behavioral health
systems to support a more complete, real-time view of patient health status.
API Implementation and Enhancement (PR-5, TD-10)
Regarding FHIR API capabilities and standardization:
● Extend existing API capabilities to accommodate behavioral health use cases, including
assessment delivery, scoring, and longitudinal monitoring.
● Advance standardized resources and workflows that reflect behavioral health-specific
practices such as care planning, risk stratification, and stepped care pathways.
● Develop implementation guidance to facilitate secure exchange of sensitive behavioral
health information, incorporating consent management aligned with regulatory
requirements.
● Enable population-level behavioral health analytics and quality reporting through
scalable, standards-based API infrastructure.
Digital Identity and Patient Access (PC-14, TD-3)
Regarding digital identity credentials for healthcare access:
● Implement solutions that address the unique privacy considerations for behavioral health
information within existing identity management frameworks
● Develop guidance on appropriate authentication requirements that balance security with
accessibility for diverse populations
● Create clear standards for delegated access by caregivers specifically addressing
behavioral health scenarios
● Support improved consent processes for sensitive behavioral health information sharing
across the care continuum
TEFCA Implementation Considerations (PC-10, PR-6)
Regarding TEFCA's role in advancing behavioral health information exchange:
● Enhance TEFCA implementation guides to specifically address behavioral health use
cases and unique privacy considerations
● Develop specific guidance on how sensitive behavioral health information should be
handled within the TEFCA framework
● Address technical and operational barriers to including behavioral health providers in
health information networks
● Create clear standards for consent management specific to behavioral health information
exchange within TEFCA

Value-Based Care Technology Requirements (VB-3, VB-10)
On aligning technology requirements with the needs of providers in Alternative Payment
Models:
● Enhance CEHRT criteria to better support behavioral health integration in value-based
care arrangements
● Provide appropriate flexibility in how APM participants demonstrate behavioral health
capability adoption
● Incorporate measurement-based care approaches into value-based care technology
requirements while allowing for implementation flexibility
● Support technology capabilities that facilitate care coordination between physical and
behavioral health providers in APM arrangements
Provider Burden Reduction (PR-7, PR-8)
Strategies to support providers in making data available while reducing burden:
● Align documentation requirements for behavioral health services with clinical workflows
● Develop streamlined quality reporting approaches that leverage existing behavioral
health measurement tools
● Support bulk FHIR data exports for quality reporting to reduce manual documentation
requirements
● Create technical guidance for behavioral health risk stratification that allows for
implementation flexibility
Information Blocking Considerations (PR-12, TD-18)
Regarding information blocking and behavioral health data access:
● Develop specific guidance on when privacy protections for behavioral health information
constitute exceptions to information blocking
● Address technical barriers to behavioral health information exchange that could be
misinterpreted as information blocking
● Create educational resources for behavioral health providers on information blocking
regulations and appropriate exceptions
● Support enhanced consent management solutions for sensitive information sharing
Conclusion
We believe that integrating behavioral health more effectively into the broader health technology
ecosystem will significantly advance care quality while reducing costs. We encourage
approaches that recognize the unique aspects of behavioral health data while leveraging
existing standards and frameworks to minimize implementation complexity.
