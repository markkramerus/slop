Petersen Former State Official | CEO, Equitable Tech Marion, Massachusetts As a long -term professional in the Health and Human Services sector and a former Massachusetts state employee, I am submitting this comment to advocate for broader inclusion of state human services agencies in the federal health technology ecosystem, parti cularly regarding standards for interoperability, information blocking, and digital modernization. I have firsthand knowledge of systemic inefficiencies, incompetence, and waste within the Executive Office of Health and Human Services (EOHHS) in Massachusetts —failures that result in disproportionate harm to our most vulnerable citizens. One critical exa mple highlights the urgent need for federal guidance and enforcement: Issue Summary Human service providers across Massachusetts —especially those working with the Department of Developmental Services (DDS), Department of Public Health (DPH), and MassHealth’s Long -Term Services and Supports (LTSS) —increasingly utilize certified Electronic Health Record (EHR) systems to promote compliance, streamline care coordination, and support data - driven decision -making. Despite this progress, state systems remain siloed and resistant to interoperability. These include: • HCSIS (Home and Community Services Information System – DDS) • EICS (Early Intervention Coordination System – DPH) • LTSS platforms (including PCM and Adult Foster Care) Providers are required to manually re -enter the same client data into each system —despite having already captured this information in their EHRs. This practice is not only duplicative and inefficient but potentially constitutes information blocking as defined by the 21st Century Cures Act. Risk of Information Blocking Per federal definitions, information blocking occurs when actors unreasonably interfere with access, exchange, or use of electronic health information (EHI). In this context: 
• State agencies that prohibit or lack infrastructure for accepting HL7/FHIR -compliant data from certified EHRs may be engaging in systemic information blocking —even if unintentionally. • Technical limitations, outdated policies , or the absence of APIs should not be accepted as valid exceptions when robust national standards exist. • No interface support, and mandatory manual entry , despite certified systems, undermines both provider investments and the intent of federal HIT regulations. Consequences for Human Service Providers This technological disconnect imposes measurable harm: • Administrative Burden: Redundant documentation wastes provider time and increases operational costs. • Workforce Strain: Direct Support Professionals (DSPs), clinicians, and care coordinators face burnout due to unnecessary paperwork. • Delayed Care & Compliance Risk: Lagging documentation compromises timely service delivery and jeopardizes compliance. • Fragmented Data: Disjointed systems prevent effective data aggregation for outcome tracking or care planning. • Stalled Innovation: Agencies lose motivation to invest in health IT when integration barriers persist. Policy Recommendations To bring state HHS systems in alignment with national health IT standards and prevent ongoing fragmentation, I respectfully recommend the following: 1. EOHHS -Wide Interoperability Roadmap Establish a state -level interoperability strategy that aligns all major HHS systems (HCSIS, EICS, LTSS) with Mass HIway and supports FHIR -based exchange. 2. Mandated API Access & Data Standards Require that state agencies publish and maintain open APIs and HL7/FHIR -based specifications for EHR integration. 3. Federated Data Exchange Model Implement a "write -once, share -many" framework —where providers input client data once into their EHR, and state systems securely retrieve needed elements. 4. Provider Incentive & Infrastructure Support Offer financial and technical support for EHR integration to community -based and rural providers through MassHealth modernization grants. 5. Enforce Anti -Blocking Oversight Direct CMS and ONC to assess state systems for potential noncompliance and publish transparency reports. Conclusion 
The lack of interoperability between provider EHR systems and Massachusetts’ state -run human services platforms reflects a failure to implement the principles and mandates established under federal health IT policy. These deficiencies inhibit person -center ed care, increase administrative waste, and ultimately harm the vulnerable populations these systems aim to support. I urge CMS and HHS to assert stronger oversight and provide actionable requirements to ensure that human service agencies at the state leve l become full participants in the digital health ecosystem.
