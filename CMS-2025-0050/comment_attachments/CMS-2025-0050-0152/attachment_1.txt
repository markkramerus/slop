To Whom It May Concern, Thank you for the opportunity to submit comments in response to CMS-0042-NC. I strongly encourage CMS and ONC to recognize diet quality assessment as a vital and currently underutilized element of clinical care and digital health infrastructure. Specifically, I urge CMS to support the use of validated tools like Diet ID as a standardized, interoperable method for dietary assessment across healthcare settings. As a physician with over 30 years of experience in clinical care, public health, and chronic disease prevention, I’ve seen firsthand how the American healthcare system routinely misses its most critical opportunity: addressing poor diet. I served as the founding director of Yale University’s Yale-Griffin Prevention Research Center for over 20 years, led the American College of Lifestyle Medicine as its President, and have authored over 200 peer-reviewed publications on nutrition and chronic disease. Despite decades of research linking food to health outcomes, I’ve watched as diet remained sidelined in clinical practice—rarely measured, barely tracked, and almost never integrated into care. This is a systemic failure with enormous clinical and economic consequences. Poor diet is the leading cause of premature death and disability in the U.S., responsible for an estimated 500,000 deaths annually.[1,2] Nearly 90% of Americans don’t meet vegetable intake recommendations,[3] and over 70% of adults are overweight or obese.[3] Diet-related conditions account for more than 80% of healthcare costs.[5] We would not manage hypertension without measuring blood pressure or treat diabetes without monitoring glucose. But when it comes to diet—the most powerful modifiable determinant of health—we continue to fly blind. If we are to build a robust, prevention-first digital ecosystem that enables personalization, equity, and population-level insight, then measuring diet quality must become standard practice. That is precisely what Diet ID enables. Diet ID is a validated, image-based dietary assessment that produces a comprehensive diet quality score, estimates nutrient and food group intake, and identifies dietary patterns—all in under 60 seconds.[6] It is self-guided, multilingual, and accessible on any device. Critically, it generates structured data compatible with FHIR standards and EHR integration, making it ideal for use across care settings—from primary care to remote monitoring. Diet ID has been used to complete over 200,000 dietary assessments—the equivalent of 40 years of data capture in the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES). Diet ID makes it possible to treat diet as a vital sign—fast, feasible, and standardized. By embedding diet quality into clinical encounters and digital care pathways, we can finally align everyday healthcare with decades of evidence showing that food is central to both prevention and treatment. In the context of value-based care, diet quality data supports risk stratification, care planning, and outcome tracking. In the context of health equity, it provides a culturally adaptable, 
literacy-sensitive method for engaging patients in their own health. And in the context of interoperability, it provides structured, high-value data that has historically been missing from patient records. I urge CMS and ONC to: ● Recognize diet quality as a key clinical data element, relevant to preventive care, chronic disease management, and value-based care. ● Include dietary assessment tools like Diet ID under definitions of “health management applications” eligible for integration with CMS-supported APIs and digital health infrastructure. ● Incorporate diet quality metrics into interoperability frameworks such as USCDI and encourage certification pathways that support diet-related data capture and exchange. ● Promote dietary assessment as a core capability in value-based models, including ACOs and other Alternative Payment Models, as part of risk stratification, care planning, and quality improvement. The digital transformation of healthcare offers a rare chance to embed prevention and lifestyle guidance into routine care. But to do so, we must first measure what matters. Diet quality is long overdue for its place in the data ecosystem of American healthcare. Respectfully, David L. Katz, MD, MPH, FACPM, FACP, FACLM Founder & CEO, Diet ID, Inc.
