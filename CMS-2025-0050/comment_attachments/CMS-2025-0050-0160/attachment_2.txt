PR-1a: What are the current obstacles?
Response from Sifter Solutions:
Several barriers hinder providers from leveraging approved digital health products:
• Workflow Integration Challenges: Many digital tools are not seamlessly
integrated into existing Electronic Health Records (EHRs), making it
cumbersome for providers to incorporate them into their routines.
• Limited Reimbursement Models: There's a lack of clear reimbursement
pathways for food-as-medicine initiatives, discouraging providers from adopting
these programs. Such is the case for Medically Tailored Meals (MTM) and MTG.
These programs are tailored by design, meaning they vary widely by provider,
patient condition, and delivery model. Without nationally accepted clinical
protocols and eligibility criteria, it's difficult to define what services a CPT code
would reimburse.
• Patient Accessibility Issues: In rural areas, patients may face challenges like
limited internet access and lack of familiarity with digital tools, reducing the

effectiveness of such programs.
• Data Privacy Concerns: Providers may be hesitant to adopt new technologies
due to concerns about patient data security and compliance with regulations like
HIPAA.
• Lack of Standardized Nutrition Interventions: The absence of standardized
protocols for integrating nutrition interventions into treatment plans can lead to
inconsistent application and outcomes.
• Financial Constraints: Providers may be unaware of the potential cost savings
associated with digital nutrition programs.
Addressing these obstacles requires collaborative efforts to integrate digital health tools
into existing systems, establish clear reimbursement models, and ensure patient
accessibility and data security.
PR-1b: What information should providers share with patients when using digital
products in the provision of their care?
Response from Sifter Solutions:
When introducing digital health products, providers should ensure patients understand:
• Purpose and Benefits: Clearly explain how the tool supports their health goals,
such as managing a chronic condition through tailored nutrition.
• Usage Instructions: Provide step-by-step guidance and training on accessing
and using the tool, including downloading apps, scanning and comparing
products and understanding benefit card restrictions.
• Data Privacy: Inform patients about how if and how their data will be used,
stored, and protected, ensuring transparency and building trust.
• Support Resources: To address any challenges in using the tool, offer
information on where to seek help. Options include a customer support line, in-
person assistance, or perhaps creating a patient community for support and
assistance for each other.
• Integration into Care Plans: Emphasize how the digital health product fits into
their overall care plan, including its role in managing specific health conditions.
• Cost Savings Potential: Educate patients on the financial benefits of using MTG
programs, such as reduced out-of-pocket expenses and potential savings on
healthcare costs.

PR-1c: What responsibilities do providers have when recommending use of a digital
product to a patient?
Response from Sifter Solutions:
Providers have several responsibilities when recommending digital health products to
patients:
• Due Diligence: Ensure the product is evidence-based, compliant with regulations,
and aligns with the patient's health needs.
• Patient Education: Offer thorough training or resources to help patients understand
and effectively use the product.
• Monitoring and Follow-Up: Regularly assess the patient's progress and address
any issues or concerns related to the product's use.
• Feedback Loop: Encourage patients to provide ongoing feedback about their
experience with the product, using this information to refine recommendations,
enhance training and improve care.
• Coordination with Healthcare Teams: Collaborate with dietitians, pharmacists, and
other healthcare professionals to ensure a multidisciplinary approach to patient care.
• Evaluation of Economic Impact: Assess the financial implications of digital health
products on both the healthcare system and the patient, considering factors like
potential reductions in hospitalizations and overall healthcare expenditures.
By fulfilling these responsibilities, providers can ensure that digital health products are
used effectively, leading to better patient outcomes and satisfaction.
PR-2: What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective utilization
of the most useful and innovative applications for physician workflows, such as quality
measurement reporting, clinical documentation, and billing tasks? How could these
obstacles be mitigated?
Response from Sifter Solutions:
The biggest roadblocks to developing and using innovative apps for physician workflows
— like quality measurement reporting, clinical documentation, and billing — come down
to three friction points: fragmented data ecosystems, burned-out provider teams, and a
lack of economic or clinical alignment.

Here's how those barriers show up, and how CMS can help knock them down:
1. Fragmented Systems & Siloed Data
Barrier:
EHRs are notoriously closed systems. Integrating third-party tools (even ones with clear
patient benefit) often requires custom IT workarounds, delaying adoption and
undercutting functionality.
Solution:
CMS should accelerate enforcement of open API standards — especially support for
Bulk FHIR exports, SMART on FHIR, and real-time data sharing — so physician
workflows can include third-party apps without disruption. Tools like Sifter’s can plug into
care planning workflows if data access is streamlined across platforms.
2. Clinician Workload & Workflow Fatigue
Barrier:
Digital tools too often add clicks instead of reducing them. Even when digital health
products offer real utility, if they require separate logins, duplicative documentation, or
interrupt the flow of care, providers won’t use them.
Solution:
CMS could incentivize “zero-lift integration” — rewarding vendors and systems that
reduce manual data entry, automate documentation, or allow clinical guidance (like food
prescriptions) to be generated directly from existing diagnoses and med lists.
Sifter’s solution, for example, enables physicians to “prescribe” medically tailored foods
based on patient conditions with no added documentation burden. By leveraging
diagnosis codes to auto-generate approved product lists (APLs), Sifter turns provider
input into patient action — bridging the provider-to-patient gap in seconds.
3. Lack of Financial & Clinical Alignment
Barrier:
Many innovations in digital health don’t get adopted because the value they generate
(reduced admissions, improved outcomes) isn’t tied to how providers get paid today.
Solution:
CMS should build value-based incentive structures that reward adoption of tools shown
to reduce total cost of care — especially in high-risk, high-cost populations.
An actuarial model found that for 100,000 Medicare Advantage members, a medically
tailored grocery benefit—like those powered by Sifter—could deliver up to $2,500 in
annual claims savings per engaged patient, with ROI ranging from 7.1:1 to 17.9:1 in a
single year. Digital tools that produce this kind of impact should be prioritized in quality
improvement programs, not sidelined as optional extras.

PR-3: How important is it for healthcare delivery and interoperability in urban and rural
areas that all data in an EHR system be accessible for exchange, regardless of storage
format (for example, scanned documents, faxed records, lab results, free text notes,
structured data fields)?
Response from Sifter Solutions:
Full EHR Data Accessibility Is Non-Negotiable for a Functional Digital Health
Ecosystem
Universal access to EHR data — across all formats — is critical for enabling equitable,
effective healthcare, especially in rural or resource-limited areas where digital tools must
work harder to bridge gaps in care.
Sifter Solutions believes that ensuring true interoperability means moving beyond
structured fields and embracing data locked in scanned PDFs, lab attachments, free-
text SOAP notes, and legacy systems. Nutrition-focused digital health tools like ours
rely on robust, contextual data to personalize care. If key diagnoses or medication
histories are buried in inaccessible formats, providers and patients are left with
incomplete information — which can derail prevention efforts.
PR-3a: Please address the current challenges in accessing different data formats.
Response from Sifter Solutions:
• Unstructured & inconsistent data entry: Scanned or hand-written notes and
outdated EHR formats make it impossible for digital tools to parse and use the
data.
• Lack of universal formatting standards: Even structured data varies between
systems.
• Minimal metadata tagging: Data retrieved is often too broad or messy for
efficient filtering or use in real-time clinical decisions.

Response from Sifter Solutions:
Plug Digital Health into the Admin Workflow
For digital health tools to be more than just “nice-to-haves,” CMS must ensure they can
plug into the administrative side of care delivery — not just clinical data. Tools like Sifter
don’t just guide dietary choices; they represent a full patient experience that starts with
a provider recommendation and ends in real-world food purchasing, benefit use, and
follow-up.
Yet right now, admin workflow access is the weakest link in the digital health chain.
Where the Bottlenecks Are
• No universal standard for schedule or intake API access.
• Provider directory data is outdated or unavailable via real-time endpoints.
• Most systems require manual entry of duplicative patient info during appointment
booking or check-in — even when that data already exists in the EHR or digital
health product.
Recommended Improvements
1. Standardize Admin APIs like Clinical APIs
CMS should expand its support of FHIR beyond clinical endpoints and require payers
and providers to expose administrative FHIR resources, including:
• Patient Demographics
• Healthcare Service / Schedule
• Appointment
• Practitioner / Practitioner Role
Third-party tools should be able to pull schedule availability, pre-fill forms, and even
trigger prior-auth workflows where needed — with patient consent, of course.
2. Create a Federated Provider Directory with Real-Time Schedule Hooks
