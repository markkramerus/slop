I. Executive Summary
The U.S. healthcare system stands at a once-in-a-century inflection point. What lies
ahead is not another framework or regulation, but a chance to rewrite the operating code
of American medicine. This response offers that code.
Salphaware, LLC is honored to respond to this Request for Information (RFI) issued by
the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) and the Office of the National
Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ASTP/ONC). As a veteran-owned
health technology company founded by professionals with deep technical, clinical, and
regulatory expertise, we bring a vision rooted in service, shaped by frontline insight, and
governed by the conviction that digital health must now serve the nation rather than the
market alone.
Patient health data has not simply been siloed. It has been rounded up, fenced off, and
made subject to proprietary captivity—no longer a public good, but a controlled
commodity. Billions of dollars and countless workarounds have failed to produce the
interoperability Americans were promised. What is needed now is neither another fix nor
interface, but a unified digital fortress, engineered for national coordination and local
execution.
This document architects a bold yet grounded redesign of the U.S. health information
system. At its center is a single, urgent principle: health data is a national asset—and
must be protected, structured, and activated as such. Our proposal untethers legacy
concepts through a National Health Data Repository, atomic-level data architecture,
universal identity layers for patients and entities, cybersecurity to rival the intelligence
sector, a harmonized public health empire, and a single federated patient portal.
Together, these elements herald the quantum era of U.S. healthcare—where emergency
response is real-time, fraud is intercepted before it spreads, AI-enabled care is the norm,
and the ecosystem’s reach—urban, rural, tribal, territory—manifests as equity.
Salphaware’s flagship offering, Sepsis Studio, automates CMS SEP-1 abstraction,
retroactive DRG appeals, and real-time compliance nudging—fully aligned to CMS’
burden-reduction priorities under Section 4001 of the 21st Century Cures Act. The proof-
of-concept demonstrates that what has long been deemed complex is, in fact, achievable.
But sepsis is only the start. Our broader roadmap is guided by the Trilateral Value
Proposition—a framework developed by Salphaware to center U.S. health system reform
around value for three core stakeholders: the patient, the provider, and the federal
government. Every technical proposal within this document answers to all three.
What follows is defensible in design, transformative in consequence, and ethically
courageous. We submit this not merely as a response—but as a call to collaborate. The
opportunity before us is nothing short of historic: to unchain American healthcare from
its legacy constraints, and to build a system worthy of its people—secure by design, self-
aware in operation, and unrelenting in its service to life.

A. The Trilateral Value Proposition: A Contractual Framework for Serving
Patients, Providers, and Government
The U.S. health system is not failing from lack of effort—it is failing from misalignment.
Policies serve reporting requirements instead of people. Contracts reward efficiency
without demanding impact. Providers are burdened. Patients are fragmented. And the
very tools built to transform healthcare have too often replicated the paperwork logic they
were meant to eliminate.
But this is our inflection point. We can all be titans of long-overdue transformation if we
align on philosophy first and negotiate later.
At the heart of this response is a single, unapologetic principle: value must flow in at
least three directions—to the patient, to the provider, and to the federal system. This is
the Trilateral Value Proposition (TVP): a practical, measurable, and morally grounded
framework pioneered by Salphaware to re-anchor federal healthcare innovation to what
matters more than any regulation or budget line.
The TVP is not just a philosophy; it should be hard-coded into procurement mechanics. It
reframes contracts not as checklists, but as instruments of design and repair. And it insists
on a symmetrical definition of success:
• If a tool improves government efficiency but increases provider burnout, it fails.
• If it supports providers but renders patients invisible, it fails.
• If it serves patients but is unscalable or inequitable, it fails [PR-2].
Adopting the TVP as a contracting standard multiplies the genomic depth of CMMI into
every transaction, converting human-scale innovation into minimum viable product and
with maximum systemic return.
The TVP asks a single litmus-test question: Does this solution deliver mission-aligned
value to all three?
• To patients: It means safer, faster, more coherent care—where continuity follows
the person, not the institution. It means deterioration caught earlier, referrals
executed smoother, and in critical moments like sepsis, outcomes determined—
not just documented.
3

• To providers: It means less documentation, less duplication, less drag. More
clarity. More time. More trust. TVP-aligned tools reinforce clinical reasoning
rather than replace it—because digital health should elevate clinicians, not
override them [PR-8].
• To CMS: It means structured, clinically grounded data—ready to support accurate
payment, real-time regulatory oversight, public health surveillance, and the
evolution to scalable, self-improving systems.
We urge CMS to elevate the TVP as a baseline for all future contracts, grants, and
initiatives. This shift would not only modernize procurement; it would reorient the
federal innovation ecosystem itself. Imagine if every submission, every solution, every
vendor had to prove not just utility but mutuality. Imagine a future where value to the
patient and provider is no longer a bonus; it is the requirement.
To accelerate this shift, CMS could:
• Integrate TVP scoring into Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) quality
metrics
• Prioritize vendors and proposals that deliver demonstrable tri-directional value
• Offer innovation grants or payment multipliers for tools that fulfill TVP criteria—
especially in high-burden conditions or across shared-risk models like
Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs) and Alternative Payment Models
(APMs) [VB-7]
The message is simple but profound: innovation is not decoration; it is obligation.
And contracts are no longer forms; they are transformative blueprints.
Somewhere in the shared complexity of our system, the patient has been lost. Patients do
not experience healthcare as line items, metrics, or dashboards. They experience it as
deeply personal and sometimes terrifying moments that determine whether they live,
suffer, or heal.
The Trilateral Value Proposition is how we honor those moments, and how we finally
build a system that sees them, hears them, and answers.
B. De-Risking Hospital Participation in Innovation Pilots
Innovation cannot transform the system if it is only tested in its wealthiest corners.
Hospitals are the proving grounds of national health reform, but without structured
support, too many are left on the sidelines. Safety-net, rural, and lower-resourced
institutions often face overwhelming legal, technical, and operational hurdles when
invited to test early-stage solutions. The result is a system where innovation scales first to
the advantaged, and slowly—if ever—to those who need it most.
CMS can and should reverse this trend.
4

We recommend the creation of structured, de-risking incentives for hospital participation
in CMS-aligned innovation pilots [TD-1]. These mechanisms would recognize hospitals
not as passive endpoints, but as active collaborators in modernizing the national health
infrastructure.
Policy options may include:
• Pilot-site stipends or technical assistance grants to offset implementation and
staffing costs;
• Regulatory flexibilities under the Quality Payment Program (QPP) to reduce
administrative load;
• Scoring bonuses within MSSP or future value-based arrangements for approved
pilot participation;
• Shared-risk or liability safe harbors for institutions testing certified tools in CMS-
authorized pilots;
• National early-adopter recognition for hospitals contributing real-world validation
across diverse care environments.
These supports could be coupled with CEHRT flexibility provisions under VB-10 to
ensure that participation in digital innovation pilots does not penalize or exclude hospitals
based on certification burdens they are structurally less able to meet.
Hospitals should not have to bear the cost of transformation alone. By lowering the risk
and raising the reward, CMS can catalyze a broader, faster, and more equitable
deployment of tools like Sepsis Studio—tools designed not for the margins, but for the
mission. These incentives would also operationalize the TVP: delivering earlier access to
innovation for patients, reducing strain on providers, and improving implementation
fidelity for policymakers.
De-risking is not a subsidy. It is a strategic accelerant. It signals that innovation is no
longer something hospitals are asked to try, but rather something they are equipped to
lead.

Strategy

The barrier to an intelligent, resilient health system is no longer technological—it is
structural. Artificial intelligence, machine learning, and advanced analytics are already
capable of supporting predictive care, real-time surveillance, and automation of clinical
and regulatory tasks. What stands in the way is not model capacity. Instead, the barrier is
the absence of clean, accessible, secure, and interoperable data [PC-8].
Today, U.S. health data systems remain shaped by outdated billing structures, proprietary
vendor silos, and encounter-based logic. These architectures serve internal accounting—
not longitudinal care, not public health coordination, and not national resilience. The
result is fragmentation, brittleness, and an inability to respond coherently to threats
ranging from sepsis to cyberattacks to bioterrorism [PC-11].
A durable future requires more than policy tweaks. It requires the re-architecture of U.S.
health data as critical infrastructure. This transformation must be led by federal
agencies—CMS, and ONC among them—with the same foresight and rigor applied to
energy, transportation, and national defense.
To this end, Salphaware proposes and supports a triad of interlocking federal constructs:
1. The National Health Data Repository (NHDR): A federally governed system of
record that supports real-time analytics, care continuity, and public health
mobilization
2. The Atomic Health Data Architecture (AHDA): A granular, extensible, time-
stamped data structure designed for AI, transparency, and reuse across the
continuum of care
3. The National Health Citizen and Entity Identifiers (NHCI/NHEI): A persistent
digital identity framework that links patients and provider organizations across
space, time, and systems
Each component reflects a simple truth: data infrastructure determines system capability.
Without transformation at this foundational level, automation remains constrained, AI
remains limited, and public trust remains undermined.
This is not a speculative vision. It is an executable blueprint. The most urgent innovation
is not artificial intelligence. It is architectural intelligence.

A secure, longitudinal, interoperable data infrastructure is the necessary substrate for
every other reform goal: quality improvement, public health surveillance, health equity,
cost transparency, emergency response, and patient empowerment. Without it, the
promise of intelligent health systems remains unrealized.
Salphaware affirms this vision as a veteran-led organization grounded in service, clinical
expertise, and federal contracting experience. Our position is clear: the future of
healthcare depends not on what machines can learn, but on what systems let them see.

A. National Health Identity Standards: NHCI and NHEI for Trust,
Continuity, and Coordination
Every system of national significance—whether Social Security, passport control, or tax
administration—relies on reliable, persistent identifiers. Healthcare remains a glaring
exception. The absence of standardized, interoperable identity structures undermines
continuity, coordination, and accountability across every node of the delivery system
[PC-8].
We propose two foundational identifiers for U.S. healthcare data infrastructure:
• The National Health Citizen Identifier (NHCI): a persistent, context-agnostic
patient identifier that enables longitudinal continuity across time, geography, and
settings of care
• The National Health Entity Identifier (NHEI): a unique identifier for each
healthcare-delivering organization—EMS agencies, primary care clinics, dialysis
centers, hospitals, nursing homes, community health centers, and others—
regardless of size or affiliation
Together, these identifiers provide a clean, scalable scaffolding for trustworthy, cross-
continuum data exchange [PR-3]. They enable traceability without reliance on local
medical record numbers, payer-issued IDs, or institution-specific constructs. They
support dynamic linkage across siloed EHRs, enabling auditability, privacy segmentation,
and equitable participation in care coordination and public health response [PR-7].
The precedent is clear. The U.S. Social Security Number (SSN), despite its limitations,
fundamentally changed how federal systems track citizen activity, benefits, and
entitlements. The National Provider Identifier (NPI), introduced in the mid-2000s,
created a universal reference model across public and private healthcare. A hyphenated
alphanumeric NHCI—human-readable, machine-parseable, and format-stable—would
mirror these proven models. The Veterans Affairs Integration Control Number (VA ICN)
and the Department of Defense Electronic Data Interchange Personal Identifier (DoD
EDIPI) have demonstrated scalable, durable use across large federated systems without
meaningful architectural criticism.
8

NHCI could also be leveraged as the foundational anchor for a national patient-facing
digital credential—enabling alignment with platforms like Login.gov or ID.me to
simplify access for patients while reducing administrative burden for providers [PR-9].
This approach also aligns with ONC’s encouragement of persistent digital identity
frameworks as foundational to national interoperability [PC-14]. Digital identity
constructs like NHCI and NHEI directly support NIST 800-63-3-aligned frameworks,
enabling implementation of standards such as IAL2/AAL2 credentialing and OpenID
Connect protocols—fortifying both cybersecurity posture and interoperability readiness
[TD-3]. That same logic—clarity, trust, interoperability—now compels a national
identity model for both patients and provider entities.
Crucially, the NHCI and NHEI are not surveillance tools—they are structural tools. Their
purpose is not to centralize data, but to normalize, align, and secure it across a federated
health network. Their use can be governed by purpose-based access rules, audit trails, and
data minimization safeguards—fully aligned with the HIPAA Privacy Rule, ONC
information blocking exceptions, and federal zero trust architecture guidance.
By pairing NHCI and NHEI standards with an atomic data model, CMS can radically
strengthen the fidelity, trustworthiness, and usability of health data for care delivery,
digital quality measurement, health equity tracking, and AI-powered analytics. This
foundational standardization would also reduce the complexity and friction that often
undermine provider participation in value-based arrangements, especially those requiring
multi-source data aggregation and attribution fidelity [VB-13]. These identifiers provide
the anchoring logic required to implement atomic data structures at national scale—as
demonstrated in the tables and architecture introduced in the following subsection.
Importantly, NHCI could also streamline patient authentication across payer systems,
encouraging broader adoption of shared credentials like Login.gov or ID.me to reduce
access friction and member confusion [PA-3].
A modern health system cannot function without persistent identity logic. The time has
come to define and deploy it.
B. Atomic Health Data Architecture: Enabling Structured Intelligence
Every high-functioning national system—whether financial, transportation, or defense—
relies on data structures engineered for scale, precision, and resilience. Healthcare should
be no different. Yet today’s health data models remain encounter-bound, non-normalized,
and vendor-defined—optimized for billing, not insight. Salphaware rejects this
architectural confinement. We propose an Atomic Health Data Architecture (AHDA) to
correct this misalignment with a patient-centric, longitudinal foundation built for
intelligence, precision, and durability.
It is a fact that patient access barriers are engineered, often unintentionally, by the
structure of health data itself. The system’s architectural rigidity and self-imposed
9

constraints on data evolution entrench fragmentation. AHDA reverses this logic. It allows
records to follow the patient, not the silo. It redefines data as a longitudinal asset—
patient-loyal across time, geography, and platform.
While Salphaware’s current implementation leverages SQL for backward-compatible
abstraction, our roadmap includes full compliance with CEHRT requirements and
certified FHIR-based APIs per the ONC Health IT Certification Program. These certified
pathways will be prioritized during deployment to ensure full interoperability with CMS
expectations.
The cornerstone of resilient health infrastructure is a normalized, extensible data model
that enables atomic precision and semantic integrity. AHDA implements variable-specific
longitudinal tables—each designed to accommodate temporal granularity, source
provenance, and cross-entity normalization.
Unlike aggregation-based models such as FHIR—which consolidate data post hoc and
suffer from semantic drift, resource bloat, and inconsistent implementation—AHDA is
architected for reuse, automation, and structural clarity. This allows integration of AI,
risk stratification, real-time quality measurement, and digital nudging into APM
workflows with minimal reengineering [VB-2]. By decoupling the data model from
vendor constraints and encounter logic, AHDA enables scalable digital quality
measurement, real-time compliance nudging, and AI-powered abstraction.

variable—heart rate—demonstrating how each observation is independently recorded
across time, systems, and care settings. Note the presence of NHCI and NHEI fields as
core components of extensibility and traceability.

Each table is variable-specific and structured around a primary key that includes patient
identity (NHCI), temporal fidelity (Timestamp), and contextual metadata (Source, Entity,
NHEI). This identity-driven schema supports credential-based interoperability
frameworks, including those aligned with NIST 800-63-3 and OpenID Connect protocols
for secure, federated transmission [TD-3]. These structured data types—time-resolved
vitals, identity anchors, and care context—are foundational for longitudinal attribution,
risk modeling, and quality analytics in value-based care [VB-4]. Together, they enable
traceability across EMS, primary care, specialty care, hospitals, dialysis centers,
community health centers, and nursing homes—supporting normalization across the
entire continuum of care.
AHDA enables longitudinal normalization, precision analytics, and secure, federated
transmission. Its extensibility supports integration with non-CMS data networks where
fidelity, traceability, and normalized identity models meet federal criteria for
endorsement [TD-12]. It directly supports CMS initiatives such as digital quality
measurement (dQM), data segmentation for privacy (DS4P), and the vision for clean, AI-
ready datasets aligned with the USCDI v3 framework.
Bleeding-edge data science principles cannot remain an afterthought in CMS’ immediate-
term vision—especially given what is already possible, with or without AI. AHDA
provides an operational pathway to meet these possibilities with minimal provider-side
lift, lowering implementation burden while increasing analytical return [VB-13]. At the
2025 State HIT Connect Summit, one theme was clear: the industry remains far from
achieving clean, computable data. Salphaware addresses this gap with AHDA—a future-
ready model built for automation, extensibility, and certified interoperability.
This work aligns with ONC’s regulatory framework under 45 CFR § 170 Subpart C,
which defines certification criteria for interoperability, clinical data exchange, and
support for patient access. It anticipates and supports USCDI v3 adoption, standardizing
core data elements for nationwide interoperability and public health exchange.
Whereas FHIR aggregates inconsistent records in complex formats, AHDA restructures
data at its core. It achieves maximum granularity in the cleanest, most portable structure
possible. AHDA does not merely comply with interoperability standards—it redefines
them.
And in doing so, it delivers the foundational logic required for reusable, model-ready,
cross-domain health intelligence at national scale.
C. National Health Data Repository: Infrastructure-Grade Intelligence for
the Nation
Most Americans have already received the letter.
11

A breach. A compromise. A warning that their medical information, Social Security
number, or insurance data has been exposed—through no fault of their own. From
ransomware attacks on major hospital systems to third-party vendor hacks affecting
millions, the message is always the same: your most private information is now in
someone else’s hands.
This is not just an IT problem. It is a national vulnerability—one that affects citizens
directly and viscerally. In the modern age, personal health data is personal safety. And
the United States has no meaningful system to protect it.
That must change.
The National Health Data Repository (NHDR) is the infrastructure-grade solution to this
national failing. It is a secure, federally governed system of record that decouples core
health data from the fragmented, vendor-defined platforms that currently dominate U.S.
healthcare. It transforms health data into a national asset—secured with quantum-resilient
safeguards, protected by clear access policies, and made available to patients, providers,
and public health officials through trusted, certified interfaces that expose full EHI access
via APIs for patients, providers, and payers—accelerating downstream innovation in the
spirit of CMS's Blue Button 2.0 and Data at the Point of Care models [PA-2]. These
interfaces would expose complete EHI via secure, standardized APIs—unleashing
innovation while resolving the limitations of partial access frameworks such as USCDI
alone [TD-13]. Also, this includes identity-verified access aligned with NIST 800-63-3
digital credentialing frameworks to ensure secure interoperability across provider systems
[PR-10].
This is not about centralizing all health data in a monolith. It is about national resilience.
The NHDR replaces point-to-point connectivity with a scalable, shared infrastructure—
reducing redundant integrations, enabling reciprocal access, and overcoming the
fragmentation seen in current federated architectures, including TEFCA [TD-16, PA-1].
Rather than replacing every EHR, it establishes a persistent backbone across life stages,
provider networks, and disaster scenarios. It is the modern equivalent of the interstate
system or the electrical grid: not a luxury, but a public good foundational to national
function.
For patients, it means never starting over. No more recounting medication lists to every
new specialist. No more missing allergy documentation. No more fractured records
scattered across five logins, three hospitals, and two state lines [PC-8]. This model
presumes identity continuity through NIST-aligned credentials and OpenID Connect
integration—ensuring verifiable access across disparate systems while reducing login
fatigue and information silos [TD-3]. A secure, persistent record—tied to a verified
health identity—follows them wherever care is needed, and supports payer-facing access
via credentialing platforms like Login.gov or ID.me [PA-3].
For providers, it means access to clean, structured, longitudinal data—without hours
spent hunting through scanned PDFs or manually reconciling outside records. For nurses,
12

physicians, EMS professionals, and pharmacists alike, the NHDR becomes a trusted
baseline for informed decisions, enabled through verified identity protocols that
streamline data access while mitigating credentialing and reconciliation burdens [PR-1,
PR-10, VB-14]. This level of data quality and consistency enables more reliable
performance measurement, streamlines compliance tasks, and supports continuous
improvement in value-based care delivery [VB-13].
For public health agencies and CMS, it means real-time situational awareness. It ends the
era of retrospective extracts, delayed case reporting, and black-box datasets. With the
NHDR in place, fraud detection, outbreak monitoring, value-based payment, and
emergency coordination are no longer siloed—they are synchronized.
And for the nation, it means security. Today, health data is protected—or not—by a
patchwork of private vendors with competing interests, uneven protocols, and no unified
defense. The NHDR replaces that friability with standards. With auditability. With a data
architecture built to withstand cyber threats, foreign interference, and cascading failures.
It also relieves hospitals and health systems of the impossible burden—and legal
liability—of securing digital infrastructure they neither built nor control. These persistent
gaps in readiness and response capability are exacerbated by fragmented access rules,
unpredictable interfaces, and information blocking behaviors that disincentivize timely
sharing [TD-18]. In doing so, it addresses structural gaps that current information
blocking exceptions have failed to remedy—enabling true access, use, and innovation
[PR-12]. No other industry demands that frontline service providers double as national
cybersecurity guardians. The NHDR ends this untenable expectation and replaces it with
a rational, federally secured backbone [PR-7].
Importantly, this model does not stifle innovation. It unleashes it. By decoupling data

special integrations. Hospitals no longer need workarounds. The floor is raised for

and decades.

transmission infrastructure.

D. National Security-Grade Technology Infrastructure: Moving Beyond
FHIR and Toward Post-Quantum Cybersecurity
The health data of 330 million Americans is now a national security asset—and a target.
Breach notifications are no longer rare or shocking; they are routine. Health systems have
been paralyzed by ransomware. Patient records have been sold on the dark web. Insurers,
labs, state agencies, and technology vendors alike have all issued the same message to the
public: “We regret to inform you…”
This ends now.
The current U.S. health IT architecture was not built to defend a national asset. It was
built to move billing codes and comply with Meaningful Use. Its foundational
protocols—especially FHIR—were designed to facilitate inter-vendor communication,
not to secure longitudinal identity or ensure cyber-resilience. While FHIR has advanced
interoperability in limited contexts, its structural limitations now pose systemic risk when
applied to infrastructure-scale demands.
FHIR’s implementation model violates several foundational principles of durable system
design. It duplicates metadata and structure across resources, violating the DRY (Don’t
Repeat Yourself) principle. It encodes context into transport formats instead of grounding
it in normalized data layers, leading to semantic drift—where terms and meanings quietly
shift over time, eroding interoperability and data fidelity. It wraps core data in vendor-
variable resources that break under versioning. And it relies on ever-changing
implementation guides that differ by use case, geography, and vendor. Most critically, it
14

presumes that EHR vendors—rather than a federal backbone—should remain the de facto
custodians of the nation’s health data. At Salphaware, we imagine a future in which the
EHR oligopoly no longer controls health data transferability through self-serving
obstinance.
FHIR is not a malicious standard, but it is the wrong substrate for national resilience. Its
use case should remain narrow: interfacing legacy vendors and transitional APIs. While
FHIR remains valuable for legacy interoperability, its architectural underpinnings limit
its viability as a national-scale substrate. Even within its intended role, FHIR is compute-
heavy, operationally complex, and costly to scale. It forces hospitals and developers to
interpret volatile implementation guides, manage constant version churn, and build or
license bespoke adapters for data normalization—each adding friction, technical debt,
and downstream risk. These limitations are well known, even within ONC FAST and
Argonaut forums, and have been acknowledged by FHIR developers themselves. Even
where bulk FHIR APIs aim to improve data flow, their operational cost, infrastructure
burden, and security surface area expand significantly—posing financial and technical
viability concerns for both providers and vendors [TD-15]. Real-time use cases collapse
under the weight. Edge devices cannot process FHIR payloads without substantial
middleware. These are not inconveniences. They are architectural disqualifications.
Although regrettably technical, several proven or emerging architectures offer
transmission models that are materially superior to FHIR, with real-world applicability.
They warrant inclusion here to ensure technical audiences are not preserving FHIR out of
familiarity at the expense of national cybersecurity. For example, DDS (Data Distribution
Service), deployed in aerospace and defense, supports real-time, QoS-governed, publish-
subscribe messaging ideal for latency-sensitive clinical scenarios. gRPC with Protocol
Buffers, widely used at Google and in Kubernetes, delivers compact, strongly typed,
high-throughput RPCs well-suited for backend health data exchange. ZeroMQ and
NATS, foundational in high-frequency trading and IoT, enable lightweight, decentralized
messaging across federated networks—with ZeroMQ operating brokerlessly and NATS
leveraging a minimal server-based architecture. IPFS (InterPlanetary File System), used
in decentralized research and provenance-focused archives, enables tamper-evident,
content-addressed storage for immutable health records. Matrix Protocol, adopted by the
French government, supports federated, end-to-end encrypted messaging applicable to
secure inter-organizational and public health communications. DAG-based ledgers,
including IOTA and Holochain, support asynchronous, provenance-preserving record
flows and are actively explored for identity and audit chains. Post-quantum cryptographic
protocols, including NIST-selected finalists such as Kyber and Dilithium, are already
being piloted in international health IT systems to harden transmissions against quantum-
era threats. These architectures—individually or in hybrid—represent viable successors
to REST-based paradigms and offer a forward-compatible foundation for a resilient,
scalable, and secure national health data infrastructure.
These transmission architectures complement or surpass FHIR’s REST-based exchange
by addressing what FHIR cannot: real-time delivery, strong typing, verifiable integrity,
15

and defense-grade resilience—without requiring abandonment of FHIR’s data schemas
where appropriate.
In short, FHIR’s use case should remain transitional and narrow. Its architecture was
designed for cooperation, not defense. It cannot meet the performance, security, or
adaptability requirements of a zero-trust, post-quantum infrastructure. Continuing to scale
it beyond its fit-for-purpose boundaries risks embedding technical debt into the very
foundation of national health security.
Atomic health data shipping—potentially under quantum-secure, entangled-node
architectures—is not speculative fantasy. It is a rational extension of current national
security needs and edge-case hospital realities where interoperability must be instant,
auditable, and functionally fail-proof. FHIR was not designed for—and cannot easily
adapt to—the demands of a national security-grade architecture. AHDA is.
The path forward requires more than patches. It requires realignment with foundational
truths:
1. Data is now infrastructure. Like energy or finance, it must be governed,
standardized, and secured as a national asset—not a fragmented commercial
product.
2. Security is not optional. Post-quantum cryptography and zero-trust enforcement
are no longer theoretical. Quantum computing will not hack systems one by
one—it will brute-force decrypt at scale, collapsing decades of cryptographic trust
in a single generation.
3. Trust is structural. Secure systems are not built with firewalls and policy
statements. They are built with architectures that eliminate compromise, enforce
transparent auditability, and align technical design with democratic values.
Salphaware supports FHIR for near-term compliance, but we urge CMS and ONC to
begin the transition toward next-generation interoperability protocols. Rather than

communities have begun reevaluating foundational technology stacks—including

is gaining favor due to its compile-time memory safety, low-level control, and resistance
to class-based vulnerabilities. These features enable secure, performant systems even in
adversarial or resource-constrained environments—essential for healthcare infrastructure

E. Federated Public Health Infrastructure: A Coordinated National Defense
Layer
In the State of Maryland, a national prototype already exists.
The Maryland Institute for Emergency Medical Services Systems (MIEMSS) is an
independent state agency responsible for the full operational command of emergency
medical services statewide. Created by statute in 1973, MIEMSS oversees trauma and
specialty center designation, statewide EMS licensure and certification, data collection
17

and analytics, and the command of Maryland’s emergency communications and medevac
dispatch systems. Through its Emergency Medical Resource Center (EMRC) and
SYSCOM center, MIEMSS coordinates real-time patient distribution, hospital diversion,
and air-medical transport—ensuring the right patient reaches the right hospital at the right
time, with precision that spans roadside response to trauma bay arrival.
In FY2023 alone, EMRC handled over 187,000 clinical calls; SYSCOM managed more
than 22,000 telephone consults and 14,000 radio dispatches. MIEMSS also operates the
CHATS alert system to coordinate hospital surge and diversion status statewide. This is
not conceptual infrastructure—it is real, operational, and replicable.
What makes MIEMSS exemplary is not its size but its structure. Data flows securely.
Standards are enforced. Real-time intelligence bridges EMS, hospitals, dispatch, and
public health responders. MIEMSS does not replace local systems—it synchronizes them.
And in doing so, it offers a living proof of how the federal government might support a
broader federated public health architecture without imposing a brittle, one-size-fits-all
monolith. MIEMSS demonstrates that coordinated, real-time public health infrastructure
is achievable. But it is not yet national [PA-1].
Today, no federal agency—neither the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention
(CDC), CMS, nor the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS)—maintains a
real-time dashboard of American health system readiness. No agency coordinates

improvised coordination. The result is not just inefficiency—it is fragility. And COVID-

It is time to make a choice. It is time to choose that America’s health is meaningful.
A federated public health infrastructure can unify these fractured capacities into a

equipment, supplies, and clinicians when local resources collapse. FEMA, already tasked
with federal disaster response, would gain real-time visibility into health system strain
and resource availability—enabling faster, better-coordinated action. The DoD could
further leverage this model as a proof-of-concept for coordinated military medical
response abroad, while the VA could harness it to maximize veteran care access and

health no longer operates in silos—it becomes a nationally orchestrated system that

A. Unified Sepsis Standard: Reducing Mortality, Denials, and Data-Driven
Fraud
The United States cannot afford to operate a healthcare system where patients are
measured by one standard, paid for under another, and denied care by a third. Yet that is
precisely what has emerged in sepsis care: CMS rightfully uses SEP-1 to measure patient
safety performance, with underlying clinical logic historically aligned to Sepsis-2.
Hospitals apply this logic in both care delivery and ICD-10-based coding for
reimbursement. But commercial payers—including Medicare Advantage plans—exploit
the more restrictive Sepsis-3 framework to deny or downgrade claims, asserting that
patients never met criteria despite receiving timely, life-saving care.
This is not an administrative inconvenience—it is a lethal misalignment of definitions,
executed through technology and payment logic that serve payers’ financial interests
while exposing hospitals to unrelenting clinical and financial threat.
Sepsis-3 Is Logically Broken and Dangerous by Design
Sepsis-3’s definitional flaws are both logically and scientifically incoherent:
• It embeds intervention-dependent criteria—such as the administration of
vasopressors—into the diagnosis itself. In practice, this means the sepsis
diagnosis is only “confirmed” after the patient has deteriorated enough to require
aggressive treatment. This cart-before-the-horse diagnostic model is circular logic
by definition—a logical fallacy—which disables early recognition and requires
failure to validate itself.
• Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) is a well-studied measure of
mortality. The authors of Sepsis-3 crudely grafted SOFA as a measure for sepsis,
and there is no shortage of clinical research opposed to this approach. This
commits a transitive property fallacy by positioning SOFA scores as both a
diagnostic marker and an outcome proxy. If SOFA = sepsis, and SOFA =
mortality, then by implication, sepsis = death—a false equivalency that distorts
both epidemiology and resource allocation. It embeds the fatal outcome into the
34

definition, rather than enabling clinicians to prevent it, and conceptually
represents a dangerous proximity to a confounding variable in this context.
Even the authors of Sepsis-3 warned in the research itself, “Neither SOFA nor qSOFA
are intended to be a stand-alone definition of sepsis.” Yet commercial payers—including
many Medicare Advantage plans—routinely use Sepsis-3 to justify DRG downgrades,
audit takebacks, and outright denials. HANYS called this out in 2019. AHIMA warned of
coding chaos in 2020. OIG launched audits in 2022. Each saw the writing on the wall—
clinical harm masquerading as payment policy—but to date, no U.S. regulating body
seems to have the upper arm strength to throw the red flag, even when it threatens human
life and defrauds hospitals.
Delays in recognition are not benign—sepsis mortality increases by approximately 8%
for every hour treatment is delayed. A framework that obscures or delays diagnosis is not
just clinically flawed; it is fatal by design. The result is a national bait-and-switch, where
hospitals are graded by a meaningful measure and reimbursed under a false one.
The Salphaware Response: Truth Through Technology
What we are witnessing across the nation is the misuse of health IT—not as a harmonizer
of clinical truth, but as an instrument of definitional disparity. Technology is being used
to automate denial logic, rather than to align care with evidence, payment with regulation,
and measurement with reality. This is the moral hazard of unregulated interoperability—
where, in its current form, definitional manipulation enables a systematic transfer of
funds from providers to payers [PR-2], bordering on institutionalized fraud, and
subsidized in part by the very government charged with preventing it. It must be
addressed by aligning technology with a single, certified, clinically valid definition of
sepsis.
To reinforce this effort, Salphaware’s Sepsis Studio proof-of-concept captures every
sepsis case across all major clinical and operational definitions in use today—including
SEP-1, Sepsis 2, and Sepsis-3—allowing for transparent comparison, appeals support,
and definitional research into whether Sepsis-3 has cost patient lives or caused harm in
exchange for payer financial advantage. This work will be measurable, data-driven, and
built to separate clinical truth from administrative distortion. It is how technology can
demystify and de-weaponize criteria ambiguity—and in doing so, protect both patient
outcomes and policy integrity.
The Precedent Exists—And I Helped Build It
When this definitional disparity first emerged in New York, I personally initiated the
advocacy to the Healthcare Association of New York State (HANYS) that led to the joint
Article 44 MCO Guidance issued by the New York State Department of Health
(NYSDOH) and Department of Financial Services (NYSDFS). That guidance—the first
of its kind—affirmed Sepsis-2, which is aligned to SEP-1, as the state-standard
framework for managed care organizations (MCOs). This guidance was supported by
35

pre-existing clinical-definitions-turned-state-law (10 NYCRR § 405.4(8)(a)). It restored
clinical consistency, protected hospitals, and slowed the proliferation of wrongful
reimbursement denials.
CMS should now reify this precedent as federal policy—not just in concept, but in
function—by requiring a singular, enforceable sepsis standard across all certified APIs,
CDS platforms, abstraction tools, and payer adjudication systems. That is the only way to
guarantee that what CMS measures is what hospitals deliver—and what payers must
honor.
A One Truth Standard for Sepsis
This is what the trilateral value proposition demands:
• Patients should not face denied coverage or downstream cost risk due to arbitrary,
retroactive thresholds that fail to reflect clinical judgment. This reflects

• Providers should be rewarded—not penalized—for early intervention that

• Government should stop footing the bill for definitional shell games played by

provider trust.
We urge CMS to act with clarity and moral force. Mandate a One Truth Standard for

B. National Pediatric SEP-1 Standard: Technology and Policy to Save

Each year, sepsis claims more pediatric lives in the United States than all childhood
cancers combined. Yet, while national attention and funding have rightly supported
aggressive standards for pediatric oncology, there remains no federal sepsis performance
measure designed specifically for children.
This omission is not simply an oversight. It is a systemic inequity that costs lives.
Hospitals are required to abstract adult SEP-1 cases, but no parallel obligation exists for
pediatric patients, despite clinical consensus that early recognition and treatment
dramatically improve outcomes across all ages. The absence of a national pediatric sepsis
standard means thousands of hospitals operate without a consistent framework for
detecting, measuring, and improving pediatric sepsis care [PA-1]. The result is a
patchwork of variable practice and missed opportunities for life-saving intervention.
These omissions contribute to preventable ICU admissions, prolonged hospitalizations,
and costly post-acute care—all of which impose financial burdens on CMS and reinforce
disparities in pediatric outcomes across rural and under-resourced facilities.
This gap is no longer technically necessary—or ethically defensible.
New York State has already demonstrated proof of concept. In response to the death of
Rory Staunton, the NYSDOH implemented the nation’s first pediatric sepsis protocol

requirement, backed by operational definitions and reporting infrastructure. The Rory

Creating a pediatric SEP-1 standard aligns directly with CMS' Health Equity Strategy and
Digital Quality Measurement goals—advancing data modernization while ensuring that
children are no longer invisible in national quality measurement frameworks. CMS is

and care;

is possible even for resource-constrained institutions;
• Encourage the development and certification of burden-negative digital tools that
translate this measure into practice without adding documentation overhead [PR-

By doing so, CMS will not only close a fatal gap in pediatric quality measurement—it

This is not a commercial recommendation. It is a moral one.
Equal protection under federal law cannot exclude the youngest and most vulnerable.
Pediatric patients deserve the same rigor, visibility, and safety scaffolding already

As a rural volunteer first responder, I first performed CPR at sixteen. Then came the
