CMS/ONC RFI Response on Digital Health Ecosystem Thank you for the opportunity to comment. I serve as Chief Medical O Ưicer for Primary Record, a patient-centered platform designed to help individuals and families aggregate, organize, and share their personal health information across fragmented healthcare systems. We serve diverse populations including complex pediatric patients, patients navigating assisted living, Medicaid/Medicare recipients, patients who require private care navigation services and others whose care requires cross-system data coordination that is rarely achievable through conventional EHR interoperability alone. While signiﬁcant policy progress has been made through TEFCA, ONC certiﬁcation, and FHIR API adoption, I respectfully submit that the patient experience remains profoundly fragmented. The technical architecture exists, but the human workﬂow burden remains largely unresolved. Most patients still struggle to collect comprehensive records, despite having the legal right to access them. In fact, many we see that are ﬁghting through complex illness still carry an outdated paper binder to advocate for their care due to the known gaps in information collection and sharing. Primary Record operates at the layer CMS is trying to unlock: empowering patients (or caregivers) to aggregate their data from multiple providers, formats, and sources. Our work shows both the promise of this vision and the remaining friction points that CMS/ONC could help address. In this, I believe we are perfectly positioned to o Ưer support in your eƯorts. PC-1 / PC-2 / PC-3 / PC-5: Patient-Centered Aggregation Remains an Unsatisﬁed Need  Most patients cannot easily assemble a comprehensive record across systems. Personal health record (PHR) tools like ours ﬁll the gap between provider portals and full interoperability. We have the ability to pull in and aggregate information available across multiple systems into one source of truth, searchable and available anywhere.  Primary Record users regularly require access to PDFs, scanned records, physician notes, lab reports, outside specialist reports, insurance data, and even non-clinical documents that are not exposed via FHIR APIs.  Existing apps generally fail because they rely too heavily on credentialed API pulls that patients cannot conﬁgure or maintain. These credentialed API pulls also vary 
wildly in quality and quantity of information available despite CURES act requirements.  CMS should recognize and support aggregation platforms that accept diverse data inputs from multiple sources including unstructured ﬁles to allow families to build usable longitudinal records. PC-8 / PC-9 / TD-2: CMS Should Expand the Scope of Data Feeds  Blue Button 2.0 o Ưers claims data, but clinical notes, imaging reports, consult summaries, and social determinants data are often missing. These are often key to eƯectively advocate for their care and get the best, most e Ưicient care possible.  Additional CMS data sets (prior authorizations, device data, appeals, care plans, assessments, etc.) would make personal health records far more useful.  CMS should also partner with state Medicaid programs, foster care agencies, and other federal partners to enable secure API-level export of state-held health records into patient-controlled PHRs. EƯiciency comes with aggregation and availability of this data for providers. PC-12 / TD-19: Personal Health Records Support Real-World Use Cases  Delays in care transitions (e.g., assisted living admissions, pediatric transplant workup, foster care transfers) often stem from fragmented records. This leads to increase cost, waste and worse outcomes.  Speciﬁcally for transition to long term care facilities: aggregation tools allow caregivers to assemble records in advance of provider or facility onboarding for improving care coordination, decreasing cost of admissions and business operations.  These are examples of how CMS policy changes could unlock operational eƯiciencies alongside improved patient experience. PR-4 / PR-7: Providers Need Simpliﬁed Data Access Support  Providers often lack simple ways to accept records families have aggregated themselves.  CMS should encourage broader standards around patient-contributed records, patient-generated data, and structured intake mechanisms for provider ingestion of content from programs like Primary Record. 
 Simpliﬁed intake of externally aggregated patient data could improve care quality while lowering administrative burden and redundant testing/waste. VB-1 / VB-3 / VB-4 / VB-11: Personal Records Are a Natural Enabler for VBC Success  Many VBC models struggle with care coordination gaps due to incomplete data.  Primary Record empowers patients and families to assemble comprehensive cross- system records that these programs can leverage for risk stratiﬁcation, care navigation, and population health analytics. As the above points state though, that information has to be available to leverage.  CMS should consider incentive models that recognize and reward VBC models who successfully incorporate patient-generated aggregated data into their operations. PC-14 / PR-9 / TD-3: Digital Identity Credentialing Would Lower Friction  We strongly support a federal identity credential (e.g., Login.gov) to eliminate the multi-portal credential problem that burdens patients today.  CMS should partner with trusted identity providers to allow patients one credential to access and authorize data across multiple payers and providers. TD-17: CMS Can Help Sustain The Aggregation Ecosystem  Non-provider aggregation platforms may not ﬁt cleanly into existing reimbursement models.  CMS should explore supportive infrastructure funding, public-private partnerships, or innovation grants to sustain independent platforms that ﬁll gaps in current interoperability frameworks. Conclusion Primary Record’s experience demonstrates that the technical standards are necessary, but not suƯicient, to achieve meaningful patient access and health system interoperability. Patient-centered aggregation platforms operate in the real-world messy middle, connecting the patient, caregiver, and provider across fragmented systems. CMS and ONC have the opportunity to actively embrace these models and reduce the remaining policy and technical obstacles that prevent full realization of the Cures Act’s vision. Thank you for the opportunity to provide input.
