CMS/ONC RFI Response on Digital Health Ecosystem
Thank you for the opportunity to comment. I serve as Chief Medical O(cid:431)icer for Primary
Record, a patient-centered platform designed to help individuals and families aggregate,
organize, and share their personal health information across fragmented healthcare
systems. We serve diverse populations including complex pediatric patients, patients
navigating assisted living, Medicaid/Medicare recipients, patients who require private care
navigation services and others whose care requires cross-system data coordination that is
rarely achievable through conventional EHR interoperability alone.
While significant policy progress has been made through TEFCA, ONC certification, and
FHIR API adoption, I respectfully submit that the patient experience remains profoundly
fragmented. The technical architecture exists, but the human workflow burden remains
largely unresolved. Most patients still struggle to collect comprehensive records, despite
having the legal right to access them. In fact, many we see that are fighting through
complex illness still carry an outdated paper binder to advocate for their care due to the
known gaps in information collection and sharing.
Primary Record operates at the layer CMS is trying to unlock: empowering patients (or
caregivers) to aggregate their data from multiple providers, formats, and sources. Our work
shows both the promise of this vision and the remaining friction points that CMS/ONC
could help address. In this, I believe we are perfectly positioned to o(cid:431)er support in your
e(cid:431)orts.
PC-1 / PC-2 / PC-3 / PC-5: Patient-Centered Aggregation Remains an Unsatisfied Need
 Most patients cannot easily assemble a comprehensive record across systems.
Personal health record (PHR) tools like ours fill the gap between provider portals and
full interoperability. We have the ability to pull in and aggregate information available
across multiple systems into one source of truth, searchable and available
anywhere.
 Primary Record users regularly require access to PDFs, scanned records, physician
notes, lab reports, outside specialist reports, insurance data, and even non-clinical
documents that are not exposed via FHIR APIs.
 Existing apps generally fail because they rely too heavily on credentialed API pulls
that patients cannot configure or maintain. These credentialed API pulls also vary

wildly in quality and quantity of information available despite CURES act
requirements.
 CMS should recognize and support aggregation platforms that accept diverse data
inputs from multiple sources including unstructured files to allow families to build
usable longitudinal records.
PC-8 / PC-9 / TD-2: CMS Should Expand the Scope of Data Feeds
 Blue Button 2.0 o(cid:431)ers claims data, but clinical notes, imaging reports, consult
summaries, and social determinants data are often missing. These are often key to
e(cid:431)ectively advocate for their care and get the best, most e(cid:431)icient care possible.
 Additional CMS data sets (prior authorizations, device data, appeals, care plans,
assessments, etc.) would make personal health records far more useful.
 CMS should also partner with state Medicaid programs, foster care agencies, and
other federal partners to enable secure API-level export of state-held health records
into patient-controlled PHRs. E(cid:431)iciency comes with aggregation and availability of
this data for providers.
PC-12 / TD-19: Personal Health Records Support Real-World Use Cases
 Delays in care transitions (e.g., assisted living admissions, pediatric transplant
workup, foster care transfers) often stem from fragmented records. This leads to
increase cost, waste and worse outcomes.
 Specifically for transition to long term care facilities: aggregation tools allow
caregivers to assemble records in advance of provider or facility onboarding for
improving care coordination, decreasing cost of admissions and business
operations.
 These are examples of how CMS policy changes could unlock operational
e(cid:431)iciencies alongside improved patient experience.
PR-4 / PR-7: Providers Need Simplified Data Access Support
 Providers often lack simple ways to accept records families have aggregated
themselves.
 CMS should encourage broader standards around patient-contributed records,
patient-generated data, and structured intake mechanisms for provider ingestion of
content from programs like Primary Record.

 Simplified intake of externally aggregated patient data could improve care quality
while lowering administrative burden and redundant testing/waste.
VB-1 / VB-3 / VB-4 / VB-11: Personal Records Are a Natural Enabler for VBC Success
 Many VBC models struggle with care coordination gaps due to incomplete data.
 Primary Record empowers patients and families to assemble comprehensive cross-
system records that these programs can leverage for risk stratification, care
navigation, and population health analytics. As the above points state though, that
information has to be available to leverage.
 CMS should consider incentive models that recognize and reward VBC models who
successfully incorporate patient-generated aggregated data into their operations.
PC-14 / PR-9 / TD-3: Digital Identity Credentialing Would Lower Friction
 We strongly support a federal identity credential (e.g., Login.gov) to eliminate the
multi-portal credential problem that burdens patients today.
 CMS should partner with trusted identity providers to allow patients one credential
to access and authorize data across multiple payers and providers.
TD-17: CMS Can Help Sustain The Aggregation Ecosystem
 Non-provider aggregation platforms may not fit cleanly into existing reimbursement
models.
 CMS should explore supportive infrastructure funding, public-private partnerships,
or innovation grants to sustain independent platforms that fill gaps in current
interoperability frameworks.
Conclusion
Primary Record’s experience demonstrates that the technical standards are necessary, but
not su(cid:431)icient, to achieve meaningful patient access and health system interoperability.
Patient-centered aggregation platforms operate in the real-world messy middle,
connecting the patient, caregiver, and provider across fragmented systems. CMS and ONC
have the opportunity to actively embrace these models and reduce the remaining policy
and technical obstacles that prevent full realization of the Cures Act’s vision.
Thank you for the opportunity to provide input.
