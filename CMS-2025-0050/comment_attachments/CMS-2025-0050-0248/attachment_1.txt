Regarding digital identity implementation: a. What are the challenges and benefits? Requiring the use of a secure, centralized digital identity platform— such as Login.gov —across all CMS programs and regulated entities would offer significant benefits in reducing fraud, enhancing access, and streamlining identity verification for beneficia ries, particularly for low -income individuals, seniors, and those transitioning between coverage types. Benefits: Mandating Login.gov as the standard digital identity for all CMS programs —including Medicare, Medicaid, the Children’s Health Insurance Program ( CHIP ), and federally facilitated marketplace (FFM) Qualified Health Plans (QHPs) —would create a unified and reusable identity that beneficiaries could use across all systems. This is especially critical for individuals who age into Medicare from Medicaid, a population that frequently faces gaps in care and administrative complexity. With a single, government -verified credential, these individuals could securely access benefits, authorize data sharing (e.g., Blue Button or payer -to-payer exchange), and navigate multiple programs without re-verifying their identity each time. This policy would also reduce identity theft and benefit fraud by establishing strong, multifactor authentication and centralized account lifecycle management. By requiring Login.gov integration across state Medicaid agencies and all CMS -regulated plans —including MA plans, QHPs, and Medicaid managed care organizations —CMS could dramatically reduce duplicate identity systems, improve auditability, and enhance trust across the healthcare data ecosystem. Challenges: Implementing a universal Login.gov requirement would involve substantial coordination across federal, state, and private -sector stakeholders. States would need technical and financial support to integrate Login.gov into Medicaid eligibility and enrollment systems, including legacy platforms. Plans would need to adjust existing member portals and workflows to comply. Ensuring accessibility for individuals with

How would requiring digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs) impact cybersecurity and data exchange? Mandating the use of digital identity credentials that meet NIST 800 -63-3 IAL2/AAL2 standards —such as Login.gov, CLEAR, or ID.me —would significantly enhance cybersecurity posture and facilitate more secure and streamlined data exchange across CMS programs. By establishing a common identity trust framework, CMS would reduce the attack surface associated with fragmented authentication mechanisms and inconsistent identity verification practices. A centralized, federally verified credential would provide strong multifactor authentication (MFA), lifecycle management, and auditability, ensuring only authorized individuals access sensitive beneficiary data. Increased use of these credentials would also standardize and simplify authentication across the CMS ecosystem, improving user experience for patients, caregivers, and application developers. For example, patients transitioning between M A and Medicaid Managed Care plans could use a single credential to authorize continuity of care through Blue Button or payer -to-payer APIs, improving data liquidity and reducing administrative burden. From a system integration perspective, aligning authenti cation protocols to OIDC or OAuth 2.0 through trusted credential service providers (CSPs) would enable interoperable, federated identity frameworks that are scalable and policy -aligned. However, implementation must account for digital equity, accessibility, and alignment with legacy systems. Skyward recommends a phased, standards -based rollout that prioritizes low -barrier identity verification pathways, maintains support for assistive tec hnologies, and includes robust stakeholder engagement across state Medicaid agencies, plans, and health IT developers. With these considerations addressed, requiring digital identity credentials would be a foundational step in advancing secure, person - cent ered health data exchange across CMS -regulated programs. c. Mandating the use of the OIDC identity protocol across CMS programs would establish a standardized, interoperable framework for federated identity management, significantly improving authentication workflows across the healthcare ecosystem. OIDC enables se cure, scalable single sign-on (SSO), allowing users —including patients, providers, and application developers —to authenticate once and be authorized across multiple systems and APIs.
