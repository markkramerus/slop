Regarding CMS Data, to stimulate developer interest— a. What additional data would be most valuable if made available through CMS APIs? For a medical AI company, several additional data types would be immensely valuable if made available through CMS APIs, beyond what's currently common: ●Imaging Data (de-identified): De-identified medical images (CT, MRI, X-ray, etc.) paired with structured reports hold immense potential for advancing AI in medical diagnostics and prognostics. Access to this data would enable the development of algorithms capable of identifying subtle indicators of disease progression, treatment effectiveness, 
and early disease detection, potentially surpassing human visual capabilities. This capability could revolutionize healthcare through improved diagnostic accuracy and earlier interventions. ●Genomic and Proteomic Data:Leveraging de-identified genomic and proteomic data from Medicare beneficiaries could facilitate personalized medicine AI. This integration could improve the prediction of drug efficacy, adverse event risks, and disease susceptibility by considering individual biological characteristics. ●Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) Data:To enhance AI models' ability to understand and address health disparities and personalize interventions, more detailed and specific social determinants of health (SDOH) data is needed. Examples include information on housing stability, food insecurity, transportation access, education level, and neighborhood safety. Linking this data to health outcomes is essential for developing AI that advances health equity. ●Longitudinal Claims and Clinical Data: More seamless access to longitudinal, de- identified data combining claims with available clinical data (e.g., EHR data such as lab results, vital signs, and medication administration records) than Blue Button 2.0 currently offers would facilitate the development of AI for predicting disease trajectories, optimizing chronic care management, and identifying high-risk patients. ●Caregiver-Reported Outcomes and Engagement Data: To effectively develop AI tools that aid caregivers and enhance patient outcomes in home and community settings, obtaining direct data from caregivers is essential. This data should encompass their experiences, patients' functional status, adherence to care plans, and the difficulties they encounter. ●Device-Specific Data (e.g., RPM data): Predictive analytics development hinges on timely access to de-identified sensor data from remote patient monitoring and connected health devices. These analytics enable alerts to providers regarding potential health crises or significant deviations from a patient's baseline. b. What data sources are most valuable alongside the data available through the Blue Button 2.0 API? While Blue Button 2.0 is an excellent start, the most valuable data sources to integrate alongside it for a medical AI company would include: ●Electronic Health Record (EHR) Data: To effectively train AI models for nuanced understanding of patient health, access to Electronic Health Record (EHR) data is crucial. While Blue Button 2.0 offers valuable claims data detailing billed services, EHR data provides essential clinical context. This includes diagnoses, symptoms, lab results, medication lists, physician notes, vital signs, progress notes, and other detailed information. This rich clinical depth is paramount for developing sophisticated AI models. ●Pharmacy Data: A comprehensive understanding of a patient's pharmacotherapy 
requires detailed prescription fill history, medication adherence data, and insights into over-the-counter medication use. This complete picture is essential for medication reconciliation, predicting adverse drug events, and implementing adherence interventions. ●Wearable and Sensor Data:Continuous physiological data from consumer wearables and medical-grade sensors provides real-world insights that can enhance episodic clinical and claims data. This combination enables proactive health management and facilitates the early identification of health changes. ●Provider-Submitted Clinical Registries and Quality Data: Specialized clinical registries, such as those for stroke or heart failure, frequently hold high-quality, curated clinical data. This information is highly valuable for developing precise AI models focused on specific diseases. Additionally, data on quality measures can offer insights into the effectiveness of healthcare. ●Public Health Data: By combining individual patient data with public health information like immunization records, disease surveillance, and environmental data, AI models can gain deeper insights into both individual health risks and broader population health trends. This integration provides a community-level context for understanding individual health. c. What obstacles prevent accessing these data sources today? ●Data Silos and Fragmentation: Fragmented healthcare data across diverse EHR systems, payers, and providers presents substantial technical and logistical obstacles to creating a complete, longitudinal view of patient information. ●Lack of Standardized Data Formats and Semantics: Accessing data is only the first hurdle; its inconsistent formats, terminology, and coding necessitate significant "data wrangling" and normalization for AI training, creating a major obstacle to scalability. ●Privacy Concerns and Regulations (HIPAA, etc.): Strict privacy rules, such as HIPAA, along with differing interpretations and reluctance to share data, create obstacles to accessing even anonymized data, despite its necessity. Furthermore, obtaining Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval for research access is often a protracted and complicated procedure. ●Commercial and Proprietary Interests: Access to numerous data sources, particularly EHRs and proprietary device data, is controlled by private companies that may impose restrictions or fees due to commercial interests. This poses a considerable financial obstacle for smaller AI businesses. ●Technical API Limitations:Current APIs might not allow querying large amounts of data, accessing it frequently, or accessing specific data fields. These limitations make it difficult to obtain the complete datasets required for thorough AI training. ●Data Governance and Trust Frameworks: The absence of clear and standardized data 
governance frameworks and trust agreements frequently hinders data sharing, particularly when involving different organizations or crossing state lines. ●Lack of De-identification Standards and Tools: Though crucial, the lack of universally adopted, clear, consistently applied standards and readily available tools for effective and provable de-identification hinders data sharing due to hesitations. d. These explainable AI (XAI) capabilities 
are essential for fostering trust and ensuring the responsible clinical application of AI. 2. Digital Identity TD-3. Regarding digital identity implementation: a. What are the challenges and benefits? Challenges ●Interoperability and Standardization: The absence of uniform digital identity standards among healthcare systems and providers severely hinders smooth data exchange and verification processes. ●Privacy and Security Concerns:Safeguarding sensitive patient health information (PHI) is critical and necessitates robust digital identity solutions with stringent security measures to prevent breaches and unauthorized access. Benefits: ●Enhanced Cybersecurity: Robust digital identity verification can substantially decrease unauthorized data access, phishing attempts, and various cyber threats, consequently enhancing the security of healthcare information. ●Improved Data Exchange and Interoperability: Secure and efficient health information sharing across systems and organizations is facilitated by standardized digital identities. This streamlines workflows and improves care coordination. ●Streamlined Workflows and Reduced Administrative Burden:Automated identity verification and access management in healthcare can streamline workflows, leading to time and resource savings by decreasing manual processes for providers. ●Enhanced Patient Experience:Secure digital identities can streamline how patients access their health information and services, leading to improved patient experience. ●Increased Trust and Accountability: Strong digital identities enhance confidence in the 
healthcare ecosystem by verifying the authenticity of individuals accessing health data, increasing trust. ●Compliance with Regulations:Digital identity solutions can assist organizations in adhering to data privacy and security regulations, such as HIPAA. b. How would requiring digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs) impact cybersecurity and data exchange? ●Cybersecurity: ○Reduced Unauthorized Access: Stronger authentication requirements significantly reduce the risk of unauthorized access to sensitive health data by making user impersonation substantially harder. ○Stronger Authentication:Implementing multi-factor authentication (MFA) and other strong authentication methods aligned with IAL2/AAL2 will become the norm, substantially reducing the likelihood of stolen credentials. ○Improved Audit Trails: Reliable identity verification improves the accuracy and trustworthiness of audit logs, which in turn strengthens incident response and forensic capabilities. ○Reduced Phishing and Social Engineering: Stronger identity verification enhances security by making it more difficult for attackers to deceive users into divulging their credentials. ●Data Exchange: ○Increased Trust and Confidence:Greater certainty regarding the identities of participants in data exchange networks would increase trust and encourage more data sharing. ○Streamlined Authorization: Directly linking digital identity credentials to access control policies enables more precise and automated authorization for data exchange. ○Improved Data Integrity:Restricting data modification to authorized individuals and systems improves the integrity of exchanged health information. ○Facilitated Interoperability Frameworks:Secure participation in health information exchange networks (e.g., TEFCA) relies on these foundational credentials c. Mandatory use of the OpenID Connect (OIDC) identity protocol would have a largely positive impact: ●Standardization and Interoperability: Adopting the mature OpenID Connect (OIDC) standard, built on OAuth 2.0, would significantly improve interoperability across healthcare applications by simplifying secure login and identity sharing.
●Developer Friendliness:OpenID Connect's (OIDC) straightforward implementation for developers can facilitate quicker development of new applications and services utilizing secure digital identities. ●Improved User Experience:A unified single sign-on (SSO) system for healthcare applications would enhance user convenience and reduce the burden of managing multiple passwords. ●Enhanced Security (Authentication Layer): OpenID Connect (OIDC) is an authentication layer built on top of OAuth 2.0. It ensures proper user authentication before issuing access tokens for resource access. ●Reduced Vendor Lock-in:As an open standard, OpenID Connect (OIDC) fosters competition within the identity provider market and minimizes dependence on vendor- specific solutions. ●Scalability:OIDC's architecture enables it to handle a significant volume of users and applications due to its scalable design. Organizations not already employing OpenID Connect (OIDC) might face a transition phase involving development work and staff training, representing possible minor disadvantages. 3. CMS can better encourage the use of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs through several mechanisms: ●Prioritize in Certification Programs: Prioritize and strengthen the requirement for open, standards-based APIs within the ONC Health IT Certification Program. This could involve awarding bonus points or making it a necessary condition for specific certifications. ●Incentivize Adoption:To promote effective use of open APIs for data exchange, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) could implement payment model incentives (e.g., within MIPS and APMs). These incentives could take the form of financial rewards or preferential treatment for providers and health IT developers who demonstrate strong support for and utilization of these APIs. ●Develop and Promote Reference Implementations: To ease development and showcase effective methods, CMS, working with ONC and industry, might fund and promote open- source reference implementations of crucial open APIs, such as FHIR-based APIs. ●Provide Technical Assistance and Training: Provide resources, workshops, and technical assistance to facilitate the understanding, adoption, and implementation of open API standards for healthcare organizations and developers. ●Publicly Acknowledge and Showcase Success Stories: Identify organizations and developers demonstrating successful open API utilization to enhance patient care, efficiency, and interoperability.
●Require Transparency and Public Documentation: Certified health IT developers should be required to publicly document their API capabilities, including their adherence to open standards, data models, and versioning practices. ●Collaborate with Standards Development Organizations (SDOs): Actively participate in and support Standards Development Organizations (SDOs) that develop open healthcare data standards, such as HL7 FHIR, to ensure that CMS's requirements are incorporated into these standards. ●Leverage Procurement Power:CMS should prioritize or mandate the use of solutions with open, standards-based APIs in its health IT system and service procurements. ●Disincentivize Proprietary APIs: Discourage the over-reliance on proprietary APIs that impede interoperability and create information silos through policy. This could involve increased scrutiny in information blocking investigations. TD-5. How could a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints improve access to health information for patients, providers, and payers? A nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints would be a transformative improvement for access to health information: Improvements to Access: ●For Patients: Patients would benefit from simple methods to link their personal health applications (PHAs) or patient portals with their different healthcare providers' systems. This would allow them to gather their health information from numerous locations, streamlining personal health record management and giving patients greater control over their data. ●For Providers: A central, reliable platform would allow providers to easily find FHIR endpoints for others, streamlining the process of requesting and receiving patient data for referrals, transitions of care, and care coordination. Who Should Publish? Ideally, a neutral, trusted, and authoritative entity should publish such a directory. Options include: ●ONC/HHS:Considering their involvement in health IT and interoperability, the Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ONC), or a similar agency within the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS), presents a viable option, possibly through a collaboration with a non-profit organization. What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform? The Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) performs several unique interoperability functions that differentiate it from previous efforts: ●Establishes a Common Agreement and Governance: The Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) establishes a unified, nationwide agreement that all Qualified Health Information Networks (QHINs) are required to follow. This single agreement establishes consistent guidelines, technical standards, and organizational 
frameworks for health information exchange across the country, replacing a fragmented system of individual agreements. ●Creates a Network of Networks (QHINs): TEFCA moves away from direct connections between individual organizations by creating a tiered framework. In this system, organizations connect to a Qualified Health Information Network (QHIN), and these QHINs then connect with each other. This forms a scalable "network of networks," which greatly decreases the need for numerous one-to-one connections. ●Mandates Universal Purpose of Use (UoP): The Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) facilitates the exchange of electronic health information (EHI) for all HIPAA-permitted purposes. This eliminates the need for individual agreements or specific "purpose of use" statements for each exchange, thereby streamlining data sharing across various use cases. ●Enforces Common Technical Standards (FHIR & IHE XCA): TEFCA requires the adoption of existing, openly accessible standards, such as FHIR for data querying and retrieval and IHE XCA for document exchange. This mandate, while not establishing novel standards, guarantees technical interoperability within the network. ●Provides a Dispute Resolution Process: To ensure accountability and maintain trust within the network, TEFCA incorporates a dispute resolution process for its participants. ●Focus on Foundational Interoperability: The Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) is designed to create a fundamental framework and set of rules that will enable extensive health data exchange. This foundational structure will then support the development of more sophisticated applications and services. a. What existing alternatives should be considered? While TEFCA is establishing a nationwide framework, existing alternatives for health information exchange (HIE) include: ●Bilateral Agreements/Direct Exchange: Direct, point-to-point connections and agreements between organizations are a common approach for data sharing. However, this method lacks the scalability needed for widespread interoperability. ●Regional/State Health Information Exchanges (HIEs): Local or state Health Information Exchanges (HIEs) support data sharing within specific geographic regions. However, a challenge for these valuable initiatives is establishing connections beyond state borders or with national networks. ●Private Health Information Networks (HINs): Existing commercial networks like CommonWell Health Alliance and Carequality enable data exchange within their memberships and have reached considerable scale. While functioning with independent governance, TEFCA's goal is to leverage and interconnect these established networks. ●Proprietary Vendor Networks: Certain EHR providers offer proprietary networks that enable data exchange among their users. However, the utility of these networks is 
frequently restricted to the specific vendor's user base. ●Patient-Mediated Exchange: While patient portals and apps enhance individual access to health data and facilitate sharing with other providers, they do not effectively address the broader challenge of provider-to-provider data exchange at scale. b. Are there redundant standards, protocols or channels or both that should be consolidated? While redundancy can exist, the current landscape reflects historical development and varying use cases. However, opportunities for consolidation and streamlining exist: ●Consolidating Document Exchange Protocols: Despite FHIR's emergence, older document exchange methods like CDA over XDM/XDR/XDS/XCA persist and warrant assessment. Although FHIR excels with granular data, document exchange remains relevant. Therefore, a strategic plan for either adopting FHIR-native document resources or guaranteeing seamless interoperability is essential. ●Standardizing Query Mechanisms:Although FHIR RESTful APIs are increasingly prevalent, some systems continue to employ older query methods. Promoting a uniform FHIR- based query strategy for all new implementations would offer advantages. ●Reducing Variation in FHIR Implementations: Despite FHIR's standardization, inconsistencies in profiles, extensions, and implementation guides hinder interoperability. These challenges could be lessened through stricter adherence to implementation guides and more uniform profiling, such as the US Core standard. ●Streamlining Consent Management: The diverse consent processes used by various Health Information Exchanges (HIEs) and networks create complexity. Standardizing both the technical models for consent (for example, FHIR Consent resources) and the associated policies would offer significant benefits. ●Leveraging TEFCA as the Primary Channel: Consolidating data exchange through the TEFCA QHIN network, instead of numerous individual agreements, would streamline channels and decrease complexity over time. This approach encourages all significant data exchange to utilize the QHIN network. TD-7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange and what are its limitations? The United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) has significantly improved interoperability and exchange by: ●Establishing a Baseline: The US Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) establishes a basic set of data elements that all certified health IT systems must support for exchange. This 
standardization mandates the consistent availability of key data points during information exchange. ●Driving Developer Compliance: Mandating USCDI within the ONC Health IT Certification Program has driven EHR vendors to support the exchange of this defined set of data. ●Facilitating FHIR Adoption: The USCDI's inherent link with FHIR and its US Core profiles promotes the faster and more consistent adoption of FHIR for data exchange. ●Reducing "Data Blocking" Arguments: Clearly defining the "core" data elements for exchange reduces the likelihood of actors claiming uncertainty about which data should be shared. ●Enabling Patient Access: The United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) serves as the foundation for patient access Application Programming Interfaces (APIs). This framework enables patients to more readily obtain their personal health information. Limitations of USCDI: ●Limited Scope (Data Elements): Although USCDI establishes a base, it lacks the complete set of data elements required for thorough patient care, research initiatives, public health efforts, and sophisticated analysis. Numerous significant data categories, such as social determinants of health, genomic information, detailed care strategies, specific medical device details, and scanned documents, are not currently explicitly included within USCDI. ●"Least Common Denominator" Challenge: Because it serves as a foundational dataset, it frequently embodies the most basic information that diverse systems can reliably offer, as opposed to the complete and detailed data that might be shared. ●Lack of Granularity/Context: Although data elements are specified, the required level of detail, context, and clinical specificity for all applications may be lacking. For instance, a "problem list" might be included, but comprehensive clinical notes clarifying the subtleties of a diagnosis could be absent. ●Format Limitations:While the USCDI specifies the data elements, their practical implementation and exchange frequently involve FHIR. This reliance on FHIR, however, permits variation through implementation profiles and extensions, potentially resulting in minor interoperability issues. a. Does it contain the full extent of data elements you need? While the USCDI offers a strong starting point and includes essential foundational data, it lacks the comprehensive data elements necessary for many complex use cases and thorough patient care. Thus, numerous stakeholders need more detailed, specialized, or context-rich information. b. If not, is it because of limitations in the definition of the USCDI format or the way it is utilized? The primary reason for USCDI's limitations in meeting comprehensive data needs lies in the 
inherent scope of its definition. USCDI is purposefully designed as a core dataset and is not intended to be exhaustive. Although variations in its application exist (such as incomplete vendor implementation), the fundamental constraint stems from its deliberately limited scope. c. If so, would adding more data elements to USCDI add value or create scoping challenges? How could such challenges be addressed? Adding more data elements to USCDI would certainly add value for many use cases (e.g., public health reporting, clinical research, complex care management, social determinants of health). However, it would create scoping challenges: ●Increased Implementation Burden: Every new element added requires all certified health IT vendors to update their systems, which can be costly and time-consuming. ●Increased Complexity: A larger, more complex USCDI could make it harder for developers to implement consistently and for users to understand. ●Data Availability Challenges: Not all organizations may collect or have standardized data for newly added elements, leading to gaps in exchange. ●Consensus Building: Reaching consensus among diverse stakeholders on what new elements to add and their precise definitions can be a lengthy process. ●Version Control: Managing different versions of USCDI with varying data elements can add complexity to interoperability. How such challenges could be addressed: ●Phased Approach/Version Control: Continue with a phased, iterative approach (USCDI v1, v2, v3, etc.), clearly communicating the additions and providing ample time for implementation. ●Modular Expansion: Instead of a single monolithic USCDI, consider "modules" or "extensions" that can be adopted for specific use cases (e.g., "USCDI for Public Health," "USCDI for Oncology"). This allows for targeted expansion without burdening all implementers. ●Leverage Industry Best Practices: Incorporate data elements and definitions that are already widely used or emerging as best practices within specific clinical domains or public health. ●Strong Governance and Prioritization: Establish a transparent and efficient governance process for evaluating and prioritizing new data elements based on demonstrated need, feasibility, and impact. ●Guidance and Tooling: Provide clear implementation guides, reference implementations, and tooling to help developers integrate new USCDI elements efficiently. ●Focus on FHIR Profiles: Emphasize the importance of robust FHIR profiles (e.g., US Core) that define how USCDI elements are represented, and provide mechanisms for extensions where specific data is needed beyond the core.
d. Given improvements in language models, would you prefer a non-proprietary but less structured format that might improve data coverage even if it requires more processing by the receiver? This is a question with significant tradeoffs. Preference for structured data remains strong for core clinical and administrative data. For many critical workflows (e.g., billing, medication reconciliation, lab results), highly structured, discrete data is essential for accurate processing, automated decision support, and reliable analytics. Language models, while powerful, still carry a risk of hallucination or misinterpretation when extracting information from unstructured text, which could have serious consequences in healthcare. However, for data elements that are inherently unstructured or exist primarily in free text (e.g., detailed clinical narratives, social determinants of health captured in notes, nuanced patient preferences), a "less structured" but universally accessible format, combined with advanced language models, could indeed improve data coverage and unlock valuable insights. Here's a breakdown of the preference: ●For critical, discrete data (e.g., medications, allergies, diagnoses, lab results, demographics): Strong preference for highly structured, standardized formats (like FHIR with USCDI) remains paramount. The processing by the receiver is minimal, and accuracy is high. ●For narrative and contextual data (e.g., physician notes, discharge summaries, patient history, social determinants of health discussions): A non-proprietary, less structured format (like standardized clinical documents or even plain text, as long as it's accessible via API) combined with language models for extraction and summarization could be highly valuable. This would enable accessing information that currently sits in silos of free text. The "more processing by the receiver" is acceptable here because that information is often inaccessible or requires manual chart review otherwise. Key considerations for this approach: ●Non-proprietary is critical: Even if less structured, the format and access mechanisms must be open and not locked to a specific vendor. ●Robust Language Models: Reliance on language models requires continuous improvement in their accuracy, explainability, and ability to handle clinical jargon and context. ●Data Quality and Validation: Mechanisms would be needed to validate the information extracted by language models to ensure accuracy before it's used for critical decisions.
●Hybrid Approach: The ideal scenario might be a hybrid approach where USCDI continues to define the core structured elements, and additional mechanisms (e.g., FHIR DocumentReference, unstructured text APIs) are standardized for accessing the broader "full EHI" that can then be processed by advanced AI/ML models. Key to Effectiveness: The effectiveness isn't just about the presence of a criterion but its specificity, clarity, and the rigor of its enforcement. Compliance TD-18. Information blocking: a. Could you, as a technology vendor, provide examples for the types of practices you have experienced that may constitute information blocking. Please include both situations of non- responsiveness as well as situations that may cause a failure or unusable response? ●Ignoring API Requests: A technology vendor's certified health IT system is supposed to expose APIs for data exchange, but they consistently fail to respond to legitimate API requests, or responses are extremely delayed without justification. ●Lack of Documentation/Support: The vendor provides minimal, outdated, or intentionally obscure API documentation, making it virtually impossible for third-party developers to integrate "without special effort." They may also be unresponsive to requests for technical support regarding their APIs. ●"No, We Don't Support That" for Standard Capabilities: Despite holding ONC certification that mandates specific interoperability capabilities (e.g., FHIR-based patient access APIs), the vendor tells prospective integrators or providers that these capabilities are "not yet supported" or "not part of their standard offering." ●Delaying Access Agreements: Prolonging the legal and contractual process for API access or data use agreements indefinitely, or imposing unreasonable legal terms that effectively block access. Situations Causing a Failure or Unusable Response: ●Proprietary API Wrapper Around Standard APIs: The vendor uses a proprietary "wrapper" 
or intermediary layer that requires specialized knowledge, libraries, or fees to access their otherwise standard (e.g., FHIR) APIs, defeating the "without special effort" intent. ●Non-Standard FHIR Implementations: Implementing FHIR APIs that deviate significantly from the US Core profiles or other common implementation guides, requiring extensive custom development for each integration. For example, using non-standard codes, unexpected resource structures, or missing required fields. ●Incomplete or Partial Data Returns: When an API request for EHI is made, the response is systematically incomplete, missing key data elements even when those elements are clearly present in the patient's chart. For example, omitting allergy details or specific lab results. ●Rate Limiting or Throttling: Imposing overly restrictive rate limits or throttling on API calls that effectively make large-scale data extraction (e.g., for population health) infeasible or extremely slow, without clear justification. ●Excessive Fees for API Access: Charging exorbitant or discriminatory fees for API access or for the data exchanged through APIs, making it financially prohibitive for many legitimate use cases. ●Requiring Unnecessary Certification/Vetting: Demanding a burdensome and expensive vetting process or redundant certifications for third-party applications or developers that go beyond what is reasonable for security and privacy. ●Data in Unusable Formats (e.g., PDFs from APIs): While technically providing data, it's delivered in an unstructured, non-machine-readable format (e.g., a PDF of lab results instead of discrete FHIR observations), requiring manual processing and making it unusable for automated systems. ●Lack of Event-Based APIs/Webhooks: Not providing event-based notifications or webhooks for new data, forcing external systems to constantly poll for updates, which is inefficient and places a burden on both sides. b. Are there specific categories of healthcare actors covered under the definition of information blocking in section 3022(a)(1) of the Public Health Service Act (PHSA) that lack information blocking disincentives? Yes, as of the time of the Cures Act Final Rule, there was a tiered approach to disincentives. ●Health IT Developers and Health Information Exchanges (HIEs)/Networks: These entities face significant financial penalties for information blocking (up to $1 million per violation).
●Healthcare Providers: The disincentives for healthcare providers are less direct and currently rely on "appropriate disincentives" to be established by CMS, potentially impacting participation in value-based care programs or MIPS scores. There isn't yet a direct, per-instance fine structure for providers as there is for developers. TD-19. Regarding price transparency implementation: a. What are current shortcomings in content, format, delivery, and timeliness? ●Content Shortcomings: ○Incomplete Data: Often missing the full scope of services, particularly for complex procedures or bundled payments. May not include all professional fees or facility fees. ○Lack of Personalization: Published prices are often "standard charges" or gross charges, not the actual out-of-pocket costs a specific patient will incur (which depends on insurance, deductibles, etc.). ○Missing Context: Difficult to understand what specific clinical services are included in a given price or how quality metrics relate to cost. ○Variation in Definitions: Different hospitals or payers may define services or bundles differently, making comparisons difficult. ○Chargemaster vs. Negotiated Rates: Often focuses on the chargemaster (list price) rather than the actual negotiated rates with payers, which are the relevant costs. ●Format Shortcomings: ○Machine-Readability: Many published price lists are in PDF format or large, unwieldy CSVs, making automated parsing and comparison challenging. ○Lack of Standardization: No consistent format across different entities, requiring significant data normalization efforts. ○Poor User Interface: Often buried deep on hospital websites, difficult to navigate, and not user-friendly for patients. ●Delivery Shortcomings: ○No API Access: Very few, if any, entities offer standardized API access to their price transparency data, hindering programmatic access and integration into third-party tools. ○Lack of Centralized Access: No single, searchable repository for prices across all providers. ○Limited Real-time Access: Typically static files that are updated infrequently, not reflecting dynamic pricing or real-time negotiated rates. ●Timeliness Shortcomings: ○Infrequent Updates: Price lists are often updated only annually or semi-annually, quickly becoming outdated. ○Lag in Negotiated Rate Updates: Changes in payer contracts and negotiated rates 
are not reflected quickly. b. Which workflows would benefit most from functional price transparency? ●Pre-Service Patient Education & Financial Counseling: Patients and financial counselors could provide accurate out-of-pocket cost estimates before a service is rendered, improving financial preparedness and reducing surprise bills. ●Referral Management: Providers referring patients to specialists or imaging centers could consider cost along with quality when making recommendations. ●Care Planning & Shared Decision-Making: Clinicians and patients could discuss treatment options not just on clinical effectiveness but also on anticipated costs. ●Prior Authorization Streamlining: Payers could leverage transparent pricing to expedite prior authorization requests, especially for common procedures. ●Benefits & Coverage Verification: Automated systems could quickly determine patient liability based on their specific insurance plan and the transparent pricing. ●Post-Service Bill Reconciliation: Patients could more easily understand and verify their medical bills against transparent prices. ●Quality and Cost Comparison: Tools could emerge that allow patients and payers to compare costs for similar services across different providers or facilities, driving competition. c. What improvements would be most valuable for patients, providers, or payers, including CMS? ●For Patients: ○Personalized, Real-Time Out-of-Pocket Cost Estimates: An easily accessible tool that shows their specific cost based on their insurance plan, deductible, and remaining benefits for a given service. ○User-Friendly Comparison Tools: Simple interfaces to compare costs and quality for specific services across multiple providers. ○Proactive Communication: Providers proactively informing patients of estimated costs before services. ●For Providers: ○Standardized API Access to Payer-Specific Rates: The ability to programmatically query specific payer negotiated rates for their services. ○Integration into EHR Workflows: Tools that integrate cost estimates into their clinical and administrative workflows (e.g., order entry, referral). ○Reduced Administrative Burden: Less time spent on manual cost inquiries and patient financial counseling. ●For Payers (including CMS):
○Machine-Readable, Standardized Data Feeds: Consistent, granular data feeds of negotiated rates and expected patient liabilities across all providers. ○Bulk Data Access via APIs: Ability to efficiently ingest large volumes of pricing data for analysis and integration into their systems. ○Improved Fraud, Waste, and Abuse Detection: Better price transparency can highlight outliers and potential areas of concern. ○More Accurate Payment Models: Access to real-time pricing data can inform and refine value-based payment models. d. What would further motivate solution development? ●Mandate Standardized APIs for Price Transparency: This is the most critical step. Requiring APIs that adhere to a specific, machine-readable format (e.g., FHIR-based price transparency resources) would enable developers to build robust tools. ●Stronger Enforcement and Penalties: Consistent and meaningful penalties for non- compliance with existing or new price transparency mandates. ●Financial Incentives for Early Adopters: Reward providers and payers who go beyond minimum requirements and implement robust, API-driven price transparency solutions. ●Centralized Data Repositories: CMS or another trusted entity could facilitate a national repository or registry of price transparency APIs/data feeds, making it easier for developers to discover and access the data. ●Define "Patient Out-of-Pocket Cost": Clearly define what constitutes "patient out-of- pocket cost" and mandate that this be the primary focus of price transparency efforts, not just gross charges. ●Include All Related Services: Require transparent pricing for all components of a service (e.g., professional fees, facility fees, anesthesia, labs for a surgery), not just the main procedure code.
