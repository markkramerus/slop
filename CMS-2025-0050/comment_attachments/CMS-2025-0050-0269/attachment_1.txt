Advancing Scalable Lifestyle Medicine: A Strategic
Imperative for CMS
A. Clinical and Economic Rationale
Lifestyle Medicine (LM) addresses chronic illness through behavioral root causes: diet,
sleep, stress, movement, substance use, and social connectedness.
Evidence-Based Outcomes:
• Type 2 Diabetes: WFPB diets reverse disease (Barnard, 2009)
• Cardiovascular Disease: Ornish’s LM program reversed atherosclerosis (Ornish,
1998)
• Mental Health: Structured LM reduces medication dependence (Baban & Morton,
2022)
Economic Rationale:
• 86% of U.S. health costs stem from chronic conditions
• American College of Lifestyle Medicine notes that a self-insured plan covering
patients with hypertension, hyperlipidemia, diabetes, or combinations thereof,
achieved a $9.64 ROI over five years through lowered pharmaceutical use,
procedures, and referrals
• Ideal for Medicare Advantage, Medicaid Waivers, and CHART models
B. Policy Recommendations for CMS
• Create LM CPT bundles for interdisciplinary delivery
• Fund Innovation Center pilots integrating LM in rural clinics

• Track lifestyle adherence in quality metrics (e.g., PROMIS, sleep, HRV)
• Reimburse AI-based LM coaching platforms integrated with EMRs

PATIENTS AND CAREGIVERS- RESPONSES
1. What policies or innovations could encourage individuals and caregivers to use digital
health tools?

• Deploy AI health agents offering lifestyle medicine coaching, real-time biometric
feedback (e.g., HRV, glucose, sleep), and education embedded in mobile platforms.
• Use tokenized rewards (e.g., $HRX token) to incentivize measurable health
improvements via PROMIS and biometric data.
• Provide mobile-first, FHIR-based access to medical records via Blue Button 2.0.
2. How can digital health tools better support health equity and reduce disparities in
care?
• Equip rural and underserved populations with low-bandwidth, device-agnostic
platforms.
• Utilize digital identity and consent frameworks to empower marginalized groups to
own and monetize their data.
• Deploy AI coaching agents trained to recognize social determinants of health (SDOH)
and route patients to local services.
3. What are the barriers to using these tools?
• Digital literacy, lack of personalization, and platform fragmentation.
• Our system addresses this with adaptive UI, predictive personalization via federated
learning, and centralized dashboards integrated across FHIR and TEFCA frameworks.
PROVIDERS-RESPONSES
1. What are the barriers to implementing digital health tools?
• Lack of EHR integration and provider bandwidth.
• Our solution embeds LM protocols and decision support via CDS Hooks into Cerner
and Epic, reducing administrative burden and improving workflow efficiency.
2. What policies or innovations could support providers in adopting and using digital
health tools?
• Reimbursement for LM-based AI decision aids.
• Inclusion of LM protocol delivery in quality metrics and MIPS incentives.
• Automated outcome documentation from REDCap via ICD/SNOMED/FHIR
mappings.
3. How can digital tools assist providers with burden reduction and burnout
prevention?
• Automate care plan generation using biometric + genomic data.
• Reduce data re-entry with FHIR-connected REDCap registries.
• Use AI to monitor lifestyle adherence and prompt necessary interventions.
PAYERS- RESPONSES

1. What are barriers to reimbursing for digital health technologies?
• Limited frameworks for preventive outcome valuation.
• Our solution uses tokenized incentive systems tied to verified biometric improvement
(e.g., HbA1c, HRV), allowing for cost-effective, VBC-aligned reimbursement
models.
2. What payment innovations or demonstrations could improve adoption of digital
tools?
• Health-to-Earn™ frameworks where digital adherence and lifestyle outcomes
generate tangible health currency, which can be converted into plan-based incentives.
• Use AI-driven forecasting tools to pre-authorize reimbursement based on predicted
improvements in high-cost patients.
3. How can CMS use digital tools to expand access to care and improve quality?
• Support expansion of federated AI platforms into rural, tribal, and Medicaid-
supported clinics.
• Incentivize lifestyle adherence and data-sharing via tokenized health rewards under
CMS pilot frameworks.
TECHNOLOGY DEVELOPERS AND DATA NETWORKS- RESPONSES
1. What standards or policies could enhance interoperability and innovation?
• Mandate FHIR, USCDI v4, CDS Hooks, and NIST 800-63 compliance across all
digital vendors.
• Support decentralized architectures and API-first development.
2. How can CMS support access to actionable data and digital identity infrastructure?
• Build on Blue Button 2.0 by allowing patients to authorize federated data aggregation
for care optimization.
• Incentivize platforms that utilize token-based patient permissioning and audit trails.
3. How can TEFCA evolve to support health equity and innovation?
• Embed AI-assisted consent tools and LM-focused federated registries within TEFCA
exchange frameworks.
• Expand TEFCA to include outcome-based reward systems for patient improvement
tracking.
VALUE-BASED CARE ORGANIZATIONS AND PAYMENT MODEL
PARTICIPANTS- RESPONSES

1. How can digital tools be embedded in VBC models?
• Link tokenized rewards to shared savings models (e.g., ACO REACH).
• Measure and reimburse on lifestyle adherence metrics and PROMIS outcomes.
2. What data are needed to support effective value-based models?
• Longitudinal biometric data (HRV, HbA1c), lifestyle engagement data, PROMIS
scores, and genomics.
• Our federated registry and AI agents consolidate this data for CMS-compatible
submission.
3. How can CMS foster broader adoption of digital health in APMs and MA?
• Recognize LM adherence as a performance metric.
• Fund pilot programs that use AI + tokenization to scale outcomes-based payment.
• Create LM innovation tracks within CMMI using tools like those offered by N2V and
WaiveDx.

• Providers: Enable automated care planning and EHR-linked LM interventions
• Payers: Integrate real-time health data for value-based adjustments
• Tech Vendors: Support decentralized architectures and FHIR-compliant pipelines

5. Technical Architecture Overview
A. System Workflow
1. Patient Data Capture: HRV, glucose, steps, PROMIS → via wearable or app
2. Data Stream: AWS Kinesis → S3 & HealthLake
3. Analytics: SageMaker ML → Risk Prediction & LM Guidance
4. Registries: WaiveDx↔ FHIR → EMR (Cerner/Epic)
5. Incentives: Health improvement → Token ($H) → Wallet
6. CMS Reporting: Outcome exports via Blue Button & USCDI standards
7. System complies with HIPAA, TEFCA, and supports low-bandwidth rural
deployment.
