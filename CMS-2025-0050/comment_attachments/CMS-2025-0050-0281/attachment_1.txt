Executive Summary
Salesforce welcomes the opportunity to respond to the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS)
and the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy/Office of the National Coordinator for Health
Information Technology (ASTP/ONC) Request for Information on advancing digital health technology for
Medicare beneficiaries. As a leading cloud platform provider serving healthcare organizations nationwide,
we offer unique insights into the challenges and opportunities facing the digital health ecosystem.
This response addresses the request concerning the evolution of the digital health ecosystem for
Medicare beneficiaries. The RFI seeks to build upon the foundational policies established by the 21st
Century Cures Act and subsequent rulemakings, which aim to enhance health data availability, secure
patient access, and promote interoperability. While significant progress has been made, particularly with
initiatives like Blue Button 2.0 and the establishment of FHIR-based APIs, the RFI acknowledges that the
patient, caregiver, and provider experience remains fragmented and often complex.
The core objective of this RFI is to gather feedback on how to accelerate the adoption of health
management and care navigation applications, reduce barriers to data access and exchange, and foster
innovation that ultimately improves health outcomes within a patient-centric learning health system.
Furthermore, it emphasizes the critical role of technology in the transition to value-based care (VBC) and
seeks input on aligning technology requirements with the needs of providers in Alternative Payment
Models (APMs).
Our response highlights the current landscape's challenges, such as the lack of consolidated health
information access for patients and caregivers across disparate systems, and the complexities faced by
app developers in integrating diverse healthcare data. We emphasize the demand for digital tools that
offer a holistic health view, personalized care plans, streamlined communication, efficient care
coordination, and cost transparency.
We propose that a comprehensive health engagement platform, integrating clinical and non-clinical data
with advanced analytics and AI capabilities, is essential for digital transformation. This platform can create
a "digital front door" for 24/7 service and hyper-personalized engagement. We detail how solutions built
on platforms like Salesforce can offer a unified patient profile, facilitate seamless data exchange through
FHIR standards and frameworks like TEFCA, and provide AI-powered insights for proactive care and
operational efficiency.
Key recommendations include:
Enhancing Patient & Caregiver Engagement: By providing intuitive user interfaces, guided workflows,
omni-channel support, and accessible features, alongside clear education and robust security.
Improving Data Access & Integration: Addressing the fragmentation of data across EHRs and other
systems by promoting unified data views, comprehensive interoperability, and bidirectional data flows.
Leveraging TEFCA: Recognizing its role in creating a standardized and secure framework for nationwide
health information exchange, streamlining patient onboarding, and enabling data harmonization for
personalized experiences.
Supporting Value-Based Care: Integrating AI, population health analytics, risk stratification, care
coordination, usability, and patient engagement into APM requirements to drive better outcomes and
reduce costs.
Ultimately, our response underscores the necessity of a scalable, flexible, and secure health IT
infrastructure that empowers patients, supports providers, and aligns with the goals of value-based care,
paving the way for a truly seamless and effective digital health ecosystem.

What are the obstacles to accessing your own or your loved ones' complete health information

limitations in functionality, user friendliness, or access to basic technology infrastructure)?
Electronic health records (EHRs) are designed to log every medical interaction a patient has with a
provider and contain clinical information about them, facilitating safe care delivery and supporting
regulatory compliance. However, EHRs are primarily encounter-focused, and their regulatory-linked
nature and medicolegal risks make innovation and changes difficult, leading to potential stagnancy in
healthcare technology. This means that while EHRs are crucial for clinical data, they may not readily
provide access to a comprehensive view of a patient that includes non-clinical data, or be easily
adaptable to integrate with other systems for a holistic patient experience.
A robust CRM, especially one integrated with an EHR, addresses these limitations by centralizing both
clinical and non-clinical data, offering a complete view of the patient. This integration is not just beneficial
for patient relationships but also for providers, who often deal with fragmented technology stacks
alongside their EHRs. By integrating with a CRM, healthcare organizations can overcome challenges like
fragmented provider network management and centralize data for a unified view, which is valuable for
onboarding providers and new clinicians, and for populating accurate provider directories.
The integration of an EHR with an AI-powered CRM eliminates data silos, harmonizing and organizing all
available data, including clinical, non-clinical, and insurance information. This unified data provides a full
view of the patient, enabling more collaborative care delivery. Furthermore, centralizing data management
ensures industry compliance and provides alerts for regulatory updates. This integrated approach
ensures that AI capabilities draw from clean, high-quality data, leading to optimal insights for patient care
and operations.
Obstacles to accessing complete health information and using it for managing health conditions or finding
the best care often stem from limitations in EHR functionality and the lack of a unified view of patient data.
The traditional EHR, while essential for clinical records, isn't designed to evolve at the pace of consumer
expectations or integrate seamlessly with the broader healthcare ecosystem. This can make it difficult for
patients to engage with the healthcare system and for providers to offer personalized experiences.

a. Could you, as a technology vendor, provide examples for the types of practices you
have experienced that may constitute information blocking. Please include both situations of
non-responsiveness as well as situations that may cause a failure or unusable response?
As a technology vendor operating in the healthcare sector, we have encountered various practices that
impede the seamless flow and usability of health information. These experiences fall broadly into
situations of non-responsiveness and those that lead to data failure or rendering it unusable.
Situations of Non-Responsiveness:
23

● Unmet Requests for Data Access or Integration: We frequently face instances where requests
for standard data access pathways or integration points with foundational health information
systems are met with slow or no response, unexplained delays, or access denial. These requests
are essential for our applications to ingest or contribute critical patient information, impacting our
ability to deliver features that enable patient access, more efficiently coordinate care, and improve
quality.
● Lack of Technical Specifications for Interoperability: A significant hurdle is the absence of
publicly available or adequately documented technical specifications, APIs, or integration
protocols from established platform providers. This forces our development teams to engage in
costly and time-consuming reverse-engineering efforts, or in many cases, makes integration
impossible, effectively isolating critical data within proprietary environments.
● Unwillingness to Establish Necessary Data Exchange Pathways: Despite clear clinical or
operational needs for data exchange, we sometimes encounter a reluctance to collaborate on
establishing necessary technical interfaces or data pathways. This can manifest as a lack of
engagement from the incumbent vendor or the healthcare provider, leading to stalled projects
aimed at enhancing data liquidity.
Situations that Cause a Failure or Unusable Response:
● Provision of Data in Incompatible or Proprietary Formats: When data is eventually provided,
it often arrives in formats that are highly specific to the originating system, requiring extensive
transformation, manual reconciliation, or custom coding to be consumable by our standard-based
applications. This increases implementation costs, introduces data integrity risks, and often
renders automated data exchange impractical or unreliable.
● Restrictive Agreements, Unreasonable Pricing, or Data Blocking: Our ability to fully leverage
health information is frequently hampered by contractual clauses, licensing agreements, or the
imposition of unreasonable prices for access to or distribution of EHR data, crucial for running or
maintaining healthcare operations. These agreements or prohibitive costs can severely limit a
customer's legitimate use of their own data with third-party solutions, effectively constituting hard
data blocking and creating barriers to innovation even when the technical means for data access
might exist.
● Consequences of Vendor Influence and Lack of Interoperability: We have observed
situations where the significant influence of a primary platform vendor on a healthcare
organization's technology strategy can sometimes lead to the deferral or discontinuation of
integration efforts with alternative solutions. In the absence of robust interoperability, this dynamic
can contribute to vendor lock-in, potentially limiting a healthcare organization's agility to innovate
and optimize care delivery.
b. What additional policies could ASTP/ONC and CMS implement to further discourage
healthcare providers from engaging in information blocking practices?
● Promote Vendor-Agnostic Health IT Platforms and Data Portability: ASTP/ONC and CMS
should provide clear guidance encouraging the adoption of health IT platforms that are inherently
vendor-agnostic. This means moving away from single-vendor, closed ecosystems towards open
architectures that facilitate seamless data portability and exchange across disparate systems,
regardless of the originating vendor. Policies should actively discourage practices that lead to
"vendor lock-in," where providers become excessively reliant on one vendor due to technical or
contractual barriers to switching. This would foster a competitive marketplace, drive innovation,
and ensure that healthcare organizations can choose the best-of-breed solutions without fear of
data entrapment.
