Inequity: Errors have greater impact on minorities and those with poorer health Minorities or those with poorer health are less likely to speak up to report errors [JAMIA 2021] “Participants who self -identified as Black or African American, Asian, “other,” or “multiple” races, or those who reported poorer health were each less likely to speak up than White or healthier respondents. Interop means errors can propagate. Stop them at the source. Interoperability mandates are improving the sharing of data between organizations. Unfortunately, that means errors get shared and propagate at an increasing rate.
The Problem is Becoming More Visible Government mandates are unlocking patients’ data to allow them to fully view data in their chart. Patients and advocates are now able to see the enormous amount of errors that could negatively impact their care. There is now more need for a standardized process for patients to report errors than at any time in the past. 
“Correction Principle” from ONC, 2008: In its Nationwide Privacy and Security Framework for Electronic Exchange of Individually Identifiable Health Information , the ONC adopted the following principle on “Correction”: “Individuals should be provided with a timely means to dispute the accuracy or integrity of their individually identifiable health information (IIHI), and to have erroneous information corrected or to have a dispute documented if their requests are denied” First released by ONC in December 2008 and has been subsequently included in the ONC Health IT Strategic Plan. 
Recommendation Over a Decade Ago Previous recommendations: The Health IT Policy Committee (in 2011) was tasked to give recommendations that will help build public trust in health information technology and electronic HIE and enable their appropriate use to improve healthcare quality and efficiency. Health IT Policy Committee recommended to ONC that they establish certification criteria to enable the HIPAA request for correction/amendment process. https://www.healthit.gov/sites/default/files/facas/07_25_11_HITPC_Letter_PrivSecTigerTeam.pdf
Use of EHR Data in Research With the advancement of FHIR, many research organizations (including Public Health) are increasing their use of EHR data. Unless we provide an easy way for patients to make sure their data is correct , researchers will be using bad data. Research using bad data can only result in bad research . (Eroding public trust in health information technology and research)
