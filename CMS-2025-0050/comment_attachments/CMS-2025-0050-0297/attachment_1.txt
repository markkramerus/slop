TD-16: Point-to-Point vs. Shared Network Models
Point-to-point models like HL7 are inherently unscalable. Shared networks are emerging
naturally from modern architecture. These market-driven shared networks are not based on
TEFCA, FHIR, or HL7—they are being built on modern cloud infrastructure and designed for
real-time, flexible collaboration. The platforms that are succeeding today do so because they
work, not because they are government-endorsed.
Any attempt by CMS or ONC to regulate the architecture of shared networks now risks
solidifying outdated paradigms. It will freeze innovation and entrench TEFCA, FHIR, and
HL7—legacy standards that cannot support the performance needs of modern value-based
care.
Recommendation: Let performance and cost-efficiency drive network evolution. Do not lock in
outdated infrastructure or standardize incomplete models. Government intervention at this stage
will only slow progress and protect underperforming incumbents. Let performance and

TD-18: Information Blocking and Regulatory Scrutiny
Startups are disproportionately penalized under current regulatory frameworks, while
incumbents exploit HIPAA and TEFCA loopholes to block access. We’ve seen real patients
harmed while compliant, patient-consented data was withheld. Innovation in data access and
transparency is already underway, but risks being frozen by premature regulatory
standardization.
Recommendation: Enforce the 21st Century Cures Act uniformly. Scale regulatory scrutiny
proportionally to organization size and impact. Focus on real-world data access for care teams.
VB-1: Incentives for ACOs to Leverage Digital Health
ACOs are financially motivated to adopt effective tools. They do not need mandates on
“how”—only incentives for “what.” Our platform is used because it works, not because it’s
required. Market-driven adoption of collaborative technologies is already happening.
Government-imposed standards risk stalling that momentum.
Recommendation: Maintain pay-for-performance as the lever. Let ACOs select technologies
that help them succeed.

VB-3: Essential Health IT Capabilities for Value-Based Care
Value-based care depends on real-time data, patient attribution, and collaborative workflows.
These capabilities are being delivered now—without TEFCA, FHIR, or mandated EHRs—by
new platforms designed for today’s challenges. Performance-aligned solutions are growing
organically. Regulation now would risk baking in outdated technologies.
Recommendation: Focus on measurable results. Refrain from certifying specific technologies
or infrastructure. Let performance targets guide adoption.
Conclusion
We urge CMS and ONC to stay focused on performance. Do not regulate technology
implementation. Market-driven platforms are already emerging, gaining adoption, and
demonstrating value. Incentivize outcomes and let innovation flourish. Any attempt to define the
"how" through regulation risks empowering legacy players and freezing progress.
