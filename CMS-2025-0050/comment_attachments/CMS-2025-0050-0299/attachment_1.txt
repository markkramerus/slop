Question TD-2b. Valuable Data Sources Alongside Blue Button 2.0
We recommend that Appointments become a required resource in FHIR APIs. When used in
combination with claims and clinical data, appointment data can significantly enhance patient
engagement and care coordination strategies. For example, outreach campaigns can be more
targeted and efficient when aware of upcoming visits. This avoids redundant touchpoints and also
prompts providers to address care gaps during already scheduled encounters. This minimizes
patient friction while maximizing clinical value.
To address privacy or workflow concerns (e.g., behavioral health exclusions), CMS could allow for
filtering or opt-outs while still promoting broad appointment data availability for general use.
Question TD-5. Nationwide Provider Directory of FHIR Endpoints
A national directory of FHIR endpoints would be a powerful enabler of interoperability for
providers, payers, and third-party application developers. For providers, it would ease the burden
of discovering where and how to share data when managing shared patients or making referrals.
For payers and population health vendors, it would streamline the process of aggregating clinical
data from disparate sources, which is critical for quality measurement, risk adjustment, and
anayltics-driven value-based care performance improvement.

We believe the directory should be published and maintained by a neutral federal entity in
coordination with existing industry efforts. The model could follow a federal-private consortium
approach, akin to the governance of TEFCA, with broad stakeholder input. To ensure accessibility
and equity, access to the directory should be free of charge to all stakeholders.
Question TD-9e. Bulk Data Transfer for Quality Reporting
We are strongly supportive of using the bulk data capabilities of EHRs to reduce the burden of
quality reporting to CMS. Today, much of the administrative overhead lies in the manual
extraction, calculation, and submission of performance measures. While bulk data access
simplifies the first step, the latter two remain barriers.
To truly reduce provider burden, CMS should consider enabling organizations to become verified
submitters through the use of expanded US Core profiles that support the data elements required
for quality calculations. This approach would allow organizations to consistently pull structured
data, calculate measures internally or through vendor partners, and submit the results directly
without the need for redundant chart reviews or vendor reprocessing. Importantly, this could be
achieved without imposing significant new reporting burdens, provided profiles are well-aligned
to existing clinical workflows and data availability.
Question TD-15: Regarding Bulk FHIR APIs
Provider networks, especially clinically integrated networks (CINs), continue to face substantial
challenges accessing clinical data across their provider groups. The current state, as we see with
many of the networks we support, often requires organizations to build numerous bespoke
integrations, resulting in significant delays, high vendor costs, and frequent abandonment of
integration efforts. This deprives providers and their partners of access to data critical for timely,
coordinated, and effective care.
Performant bulk FHIR APIs would dramatically improve data access for high-value use cases, such
as timely follow-up after hospital discharges, intelligent referral workflows, and care model
prioritization. However, in their current form, bulk APIs often lack the scalability and
responsiveness needed to support these operational needs. As a result, many organizations are
forced to rely on individual patient FHIR APIs, which are not designed for large-scale data
retrieval and require inefficient looping mechanisms. Unreliable, non-performing bulk APIs result
in wasted development resources, inefficiencies, and avoidable delays.
To unlock the full value of clinical data in population health and value-based care programs, bulk
APIs must be able to return all relevant resources for an entire practice patient panel—ideally
within 3–4 days. This timeline supports workflows that depend on weekly data refreshes and
allows adequate time for downstream analytics such as quality, risk, and utilization analysis.

Requiring implementation of the `_since` parameter will also encourage adoption, as it alleviates
the need to pull full history with every API call.
Notably, performant bulk APIs would also enable access to data elements not typically available
via traditional connections, including clinical notes. Notes provide valuable narrative context that
can meaningfully influence care planning. Clinical notes often contain unstructured insights such
as informal observations, SDOH barriers, and differential diagnoses, all of which can be useful for
care coordination and decision-making.
