Executive Summary
SAS Institute Inc. (SAS) appreciates the opportunity to provide comments on the future
of health care IT in the United States and celebrates the Centers for Medicare &
Medicaid ServicesвЂ™ (CMS) and the Office of the National Coordinator for Health ITвЂ™s
(ONC) decision to explore and request feedback on powerful topics in health care
information technology. As a leader in data, analytics and AI for nearly 50 years, SAS
has the distinct pleasure of helping hundreds of health care organizations across the
world make sense of their data. SAS offers advanced analytics and artificial intelligence
to provide value on an open and transparent platform.
SAS agrees with CMSвЂ™ vision for a more open, interoperable and data-driven health care
future. To build this future, our response reflects lessons learned from decades of
experience working with leading health care organizations, specifically the power that
advanced analytics can bring to a large health system. As an example, SAS has worked
with the Emirates Health Service to build their goal of a вЂњdigital hospitalвЂќ, by developing a
complete patient registry, predicting fatal outcomes and providing these data clinical
decision makers insights to better drive patient care. Similarly, SAS worked with Duke
Health to develop AI models to optimize patient flow through an infusion clinic, with the
potential to improve patient care and reduce overhead and waste in this expensive care
area.
In the area of interoperability, SAS is the analytics software for the North Carolina Health
Information Exchange, NC HealthConnex. In this work, we find a system that often
struggles to normalize data without access to costly third-party reference data and one
that has been slow to move to Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR), but
one that shows promise and potential when uniting connected health data with powerful
data management and analytics.
SASвЂ™ motto has always been The Power to Know, for we understand that the open flow
of data, in a secure and appropriate fashion, combined with powerful and transparent
analytics leads to real knowledge. This is why our SAS Health platform was built with a
FHIR-first design and utilizes SASвЂ™ Model Cards capability which provides transparent
insights into potential bias within AI models. It is with this experience and more that SAS
is glad to present input back to CMS and ONC.

B. Patients and Caregivers
This section is intended for all stakeholders to provide input on questions as they relate to use
cases and workflows that involve patients and caregivers. While we certainly want patients and
caregivers to answer questions in this section (and in other sections) from the patient/caregiver

1. Patient Needs
PC-1. What health management or care navigation apps would help you understand and manage
your (or your loved ones) health needs, as well as the actions you should take?
The ideal app would be fully integrated, sitting on top of a single, secure platform that aggregates
data from electronic health records, lab results, wearables, prescription fills/refills, etc. to provide
a real-time, 360-defgree view of vitals, medications, and upcoming health related tasks. Ideally,
there would be built-in decision-support algorithms to convert raw data into concrete prompts, for
things such as scheduling a telehealth-visit, requesting a refill, or ordering follow-up labs, so that
next step(s) are never missed. When chronic conditions are in play, continuous remote
monitoring and coaching keep both patients and clinicians ahead of potential issues by surfacing
early warning trends. A caregiver-sync feature lets assigned family members access the same
dashboard to help manage, replacing scattered texts and missed reminders with coordinated,
accountable action.
a. What are the top things you would like to be able to do for your or your loved onesвЂ™ health that
can be enabled by digital health products?
The following are the top desirable things that can be enabled by digital health products:
вЂў Integrated care itinerary that tracks upcoming appointments, lab orders, imaging, and
referrals on a single timeline with push reminders
вЂў Real-time benefit look-ups for out-of-pocket estimates, prior-authorization status, in-network
alternatives with pricing compare/contrast before care is scheduled, and alerts for generic
alternatives, medication bundles etc.
вЂў A medication command and reconciliation center with refill alerts, drug-interaction checks,
and price-comparison coupons in one place
вЂў Symptom triage with escalation based on evidence-based self-care advice that can be
handed off to telehealth, urgent care, or 911 as needed
вЂў Secure family sharing for caregiver views limited to what the patient authorizes, especially for
individuals who are experiencing any cognitive impairment
вЂў Chronic condition management dashboards integrate vitals, labs, and medication adherence
for patients with diabetes, hypertension, heart failure, etc., with alerts to providers when
thresholds are breached
b. If you had a personal assistant to support your health needs, what are the top things you would
ask them to help with? In your response, please consider tasks that could be supported or
facilitated by software solutions in the future.
Tasks that could be supported or facilitated by software solutions in the future include:
вЂў Handle appointment booking, rescheduling, and transportation set-up automatically
вЂў Scan new test results, flag anything outside normal range, and explain next steps in plain
language
вЂў Track quality and satisfaction metrics for each care provider and suggest the best local
options
вЂў Keep a running list of programs a patient qualifies for like free vaccines, preventive
screenings, food-as-medicine pilots, and sign me up and/or nudge me before deadlines

PC-5. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in these
digital health products?
Sharing use cases of where these tools have improved the patient experience can bridge the gap
between technical demonstrations of the power of these tools and the human factor at play in the
caregiver relationship.
a. What role, if any, should CMS have in reviewing or approving digital health products on the
basis of their efficacy, quality or impact or both on health outcomes (not approving in the sense of
a coverage determination)? What criteria should be used if there is a review process? What
technology solutions, policy changes, or program design changes can increase patient and
caregiver adoption of digital health products (for example, enhancements to data access,
reimbursement adjustments, or new beneficiary communications)?
Aside from other factors (e.g., security), CMS should develop quality checks and easily
deployable models to gauge the effectiveness of digital health products on an objective basis. For
example, comparing health outcomes and spending for patients using the products compared to
those which are not using the products. Although the model-building tools may originate
elsewhere, there should be roles nested within CMS to conduct the actual evaluations.
b. What changes would enable timely access to high quality CMS and provider generated data on
patients?
An end to legacy, monolithic data management systems at all levels would facilitate the
integration of CMS/provider data. This would require a significant investment on the part of CMS
but would produce unprecedented efficiencies in the long-term.
PC-6. What features are most important to make digital health products accessible and easy to
use for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers, particularly those with limited prior experience
using digital tools and services?
Based on a quick survey of the literature, there are several must-haves for proper engagement of
Medicare recipients. Big text, high contrast screens, and simple workflows to keep navigation
effortless. Plain-language prompts and brief, built-in tutorials teach as they guide. Proxy logins
with shared alerts that allow caregivers to step in without friction. Finally, automatic pulls from
EHRs, pharmacies, and wearables to eliminate manual data entry.
PC-7. If CMS were to collect real-world data on digital health productsвЂ™ impact on health
outcomes and related costs once they are released into the market, what would be the best
means of doing so?
CMS could require standardized FHIR feeds from digital tools that track usage, outcomes, and
costs, integrating them into existing environments like the Chronic Conditions Data Warehouse.
From there, platforms such as SAS Viya can link, analyze, and visualize the combined claims,
EHR, and device data, allowing CMS to detect real-world impact without creating extra reporting
work for vendors.

PC-11. How are health information exchanges (HIEs) currently helping to advance patient access
to health information in the real world?
HIEs may have a stated goal of being able to provide patient access to data stored within the HIE
system. Currently, the North Carolina HIE of which SAS is the analytics provider, does not allow
this function. The HIE does allow patients to view where their data has been sent but is not
required to disclose the patient data housed within (separate entities provide that function).
d. What is the ongoing role of HIEs amidst other entities facilitating data exchange and broader
frameworks for data exchange (for example, vendor health information networks, TEFCA, private
exchange networks, etc.)?
HIEs are the communityвЂ™s neutral, ethical trust broker, linking hospitals, clinics, public-health
agencies, and social-service partners. By normalizing disparate EHR feeds, EMS reports, lab
results, and social-determinant data, HIEs close last-mile gaps and keep clinicians and public-
health officials aligned. As they are typically locally governed as nonprofits, they enforce
community-driven consent rules, rigorous bias checks, and transparent audit trails that extend
beyond HIPAA, protecting marginalized groups while enabling research and quality improvement.
Their longitudinal, cross-provider datasets make them the logical proving ground for ethical AI
alerts, predictive risk scoring, and real-time public-health dashboards. National networks may
move the bulk data, but HIEs ensure that information remains trustworthy, inclusive, and
actionable for the communities that generate it.

3. Information Blocking and Digital Identity
PC-13. How can CMS encourage patients and caregivers to submit information blocking
complaints to ASTP/ONCвЂ™s Information Blocking Portal? What would be the impact? Would
increasing reporting of complaints advance or negatively impact data exchange?
CMS could encourage patients and caregivers to submit information blocking complaints to
ASTP/ONCвЂ™s Information Blocking Portal by:
вЂў Placing a вЂњReport data-access problemвЂќ button inside every point of interaction and allowing
voice or SMS submissions for those with low digital literacy
вЂў Auto-populating the complaint form with as much context as possible
вЂў Highlighting success stories where complaints triggered quick fixes, shifting the messaging
from punitive towards improvement focus, creating a safe, no-fault feedback loop
вЂў Offering an anonymous option and reassure users of confidentiality protections, as well as
publish aggregate complaint trends so providers see systemic gaps over personal attacks.
вЂў Linking reporting to incentives (positive reinforcement), possibly tying remediation of
validated complaints to positive points in CMS quality programs.
A higher volume of well-documented complaints provides a clear map of where information
blocking occurs. That evidence allows organizations to target appropriate guidance, technical
help, and enforcement (as needed), which in turn pressures vendors and providers to clean up
access barriers faster. If CMS is empowered to triage complaints back to the market quickly,
more reporting can become a force-multiplier for positive change. The risk of this being a net
negative is if the process starts to feel punitive, arbitrary, or excessively bureaucratic in nature.

1. Digital Health Apps
PR-1. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage providers, including those in rural areas,
to leverage approved (see description in PC-5) digital health products for their patients?
SAS cares deeply about widening access to proven digital health tools, especially in rural and
underserved regions, and applauds CMSвЂ™s resolve to do the same. CMS can offer start up
incentives, extra shared savings credit for rural practices, targeted broadband subsidies, and on
demand technical assistance so resource constrained providers can adopt approved apps without
financial strain. A single FHIR based interface standard, a public catalog of certified products, and
regular best practice playbooks will keep vendors, EHR teams, and clinicians on the same page.
SAS can then supply secure analytics that track adoption and outcomes in real time, turning raw
data into clear performance feedback that rewards what works and flags gaps before they widen.
a. What are the current obstacles?
Current obstacles include:
вЂў Less than half of households in rural health-care deserts subscribe to fixed broadband or own
devices like laptops or tablets, making reliable real-time apps or video visits difficult.
вЂў Rural providers lag in adopting certified electronic health records, hindered by limited
technical support, connectivity challenges, and lower interoperability.
вЂў Tight margins and unclear reimbursement models discourage rural practices from investing in
digital tools.
вЂў Rural areas have an insufficient number of physicians, many of whom are over age 50.
вЂў Rural seniors face digital-health literacy that comes along with trust gaps, frequently requiring
personalized, local support before adopting apps for routine health management.
вЂў Rural clinics identify cybersecurity as a barrier, lacking resources and expertise needed to
confidently integrate new digital health devices and services into existing IT systems.
b. What information should providers share with patients when using digital products in the
provision of their care?
Information that providers should share with patients include:
вЂў Providers should explain the appвЂ™s purpose, detailing what it tracks and how it will enhance
diagnosis, monitoring, or treatment.
вЂў They need to specify what data the app collects, where it is stored, who can access it, and
how long it will be retained.
вЂў Providers should spell out the patientвЂ™s responsibilities: how often to use the tool, which alerts
require action, and when to call the clinic instead.
вЂў They must outline privacy and security protections, such as encryption and authentication
steps, while reminding patients they can revoke access at any time.
вЂў Any potential costs, data charges, or insurance coverage details should be disclosed upfront.
вЂў Clear support channels must be provided, identifying whom to contact for technical issues or
to report side effects and device problems.
вЂў With the further adoption of AI within digital health product, CMS should provide consumers
support in understanding and deciphering what the AI model is doing and any bias that may
be present, like the CMS Care Compare Tool. SAS has developed a SAS Model Card, which
is a вЂњnutrition labelвЂќ for AI models, which take any AI model and provides insights into biases
and drift in an easy-to-understand format.

PR-2. What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective utilization of the
most useful and innovative applications for physician workflows, such as quality measurement
reporting, clinical documentation, and billing tasks? How could these obstacles be mitigated?
Obstacles and mitigation recommendations include:
вЂў Data silos and non-standard formats keep apps from pulling or pushing the metrics
physicians need. Mandating FHIR R4 APIs and a national digital-quality-measure library
would cut integration costs and speed development.
вЂў Misaligned incentives and unclear reimbursement make it hard for practices to justify new
workflows. CMS can tie MIPS and APM bonuses to verified digital reporting and reimburse
AI-assisted documentation directly.
вЂў EHR vendor lock-in and high app-store fees deter innovators. Enforcing open marketplaces
and capping transaction fees would widen the pipeline of useful tools.
вЂў Clinicians distrust вЂњblack boxвЂќ AI and resent extra clicks. Requiring model transparency,
rigorous usability testing, and giving credit for ambient, click-reducing documentation can turn
skepticism into uptake.
вЂў Cybersecurity and liability concerns stall procurement. A shared federal security-certification
program and safe-harbor protections for certified tools would lower perceived risk.
вЂў Small practices often lack capital and IT staff. Targeted technical-assistance grants and low-
cost, cloud-hosted managed services can deliver turn-key deployment without new overhead.
PR-3. How important is it for healthcare delivery and interoperability in urban and rural areas that
all data in an EHR system be accessible for exchange, regardless of storage format (for example,
scanned documents, faxed records, lab results, free text notes, structured data fields)? Please
address all of the following:
a. Current challenges in accessing different data formats.
Lack of integration and continued data siloing will hamper any technical progress made in app
development and the like.
b. Impact on patient care quality.
When providers have access to the full patient record, including scanned documents, faxed
notes, free-text entries, and structured labs, they can make faster decisions, avoid redundant
tests, and reduce medication errors. If any part of the record remains siloed due to format,
clinicians are forced to operate with incomplete information, which directly compromises quality of
care and outcomes.

c. Technical barriers to full data accessibility.
This has already been addressed in several sections but in summary, many EHRs store data in
fragmented or proprietary formats, making it difficult to extract and exchange complete records.
Scanned documents and free-text notes often lack metadata, which limits searchability and
automated processing. Older systems may not support modern APIs like FHIR, and even those
that do often apply inconsistent standards, slowing integration and raising costs.
d. Cost or privacy implications of making all data formats interoperable.
Converting legacy formats and unstructured data into interoperable, machine-readable content
can be expensive, especially for smaller systems without existing infrastructure. Privacy risks also
increase when broad access is granted to sensitive documents that were never designed for
digital exchange. Ensuring proper tagging, audit trails, and access controls adds complexity but is
necessary to prevent breaches and maintain trust.
e. Priority level compared to other interoperability needs.
App-level interoperability is a growing priority but is still secondary to core system-to-system
exchange. Until foundational interoperability across EHRs, labs, pharmacies, and payers is
consistent and reliable, the value of app integration will be limited. However, as the infrastructure
matures, app-level access becomes essential for patient-facing innovation and real-time decision

D. Payers
PA-5. What are ways payers can help with simplifying clinical quality data responsibilities of
providers?
a. How interested are payers and providers in EHR technology advances that enable bulk
extraction of clinical quality data from EHRs to payers to allow them to do the calculations instead
of the provider-side technology?
There is a mutual interest and benefit for both payers and providers to share clinical data and
ensure that the data quality is of the highest standard possible. The underlying issue is the
financial burden to implement and maintain a technology platform that does not unduly cause a
hardship for smaller providers with the requirement to purchase the necessary EHR technology to
collect quality clinical data and share with payers via bulk extract of the clinical data via
standardized EHR interoperability standards.
Payers should offer providers EHR client applications as the interface into the payerвЂ™s claims
processing system.
Claims processing infrastructure standardization is necessary to implement mechanisms to
enable bulk extraction of clinical quality data regardless of provider type, size, location, or payer
enrollment. Standardization creates opportunities for interoperability to take root and grow. To
extract quality clinical data, which is a form of data exchange, the data must adhere to the same
standards. Similarly, HIEs allow the electronic exchange of healthcare information among
different healthcare organizations. A Healthcare Payer Exchange (HPE) would enable payers and
providers to share quality clinical data within the HIE.
b. In what ways can the interoperability and quality reporting responsibilities of providers to both
CMS and other payers be consolidated so investments can be dually purposed? Are there
technologies payers might leverage that would support access to real time quality data for
healthcare providers to inform clinical care in addition to simplifying reporting processes?
The interoperability and quality reposting responsibilities of providers to payers and CMS could
be consolidated by adopting standardized tools to automate the collection of clinical quality data
and generation of standardized reporting in real time. Currently available technologies include
text analytics and natural language processing (NLP), large language models (LLMs), generative
AI, AI-driven document analysis, and agentic AI.

3. Technical Standards and Certification
TD-4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs
over proprietary APIs?
CMS must continue to lead in the development of, and use of open, standards-based, publicly
available APIs. Building on the Blue Button 2.0 work for consumers, CMS should strive to meet
the requirements set forth in its Interoperability and Patient Access final rule (CMS-9115-F) for
Medicare FFS. Through this leadership, CMS will be able to encourage and at times require the
use of open, standards-based APIs over proprietary APIs. This will also provide clear guidance
on when FHIR transactions conflict with HIPAA requirements, giving the health care community
clear guidance and a path forward. SAS believes that open, appropriate, standards-based
exchange of information is imperative to a better functioning health care system.

TD-15. Regarding bulk FHIR APIs:
a. How would increased use of bulk FHIR improve use cases and data flow?
Increased use of bulk FHIR supports scalable, efficient population-level data exchange and
enhances analytics, care coordination, and reporting. The use of bulk FHIR toolkits would enable
more free-flowing data through appropriate means for additional research and innovation. The
current restriction on exporting data from current data systems lacks a standard which bulk FHIR
could replace and provide new means for data sharing beyond the current transactional systems
FHIR has provided. A bulk FHIR could support this and reduce the friction of population level data
exchanges.
b. What are the potential disadvantages of their use?
While bulk FHIR use will require additional infrastructure, security and patient consent
management and other implementation, the value gained by providing a clear and standardized
way to share population-level data through a known standard would be well achieved. The risks
from not implementing a bulk FHIR standard include additional reductions in innovation and
quality improvement.
