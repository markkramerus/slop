Re: Comments of the California Medical Association (CMA) in response to Request for

To Whom it May Concern:
On behalf of the more than 50,000 physician and medical student members of the California
Medical Association (CMA), I am writing to offer our comments in response to the Request for
Information; Health Technology Ecosystem, CMS-0042-NC.
CMA has been deeply involved in the development of the health IT ecosystem in California,
having founded the largest Regional Extension Center (REC) in the country, secured multiple
rounds of state and federal funding to support provider adoption and use of data exchange,
and constructing the California Health and Human Services (CalHHS) Data Exchange
Framework (DxF). CMA strongly supports physicians and patients having real-time access to
all of the data needed to make smart treatment decisions.
Physicians, however, also see firsthand the challenges that providers and patients continue
to face in accessing data that should be available. While great strides have been made in
promoting interoperability, the work is far from finished.
With that in mind, we offer the following comments:
Providers
1. Digital Health Apps
PR-1a. a. What are the current obstacles?
The most commonly cited obstacle physicians face in using digital health apps is the lack of
EHR integration. Despite the promise of the 21st Century Cures Act, many apps cannot
download data directly into CEHRT. Instead of facilitating the flow of data, many digital
health apps create additional data silos.
In addition to EHR integration, physicians also face concerns about the privacy of patient
data and liability should that data be breached. The universe of digital health apps has

expanded very rapidly, and in a largely unregulated space. It is not always clear to physicians
or their patients who has developed a given app, how data is stored, and how data might be
used. This uncertainty leads many risk-averse physicians to avoid digital health apps all
together.
The uncertainty is compounded when the digital health apps utilize artificial intelligence (AI),
as there is currently no federal regulatory framework governing the use of AI in health care.
Developers, physicians, and patients are all uncertain about the “rules of the road” for using
AI tools, and this is impeding the growth of the technology.
PR-2. What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective utilization
of the most useful and innovative applications for physician workflows, such as quality
measurement reporting, clinical documentation, and billing tasks? How could these
obstacles be mitigated?
The biggest obstacle to using innovative digital health apps for quality improvement and
documentation, and billing is inconsistent rules and requirements for these tasks across
payers and programs. It is difficult for digital health apps to extract and compile data for
reporting purposes when every program requires a different set of data elements. To
mitigate this challenge, CMS should decide upon a core set of quality measures that would
be mandated across federal programs and strongly encouraged for state and private payers.
2. Data Exchange
PR-7. What strategies can CMS implement to support providers in making high-quality,
timely, and comprehensive healthcare data available for interoperability in the digital
product ecosystem? How can the burden of increasing data availability and sharing be
mitigated for providers? Are there ways that workflows or metrics that providers are
already motivated to optimize for that could be reused for, or combined with, efforts
needed to support interoperability?
1) CMS should enforce requirements for payers to have open access APIs, as outlined in
the 21st Century Cures Act Final Rule. After that final rule was published, CMA took the
baton from CMS and sponsored California Senate Bill (SB) 1419 (Becker, 2022), which
mirrored the open API requirements in state law. This applied the requirements to
state-regulated plans. Unfortunately, implementation of SB 1419 has now been
delayed to January 2027, due to the delays on the federal side.
2) CMS and ASTP should clarify for the vendor community that excessive fees for
interfaces and EHR integration constitute information blocking and will be regulated
as such. Excessive fees – which often run into the tens of thousands of dollars –
prevent many small and safety net practices from being able to engage in data
exchange.

PR-14. How can CMS encourage providers to submit information blocking complaints to
ASTP/ONC's Information Blocking Portal? What would be the impact? Would it advance
or negatively impact data exchange?
CMS should strengthen deterrents against information blocking by vendors, payers, and
institutions, especially through auditing and transparent reporting. Physicians often face
barriers when requesting external patient records, resulting in care delays. But they are
reluctant to report these delays because enforcement to date has been almost nonexistent.
Value-Based Care
1. Digital Health Adoption
VB-1. Incentives & Obstacles for Digital Health in APMs:
To encourage broader use of digital health tools in APMs and ACOs, financial incentives tied
directly to digital engagement metrics (e.g., remote monitoring utilization, patient portal
engagement rates) would be effective. Other incentives could include shared savings
bonuses for meeting digital equity and access benchmarks.
Obstacles include:
• Fragmented IT infrastructure across practices and facilities
• Lack of interoperability with legacy EHRs
• Digital literacy and access disparities among patient populations
• Upfront cost of adoption with delayed or unclear ROI
VB-2. Integration of Key Technologies in APMs:
AI, risk stratification, and population health tools should be required capabilities within APM
infrastructures. For example, risk-adjusted care management tools could become a core
reporting requirement. Quality measurement and patient engagement tools should be
embedded in daily clinical workflows rather than layered on as administrative burdens.
Integration into APMs must focus on usability, seamless workflows, and automation of
redundant tasks (e.g., auto-populating care gap alerts).
VB-3. Essential Health IT Capabilities for Value-Based Care:
Critical capabilities include:
• Real-time care planning and shared decision-making platforms
• Event notification systems (ED visits, hospital discharges)
• Data normalization across disparate EHRs
• Automated quality metric dashboards

• Robust attribution matching and longitudinal patient ID resolution
• Interoperability with remote devices and home-based care tools
Additional valuable tools: patient engagement apps with bidirectional messaging,
social determinants of health (SDoH) screening tools, and analytics to predict
readmissions or care gaps.
VB-4. Essential Data Types:
• Clinical encounter data (structured and unstructured)
• Pharmacy claims and medication adherence data
• SDoH data elements
• Real-time hospitalization/discharge/ED utilization events
• Lab and imaging results
• Functional status and patient-reported outcomes (PROMs)
• Behavioral and mental health data
• Risk adjustment and HCC coding data
2. Compliance and Certification
VB-5. Support of Certification Criteria for Value-Based Care:
While CEHRT requirements support data capture and exchange, current standards fall short
on real-time interoperability, usability, and workflow integration. Certified systems often
check compliance boxes without improving clinician efficiency or patient outcomes.
VB-6. Missing Capabilities in Certification:
• Integration of SDoH and behavioral health data
• Native support for risk stratification and predictive modeling
• Patient engagement tools beyond portal access
• Remote monitoring data integration
• Tools to coordinate multi-disciplinary teams across care settings

VB-7. Reducing Complexity While Preserving Flexibility:
Certification could shift from a checklist model to a performance-based certification—
rewarding IT systems that demonstrably reduce provider burden and improve outcomes.
Modular certification could also allow APMs to select technology stacks tailored to their
populations without being penalized.
VB-8. Supplementing CEHRT with Other Policies:
Many ACOs lack access to timely ADT data due to payer or hospital system silos. Policies
could encourage universal data sharing agreements with community-based providers and
prioritize real-time APIs for data exchange.
VB-9. Technology Requirements for APM vs. Non-APM Organizations:
APMs need enhanced analytics, patient stratification tools, and real-time outcomes
tracking—capabilities that non-APM models may not require. Interoperability requirements
should go further in APMs to include integration with social services, community care
networks, and non-traditional health partners.
VB-10. CEHRT Flexibility for Advanced APMs:
CMS should recognize domain-specific needs: e.g., behavioral health ACOs need tools for
mental health screening, while rural APMs may need offline capabilities and low-bandwidth
data syncing. CEHRT flexibility must preserve core capabilities (data exchange, quality
tracking) but allow for modular, cost-conscious adoption in under-resourced settings.
Thank you in advance for your consideration of our comments.
