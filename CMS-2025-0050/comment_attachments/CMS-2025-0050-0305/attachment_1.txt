For most patients and caregivers, the single most useful —and already widely available —set of clinical data is the Current Procedural Terminology (CPT®) -coded record of procedures, services that appears in every EHR portal, Explanation- of-Benefits statement, and claims summary. Because CPT is the nation’s uniform “common language” for medical services— maintained through an open, evidence - based process led by the AMA —it delivers four things pa tients say they need: (1) a concise, plain - language descriptor expl aining what was done ; (2) a reliable bridge to price -estimation tools, since payers base cost -sharing on CPT codes; and (3) drives the identification of patient to receive targeted clinical interventions through innovative, value -based care arrangements. Importantly, patients never pay to view their CPT -coded information; the descriptors travel with the clinical record at no charge, and their universal use across physicians, hospitals, and payers already underpins care coordination and longitudinal persona l-health records. Opportunities and Challenges . To unlock even more value for research and “actionable evidence,” CMS should require every certified API to expose FHIR Procedure resources populated with CPT codes, support services that advance CPT harmonization with systematized nomenclature of medicine clinical terms (SNOMED CT), international classification of diseases (ICD -10), and Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System (HCPCS). These steps would let clinica l, claims, and device data line up automatically across settings, powering cohort discovery and AI analytics without reinventing new vocabularies. The chief challenges are operational: EHR developers must upgrade interfaces, small and medium sized practices need low-cost implementation support, and privacy safeguards must ensure that richer data flows still respect state and federal protections. Digital- identity credentials could one day spare patients the password chaos of multiple portals and make it easier to share records securely but rolling them out before the underlying risks are fixed will backfire. Centralized biometric hubs —like those some commercial vendors now propose —create a single treasure- trove of immutable data that can be repurposed for surveillance, marketing, or AI trai ning, and once breached, can never be “re- issued.” Patients would lose meaningful choice as these systems becom e embedded in pharmacies, hospitals, and insurers’ apps, yet still shoulder the consequences of any data compromise or billing error triggered by an algorithm that misidentifies them. The public’s trust is already shaken by reports of high- resolution facia l images being used for commercial profiling and by large -scale breaches of genetic and biometric databases; pushing adoption too aggressively will deepen that skepticism and slow, not accelerate, digital -health uptake. CMS should therefore condition any future incentives on five concrete safeguards: (1) require decentralized, wallet -based credentials stored locally in secure personal apps, with biometric data retained on the user’s device rather than in a national databa se (2) an explicit ban on secondary data use or sale; (3) require mandatory breach notification along with a vendor -funded remediation fund, so patients are not left to deal with the consequences on their own; (4) annual, independent security audits with public “report cards” on privacy, uptime, and complaint volume; and (5) a clear opt -out pathway that guarantees equal access to care for those who choose passwords over biometrics. In this model, health IT and app developers build and certify the technology, payers pilot large -scale deployments, and CMS collaborates with OCR and ASTP/ONC to enforce HIPAA’s minimum- necessary standard for any disclosures made to a person or entity who is not the patient and extend EHR transparency attributes to identity tools. Physicians will adopt digital IDs only after the ecosystem proves it can protect patients, integrate seamlessly into clinical workflows, and remain affordable —costs that belong on the vendors’ balance -sheet, not on small and medium sized practices already stretched thin.

PR-8. What are ways CMS or partners can help with simplifying clinical quality data responsibilities of providers? An ongoing problem for physician quality reporting, whether MIPS Value Pathway (MVP) or traditional MIPS, as well as Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP), is the high data completeness threshold CMS requires for successful reporting on a quality measure. The threshold is particularly problematic for physicians who practice at multiple sites of services and report on an eCQM or registry measure (and dQM in the future). There is a lack of understanding about the maturity of health IT standards to seamlessly aggregate data from EHRs or registries. Challenges include lack of agreed upon semantic and syntactic standards, data privacy concerns, and patient misidentification. Many physician practices also lack knowledge on how to access providers’ “digital endpoi nts” to collect the data needed for aggregation. The high data completeness requirement runs counter to CMS’ goal of reducing administrative burden within the MIPS program and CMS has not yet adequately addressed our concerns. Since 2020, CMS has required physicians to successfully report on a quality measure for 70 percent of all eligible patients (otherwise known as the data co mpleteness requirement within the MIPS program). Starting in 2024, CMS increased the data completeness requirement to 75 percent of all eligible patients, and we continue to question the feasibility and necessity for such a high threshold. The challenges w ill be further exacerbated for participants in the MSSP program since ALL MIPS quality policies now appl y to the MSSP quality requirements. In your experience, how do current certification criteria and standards incorporated into the ONC Health IT Certification Program support value -based care delivery? The ASTP/ONC Health IT Certification Program provides a foundational framework that supports value - based care delivery by promoting interoperability, data exchange, and patient access. CEHRT requirements ensure that essential functionalities —such as suppor t for electronic transitions of care, closing referral loops, and making the process for patients to get their personal health information less time-consuming —are available across care settings. These capabilities facilitate care coordination, quality meas urement, and patient engagement, which are central to value -based models. However, the AMA notes that certification criteria often lag behind the evolving needs of APMs, particularly in areas such as real -time data exchange, SDOH, and advanced analytics. The program’s emphasis on structured data and standardized APIs, including the HL7 FHIR specification, is a positive step toward enabling more seamless data sharing. Yet, implementation variability and usability challenges continue to burden physicians and limit the full potential of certified tools, especially in small and mediu m-sized practices that lack health IT expertise and extensive technical infrastructure. Certification should more explicitly support criteria that prioritizes physician -centered design principles and reduces documentation burden. Greater alignment between the ASTP/ONC Certification Program and CMS value -based care requirements would enhance t he coherence of both programs and reduce complexity for physicians and other providers. We also emphasize that ongoing stakeholder engagement is essential to ensure that certification evolves in tandem with clinical and operational realities, especially th ose challenges facing small and medium -sized physician practices. What specific interoperability challenges have you encountered in implementing value -based care programs? Interoperability remains a significant barrier to the effective implementation of value -based care programs, particularly due to the lack of standardized data formats and inconsistent implementation of FHIR APIs across vendors and health systems. We recommend the use of a minimum standardized data set and data elements to facilitate more meaningful and useful sharing of clinical and financial data. VB-13. What improvements to existing criteria and standards would better support value -based care capabilities while reducing provider burden? To better support value -based care while reducing provider burden, existing certification criteria and standards must be refined to emphasize usability and alignment with clinical workflows. Systems and technology must serve clinicians, not the other way around. As we previously discussed, the AMA recommends expanding the use of standardized APIs and FHIR - based tools to facilitate seamless interoperability and reduce custom integration costs. However, without an EHR certification requirement, many physicians—espec ially those in small and rural practices —will not be able to access new APIs or take advantage of their promising capabilities. Establishing a process to evaluate, test, and certify new API technology will create standard APIs, promote end -to-end interoper ability between EHRs and payer systems, and allow for an electronic process that is meaningfully integrated within the EHR workflow while supporting value -based care delivery. In addition to standards and criteria, there needs to be a focus on how systems are architected in order to minimize burden. For value -based care to function effectively and efficiently, digital quality measurement should allow for seamless quality reporting that reduces burden and provides real -time performance data that can be used to improve patient care. There should also be a focus on limiting the number of platforms and systems participating practices must interact with to access patient and performan ce data to minimize burden, ideally by maintaining quality and financial performance data in the same platform or application. We also encourage dynamic dashboards to be built in ACOs to display quality, cost, and utilization data with helpful contextual o r comparative information and actionable data - driven insights for participating practices to easily and quickly monitor performance and identify areas for improvement. VB-14. How could implementing digital identity credentials improve value -based care delivery and outcomes? Implementing digital health credentials could one day improve value -based care delivery for both patients and physicians. These credentials can significantly enhance the delivery and outcomes of value -based care by ensuring accurate, secure, and efficient identification of patients and physicians across ACOs — reducing administrative errors, improving patient matching, and streamlining care coordination. For patients, verified digital identities can improve their access to personal health information, enable consent management, and support engagement in their own care journey through digital tools. However, as previously discussed, there are underlying privacy and security risks in creating centralized biometric data hubs with individuals’ digital health credentials. Public trust is already unsettled by recent reports of data misuse and breaches, and pushing adoption of credentials too aggressively will deepen that skepticism and slow, not accelerate, the uptake of digital health. For physicians, these credentials can help facilitate secure access to IT systems and enhance accountability and trust in documentation as well as patient -centered data exchange. Integration with existing health IT infrastructure, including EHRs, and patie nt portals, would be essential for seamless adoption. Implementation in value -based care should proceed cautiously as integrating third -party identity

There may be some economies of scale in implementing digital credentials across an ACO, but small and rural practices remain under stra in as declining physician reimbursement rates impact physicians’ ability to invest in their practices. If CMS moves forward with implementing digital credentials in value -based care delivery, we recommend that adoption be voluntary across ACOs or piloted in a small number of models to demonstrate value and prove effectiveness. Physicians will only adopt digital credentials in value -based care when the technology proves it can protect patients, integrate seamlessly into clinical workflows, and remain affordable to physicians.
