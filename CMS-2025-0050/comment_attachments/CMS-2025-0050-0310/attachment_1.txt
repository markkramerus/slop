E. Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks, Answers to Questions
TD-1: Stimulating Developer Interest
In the short term (next two years), CMS can stimulate developer interest by expanding pilot
programs, offering innovation grants, and increasing the availability of test data through
sandboxes. Improving the developer experience and aligning application programming interfaces
(APIs) with real-world use cases will foster greater engagement.
In the longer term, CMS should consider formalizing public-private innovation accelerators,
providing fast-track certifications for applications that demonstrate patient engagement value,
and aligning reimbursement incentives with health IT adoption.
TD-2: Valuable CMS Data for Developers
(a) Real-time eligibility data, appeals statuses, authorization workflows, and patient-reported
outcomes would be especially valuable additions to CMS APIs.
1

(b) Data sources such as provider directories, pharmacy benefits, and social determinants of
health from Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) databases offer significant value
when paired with data available through the Blue Button 2.0 API.
(c) Major obstacles preventing access to these data sources include data fragmentation, latency in
updates, and inconsistent file and transmission formats.
(d) CMS and its federal partners should consider supporting APIs for scheduling, clinical quality
measures, and real-time price transparency to further encourage innovation and empower
patients.
TD-3: Digital Identity
(a) Implementing digital identity presents several benefits, including enhanced security and
reduced fraud. However, it also introduces challenges such as barriers to access for populations
with limited digital literacy or technology access.
(b) Requiring digital credentials from platforms like CLEAR, Login.gov, or ID.me would
improve cybersecurity and support safer, more accountable data exchange across systems.
However, successful implementation depends on providing adequate onboarding support for
beneficiaries.
(c) Making OpenID Connect a mandatory identity protocol would standardize access and
authentication across platforms, allowing for more seamless API integration and improved user
experience.
TD-4: Open Standards
CMS can promote the use of open, standards-based APIs by tying program participation and
funding eligibility to the adoption of open technologies. Over time, it should consider phasing
out reimbursement policies that allow the use of proprietary systems that restrict interoperability.
TD-5: Nationwide Provider Directory
A centralized directory of Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) endpoints would
simplify care coordination, support provider referrals, and enable third-party application
development. This directory should be maintained by CMS or ONC, and access should be free
for patients, providers, and developers to maximize public benefit.
TD-6: TEFCA Functions
The Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) enables cross-network
trust and facilitates national-scale, patient-directed data exchange.
(a) Existing alternatives to TEFCA include Carequality, CommonWell, and various private
health information networks.
2

(b) Some redundant or overlapping standards include identity management protocols and certain
e-prescribing formats, which could be streamlined to improve efficiency.
TD-7: USCDI Evaluation
(a) The United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) has improved data standardization,
though its coverage remains incomplete and is still expanding.
(b) Limitations are more often a result of uneven implementation across platforms than
deficiencies in the USCDI itself.
(c) Adding more data elements would improve value but could create scope and complexity
challenges. A phased rollout strategy and collaborative stakeholder governance could address
this.
(d) Given recent advances in language models, semi-structured data formats could offer broader
coverage while relying on natural language processing to fill gaps, even if they are less
structured than USCDI.
TD-8: Effective Certification Criteria
The most effective certification criteria emphasize full patient record access through APIs,
support for real-time data exchange, and compliance with SMART on FHIR and similar
interoperability frameworks.
TD-9: API-Centric Certification
(a) Redefining certification to prioritize API-enabled capabilities would enhance flexibility and
promote broader interoperability across platforms.
(b) A potential drawback is the risk of non-uniform functionality across vendors if API standards
are not uniformly enforced.
(c) Certification should be updated to include real-world testing across diverse patient record
types, including scanned or faxed content.
(d) CMS can further incentivize data quality by supporting data validation audits and recognizing
high-performing data providers.
(e) The ability to perform bulk data transfer via FHIR would significantly reduce provider
reporting burdens but would require efficient computing infrastructure and clearly defined
metadata protocols to maximize effectiveness.
3

TD-10: 21st Century Cures Act API Implementation
To meet the intent of the 21st Century Cures Act, ONC should implement testable benchmarks
and enforce rules requiring access to all permissible patient data. These APIs must not be subject
to undue licensing restrictions or technical barriers.
TD-11: EHI Export
(a) CMS should revise the export capability to include standardized API formats to ensure
consistency across developers.
(b) The export process should be embedded in clinician and administrative workflows and
visible in health IT dashboards.
(c) CMS should consider policy levers—such as program reporting incentives or public
scoring—to promote widespread use of this capability.
TD-12: Endorsing Non-CMS Networks
CMS should endorse external data networks that meet defined criteria, including performance
reliability, adherence to security standards, patient experience ratings, and certification for
interoperability.
TD-13: Full EHI Access
(a) Primary obstacles to enabling access to full electronic health information (EHI) include
inconsistent data capture across systems and organizational resistance to sharing complete
records.
(b) While the USCDI standard is easier to implement, full EHI provides more comprehensive
insight. A hybrid strategy using enhanced tools and governance frameworks could close this gap.
TD-14: FHIR Network Connections
Our network is connected to hundreds of endpoints using FHIR (g)(10), Carequality, and
Integrating the Healthcare Enterprise Cross-Community Access (IHE-XCA). These endpoints
span both national and regional networks and are searchable using National Provider Identifiers
and organization IDs.
TD-15: Bulk FHIR Use
(a) Broader use of bulk FHIR APIs would accelerate use cases in public health, research, and
population analytics by streamlining data access across cohorts.
(b) Challenges include the need for robust infrastructure and sound data governance frameworks
to ensure appropriate use and avoid misuse or overload.
4

TD-16: Point-to-Point vs Shared Infrastructure
(a) Current regulations do not strongly favor scalable shared networks. As a result, point-to-point
connections persist, despite their limitations.
(b) Policy alignment is needed to encourage network unification through common APIs,
reciprocal access rules, and shared authentication protocols.
TD-17: Ensuring Network Viability
CMS and ONC should help ensure a viable healthcare data-sharing ecosystem by funding high-
impact use cases and supporting participation in resource-limited settings. These actions can help
generate sufficient supply and demand to drive meaningful data exchange.
6. Compliance
TD-18: Information Blocking
(a) Examples of potential information blocking include delayed responses to API queries and the
imposition of excessive fees for custom interface development. These practices inhibit seamless
data sharing and limit application functionality.
(b) CMS and ONC should consider implementing stronger financial penalties and increasing
transparency through public dashboards that track compliance.
(c) Some healthcare actor categories—such as small post-acute care providers and certain device
manufacturers—may currently lack sufficient enforcement or disincentives.
TD-19: Price Transparency
(a) Current limitations include inconsistent file formats, lack of machine readability, and
insufficient update frequency.
(b) Pre-visit workflows such as cost estimation and benefit design comparisons would benefit the
most from functional price transparency.
(c) For patients, providers, and CMS alike, improvements such as real-time pricing feeds,
searchable benefit structures, and benchmarking tools would increase value and usability.
(d) CMS could further motivate development by launching competitions, providing technical
assistance, and integrating price transparency metrics into reimbursement programs.
