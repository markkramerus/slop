TD-5. How could a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints improve
access to health information for patients, providers, and payers? Who should
publish such a directory, and should users bear a cost?
Right now, the lack of a nationwide entity (providers, payers, vendors, etc.) directory causes
distinct, complex processes and long onboarding times to access each individual provider
system; makes accessing existing, federally-mandated healthcare data inefficient and
inconsistent; and is a barrier to innovation due to lack of standardization. A nationwide, publicly
accessible directory of FHIR endpoints would significantly enhance interoperability by
simplifying access to health records, improving care continuity, and reducing redundant
procedures by having all the information standardized in one easy-to-access format. The
directory would also drastically improve interoperability in conjunction with the industry-wide
implementation of the FAST Scalable Security for Registration, Authentication, and
Authorization (SSRAA)–coming in 2026–to enable dynamic registration and access to data
exchange APIs, reducing the need to spend time and money on bespoke integrations with every
entity.
We believe CMS and ASTP/ONC should oversee the implementation and publication of a
nationwide, publicly accessible directory of FHIR endpoints and entity organizational metadata,
to advance their mission of fostering healthcare transformation to deliver care that can lower
costs and improve care. By moving away from legacy, high-cost data exchanges will improve
CMS’ bottom line spending, reducing monthly expenditures for Medicare, Medicaid or CHIP
beneficiaries. CMS already has access to a qualified pool of vendors capable of completing this
project through the Agile Collaboration and Modernization Endeavors (ACME) Blanket
Purchase Agreement (BPA).
We believe that through a modest investment by CMS, with nominal on-going maintenance
burden, CMS has the opportunity to enable broad and impactful innovation in the private sector
by unlocking the value that has already been created, in the private sector by prior legislation, but
remains largely unrealized due to the undiscoverable nature of existing data and resources.
TD-6. What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform?
By designating Qualified Health Information Networks (QHINs), TEFCA creates a “network of
networks,” facilitating seamless data exchange among participants. This structure simplifies
connectivity, reduces the need for multiple integrations, and promotes trust among entities by
ensuring adherence to common standards and policies. By providing a unified approach to data
exchange, TEFCA enhances the ability of patients, providers, and payers to access and share
health information efficiently, improving care coordination, reducing administrative burdens, and
supporting health initiatives.
TEFCA offers unique connectivity, such as its Record Locator Services which enables patients,
their delegates, and healthcare providers to discover then acquire the patients healthcare records
across diverse and disparate connected systems. We believe that CMS should leverage TEFCA

TD-11. As of January 1, 2024, many health IT developers with products certified
through the ONC Health IT Certification Program are required to include the
capability to perform an electronic health information export or “EHI export” for a
single patient as well as for patient populations (45 CFR 170.315(b)(10)). Such
health IT developers are also required to publicly describe the format of the EHI
export. Notably, how EHI export was accomplished was left entirely to the health IT
developer. Now that this capability has been in production for over a year, CMS and
ASTP/ONC seek input on the following:
TD-11a. Should this capability be revised to specify standardized API requirements
for EHI export?
Yes, the capabilities should be revised to specify standardized API requirements for EHI export.
Lack of standardization is often a hindrance to the development of and innovation in Health IT;
without a specific FHIR Implementation Guide, like the Argonaut Project's EHI Export API IG,
varied implementations require bespoke, costly integrations. Given the wide variety of use cases
enabled by an effective and universally available EHI export capability, CMS and ASTP/ONC
should definitely refine the legislative requirements to specify a FHIR Implementation Guide.
TD-11c. Should CMS consider policy changes to support this capability’s use?
Yes, CMS should implement policy changes to support standardized API requirements for EHI
export. We believe that policy that focuses on providing guidance and technical requirements
increases the ease of implementation within the Health IT community. As with previous
legislation around interoperability, standards such as the United States Core Data for
Interoperability (USCDI) and the recommended FHIR Implementation Guides, ensured that
organizations could conform to the final rule(s) and build dependable testing for that
conformance based on the IGs.

TD-15. Regarding bulk FHIR APIs:
TD-15a. How would increased use of bulk FHIR improve use cases and data flow?
Like the national directory, bulk FHIR APIs are a critical piece of interoperable functionality.
Implementing Bulk FHIR APIs can significantly enhance data exchange by enabling efficient,
large-scale data transfers. Bulk FHIR APIs allow for comprehensive analytics, leading to
improved patient outcomes and streamlined healthcare operations. The asynchronous nature of
bulk FHIR APIs reduces system load and latency, making it feasible to process extensive data
requests without compromising performance. The asynchronous transfer of large data sets is an
ideal solution for the transfer of patient data to quality organizations such as CMS, NCQA and
others. The value of Bulk Data in this use case has been demonstrated by the NCQA Bulk FHIR
Quality Coalition. This capability supports various bulk use cases, including population health
management, clinical research, digital quality measurements, value-based care, and public health
reporting.
TD-15b. What are the potential disadvantages of their use?
There are some potential disadvantages of bulk FHIR, such as:
● Infrastructure and Scalability Concerns: Implementing bulk FHIR APIs requires
intensive infrastructure requirements capable of handling large volumes of data
efficiently. Healthcare organizations may lack the necessary resources or technical
expertise to support such infrastructure, leading to potential performance issues and
system bottlenecks.
● Data Privacy and Security: Transferring large datasets increases the risk of data
breaches and unauthorized access. Ensuring data privacy and security during bulk
transfers is paramount, necessitating stringent security protocols and compliance with
regulations).
● Operational Challenges: The asynchronous nature of bulk FHIR operations can result in
inefficient data synchronization and consistency across systems. Additionally, varying
levels of FHIR implementation among EHR vendors can lead to interoperability issues,
hindering seamless data exchange.
● Regulatory and Compliance Issues: Lack of standardized guidelines for bulk FHIR API
usage can result in inconsistent implementations and compliance challenges. Establishing
clear regulatory frameworks and performance benchmarks is essential to promote
uniform adoption and operational efficiency.
While there are some disadvantages, we believe that they are easily overcome by CMS and ONC
establishing a regulatory framework for performance metrics.
