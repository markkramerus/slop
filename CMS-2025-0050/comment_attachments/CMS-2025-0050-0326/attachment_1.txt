Full Comment
To support CMS's goal of a learning health system, Blue Button 2.0 should evolve beyond claims data to include clinical
observations, social risk factors, and patient-generated health data. Structured inclusion of these elements--paired with
provenance metadata (e.g., FHIR Provenance, AuditEvent)--would enhance trust and downstream reusability. In our federal data
modernization work, we've found such metadata critical for auditability, especially when empowering third-party apps to
surface actionable insights to Medicare beneficiaries.
CMS/ONC currently defines “digital tools” and “digital health products” separately—an important distinction that should be
maintained and clarified throughout the initiative. “Digital tools” may include any web, mobile, or sensor-based
applications, while “digital health products” refer specifically to tools that directly support individual health needs. We
suggest adopting common industry-aligned categories such as Wearables (e.g., Fitbit), Software as a Medical Device (SaMD;
e.g., medication tracking apps), and Software in a Medical Device (SiMD; e.g., CPAP firmware). These distinctions align with
FDA frameworks and help anticipate unique challenges in safety, integration, and evaluation.
CMS/ONC should consider referencing specific categories of digital health products—particularly Wearables (e.g., Fitbit,
Apple Watch), Software as a Medical Device (SaMD; e.g., medication adherence apps), and Software in a Medical Device (SiMD;
e.g., smart CPAP machines). These product types pose unique interoperability, data governance, and patient safety
challenges. Based on our work with FDA and CMS, clearly naming and planning for these categories early ensures better
alignment with regulatory guidance, facilitates integration into clinical workflows, and supports data quality for real-

To realize the 21st Century Cures Act vision of patient-directed access 'without special effort,' certification must go
beyond API availability to include conformance, completeness, and usability. Test suites should mimic real-world complexity--
multi-payer, overlapping consents, and unstructured data. Based on our CMS and FDA implementations, we recommend CMS enforce
API payload integrity and performance benchmarks to ensure these APIs are usable at scale.

be accompanied by clear lineage and provenance standards to maintain trust and reproducibility.
