Full Comment To support CMS's goal of a learning health system, Blue Button 2.0 should evolve beyond claims data to include clinical observations, social risk factors, and patient-generated health data. Structured inclusion of these elements--paired with provenance metadata (e.g., FHIR Provenance, AuditEvent)--would enhance trust and downstream reusability. In our federal data modernization work, we've found such metadata critical for auditability, especially when empowering third-party apps to surface actionable insights to Medicare beneficiaries. CMS/ONC currently defines “digital tools” and “digital health products” separately—an important distinction that should be maintained and clarified throughout the initiative. “Digital tools” may include any web, mobile, or sensor-based applications, while “digital health products” refer specifically to tools that directly support individual health needs. We suggest adopting common industry-aligned categories such as Wearables (e.g., Fitbit), Software as a Medical Device (SaMD; e.g., medication tracking apps), and Software in a Medical Device (SiMD; e.g., CPAP firmware). These distinctions align with FDA frameworks and help anticipate unique challenges in safety, integration, and evaluation. CMS/ONC should consider referencing specific categories of digital health products—particularly Wearables (e.g., Fitbit, Apple Watch), Software as a Medical Device (SaMD; e.g., medication adherence apps), and Software in a Medical Device (SiMD; e.g., smart CPAP machines). These product types pose unique interoperability, data governance, and patient safety challenges. Based on our work with FDA and CMS, clearly naming and planning for these categories early ensures better alignment with regulatory guidance, facilitates integration into clinical workflows, and supports data quality for real- world evidence generation. To reduce variation and burden across providers, CMS should support intermediary services for API normalization, terminology mapping, and identity reconciliation. These services--delivered through shared, standards-based infrastructure--can simplify provider compliance while advancing CMS's interoperability goals. Our experience integrating diverse EHR feeds into reusable, enterprise-grade data pipelines demonstrates the value of centralizing these capabilities. To attract sustainable innovation from the developer ecosystem, CMS should provide access to realistic, synthetic reference datasets--aligned with USCDI and FHIR--that reflect common Medicare journeys. In parallel, a unified developer onboarding portal with harmonized APIs (e.g., Blue Button, Data at Point of Care) would reduce fragmentation and accelerate adoption. As experienced integrators, we've seen how standardized entry points and metadata transparency significantly lower integration barriers.Value-based care organizations need decision-ready data--curated, timely, and aligned to shared attribution and care gap logic. We recommend CMS certify modular services (e.g., HEDIS engines, risk segmentation tools, patient engagement trackers) that can be reused across APM models. Our past performance delivering these capabilities within CMS platforms shows how modular components reduce burden while expanding digital enablement. To realize the 21st Century Cures Act vision of patient-directed access 'without special effort,' certification must go beyond API availability to include conformance, completeness, and usability. Test suites should mimic real-world complexity-- multi-payer, overlapping consents, and unstructured data. Based on our CMS and FDA implementations, we recommend CMS enforce API payload integrity and performance benchmarks to ensure these APIs are usable at scale. While USCDI provides a strong foundation, our federal data modernization work has shown that key data elements--such as device data, consent history, and enriched unstructured text--are essential for comprehensive digital tools. CMS should consider optional extensions to USCDI, supported by non-proprietary NLP models, to surface actionable insights. These should be accompanied by clear lineage and provenance standards to maintain trust and reproducibility.
Full EHR interoperability requires structured access to all data formats, including scanned documents, free text, and legacy image-based formats. Based on our experience building scalable intake and normalization pipelines, we recommend CMS establish profiles or certification criteria for these data types. Without this, digital health tools will remain blind to critical portions of the patient record--particularly those most relevant to longitudinal care management in Medicare populations.To better align certification with APM needs, ONC should consider certifying modular health IT services (e.g., attribution logic, consent history, care gap calculators) that can plug into diverse infrastructures. We've delivered such services across CMS and know that modularity reduces both onboarding time and compliance risk. This approach also supports CMS's goal of lowering operational barriers for rural and resource-constrained providers.
