Call to Action: Transforming Medicare into a Patient-Empowered Healthcare
Ecosystem
Imagine a Medicare beneficiary who wakes up each morning with complete visibility into
their healthcare journey. Through a unified digital platform, they can access their complete
medical history, view upcoming appointments with clear explanations of what each visit
will accomplish, and see real-time cost comparisons for any recommended procedures
across all providers in their area. When their physician recommends a specialist, they can
instantly review that specialist's patient outcomes, satisfaction scores, and evidence-
based care adherence—then schedule an appointment online within minutes.
This beneficiary receives financial rewards when they choose high-value providers or
adhere to generic medication protocols. Their AI-powered health assistant explains
complex test results in plain language and alerts them to preventive care opportunities.
When they transition between providers, their complete health record flows seamlessly
ahead of them, and a dedicated care navigator ensures nothing falls through the cracks.
Most importantly, they participate as an informed partner in every healthcare decision,
understanding both the clinical rationale and financial implications of their choices.
This is not a distant fantasy—it is an achievable vision that requires immediate,
coordinated action across six critical transformation areas.
We must act now to build transparent information infrastructure. This means
mandating standardized quality reporting across all Medicare providers with public
dashboards updated monthly, creating a unified federal cost transparency portal, and
implementing interoperable care journey APIs that patients can access through a single
interface. The technology exists; we need the regulatory mandate to deploy it universally.
We must align economic incentives with patient empowerment. This requires piloting
Medicare Choice Accounts that reward beneficiaries for choosing high-value care,
expanding Medicare Advantage value-based benefits to include cash rewards for cost-
effective decisions, and creating provider performance bonuses that share Medicare
savings directly with patients who consistently choose quality providers.
We must eliminate information silos that trap patient data. This demands accelerating
TEFCA implementation with mandatory participation for all Medicare providers by 2026,
developing a Medicare Health Passport system for universal record access, and requiring
1

standardized FHIR APIs that allow patients to aggregate their health data through any
application they choose.
We must revolutionize care coordination and communication. This means establishing
care navigation as a standard Medicare benefit, implementing care team communication
standards that include patients in all planning discussions, and creating transition-of-care
protocols with mandatory patient education and follow-up for every discharge and referral.
We must modernize the regulatory framework to serve patients, not bureaucracy. This
requires developing federally mandated plan comparison tools with consistent metrics,
strengthening information blocking penalties with significant financial consequences, and
harmonizing quality reporting between traditional Medicare and Medicare Advantage to
enable meaningful comparisons.
We must build supporting infrastructure that serves all beneficiaries. This means
funding digital health literacy programs at community centers nationwide, developing AI-
powered decision assistants that explain options in plain language, and mandating
accessibility compliance with multilingual support for all Medicare-related digital
platforms.
The path forward is clear, and the foundation exists today. Data standardization, care
navigation benefits, and accelerated interoperability represent the critical first steps that
will enable every other improvement. These actions create the infrastructure for patient-
centered benefit design, AI-powered health assistance, and comprehensive care
coordination.
Medicare serves over 65 million Americans today, with millions more joining each year.
These beneficiaries deserve a healthcare system that empowers rather than confuses,
rewards rather than penalizes smart choices, and coordinates rather than fragments their
care. The vision of truly empowered Medicare patients is within our grasp—but only if we
commit to systematic, comprehensive implementation of these transformative reforms.
The question is not whether we can create this patient-empowered Medicare ecosystem,
but whether we have the political will and institutional commitment to make it reality.
America's seniors have waited long enough for a healthcare system that truly serves their
needs. The time for incremental change has passed—the moment for transformational
action is now.
Comment Overview
These comments address critical barriers to successful value-based care (VBC) program
participation, patient engagement in healthcare decision-making, and the role of health
2

technology in supporting meaningful healthcare transformation across multiple
stakeholder groups. They envision a future where interoperable data systems, artificial
intelligence, and aligned incentives create a truly patient-centered, value-driven healthcare
ecosystem.
Key Policy Position: Rather than having CMS serve as a health information aggregator, we
advocate for strengthening existing interoperability requirements and expanding them to
cover all healthcare ecosystem participants, with a focus on functional implementation
rather than attestation-based compliance.
1. Financial Incentive Structure Reform (VB-1, VB-2, PR-2, PR-7)
Current Challenge: Existing VBC programs lack sufficient financial incentives to drive
meaningful behavioral change among healthcare stakeholders. Programs with minimal
upside potential and limited downside risk fail to create the urgency necessary for
organizational transformation.
Recommendation: CMS should restructure VBC programs to include:
• Increased Financial Stakes: Expand both upside and downside risk corridors to
create meaningful financial consequences that justify the operational investments
required for success,
• Performance-Based Technology Requirements: Link health IT adoption incentives
directly to measurable care coordination and patient engagement outcomes,
• Provider-Level Incentives: Address the fundamental issue that providers often lack
financial incentives for the extra work required by VBC programs (PR-2),
• Penalty and Reward Structure: Implement clear penalties for non-participation or
poor performance while providing substantial rewards for high-performing
organizations (PR-7),
• Mandatory Participation: Transform VBC programs from elective to mandatory
participation models to ensure widespread adoption and meaningful market
transformation,
• Patient Gainsharing: Develop mechanisms to share positive VBC outcomes
directly with patients through reduced cost-sharing, premium reductions, or direct
financial incentives when cost-of-care targets are met,
3

Supporting Evidence: Organizations participating in programs with minimal financial
exposure often treat VBC initiatives as compliance or “hobby” exercises rather than
transformation opportunities. Without substantial financial incentives, the business case
for investing in advanced health IT capabilities, care coordination platforms, and patient
engagement tools remains weak.
2. Universal Provider Participation and Functional Interoperability (PC-2, PR-12, PR-13,
TD-4, TD-5)
PC-2 Response: Do you have easy access to your own and all your loved ones' health
information in one location?
Our Response: No, not all health information is accessible in one location. However, we
don't believe it's appropriate for CMS or other government agencies to serve as health
information aggregators. Instead, most regulatory frameworks already exist that would
allow consumers to view their aggregated health information regardless of provider or EHR
system. The critical issue is implementation gaps and enforcement.
Provider Coverage Gaps
Current Challenge: Significant categories of healthcare providers remain outside
interoperability requirements, creating data silos that prevent comprehensive patient care
coordination.
Critical Gap - Uncovered Provider Types (Examples):
• Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASCs): Not subject to Promoting Interoperability (PI)
requirements despite performing significant procedures,
• Skilled Nursing Facilities (SNFs): Critical for care transitions but excluded from
electronic data exchange mandates,
• Radiology/Pathology Groups: Often operate independently and hold crucial
diagnostic information, but typically excluded from the requirements to “send and
receive” health information electronically,
• Independent Diagnostic Testing Facilities (IDTFs): Provide essential diagnostic
services without data sharing requirements,
• Laboratory Services: Critical results often trapped in separate systems and again
no electronic data sharing requirements,
4

• Specialty Providers: Many specialists not subject to PI (send and receive)
requirements despite central role in care coordination,
Implementation vs. Attestation Problems
Current Challenge: Most healthcare organizations treat interoperability as a compliance
exercise rather than a useful functional capability.
Specific Issues:
• "Checking the Box" Mentality: Organizations focus on meeting attestation
requirements rather than achieving meaningful data exchange,
• EHR Vendor Workflow Deficiencies: Many vendors provide extremely difficult-to-
use workflows that technically meet requirements but prevent practical
implementation (e.g., if it takes 10 minutes per patient to pull down a CCDA and
reconcile it for a patient with an upcoming appointment, the workflow will not be
adopted),
• Lack of Real Utilization: Providers may have technical capability but lack
incentives or workflows to actually exchange information,
Recommendations:
• Universal Participation Mandate: Require ALL healthcare ecosystem participants
who bill federally funded programs to send (and receive) medical records
electronically to/from ordering providers and or HIEs,
• Eliminate Attestation-Based Measures: Remove self-reporting mechanisms for
HIE exchange while maintaining measurable send/receive requirements,
• Performance-Based Penalties: Implement substantial program penalties for not
reaching high send/receive thresholds,
• EHR Vendor Accountability: Require EHR vendors to provide reasonable and
effective workflows that enable practical interoperability,
• Functional Testing Requirements: Mandate actual testing of data exchange
capabilities rather than theoretical compliance,
• Audit Results: For most, if not all, VBP programs that contain patient information
exchange requirements, the current CMS audit process is best described as a
"drive-by" or desk review with no implications for failing. We suggest establishing a
real audit process along with escalating adverse penalties for not passing an audit.

3. Interoperability and Central Data Infrastructure (TD-12, TD-13, PC-9, PC-10)
Current Challenge: Healthcare data remains fragmented across disparate systems,
limiting the ability to deliver coordinated care and meaningful analytics for both providers
and patients.
Central Data Warehouse and API Strategy
Recommendation: CMS should develop a comprehensive interoperability framework that
includes:
• Centralized API Infrastructure: We recommend that the national health data
warehouse serve as the central repository for standardized APIs, enabling seamless
interoperability across the entire healthcare continuum and eliminating the current
fragmented approach to data exchange between providers, payers, and other
stakeholders,
• Universal FHIR API Access: Require all healthcare entities receiving CMS
payments to maintain FHIR-compliant APIs with standardized data elements,
• Real-Time Data Exchange: Move beyond batch processing to real-time or near real-
time data sharing capabilities,
• Cross-Payer Data Integration: Expand APIs to include Medicaid, Medicare
Advantage, and commercial payer data for comprehensive patient views,
Enhanced Data Utility
Making Data More Useful: Transform raw data into actionable intelligence:
• Standardized Care Plan Structure: Require all EHRs to support interoperable care
plan formats that can be shared across providers and care settings,
• Clinical Decision Support Integration: Embed alerts and recommendations
directly into provider workflows based on population health analytics,
• Predictive Risk Analytics: Leverage AI to analyze patient risk factors (family history,
social determinants, clinical trends) to proactively notify care teams of potential
future health events,
• Incidental Findings Management: Implement automated alerts for clinical findings
that may otherwise go unnoticed, including trending abnormal lab values and
incidental diagnostic discoveries (e.g., imaging findings),
Data Accessibility Enhancement
6

End-User Focused Design:
• Provider Dashboard Integration: Ensure all data APIs integrate seamlessly with
existing provider workflow systems,
• Patient-Facing Simplification: Leverage AI to translate complex clinical reports into
patient-understandable format, content, and actionable recommendations,
• Mobile-First Design: Prioritize mobile accessibility for both patient and provider
interfaces,
• Multi-Language Support: Provide data access and interpretation tools in multiple
languages to serve diverse patient populations,
Encounter Notification Services (ENS) Standardization (PC-11)
Critical Gap: Current HIE capabilities vary significantly in their encounter notification
services, creating inconsistent care coordination.
Current ENS Problems:
• Inconsistent Implementation: Some HIEs provide ENS data showing real-time
patient movement through healthcare systems, others don't,
• Incomplete Coverage: ENS data often excludes community providers, missing
critical care transitions,
• No Delivery Standards: Lack of clean delivery mechanisms for ENS data to reach
providers, patients, or family members,
• Missing ONC Requirements: No standardized format or requirements for feeding
or consuming ENS data,
• Care Plan Integration Gap: ENS data not integrated with care plans to show real-
time patient status,
Recommendations:
• Universal ENS Requirements: Mandate all HIEs provide comprehensive encounter
notification services,
• Standardized ENS Format: Develop ONC standards for ENS data format and
delivery mechanisms (similar to CCD standardization),
• Complete Provider Coverage: Require ENS to include ALL community providers,
not just select participants,
7

• Care Plan Integration: Enable automatic integration of ENS data into patient care
plans and provider dashboards,
• Multi-Stakeholder Access: Provide ENS access to providers, patients, and
authorized family members,
4. Enhanced Patient Data Access and Understanding (PC-1a, PC-1b, PC-8, PC-8a, PC-
12)
Patient-Understandable Health Information (PC-8)
PC-8 Response: What health data is readily available and valuable to patients or their
caregivers?
Current Reality: The only easily accessible data is what's in medical records (notes,
reports, etc.) from systems like Epic and Allscripts. However, this information is:
• Fragmented across multiple portals requiring separate logins
• Written in medical jargon that most patients don't understand
• Requires AI translation to become actionable for patients
PC-8a Response: What data is valuable, but hard for patients and caregivers to
access?
Critical Missing Data Categories:
Quality Information Gaps
• Limited MIPS Results: Current quality sources contain limited results for much of
the industry as not all providers are required to report quality measures,
• Provider Experience Data: Number of specific procedures performed annually by
individual providers,
• Specialty-Specific Outcomes: Procedure-specific success rates and complication
data,
Cost Information Deficiencies
• Price Comparison Impossibility: Despite Price Transparency rules, it's nearly
impossible to price-compare providers,
• Episode Cost Visibility: No access to expected total episode costs or provider
comparisons at the episode level (e.g., the total expected cost of a pacemaker
8

including pre-procedure visit, hospital or ASC cost, surgical cost, anesthesia cost,
post-discharge SNF or IP rehab, etc.),
• Patient Financial Impact: Lack of tools showing patient's specific cost
responsibility for different care options,
Patient Experience and Outcomes Data
• Limited Satisfaction Data: Patient satisfaction data typically unavailable unless
provider participates in ACO,
• Irrelevant Survey Results: Generic survey results are often not relevant to specific
providers being considered. Typically, the questions are centered around primary
care providers, not surgeons, radiologists, anesthesiologists, etc,
• Delayed Survey Timing: Survey timing is often so delayed that results do not
accurately capture the patient experience and are likely biased toward those who
had negative experiences.
• Post-Discharge Outcomes: Urgent care, ED, and readmission data difficult to
access,
• Functional Outcomes: Patient-reported outcomes including post-procedure
functional status and recovery times missing,
Care Coordination and Scheduling (PC-1a, PC-1b, PC-12)
PC-12 Response: What are the most valuable operational health data use cases for
patients and caregivers?
Current Problem: Most patients rely on PCP referrals or friend recommendations rather
than empirical data for healthcare choices. The level of data-driven healthcare decision-
making is virtually non-existent.
Essential Patient Support Functions (PC-1b):
• Healthcare "To-do" Identification: Automated systems that identify pending health
actions,
• Best Provider Search: Evidence-based tools for provider selection,
• Simplified Appointment Making: Online scheduling capabilities across all provider
types,
• Care Plan Management: Accessible, actionable care plan tracking and
management tools,
9

Recommendations:
• Patient-Understandable Documentation Standard: Establish new required
standard for "create a document I can easily understand" functionality,
• Comprehensive Provider Performance Data: Publish provider experience,
outcomes, and cost data in patient-accessible formats,
• Universal Online Scheduling: Require online scheduling capabilities for all
providers receiving federal payments,
• AI-Powered Translation: Mandate AI-assisted translation of medical information
into patient-understandable language,
5. Enhanced Provider Accountability and Clinical Workflows (PR-12, PR-13, PR-14, PR-
8, PA-5a)
Current Challenge: Many healthcare providers, particularly those not currently subject to
Promoting Interoperability requirements, lack mandates for data exchange participation.
Additionally, current quality reporting creates significant administrative burden without
meaningful clinical integration.
Clinical Quality Data Simplification (PR-8, PA-5a)
PR-8 Response: What are ways CMS or partners can help with simplifying clinical
quality data responsibilities of providers?
Current Problem: Clinical quality data reporting represents a significant administrative
burden that diverts resources from patient care without providing meaningful clinical
decision support.
PA-5a Response: How interested are payers and providers in EHR technology
advances for clinical quality data?
Industry Need: This burden requires immediate attention and innovative solutions to
streamline reporting while maintaining quality measurement integrity.
Universal Data Exchange Requirements
Recommendation: Expand interoperability requirements to include:
• All CMS Payment Recipients: Require any provider receiving CMS payments
(including those exempt from current PI requirements) to participate in data
exchange networks,
10

• Specialty Provider Integration: Mandate FHIR API capabilities for specialists,
ancillary service providers, and post-acute care facilities,
• Information Blocking Enforcement: Strengthen penalties for providers who fail to
participate in legitimate data exchange requests,
Clinically Meaningful Measurements
Addressing Workflow and Accountability Challenges:
• Provider-Controllable Metrics: Develop quality measures that reflect care
processes providers can directly influence rather than population-level outcomes
beyond their control,
• Workflow-Integrated Measurement: Design measurement systems that capture
data through normal clinical workflows rather than requiring separate reporting
activities,
• Real-Time Feedback: Provide immediate performance feedback to enable course
corrections during active patient care rather than retrospective reporting,
6. Enhanced Data Transparency and Operational Support (VB-3, VB-4, VB-11, VB-15,
PR-8, PA-5b)
Current Challenge: Programs like MIPS provide limited actionable data and tools, creating
disconnect between performance measurement and operational improvement capability.
Advanced Analytics and Reporting
Recommendation: CMS should prioritize development of:
• Real-time Claims Data APIs: Expand beyond quarterly reporting to provide near
real-time claims data through standardized FHIR APIs
• Actionable Cost Attribution: Provide detailed, patient-level cost attribution data
enabling providers to identify specific intervention opportunities
• Benchmarking Tools: Develop comparative analytics platforms allowing
organizations to understand performance relative to peers
• Treatment Plan Transparency: Define and publish standardized treatment plans
available to patients for common conditions, enabling informed care decisions
Standardized Reporting Methodologies (PR-8, PA-5b)
11

Critical Issue: Current reporting requirements create unnecessary burden through
duplicative methodologies across Medicare, Medicaid, and Medicare Advantage programs.
• Unified Reporting Standards: Standardize reporting methodologies across all CMS
programs where clinically appropriate
• QRDA File Utilization: Enable QRDA files for all quality reporting requirements,
eliminating separate reporting modalities
• Cross-Program Efficiency: Eliminate requirements for providers to report on same
populations using different methodologies for different programs
Enhanced Operational Infrastructure
• Provider Directory with FHIR Endpoints: Implement comprehensive, nationwide
provider directory including FHIR endpoint information
• Standardized Care Plan Interoperability: Require every EMR to support
standardized, interoperable care plan structures
• Encounter Notification Standardization: Standardize encounter notification files
(similar to CCDs) enabling EMRs to automatically ingest and incorporate this data
into care plans

Interoperability, including separate measurements for:
• Patients enrolled in care plans: Track percentage of eligible patients with active,
accessible care plans
• Patients who have access to their care plans: Measure patient engagement with
and understanding of their care plans

Current Gap: Current ONC requirements do little to advance value-based care. Critical
missing elements include:

Essential VBC Technology Capabilities:

• Cost Transparency Integration: Require real-time cost estimation capabilities

• Population Health Analytics: Mandate tools for patient risk stratification and care
gap identification
• Patient Engagement Measurement: Track actual patient interaction with health

• Simplified Clinical Communication: Use AI to translate complex medical reports,

9. Vision for the Perfect Future Healthcare Ecosystem
Integrated Care Delivery Model
What the Perfect Future Looks Like:
• Seamless Data Flow: Patient health information flows effortlessly between all care
providers, payers, and patients themselves through standardized, secure APIs
• Proactive Care Management: AI systems continuously monitor patient health data,
alerting care teams to potential issues before they become serious problems
• Patient-Centered Care Planning: Patients actively participate in their care through
accessible, understandable health information and shared decision-making tools
• Value-Based Outcomes: All stakeholders—providers, payers, and patients—share
financial incentives aligned with health outcomes rather than service volume
Technology-Enabled Coordination
• Universal Provider Directory: Every healthcare provider accessible through a
comprehensive directory with real-time availability, specialties, quality scores, and
direct FHIR connectivity
• Intelligent Care Coordination: AI-powered systems automatically identify care
gaps, coordinate referrals, and ensure follow-up care occurs seamlessly
• Real-Time Quality Measurement: Quality metrics calculated continuously through
normal workflow rather than separate reporting processes
• Predictive Population Health: Community health trends identified proactively,
enabling preventive interventions at the population level
10. Implementation Recommendations
1. Mandatory VBC Participation: Transition from voluntary to required participation in
value-based care programs for all CMS payment recipients
2. AI Investment Initiative: Fund development and deployment of AI tools for clinical
decision support, patient communication, and predictive analytics
3. Universal Interoperability Requirements: Expand data exchange mandates to all
healthcare entities receiving federal payments
15

4. Patient Gainsharing Pilots: Test direct patient financial incentives tied to positive
VBC outcomes
5. Standardization Acceleration: Fast-track development of standardized care plan
formats, encounter notifications, and quality measurement workflows
6. Technology Infrastructure Support: Provide enhanced funding and technical
assistance for smaller organizations to achieve interoperability compliance
Conclusion
The transformation to value-based healthcare requires fundamental changes in how we
structure incentives, exchange data, measure performance, and engage patients. CMS has
the opportunity to lead a comprehensive transformation by:
• Creating mandatory VBC programs with meaningful financial consequences for all
stakeholders, including patients
• Establishing universal interoperability requirements supported by central data
infrastructure and standardized APIs
• Leveraging artificial intelligence to simplify complex healthcare information and
enable predictive, proactive care
• Empowering patients as active participants through transparent cost and quality
information, simplified health communication, and direct financial incentives
• Standardizing workflows and measurements to reduce administrative burden
while improving care quality
This vision represents a fundamental shift from our current fragmented, volume-based
system to an integrated, value-driven ecosystem where technology enables rather than
burdens healthcare delivery. Success requires coordinated implementation across
financial incentives, technology infrastructure, regulatory requirements, and stakeholder
engagement. Without this comprehensive approach, healthcare transformation efforts will
continue to achieve only incremental improvements rather than the systemic change
necessary to improve outcomes while controlling costs.
