1.0 PATIENTS AND CAREGIVERS (B)
1.1 Patient Needs (B.1)
PC-9. Given that the Blue Button 2.0 API only includes basic patient demographic, Medicare
coverage, and claims data (Part A, B, D), what additional CMS data sources do developers view
as most valuable for inclusion in the API to enable more useful digital products for patients and
caretakers? What difficulties are there in accessing or utilizing these data sources today? What
suggestions do you have to improve the Blue Button 2.0 API experience? Is there non-CMS data
that should be included in the API?
Kentro Response: The most valuable additions to CMS Blue Button 2.0 API would address its
current limitations regarding the absence of non-claims-based patient data and the exclusion of
Medicare Advantage beneficiaries. The most valuable additional data sources would be:
 Clinical Data (e.g., from EHRs): Information such as lab results, vital signs, clinical notes,
immunizations, and allergies would provide a far more complete picture of a patient's
health. While CMS does not directly hold all of this data, enabling access through other
means (such as TEFCA) would be critical. This clinical context would transform the API
from a claims-based view to a comprehensive health record, enabling developers to create
more clinically relevant care coordination and patient engagement tools.
 Medicare Advantage (Part C) and Medicaid Data: While Blue Button 2.0 focuses on
Medicare Fee-for-Service, including data for beneficiaries in Medicare Advantage plans,
Medicaid would significantly broaden its utility. Applications utilizing Blue Button 2.0 data
are more generally applicable than today's scope, which is limited to original Medicare
beneficiaries. This expansion is particularly important given that over 50% of Medicare
beneficiaries are now enrolled in Medicare Advantage plans.
There are currently access difficulties to these valuable data sources from CMS perspective. They
include:
 Fragmentation across multiple systems and a lack of standardized APIs for clinical data
 Privacy and consent management complexities when integrating data from multiple sources
 Technical barriers in reconciling different data formats and identifiers across systems
To address these challenges, we suggest the following API improvement:
 Implement standardized data models (such as FHIR) consistently across all data types
 Provide robust developer documentation with real-world use cases and sample
implementations
 Enable bulk data access options for population health management applications
 Improve API performance and reliability for production-scale applications
Lastly, integrating other non-CMS data would be valuable. Beyond CMS-held data, integration
with social determinants of health data, pharmacy fill data from retail pharmacies, and patient-
generated health data from wearables and home monitoring devices would create a truly holistic
view of patient health and enable more impactful digital health solutions.

Kentro Response: TEFCA represents a transformative opportunity to address the fragmented
nature of health data exchange in the United States. While still in early implementation phases, its
framework for nationwide health information exchange offers the potential to enhance patient
access to comprehensive health records dramatically. The integration of TEFCA with existing
infrastructure like Blue Button 2.0 could create unprecedented opportunities for developers to
build truly holistic patient-centered applications.
Specifically, this integration could include transforming TEFCA’s framework to establish Fast
Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) as its foundational data standard, leveraging FHIR’s
comprehensive data attributes and models, resource types, and standardized schemas. This
strategic alignment would be synergistic—Blue Button 2.0 would handle patient authorization and
consent management, while TEFCA would provide the nationwide exchange infrastructure using
FHIR-native data products and services (e.g., Databricks, Azure Data Fabric, Snowflake, etc.).
This proposed infrastructure would then seamlessly support bulk data operations and real-time
API interactions with disrupting TEFCA compliance, enabling patients and healthcare
organizations to leverage modern FHIR-native data products to support clinical needs. Below, we
further outline the benefits and examples of this transformation:
a. Current TEFCA impact and examples: TEFCA, by establishing a framework for
nationwide EHI exchange, has the potential to allow Blue Button 2.0 (or applications built on
it) to access non-CMS data, specifically clinical data from EHRs. Early implementations
through QHINs (Qualified Health Information Networks) are beginning to demonstrate
cross-network data exchange capabilities, though widespread patient-facing applications are
still emerging.
b. Suggested changes: The biggest limitation of Blue Button 2.0, as currently structured, is its
reliance solely on CMS claims data. If Blue Button 2.0 could leverage TEFCA-enabled
exchange, it would mean that an application authorized by a Medicare beneficiary could not
only pull their CMS claims data but also (with proper authorization) access their clinical data
from their various healthcare providers, regardless of which EHR system they use. This
would create a much more comprehensive and unified view of the patient's health.
c. Examples of high-impact use cases: The most transformative use cases for TEFCA
implementation center around comprehensive care coordination and patient empowerment.
Imagine applications that could perform real-time medication reconciliation by combining
pharmacy claims data with clinical prescriptions and allergy information from multiple
providers. For complex patients managing chronic conditions across multiple specialists,
TEFCA could enable seamless care coordination where each provider has access to the
complete picture of a patient's health journey. In emergency situations, clinicians could
instantly access a patient's complete medical history, regardless of where previous care was
delivered. Additionally, population health management initiatives could leverage integrated
clinical and claims data to identify care gaps and improve outcomes at scale.
d. Standards: The current ecosystem benefits from several mature standards that have proven
effective in advancing health data exchange. FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability
Resources) has emerged as the dominant standard for structuring and exchanging health data,

2.0 PROVIDERS (C)
2.1 Data Exchange (C.2)
PR-7. What strategies can CMS implement to support providers in making high-quality, timely,
and comprehensive healthcare data available for interoperability in the digital product ecosystem?
How can the burden of increasing data availability and sharing be mitigated for providers? Are
there ways that workflows or metrics that providers are already motivated to optimize for could be
reused for, or combined with, efforts needed to support interoperability?
Kentro Response: Recent ASTP and GAO studies have found that large and system affiliated
hospitals engage in health information exchange across the boundaries of their health care
institutions or affiliated partners more frequently than independent, small, rural, critical access
counterparts. Similarly, small physician practices and those in rural areas engaged in health
information exchange less frequently than larger and urban practices. Lack of interoperability
creates significant challenges, including delays in healthcare, unnecessary medical testing and
costs and adverse events.
More than one-fifth of the U.S. population lives in rural areas and this population tends to be older
and have worse health conditions than urban dwellers. Patients with chronic conditions and
complex medical needs will likely have multiple providers involved in their care. The need for
seamless data exchange with rural, small, unaffiliated hospitals and provider practices providers is
significant.
CMS could take several strategic steps to address underlying barriers to interoperability affecting
these organizations and entities. This would improve care coordination and quality, minimize

3.0 TECHNOLOGY VENDORS, DATA PROVIDERS, AND NETWORKS (E)
3.1 Technical Standards and Certifications (E.3)
TD-6. What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform? What existing alternatives
should be considered? Are there redundant standards, protocols or channels or both that should be
consolidated?
Kentro Response: The Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health
(HITECH) Act promoted the adoption and implementation of certified EHRs by providing
financial incentives that helped establish regional HIE networks. Recent reports show that 96%
of acute care hospitals and 80% of primary care providers have implemented certified EHRs with
interoperability capabilities; however, actual use of the data from an external provider received
through health information exchange remains low.
To promote and accelerate interoperability, the 21st Century Cures Act mandated the sharing of
certain data elements (e.g., USCDI), promoted use of FHIR APIs and placed restrictions on
information blocking. The Cures Act also established TEFCA to provide an infrastructure model
and common governing approach for HIE networks. TEFCA pushes interoperability by expanding
the number and type of organizations engaged in exchange; and implements policies to widely
recognize and make standard practice, exchange for Health Care Operations, Public Health,
Payment, Individual Access and Government Benefits, with more XPs promised. TEFCA is
driving an increase in WHAT information is exchanged (e.g., USCDI) and how the information is
exchanged (e.g., FHIR APIs).
TEFCA has an opportunity to drive the usability of data exchanged to increase trust, and reliability
and reduce provider burden. Efforts to enforce quality standards to reduce instances of missing
and misplaced data in documents and FHIR messages should be pursued. Incentivizing EHR
vendors to create and integrate workflows which allow providers to specify date ranges for desired
information easily and to direct queries to specific recipients is needed. Lastly, to affect real

Nationwide healthcare interoperability, historically, has been a challenging task to conquer due to
the complexities of health information technology (HIT). The 21st CURES Act set the stage for a
unique, patient focused framework (i.e., TEFCA) in hopes to bridge the gaps within healthcare
interoperability.
TEFCA’s main function in creating a unique interoperability framework starts with its
foundational purpose, establishing a universal governance, policy, and technical floor to
accomplish the goal of nationwide interoperability.
Although Qualified Health Information Networks (QHINs), which serve as connectivity brokers
to ensure interoperability between different health information networks, may have flexibility in
its approach for data exchange within TEFCA, they must do so in adherence to the functional
requirements as described by the QHIN Technical Framework (QTF).
Unique functions to TEFCA include, but are not limited to:
1. Expanded exchange purposes (XPs):
A. Despite national health information exchange networks or frameworks permitting exchange
purposes outside of Treatment, they are not widely adopted or encouraged. TEFCA not
only expands on permitted XPs (as listed below) but also encourages and requires response
outside of Treatment.
a. Treatment
i. Level 1 – Response not required
ii. Level 2 – Response required. The benefit is that entities permitted to use
Level 2 have been vetted and therefor, responders should have a higher
level of trust in making the Required disclosure
b. Payment
i. Not currently required, but may require response in the future
c. Healthcare Operations (HCO)
i. In addition to the generic HCO XP, three granular HCO XPs have been
added as permissible use cases for exchange (all three will require
responses by Feb. 2026):
i. Care Coordination/Case Management
ii. HEDIS Reporting
iii. Quality Measure Reporting
d. Public Health
i. In addition to the generic Public Health XP, two granular Public Health
XPs have been added as permissible use cases for exchange:
i. Electronic Case Reporting
ii. Electronic Lab Reporting
e. Government Benefits Determination
i. Not currently required, but may require response in the future
f. Individual Access Services (IAS)
i. Response required

h. Lastly, TEFCA has adopted a unique policy that allows QHINs to assess fees on

2. Additional trust in exchange:
A. For interoperability to succeed, advance, and be widely adopted, all parties involved must

a. More robust policies and requirements for individuals querying on behalf of

b. Multiple levels of XP codes provide more granularity/specificity to ensure the

c. QHINs must follow strict vetting processes and attest that
organizations/participants (and their subparticipants) within their QHIN/exchange
are permitted to participate in TEFCA as well as query for each specific XP
3. Facilitated Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) Exchange and United States
Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI):
A. No other national network or framework has standards that require FHIR and USCDI v3
adoption. Beginning in 2026, organizations participating in TEFCA must adhere to certain
FHIR exchange requirements and guidelines, as well as conform to USCDI v3 standards
(e.g., US@ address format standardizations)
4. Targeted queries:
A. TEFCA’s QTF permits the ability for participants/subparticipants of a QHIN to initiate a
directed query at a specific endpoint, rather than querying multiple organizations based on a
geospatial radius or other smart query parameters
TEFCA’s unique and progressive framework will help push the industry forward by advancing
standards adoption for interoperability. National health information networks and frameworks are
meeting the challenge by conforming to these new standards (e.g., the eHealth Exchange Network
built functionality to meet TEFCA’s Payment XP based on the Da Vinci HL7 FHIR standard, to
support the automation of prior authorizations; The CommonWell Network has recently built
functionality to allow its members (inside and outside of TEFCA) to send direct/targeted queries),
rather than operating in silos as was permitted in past national exchanges.
In conclusion, we feel confident in stating, as part of the industry at large, that TEFCA is not only
the here and now, but also the future of healthcare interoperability. TEFCA should and can help
our nation accomplish the age-old goal of nationwide interoperability that meets both the
healthcare organizations’ and patients’ needs. Therefore, we believe we should not consider any
alternatives, but rather press forward with adopting and advancing TEFCA, which, within its first
year and a half, has advanced interoperability standards adoption, has required participants to
conform with implementation requirements, and can focus the industry to address data quality and
usability challenges with broad representation in governance.
