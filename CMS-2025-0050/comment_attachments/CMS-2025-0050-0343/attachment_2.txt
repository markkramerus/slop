A. Patients and Caregivers
1. Patient Needs
PC-1. What health management or care navigation apps would help you understand and manage
your (or your loved ones) health needs, as well as the actions you should take?
Answer:
At Patient.com, we believe the key to solving healthcare’s inefficiencies isn’t just
technology—it’s empathy. And that begins by naming what so many Americans
experience: Patient Flustration™. It’s the compound feeling of being both flustered and
frustrated by a system that claims to be digital, yet still buries people in paperwork,
portals, passwords, and phone trees.
This emotional friction leads to real-world consequences—collection issues,
delayed communication, missed opportunities for efficiency, and unnecessary stress for
patients and caregivers alike. It also places avoidable strain on the entire healthcare
ecosystem. Patient.com is on a mission to end Patient Flustration by putting patients back
in control—because when you empower the patient, you transform the system.
a. What are the top things you would like to be able to do for your or your loved ones’ health that
can be enabled by digital health products?
Answer:
Track health history and needs for the entire family and as a non-family caregiver.
One app to access a complete immunization and clinical history across EMR systems,
1

states and without having to remember login credentials for all and old portals.
b. If you had a personal assistant to support your health needs, what are the top things you would
ask them to help with? In your response, please consider tasks that could be supported or
facilitated by software solutions in the future.
Answer:
A personal assistant would help most with appointment setting and administrative
task management. Current applications for setting appointments can be incredibly
laborious with multiple steps. AI and FHIR resources for appointment scheduling would
help consumers be able to have fewer steps to complete an appointment. Then,
completing all the pre-appointment consents and forms would be far easier by having
standard, shareable consents and use of FHIR resources.
PC-2. Do you have easy access to your own and all your loved ones’ health information in one
location (for example, in a single patient portal or another software system)?
Answer:
Not in one location, but on multiple portals. Family sharing is sometimes possible and there is a range of
easy to difficult set-up. If a family member has separate insurance, separate portal access is required
after access is granted by the owner. Access to all date through multiple portals remains a very disjointed
process that needs to be updated often.
a. If so, what are some examples of benefits it has provided?
Answer:
Having access to a family member’s portal makes it easier for a caregiver to track
clinical care, including labs and immunizations.
b. If not, in what contexts or for what workflows would it be most valuable to use one portal or
system to access all such health information?
Answer:
Still need better access to data, not through portals and across health systems,
EMRs and states.
c. Were there particular data types, such as x-rays or specific test results, that were unavailable?
What are the obstacles to accessing your own or your loved ones’ complete health information
2

electronically and using it for managing health conditions or finding the best care (for example,
limitations in functionality, user friendliness, or access to basic technology infrastructure)?
Answer:
Radiology and Pathology results can still prove challenging to access. For instance,
mammogram results come in a mailed letter and the report appears in an app as a FHIR
resource that does not appear to include the interpretation. Radiology images are so
important and so burdensome to obtain and store. Nobody can manage a burned CD or
DVD, and even a USB is still a physical object that needs to be transported. The industry
could do better by providing access to DICOM and other imaging with efficient, short-term
storage.

of their efficacy, quality or impact or both on health outcomes (not approving in the sense of a
coverage determination)? What criteria should be used if there is a review process? What technology
solutions, policy changes, or program design changes can increase patient and caregiver adoption of digital
health products (for example, enhancements to data access, reimbursement adjustments, or new
beneficiary communications)?

PC-6. What features are most important to make digital health products accessible and easy to use
for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers, particularly those with limited prior experience using
digital tools and services?
Answer:
Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers deserve radical usability; clear, explanatory
4

applications that initially present a high-level view of data and processes, that can
become more robust as the user needs. Applications should be flexible and easily user-
configurable, ADA-compliant and apply device operating system settings like large font or
text-to-voice without the user having to set these preferences again and again.
PC-7. If CMS were to collect real-world data on digital health products’ impact on health outcomes
and related costs once they are released into the market, what would be the best means of doing
so?
Answer:
A CAHPS question on the value of digital health products would be one direction.
For specific apps’ health outcomes benefits, perhaps payers and providers could be asked
to report on the apps used for patient engagement and provide qualitative analysis on
health outcome benefit as a starting point, that could evolve into quantitative once large
volume of patient engagement in the app is realized.
2. Data Access and Integration
PC-8. In your experience, what health data is readily available and valuable to patients or their
caregivers or both?
Answer:
On patient portals and some PHR apps, lab data seems to be most readily
available. Sections of the medical record, such as open notes, allergies, problem lists and
often diagnosis and procedure lists are also available for the connected data sources,
which may not be all sources that the patient needs.
a. What data is valuable, but hard for patients and caregivers, or app developers and other
technical vendors, to access for appropriate and valuable use (for example, claims data, clinical
data, encounter notes, operative reports, appointment schedules, prices)?
Answer:
Claims and clinical data are relatively available, depending on the connected data
sources for providers but not easily for patients.

transaction data through HETS and other clearinghouses without the necessity of a
provider’s credentials within the request. The current X12 transaction set requests are

From notes and reports, the diagnosis/ problem list, assessment and plan or the
interpretation of the radiology reports would be valuable subsets of data for patients, as
opposed to the entire note or report.
Radiology images and videos are probably the most sought after and difficult data
types to obtain. CMS could lead the way in efficient transmission and short-term cloud
storage of cine and images.

Benefits information and Prior Authorization status updates would be very helpful
for patients.

TD-7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange and what are its
limitations?
Answer:
United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) has been very helpful as a
standard to use for clinical data categorization and resource or document creation.
We recommend that the Standards Version Advancement Process (SVAP) be
reviewed for any concerns and if none, promote the United States Core Data for
Interoperability (USCDI) to the current approved version, 5. Review and approve new
versions of USCDI faster, at least quarterly. Incorporate more data classes and elements
in USCDI versions.
a. Does it contain the full extent of data elements you need?
Answer:
United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) does not yet contain all
needed data classes and elements. Many are in Levels 0-3 awaiting approval. Patient
entered data elements need to be considered more closely, although attribution to a
patient with currently available data elements may be enough.
b. If not, is it because of limitations in the definition of the USCDI format or the way it is utilized?
Answer:
The limitation of the United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) is that is
still does not yet contain all needed data classes and elements. We recommend review
and approval new versions of USCDI faster, at least quarterly and incorporating more data
classes and elements in USCDI versions.
c. If so, would adding more data elements to USCDI add value or create scoping challenges? How
could such challenges be addressed?
Answer:
The limitation of the United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) is that
is still does not yet contain all needed data classes and elements. We believe adding
17

more and faster will result in better standards for interoperability, data sharing and
comprehension of a more complete clinical picture.
d. Given improvements in language models, would you prefer a non-proprietary but less structured
format that might improve data coverage even if it requires more processing by the receiver?
Answer:
The structure of United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) should be
maintained even within language models.

environment while a shared network allows for a larger system with more flexibility in
pathways of data. Both are useful in the right scenario.

product has auditable patient consent and complies with IAL2 and AAL2 processes.

Digital Health Products should be able to use IAL2 and AAL2 service which is
developed and managed internally or from any suitable vendor and not only the six
certified Credential Service Providers.
Instead of requiring individual login and password credentials, as with Blue Button
2.0 and other Payer Patient Access APIs, allow bulk access to the data using demographics
gathered from the IAL2 process.
Reconsider the administrative necessity of the Common Agreement for Trusted
Exchange Framework. A contract in this scenario may be limiting possible networks,
participants and sub-participants from joining. Decreased management of this contract
process would lead to cost reduction. Multiple Rules, Requirements and Standard
Operating Procedures are well established to maintain a Trusted Exchange Framework.
Ensure all data queried is returned without geofencing: limiting data returned
within an arbitrary geographic region thought to be the most likely area within which a
patient received clinical services.
b. What additional policies could ASTP/ONC and CMS implement to further discourage healthcare
providers from engaging in information blocking practices?
Answer:
Encourage and incentivize participation of Covered Entities in the Trusted
Exchange Framework Common Agreement (TEFCA) Qualified Health Information Network
(QHIN).
Penalize TEFCA QHIN participants and subparticipants who do not return data for
the currently required exchange purposes of Treatment (T-TREAT) and Individual Access
Services (T-IAS). Various disincentives are currently used by CMS, ASTP and OIG which are
a progression from probation and then exclusion from the QHIN, or monetary penalties
such as fines or reduction of Medicare payments as in the Hospital Readmissions
Reduction Program. Unfortunately, the impacts of these programs have not yet had the
desired effect and need advancement.
The Recognized Coordinating Entity (RCE) manages the QHIN directory. Access to
this listing remains limited to RCE and QHINs but should be made public as soon as
possible and this could help pressure non-responding participants.
19

Create a modified version of TEFCA for Individual Access Services with terms that
recognize the generation of queries with a patient’s consent, invoking FTC Actvii and the
Health Breach Notification Ruleviii obligations instead of HIPAA.
Ensure the availability of complete price information in a standard data format for
clinical products and services to patients prior to encounters and order fulfillment that is
easy to access and comprehend. As per the Executive Order 14221, “The Secretary of the
Treasury, the Secretary of Labor, and the Secretary of Health and Human Services shall
take all necessary and appropriate action to rapidly implement and enforce the
healthcare price transparency regulations”. Similar disincentives should be invoked as
mentioned above: progression from probation and then exclusion from Medicare, or
monetary penalties such as fines or reduction of Medicare payments as in the Hospital
Readmissions Reduction Program.
