PC-4: The Future of Digital Health (Apps and Beyond)
Most health apps today ask patients to do the heavy lifting. They ask patients to interpret charts, remember
appointments, figure out food swaps, stay motivated, sync devices, and make sense of a fragmented system.
What’s missing are apps that act as intelligent, proactive partners in our daily lives, understanding people’s
motivations, adapting to their struggles, and connecting the dots between healthcare, daily life, and long-term
goals.
From Lirio’s perspective (and that of behaviorally aware patients) here’s what’s missing:
 Just-in-time personalization. Real-time, context-aware guidance based on individual needs and
behavioral patterns
 Emotional & motivational support. Tools that respond not just to vitals or appointments, but
to subjective experience
 Multi-source integration. Most apps don’t unify clinical, behavioral, social, and personal data (EHR +
pharmacy + nutrition + mood), forcing patients to cobble together their own 360-degree view of their
Local: 865.243.8000 | Toll Free: 877.819.2188 | www.lirio.com

health. Ultimately, most people never achieve that holistic view because of the effort and knowledge
required to get there.
 Support for health outside of the medical office. Solutions are often narrowly focused on clinical
episodes rather than whole-person wellness and everyday life goals. Technology is not meaningfully
integrated into the rhythms and contexts of everyday life.
 Adaptive learning. Most apps don’t evolve over time. If a reminder didn’t work last month, why is the
app still sending it?
 Trustworthy design. Patients often don’t trust commercial apps with their health data, and public
options are limited or generic.
 Feedback loops. Most apps don’t show the impact of behavior change, which is especially important for
showing how everyday choices can support good health over time.
From Lirio’s standpoint, and with the Cures Act as a foundation, CMS should encourage:
 Behavior-aware digital care navigators that understand people’s unique barriers to desired health
behaviors (e.g., fear of diagnosis, transportation, forgetfulness) and adapt the path forward
accordingly.
 Empathetic Digital Twin companions that offer patients a longitudinal, virtual representation of their
health journey, and evolve to reflect behavioral and health changes over time. These digital twins can
be informed by data from both patient and care team to forecast, coach, and adapt in real-time.
 Prevention-first, habit-building apps that don’t just tell people what to do but also help them become
the kind of person who does it. These are missing because the market rewards acute, reimbursable
events (e.g., prescriptions, visits), not upstream, daily improvements.
 Health + Life coordination, provided either via apps or via intelligence layers that can coordinate
between different point solutions, that link care plans to people’s schedules, grocery lists, locations,
moods, etc., to infuse healthcare into daily routines. These don’t exist yet because no one owns that
intersection. CMS could be the one to convene it.
CMS is uniquely positioned to support workflows by building the public scaffolding for a new class of apps.
This includes:
 Standardizing access to patient-level data across payers, providers, and systems, including
behavioral, social, and engagement data — not just clinical.

 Defining behavior-change interoperability protocols (e.g., goals, nudges, adherence feedback)
alongside FHIR and EHR standards.
 Creating a “Trusted Health App Framework” similar to what the VA or NHS have done — a whitelist
of secure, evidence-based tools with approved access to Medicare/Medicaid data, particularly when
those tools have demonstrated strong outcomes and effectiveness to improve health and reduce cost.
 Incentivizing workflows that link benefits to behaviors, such as earned incentives for completing
nudged activities (screenings, preventive tasks).
 Piloting AI-agent–based workflows where behaviorally aware systems can interface with EHRs, care
managers, transportation, or pharmacy services — agent-to-agent coordination on behalf of the
patient.
PC-5: Encouraging Patient and Caregivers to Use Digital Health
While CMS programs center on Medicare and Medicaid, the behaviors and systems that shape health outcomes
extend far beyond program eligibility. Digital engagement must support all people in building sustainable health
habits across their lifespan. To foster greater adoption of digital health products, CMS must focus not only on
technology access but also on behavioral engagement. Many patients and their caregivers face systemic,
environmental, and motivational barriers that technology alone cannot solve. Engagement isn’t solved by
access to information alone; it’s a behavioral design challenge. People need systems that recognize their
motivations, barriers, and real-life contexts, and support their agency in choosing how, when, and through what
means to engage.
A Behaviorally Intelligent Path Forward: In our response to PC-4, we detail what’s missing from today’s digital
health ecosystem. Our recommendations on how to advance digital health will also serve to spark interest from
patients and caregivers in using these tools. To meaningfully support people across the health spectrum, not
just within formal care settings, CMS should envision an ecosystem where digital health products extend into
everyday life. From grocery stores and fitness platforms to transportation and housing services, tools that
contribute to healthier choices and improved access to care must be able to participate in a secure,
interoperable, and human-centered environment.
CMS should catalyze the development and deployment of tools that:

 Personalize outreach using behavioral science — not just demographics or conditions, but individual
motivations, fears, capabilities, routines, and goals — empowering people through deep, context-aware
personalization.
 Leverage evidence based behavioral science frameworks and approaches to surface and resolve what
stands in the way of healthy action.
 Support caregiver workflows that reinforce and align with patient-facing interventions including
communication prompts, decision nudges, and wraparound support.
 Empower people and caregivers as active agents in their health, giving them meaningful choice,

 Focus on people and health, not just patients and care — enabling proactive support in everyday life,

PC-8 b: High Value Data Sources
Our approach leveraging behavioral science and AI seeks to use all relevant, available data to personalize
messages to address individual patient contexts and barriers and subsequently drive positive health behaviors.
As part of the learning process, we generate unique behavioral insights as the system matches behavioral
science strategies to individual patients to drive behavioral objectives and health outcomes. In the course of our
work, it has become clear that key pieces of data that can help reveal individual patient’s barriers include both
longitudinal behavioral records including communication interactions, and the outcomes that result from
matching behavioral science techniques to patients in the context of observed outcomes.
In large populations, through this type of learning process, it becomes possible to accurately predict which
users are most likely to perform various health behaviors, such as attending a cancer screening, getting a
vaccine, etc. More importantly, in the course of learning across these large populations, the conditional
probability that includes how you message that individual becomes much more useful, revealing the best
methods by which to change an individual’s behaviors in ways that lead to better outcomes. Thus, learnings
that come from patient interactions include extremely valuable data that is difficult to obtain. However, this data
is in fact obtainable, and adoption of tools that can obtain and leverage such data should be encouraged, given
the large impacts this could have on population health.
Another source of similarly useful data is in the conversations that clinicians have with patients, in which they
may reveal their personal barriers to performing behaviors that both the physician and patient acknowledge are
important to their overall health outcomes. This type of data can be crucial in supporting patients, particularly
high-risk patients whose behaviors have a great, short-term impact on their health.
TD-1: How to Stimulate Developer Interest in Innovation
CMS can accelerate developer participation by establishing a public digital infrastructure that supports scalable,
behaviorally intelligent health engagement. Developers are currently held back by:

• Lack of trustable, standards-based APIs
• Fragmented messaging channels
• Limited access to timely and actionable data
• Ambiguous or burdensome regulatory environments
• Few incentives for long-term, person-centered innovation
To shift this dynamic, CMS should become a digital infrastructure convener — lowering technical barriers,
aligning incentives, and enabling new forms of proactive care. Crucially, this infrastructure must serve not only
Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers but support broader population health engagement to reduce long-term
burden on public health resources. By enabling proactive outreach and behaviorally informed engagement
earlier in life — before individuals become patients — CMS can help shift the focus of care "leftward" along the
health continuum. This approach fosters better daily habits, reduces avoidable interventions, and creates a
healthier, more resilient population over time. This approach must also affirm human agency — enabling
individuals to shape their own health journeys through digital tools that adapt to their goals, contexts, and
motivations. Deep personalization, informed by behavioral science and real-time data, is key to designing tools
that do not just inform, but empower and inspire.
Short-Term Actions (Next 2 Years)
• Launch a certified, API-first communication framework that allows developers to plug into a standardized,
privacy-preserving system for secure, real-time messaging — enabling behavioral nudges, reminders, and care
coordination across native apps, RCS protocols, IoT devices, and other endpoints. This framework should also
support certification of client applications (such as inbox apps, care portals, or behavioral assistant tools) that
integrate with the infrastructure — ensuring ecosystem partners can tailor experiences while maintaining trust
and interoperability.
• Establish a longitudinal identity and consent framework that allows individuals to securely authorize trusted
tools and caregivers to engage on their behalf across time, settings, and life contexts. Drawing from models like
Login.gov and TEFCA, this would support consistent, privacy-respecting participation in digital health services
— even outside clinical encounters — while enabling continuity and trust across the ecosystem.
• Ensure opt-in participation and clear consent pathways, allowing CMS program participants to receive a
baseline level of communication required for engagement, while enabling the broader public to voluntarily
access proactive health tools through certified platforms. This helps normalize health support as part of
everyday life while respecting privacy, trust, and choice.
• Create a developer engagement program modeled after Blue Button and Login.gov — offering support,
certification, and reference implementations to ease compliance and trust-building.

• Provide financial and reputational incentives such as grants, scorecard credits, or tax benefits for developers
building tools aligned with CMS-endorsed behavioral engagement protocols.
• Enable tools that operate outside of formal clinical workflows, expanding participation to community health
partners, grocery and food providers, fitness platforms, and transportation services — shifting health support
upstream. These tools must also respect and reinforce individual autonomy — enabling proactive choices in

Longer-Term Vision
• Establish a national trust framework for health engagement APIs, modeled after federal authentication and
data exchange efforts (e.g., Login.gov, TEFCA). This framework should define secure, interoperable, and
ethically governed pathways for real-time engagement — while also providing regulatory clarity that
streamlines compliance rather than adding burden. Clear guardrails, consistent certification processes, and
transparency around expectations will allow developers to invest confidently in tools that improve outcomes
without being slowed by ambiguity or costly fragmentation.
• Support an open ecosystem in which certified client tools (apps, devices, interfaces) can securely access
communication channels and behavioral health signals — allowing individuals to choose how they engage.
• Define regulatory clarity and flexible interoperability standards to de-risk long-term investment in digital
health innovation.
• Position CMS as a national convening layer, coordinating private and public efforts in behavioral health
promotion and personalized engagement.
• Center engagement around people, not just patients — embedding human agency into the design of digital
tools and enabling dynamic, deep personalization that evolves over time.
By enabling earlier, more personalized, and context-aware engagement, CMS can reduce the need for reactive
clinical care — using public infrastructure to drive better health outcomes and more efficient use of healthcare
resources. This vision lays the foundation for a lifelong health ecosystem, powered by public standards and
private innovation.
TD-4: CMS’ Role in Establishing Publicly Available Standards-Based APIs
The future of digital health does not rest solely on opening access to patient data. It rests on opening access
to all data that impacts health, and enabling intelligent systems, including agentic AI, to support human agency
by coordinating the needs of individuals in context.
Local: 865.243.8000 | Toll Free: 877.819.2188 | www.lirio.com

Today, even the limited subset of patient data that can be accessed is often constrained by paywalls, opaque
licensing practices, or narrow interpretations of HIPAA that err on the side of inaction. While the law technically
supports patient data access, the practical reality is that:
 Patients often cannot retrieve their full longitudinal record, even if they build or license their own
software agent to do so.
 EHR systems and their API layers are typically not interoperable, not real-time, and often charge fees
that deter innovation.
 Event-driven access is largely unavailable, meaning that opportunities to intervene are missed and AI
tools cannot help patients at the moment when they are most able or willing to act.
Without stronger standards and enforcement, we risk an ecosystem where the most important data needed for
behavior change, prevention, and empowerment remains locked behind infrastructure that was never designed
to serve the patient. In such a system, even CMS's best engagement programs may fail — not for lack of
intention, but for lack of actionable, timely, contextual data.
We are not advocating that all data should be made public or unconditionally accessible. But if we do not create
public, enforceable protocols and governance mechanisms that respect patients’ right to direct their data —
across systems, in real time, and through software agents — then we will never unlock the ecosystem needed
for truly personalized, proactive care.
This is not just a technical problem. It is a systems-level failing that CMS is uniquely positioned to address by:
 Establishing enforceable API access expectations, including caps on per-call pricing and timelines for
fulfillment.
 Standardizing event-driven, real-time data publishing models — critical for agentic tools that rely on
just-in-time intervention.
 Convening an Agentic Health Data Access Task Force, inclusive of technical, policy, and behavioral
science experts, to chart a path toward intelligent, equitable data flow.
We envision a new class of public, open protocols that support:
 Agentic orchestration enabling systems, agents, and workflows to act on behalf of individuals,
coordinating between organizations, devices, and services.

 Context-rich personalization where AI models can send and receive structured, standardized inputs
(like a public version of “Model Context Protocol” or MCP) to enable transparent, auditable
personalization decisions.
 Health barrier data exchange that includes not just clinical data, but data about social risks, behavioral
friction, service availability, and more.
It’s time to move beyond APIs that let us read records, and toward protocols that let us change lives. CMS is
uniquely positioned to catalyze an open, equitable, and intelligent health technology ecosystem by defining and,
more importantly, utilizing the standards that put patients and their goals at the center of interoperable action.
VB-2: Integrating Key Themes into APM Requirements
Current APM frameworks often reward retrospective outcomes while overlooking the tools that drive those
outcomes — tools that anticipate needs, engage individuals meaningfully, and adapt in real time. Without
incentivizing technologies that enhance human agency, personalize outreach, and close gaps in care before
clinical deterioration, we dramatically limit the effectiveness of value-based care.
What CMS Can Do to Modernize APM Requirements
• Incentivize platforms that adaptively guide and support individuals across longitudinal health journeys to
improve overall outcomes. Such platforms should support not only clinical decision-making but also
individualized engagement that reflects a person's context, preferences, and readiness to act.
• Offer reimbursement or scoring credit for AI-enabled orchestration platforms that support services such as
care coordination, transportation, digital access, and caregiver engagement. These platforms must adapt
flexibly to changing needs and support whole-person care across time.
• Incorporate more intermediate behaviors into APM metrics, such as appointment scheduling, screening
completion, or health goal setting, where there is evidence that these actions are meaningful contributors to
long-term outcomes. In other words, health behaviors that have strong evidence links with positive health
outcomes must be considered for inclusion in APM metrics, even if they are far upstream from the sick care that
one hopes to forestall or obviate.
• Require or reward the use of interoperable orchestration tools that coordinate across systems, support care
team collaboration, and communicate effectively with both patients and caregivers. These tools must go
beyond traditional EHR integration and confront the deeper issue of data lock-in, where health information
remains siloed in proprietary systems, limiting access and engagement. Ecosystem-wide interoperability should

be reframed as a shared infrastructure challenge, requiring standards, policy enforcement, and incentives to
ensure data can flow securely and responsibly across care settings, organizations, and supportive services. This
enables person-centered coordination to extend across the full continuum of care and life — with human
agents and digital agents working together to reinforce trust, reduce administrative burden, and improve
outcomes.
• Promote real-time behavioral data as a critical input to population health and quality measurement.
Incorporating measures of patient motivation, barrier resolution, goal progress, and engagement response can
offer a more comprehensive view of health system performance and equity.
• Support solutions that enhance human agency by enabling individuals to participate actively in their care
decisions through platforms that listen, learn, and adapt. This goes beyond compliance to empower people with
behavioral nudges, information, and support that are deeply personalized.
• Embed usability and engagement criteria into APM performance metrics to ensure that digital tools improve
the lived experience of care for patients and caregivers, rather than adding friction.
• Fund pilot projects that demonstrate longitudinal engagement, especially in high-risk populations, using AI-
powered personalization and behavioral science.
• Allow flexibility in tool selection while standardizing data and reporting layers, ensuring a healthy innovation
ecosystem while reducing implementation complexity and cost.
APMs will only succeed at scale when they reward the technologies that make prevention, personalization, and
coordination real. CMS has a unique opportunity to lead this shift — from measuring care after the fact to
enabling it proactively, through smart, trusted infrastructure that supports both human and digital agents
working in service of better health.
