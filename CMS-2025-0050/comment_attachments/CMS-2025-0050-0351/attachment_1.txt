Dear Center for Medicare and Medicaid Services:
Waymark, Inc. (“Waymark” or “we”) is a public benefit corporation that contracts with managed
care organizations and providers to provide tech-enabled community-based care to people
enrolled in Medicaid programs. Waymark provides behavioral health and pharmacy services
through its employed provider workforce and delivers wrap-around support to primary care
providers caring for Medicaid patients using community health workers and care coordinators,
reducing provider burden and addressing health-related social needs. Waymark enters into
value-based contracts with Managed Medicaid Care Organizations (MCOs) using Alternative
Payment Models to fund, deliver, and scale our model and align clinical outcome delivery and
cost savings with our financial success. While our primary experience is in Medicaid, the
principles and technology infrastructure needs we've identified are directly applicable to
Medicare VBC deployment.
Waymark’s care delivery model relies on using technology to improve the outcomes of
Medicaid and dual eligible beneficiaries, to generate cost savings to the healthcare system,
while helping scale the healthcare workforce. We commend the Center for Medicare and
Medicaid Services’ (“CMS”) efforts to encourage, support and cultivate an ecosystem that
increases data availability for stakeholders contributing to health outcomes and improves
patient access to digital capabilities to inform health decisions.
Our comments and recommendations on specific areas of the RFI are set forth below.

PC-8c. What specific opportunities and challenges exist to improve accessibility,
interoperability and integration of clinical data from different sources to enable more
meaningful clinical research and generation of actionable evidence?
HHS should standardize data and data sharing formats to better enable cross system data
sharing across stakeholders in the system. In addition, HHS should establish secure data

sharing protocols for VBC entities participating in federal programs, with particular focus on
Medicare ACO and MA plan data integration.
There is opportunity to scale and improve the data infrastructure that is foundational to
delivering better care outcomes and cost savings to the healthcare system. As a VBC provider
that uses technology to identify Medicaid and dual eligible patients at risk for avoidable acute
care utilization, timely and accurate data from different sources is key to finding and impacting
these patients before they become high cost claimants. Below are three specific opportunities
to improve accessibility, interoperability and integration of clinical data from different sources
to generate actionable evidence.
1. Standardize ADT Feed Access: HHS should require health systems that participate in
Medicare VBC programs to provide real-time admission/discharge/transfer data to VBC
entities caring for patients as this would enable timely intervention and more effective
preventative care.
2. Enable Cross-System Data Sharing: HHS should establish secure pathways and
standardized data formats for sharing eligibility, claims, and clinical data across
Medicare Advantage plans, ACOs, providers, and VBC organizations. A more
comprehensive and real-time snapshot into the health status of patients would support
upstream interventions in service of better clinical outcomes.
3. Community Resource Integration: HHS should create standardized application
programming interfaces (APIs) for connecting patients to social services,
transportation, and community-based organizations that provide key social support
resources to enable individuals to achieve and maintain better health. Our experience
has shown that access to social supports to enable care teams to address health
related social needs is particularly important for dual-eligible populations and is key to
achieving better clinical outcomes and cost savings through the reduction of avoidable
acute care utilization.

Based on our experience deploying community based models of care for Medicaid and dual
eligible populations, there are challenges implementing a multidisciplinary model of care that
ensures evidence based prevention and reduces administrative burden that limits access.
Specifically, to address those challenges, the technology infrastructure needed for VBC
success and care model innovation should include 1) regulatory changes that allow and enable
easier patient identification for members of a patient’s care team, including trusted third party
vendors, 2) regulatory clarity and support for automated scheduling and care coordination, and
3) real time data access to data needed to inform evidence based clinical and preventative
care.
Enabling Patient Outreach and Engagement. Current interpretations of the HIPAA Privacy Rule
create unnecessary friction in patient outreach, which does not serve the purpose of reaching
patients and providing them with key information to inform decision making and health related
social supports to help maintain and achieve better health. VBC entities engaging in or
partnering with other entities in the care workflows need clearer guidance on when and how
they can proactively contact beneficiaries without prior explicit consent, particularly for
preventive interventions and care gap closure. Current TCPA regulations, even under the
healthcare exemption, put up unnecessary barriers to contacting patients via text messages,
the primary means of communication for many Americans.
In short, engagement of members is the first step in creating the value around disease
prevention, health promotion, and reduced overall costs.
Automated Scheduling and Care Coordination: Technologies like AI-powered patient
schedulers can dramatically improve care gap closure rates, but in order to encourage
investment in the development and deployment of these technologies, companies would
benefit from regulatory clarity around patient consent requirements, liability, if any, for use of
automated technologies, and requirements for workflow integration.
With respect to the deployment of automated schedulers, appointment reminders, and other
automated tools that support care coordination, it is unclear what type of patient consent
should be secured and how. To provide regulatory clarity and encourage investment in the
deployment and utilization of automated scheduling tools, HHS could establish the following
tiered consent model.
Tier 1 (Opt-in): Explicit written consent for AI systems to schedule appointments directly
with provider offices on patient's behalf

Tier 2 (Notification-based): Allow automated scheduling of patient appointments with
48-hour advance notification and opt-out option for established patient-provider
relationships
Tier 3 (Emergency/Gap Care): Permit automated scheduling for urgent care gaps (e.g.,
overdue cancer screenings) with same-day notification and immediate opt-out
capability
Across all of these options, patients should retain the right to revoke consent for using
automated scheduling tools at any time, via a simple, and easy to follow process.
In addition, there is opportunity for HHS to incentivize and support integration with provider
workflows. Specifically, HHS should mandate that EHR vendors cannot block legitimate third
party scheduling integrations that meet FHIR R4 standards and patient consent requirements.
Further, building upon the work of the information blocking rules, HHS should establish “must
accommodate" standards for appointment scheduling APIs and clarify that sharing
appointment availability and scheduling data with permitted third parties falls under the
existing use and disclosures of patient information HIPAA rules that do not require explicit
patient consent.
Last, for providers and entities that are willing to deploy innovative applications in provider
offices, HHS can create a safe harbor where providers who accept AI scheduled appointments
are protected from liability as long as the following criteria are met:
● Patient consent has been provided (see proposal above re: patient consent); and
● Scheduling systems clearly identify themselves as automated agents to provider staff
● Use of automated scheduling systems that maintain ≥95% accuracy in appointment
details (time, location, provider, reason for appointment) and include a mandatory audit
trail that can be monitored and reviewed for all automated scheduling activities
These digital tools will still require a human in the loop to ensure the digitally-enabled workflow
is progressing as intended and high quality care.
Real-Time Data Access to Enable Deployment and Effective Utilization of Technology Tools:
Technology is impactful in driving outcomes if in its development, it is designed to be accurate,
without bias and robust. HHS should play a role in facilitating access to robust, aggregated
data sets, to ensure the technology developed is robust and accurate in promoting and driving
evidence based prevention.

VALUE BASED CARE ORGANIZATIONS
VB-1. What incentives could encourage APMs such as accountable care organizations
(ACOs) or participants in Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) to leverage digital
health management and care navigation products more often and more effectively with their
patients? What are the current obstacles preventing broader digital product adoption for
patients in ACOs?
Digital health management and care navigation products must enable, not replace,
human-centered care. The failure to develop products without this reality in mind hinders the
most effective deployment of digital products for the improvement of healthcare delivery and
healthcare systems. HHS should mandate algorithmic bias testing for all predictive models
used in federal VBC contracts to ensure that evidence-based interventions are being deployed
to address the health needs of Medicare and Medicaid patients. HHS could further incentivize
the use of digital health management and care navigation products by issuing guidance that
clarifies how entities can comply with HIPAA requirements while engaging in patient outreach,
particularly for preventive care and care gap closure activities.

Enabling Human Centered Care, Published outcomes1 demonstrate that technology alone is
insufficient to significantly improve patient outcomes for many major chronic and behavioral
health conditions. In behavioral health, the American Society of Addiction Medicine and the
American Academy of Addiction Psychiatry explicitly caution against the use of standalone
technology-delivered interventions for stimulant use disorder, noting insufficient evidence for
their effectiveness as monotherapy and emphasizing the need for clinician involvement to
monitor for decompensation and address access disparities.2 Similarly, meta-analyses in digital
mental health highlight that interventions with some degree of human support are more
effective than purely self-administered, technology-only approaches.3
Furthermore, high attrition rates and limited patient engagement with digital tools are
well-documented barriers, and the literature underscores that active patient-provider
collaboration, coaching, and integration with traditional care are critical for achieving
meaningful clinical benefit.4 Thus, the consensus in the medical literature is that technology
should be viewed as an adjunct to, rather than a replacement for, comprehensive care.
The combination of predictive analytics with trained community health workers, therapists, and
pharmacists achieves superior results compared to purely technological interventions. VBC
payment models should recognize and reimburse the essential human workforce required for

VB-2. How can key themes and technologies such as artificial intelligence, population health
analytics, risk stratification, care coordination, usability, quality measurement, and patient
engagement be better integrated into APM requirements?
HHS should make technology, data, and analytics investments to support engagement of
members that are not attributed to a provider or health plan. With such investments, APM
requirements that reward engagement of unattributed members, and incorporate
evidence-based activities that have been shown to improve clinical outcomes would position
and encourage more providers to pursue preventive care activities and drive cost savings in the
system via APM. HHS should launch Medicare VBC pilots specifically designed around
rising-risk identification and early intervention, building on existing ACO and MA programs to
further encourage preventative and personalized activities that lead to better health outcomes
and cost savings for the system.
We recommend that HHS incorporate the following items into APM requirements –
1. Patient Engagement. HHS should set an expectation in value based contracts for
engaging unattributed members, and provide both investment in, and incentives to, VBC
providers to do so. Value is driven by engaging members, including the underutilized
opportunity to engage “rising risk” members, by providing education and encouraging

behavior change and modification, there should be recognition and support in VBC
contract structures for engagement given the critical role it plays in driving value.
2. Algorithm Transparency Requirements: HHS should mandate that VBC contracts using
AI/ML provide explainable predictions and bias testing results. In that way, outcomes
generated would not be skewed and would reflect the work put in to improve a patient’s
health.
3. Performance Standards: HHS should establish minimum accuracy thresholds for
predictive models used in VBC payment calculations.
4. Continuous Monitoring: HHS should require ongoing validation of algorithm
performance across demographic groups. Without ongoing validation, it is likely
providers will continue to use models that no longer meet their intended goal.
5. Risk-Adjusted Shared Savings: HHS should tie VBC payments to risk-adjusted
outcomes that account for patient social complexity. CMS should establish prospective
baseline methodologies that account for social risk factors, ensuring that VBC entities
serving more complex populations are not penalized. This could include standardized
social risk adjustment similar to the Dual Eligible Special Needs Plan (D-SNP) model,
with baselines set prospectively using validated social complexity measures.
6. Early Intervention Bonuses: HHS could provide additional payments for successful
interventions with rising-risk patients before they become high-cost, particularly
relevant for Medicare populations where early intervention can prevent expensive
complications. In doing so, that would encourage VBC entities to make the investments
in proactive preventative healthcare measures for patients.
7. Technology Investment Support: HHS should create mechanisms for VBC entities to
recover technology development costs through sustainable payment models. Specific
recommendations include:
○ Multi-year contract guarantees (minimum 3-5 years) to justify upfront
technology investments
○ Technology development amortization allowing VBC entities to recover AI/ML
development costs over contract duration
○ Performance-based investment recovery where successful outcomes unlock
additional payments to fund continued technology advancement
○ Shared risk corridors that protect VBC entities from excessive downside risk
during technology implementation periods

VB-3. What are essential health IT capabilities for value-based care arrangements?
Value-based arrangements should incorporate predictive analytics that target "rising risk"
populations, capitalizing on using evidence to prevent broader disease and promote health.
HHS should establish standards for data integration of social factors in VBC contracts and
create pathways for sharing community resource databases across regions. Medicare ACOs
and MA plans should have access to standardized social factors datasets to improve risk
prediction and intervention targeting.
Traditional VBC models focus on high-cost patients after they become expensive, missing
critical intervention opportunities. Better patient outcomes and cost savings could be realized
by deploying machine learning models that identify "rising risk" patients before they become
high utilizers. HHS should incentivize VBC contracts that reward early intervention with
rising-risk populations, not just management of existing high-cost patients. This applies
particularly to Medicare Advantage and ACO programs where early intervention can prevent
costly acute episodes.
Most VBC models inadequately address health related social needs (HRSNs) that drive acute
care utilization across all populations, including Medicare beneficiaries. HRSN data integrated
into predictive models and clinical workflows would yield the following benefits –
● Area-level social determinant variables improve model performance
● Generative AI tools match patients to appropriate community resources
● Automated workflows trigger interventions based on social risk factors
