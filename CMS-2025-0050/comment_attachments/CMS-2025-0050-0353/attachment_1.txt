Interoperability and Data Exchange What are the biggest barriers to seamless health information exchange across different systems? Many barriers exist in creating seamless data exchanges. Data, specifically health information, has had several challenges with interoperability, security, and 
accessibility across multiple healthcare systems. Here are some of the biggest challenges: 1. Lack of Standardization Across Systems - Different Electronic Health Records (EHRs) use proprietary formats, making data exchange difficult. - Inconsistent terminology and data structures prevent seamless integration. This can lead to fragmented and inconsistent deployment, and subsequent adoption of data exchanges. CMS can define, require, monitor, and enforce data exchange standards to ensure adoption. This is desperately needed in prior authorizations, claim documentation attachments, and others. 2. Information Blocking & Regulatory Challenges - Some healthcare entities restrict data sharing due to competitive concerns or legal uncertainties. - Regulatory frameworks, such as HIPAA, can create complex compliance hurdles. HIPAA reform to bring into the digital age is sorely needed. Smart contracts enabled through blockchain could securely democratize permissions and exchange data in a secure, seamless, auditable infrastructure. 3. Security & Privacy Concerns - Cybersecurity risks and data breaches make organizations hesitant to share sensitive health information. - Strict authentication protocols can slow down real-time data access. Simple requirements, such as multi-factor authentication (MFA), password complexity and monitoring, and others would mitigate hinderances to access in a seamless way. 4. Behavioral & Organizational Resistance - Some providers resist adopting new interoperability standards due to workflow disruptions. Healthcare is a lagging industry for technology adoption, and as such has tremendous opportunity to innovate. Antiquated workflows can be replaced with AI in areas of automation, machine learning, large language models, agentic AI, and others. - Lack of training and technical expertise can hinder adoption. It is imperative the healthcare industry evolve from transactions to analytics and having data scientists, business analysts, and database engineers alleviate these burdens – often more efficiently and at a lower labor cost holistically. 5. High Costs & Infrastructure Limitations - Upgrading legacy systems to support modern interoperability standards requires significant investment. Meaningful use afforded incentives for healthcare entities to democratize the paper chart into an Electronic Medical Record (EMR). A similar approach, following the HIMSS EMR Adoption model, 
could be applied for healthcare technologies in the digital space, similarly, or even based upon some of the elements from FinThrive’s Revenue Cycle Technology Adoption Model (RCMTAM) - details and a link to the RCMTAM model are later in our comments. - Smaller healthcare providers may struggle with financial constraints. Health information technology solutions should be designed to eliminate barriers to health information access across different systems by leveraging automation, interoperability, and real-time data exchange. There are many best practices to ensure barriers to health information are mitigated. Here are a few examples: - Seamless EHR Integration using HL7, API, and other data exchange methods. -Patient registration and intake should integrate directly with electronic health records (EHRs), ensuring real-time patient eligibility verification and prior authorization automation. - These solutions should eliminate manual data entry errors, speed up insurance verification, and enhance care coordination. - Insurance eligibility & authorization checks should be automated, integrated and actionable within the EMR. -The representation of payer connections should be a large scope (>800 payer) to ensure organizations are effective at reducing denied claims and incorrect billing. - By automating eligibility screening, providers can quickly confirm coverage, reducing administrative burdens. How can CMS strengthen interoperability using open, standards-based technologies? CMS can strengthen interoperability through leveraging a platform framework with integrated data fabric to ensure stability, security, and access with transacted datasets. Specifically, organizations could be best positioned to enhance interoperability through: - FHIR-Based APIs: Health Information Technology companies would widely adopt Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) to enable structured, standardized data sharing. - SOC & HITRUST Compliance: This is to ensure secure data handling and regulatory compliance across all transactions. - AI & Machine Learning: This can be applied to automating claims processing, fraud detection, and billing optimization for greater efficiency. -Particular attention should be paid to the communication protocol between payer and provider for authorization requests, denials, and clinical documentation delivery and review. Currently, antiquated methodologies exist including varying websites, fax transmission, and even phone calls. Costs and delivery of care could be 
dramatically reduced through the introduction of standardized communication methods. What policy changes would help improve data-sharing between providers, payers, and patients? Policy changes should focus on regulations similar to the Hospital Price Transparency rules, where electronic solutions (estimators for example), can be used to meet the regulatory requirements seamlessly. Technology solutions should be uniquely designed to enhance interoperability and secure access to health data for payers, providers, and patients by leveraging open, standards-based technologies that facilitate seamless data exchange. CMS focus should be on the following: Interoperability & Secure Data Access Across Key Stakeholders Payers - Automated Claims Processing: Integration with payer systems to streamline claims adjudication, reducing errors and delays. - Real-Time Eligibility Verification: Using FHIR-based APIs, providers, patients, and payers should be able instantly verify coverage, and improve billing accuracy. -Authorization Management: FHIR-based APIs can drastically improve a complicated and broken process, making it easier for patients to receive care they need. - Fraud Prevention & Compliance: Advanced identity verification can ensure secure transactions, minimizing fraudulent claims. Providers - Seamless EHR Integration: Digital health platforms should connect with electronic health records (EHRs), enabling real-time access to patient financial data. - Data at the Point of Care: Providers should have the ability to retrieve Medicare claims data directly within their workflow, improving care coordination. - Revenue Cycle Optimization: A focus on AI-driven billing would automate workflows, reduce denials and accelerate reimbursement. Patients - Blue Button 2.0 Expansion: Patients can securely access their health and financial records through expanded patient-facing tools.
- Cost Transparency & Financial Insights: Real-time billing estimates should be required for every patient encounter, helping patients navigate healthcare expenses, coverage, and payments. - Secure Identity Verification: Modern authentication protocols should be deployed and enforced to ensure safe access to personal health data. - Trusted Data Exchange Networks: Healthcare information technology partners should actively participate in secure data-sharing ecosystems, ensuring real- time interoperability. What best practices exist for ensuring secure patient access to their health data? Ensuring secure patient access to health data requires a combination of strong authentication, encryption, regulatory compliance, and user education. There are several best practices in this area: 1. Strong Authentication & Identity Verification - Implement robust multi-factor authentication (MFA) to prevent unauthorized access. - Adopt single sign on solutions (SSO) to give patients control over their data. 2. Encryption & Secure Data Transmission - Encrypt patient health records both in transit and at rest to prevent breaches. - Use end-to-end encryption for secure communication between providers and patients.
- Ensure the use of NIST or FIPS cryptographic standards and guidelines s for all digital health platforms. 3. Regulatory Compliance & Access Controls - Follow HIPAA Privacy Rule , and other state mandated privacy regulations to protect patient data. - Implement role-based access controls (RBAC) to restrict data access to authorized personnel. - Regularly audit access logs to detect unauthorized data access attempts. 4. Patient Education & Transparency - Provide clear consent forms explaining how patient data is used and shared. - Educate patients on secure login practices and phishing prevention. - Offer user-friendly portals with real-time access to health records. 5. Secure API & Interoperability Standards - Use FHIR-based APIs to enable secure data exchange between healthcare systems. - Ensure trusted data exchange networks to prevent information blocking. - Adopt blockchain technology for tamper-proof patient records. How can automation and AI improve claims processing and fraud detection? Automation and AI are transforming claims processing and fraud detection, making healthcare operations faster, more accurate, and more secure. AI-powered claims editing ensures accuracy before submission, reducing denials, while real-time reconciliation minimizes errors and accelerates payment cycles. AI-driven claims automation integrates seamlessly with electronic health records (EHRs), ensuring real-time updates and maintaining a single source of truth. Custom edits allow providers to tailor claims processing to specific payer rules, improving first-pass acceptance rates. Additionally, Machine learning models analyze historical claims data to identify patterns leading to denials, while AI-driven alerts help providers correct errors before submission, reducing rework and revenue loss. In fraud detection, AI identifies anomalies in claims data and flags suspicious billing patterns, continuously evolving to recognize emerging fraud tactics. AI-powered 
fraud prevention cross-references claims against vast datasets, detecting inconsistencies before payouts occur, while behavioral analysis helps uncover synthetic identities and deepfake-generated documents. AI-driven fraud prevention also ensures compliance with CMS regulations, reducing audit risks, while predictive analytics anticipate fraud trends and enable proactive risk mitigation. How can CMS modernize provider directories to improve care coordination?
CMS has made significant strides in modernizing provider directories to improve care coordination, but there are additional steps it can take to enhance interoperability, accessibility, and efficiency beyond its current initiatives. 1. Expanding the National Provider Directory Initiative While CMS is developing a centralized provider directory, it should also integrate real-time provider availability and appointment scheduling capabilities. By enabling direct booking through the directory, patients and providers can reduce administrative delays and improve care access. 2. Enhancing Federated Identity Verification CMS is implementing modern identity verification solutions, but it should expand these efforts by adopting biometric authentication and decentralized identity management. This would allow secure, single-sign-on access across multiple healthcare platforms, reducing duplicate credentialing efforts. 3. Advancing Provider Directory API Capabilities CMS requires payers to maintain a publicly accessible Provider Directory API, but it should also mandate standardized data formats across all healthcare entities. By enforcing FHIR-based interoperability, CMS can ensure seamless data exchange between providers, payers, and patients. 4. Integrating AI for Provider Data Accuracy CMS’s provider directory modernization efforts should incorporate AI-driven data validation to automatically detect outdated or incorrect provider information. AI can flag inconsistencies, reducing manual updates and improving directory reliability. 5. Expanding Digital Insurance Card Functionality CMS is developing digital insurance cards, but it should also integrate real-time benefits tracking and cost estimation tools. This would allow patients to see coverage details instantly, improving financial transparency and care planning.
