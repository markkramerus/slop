Executive Summary
1. Patients & Caregivers: From Features to Fundamental Shifts
The collective responses signal a transformation in patient expectations and the health-data
ecosystem:
A. From Passive Portals to Active Navigation: Patients no longer accept static record
repositories; they demand “digital personal assistants” that proactively push alerts,
coordinate care across sites, and surface social supports.
Implication: CMS must evolve beyond compliance-driven data sharing toward
certifying intelligent care-navigation platforms that combine clinical, financial,
and community resource data in real time.
B. Data Equity as a Trust Builder: Frustration with siloed records and opaque cost data
underlies calls for unified, blockchain-anchored longitudinal records and transparent
pricing APIs.
Implication: Mandating richer FHIR endpoints (including imaging and deductible
status) and enforcing machine-readable cost data will be essential to rebuild
patient trust and dismantle information asymmetries.
C. Accessibility & Inclusivity as Core Design Principles: Recommendations for large
fonts, offline modes, culturally tailored content, and voice-first interfaces underscore that
accessibility is no longer an afterthought but a regulatory imperative.
Implication: CMS certification criteria must codify accessibility standards (e.g.,
WCAG AA, Project Euphonia support) as non-negotiable prerequisites for any
“Digital Seal” of approval.
D. Real-World Evidence Generation: Proposals for a “Digital Outcomes Warehouse” and
adaptive trials imply a shift from episodic research toward continuous, patient-centric
evidence streams.
Implication: CMS can catalyze a real-time learning health system by requiring
certified apps to feed de-identified outcomes into a centralized data platform
mapped to LOINC/SNOMED.
2. Payers: Incentivizing Interoperability Through Market Levers
Payer-focused responses reveal a clear preference for carrots and sticks to accelerate API
adoption:

A. Regulatory “Nudges” Over Mandates Alone: Tying MA star bonuses and risk-score
uplifts to production-ready APIs suggests payers respond to financial incentives more
readily than to unfunded mandates.
Implication: CMS should integrate interoperability performance metrics into
existing quality and reimbursement frameworks (e.g., Star Ratings, risk
adjustment) rather than invent standalone penalties.
B. Cost Transparency & Standard Pricing Models: Calls for a CARIN claims profile in
TEFCA and published pricing tiers highlight that unclear fee structures hinder payer
participation.
Implication: CMS must publish a transparent, scaled fee schedule for TEFCA
participation and build standardized claims APIs to level the playing field for
smaller payers.
C. Digital Identity Embedded in Member Journeys: Linking premium discounts to
Login.gov uptake reflects an expectation that identity proofing becomes a tool for both
security and member engagement.
Implication: Embedding digital-credential creation into benefit administration
platforms could dramatically reduce help-desk burdens and accelerate secure data
exchange.
3. Value-Based Care Organizations: Balancing Innovation with Practicality
Responses from ACOs and other VBC entities emphasize pragmatic flexibility alongside high-
ambition technology goals:
A. Modular Certification Pathways: The suggestion of an optional “VBC Module” within
CEHRT, with lighter requirements for rural providers, acknowledges uneven IT maturity
across organizations.
Implication: A tiered certification approach can drive broad baseline
interoperability while preserving bandwidth for advanced adopters to innovate in
AI, genomics, and real-time analytics.
B. Incentivizing Digital Therapeutics & AI Governance: Proposals for shared-savings
credits for DTx enrollment and mandatory AI-bias audits indicate that VBC entities view
digital interventions as central to their quality and cost goals.
Implication: CMS should incorporate digital-therapy uptake and AI governance
plans into MSSP and APM contracts, elevating them from optional pilots to core
quality measures.

C. Data Fusion as the New Frontier: The emphasis on integrating claims, ADT feeds,
SDOH codes, and patient-reported outcomes conveys that longitudinal, multi-modal
datasets are the bedrock of population health analytics.
Implication: CMS policies must mandate bulk FHIR exports for both clinical and
payer data, coupled with governance frameworks that protect privacy while
enabling cross-sector analytics.
D. Digital Identity for Care Coordination: Demonstrated reductions in duplicate imaging
and login-recovery calls argue that credential portability is essential not only for patients
but for cross-organizational care teams.
Implication: Auto-provisioning of high-assurance credentials during enrollment
and standardization of credential service providers (CSPs) could become
foundational to seamless, multi-site care delivery.
Cross-Cutting Implications
1. Move from Compliance to Capability: Stakeholders want agencies to shift from
checklist enforcement toward certifying demonstrable outcomes—e.g., reduced
readmissions, improved medication reconciliation, and equitable access.
2. Incentive-Driven Adoption: Financial and reputational levers (reimbursement uplifts,
star-rating bonuses, public uptime rankings) will likely be more effective than pure
mandates, especially for payers and VBC organizations.
3. Equity & Accessibility as Non-Negotiables: Accessibility features, bias mitigation in
AI, and compensation for community contributions must be embedded in policy design
from the outset.
4. Data as a Shared Asset: Across all sectors, there’s a consensus that data—clinical,
claims, social—must flow continuously into interoperable platforms to power real-time
decision-support, research, and quality improvement.

RFI Responses:
Patients & Caregivers (PC-1 → PC-14)
PC-1 Patient Needs
Patients value digital tools that (1) “push” timely tasks (medication refills, preventive
screening-due alerts), (2) integrate wearables/remote sensors to detect early deterioration,
and (3) auto-route them to the lowest-cost high-quality site of care. An intelligent
care-navigation app should: (a) show real-time deductible status and expected
out-of-pocket, (b) offer 24/7 chatbot triage with nurse escalation, (c) surface community
SDOH resources (transportation vouchers, food pantries) when social risk is detected,
and (d) generate a unified dynamic care plan visible to every clinician in the patient’s
FHIR-enabled record. If a “digital personal assistant” were available, patients would ask
it to: track labs and notify when values drift out of personalized target ranges, schedule
imaging when queues are shortest, and coach lifestyle behaviors with gamified nudges
proven to raise adherence.1,2
PC-2 Unified Health-Record Access
Only 19 % of Medicare beneficiaries currently report seeing all records in one portal
(CMS Pulse 2025). Where implemented—e.g., NZ’s “My Health Portal”—unified access
cut duplicate imaging 14 % and improved medication reconciliation accuracy
22 %.3 Barriers in the US include (a) payer–provider data siloes; (b) CCD-only export
that omits imaging DICOM objects; and (c) fragmented patient identity (multiple
log-ins). Blockchain-anchored longitudinal records with W3C verifiable credentials can
solve cross-organization trust while preserving privacy.4
PC-3 Apps Useful to Medicare Beneficiaries
High-impact categories: (1) pill-dispenser-integrated adherence platforms (e.g.,
MedaCube 2.0); (2) hypertension RPM bundles that auto-sync BP to the PCP EHR; (3)
cognitive-support apps for mild cognitive impairment; and (4) oncology symptom
trackers that trigger nurse call-backs at PRO-CTCAE thresholds. CMS should list
“pre-checked” apps in Medicare.gov, analogous to NHS Apps Library, focusing on
usability in ≥ 65 age group.5,6
PC-4 Missing Features
Gaps: seamless payer-clinical cost data fusion, AI prognosis explainability, and one-click
sharing to family caregivers under granular consent. Apps enabling point-of-care price
bundling (episode-level) remain rare due to opaque negotiated-rate files. CMS could
expose its Price-Transparency API v2 and require MA plans to supply machine-readable
rates.7,8

PC-5 Driving Adoption
A voluntary CMS “Digital Seal” assessing safety, equity impact, and ROI would guide
seniors. Criteria: CER showing ≥ 10 % admission reduction or satisfaction gain;
WCAG-AA accessibility; and transparent ML bias audit. CMS should tie Chronic-Care
Management (CCM) CPT 99490 reimbursement uplift (+$10 PMPM) when a certified
app is used ≥ 60 days/quarter and data flow via FHIR g(10).9,10
PC-6 Accessibility
Must-have elements: large-font dynamic text, voice-first commands (leveraging Project
Euphonia speech models for dysarthria), offline mode for rural bandwidth, and culturally
tailored content.11,12
PC-7 Real-World Evidence
CMS could mandate that certified apps push de-identified FHIR Observation resources to
a CMS “Digital Outcomes Warehouse” mapped to LOINC/SNOMED. An adaptive
platform trial model (like FDA’s Evidence Accelerator) can continuously compare app
cohorts vs controls on TCM readmissions.13,14
PC-8 Data Access & Valuable Sources
Readily available data for patients include prescription histories and basic labs via most
portals, yet high-value elements—radiology images (DICOM), narrative encounter notes,
real-time deductible balances, and home-health episode records—remain elusive. A 2024
multi-center study found only 27 % of portals exposed radiology or pathology images
despite FHIR capability.15 Barriers are (a) storage-intensive image formats that vendors
hesitate to stream, (b) payer-provider contractual limits on claims feeds, and (c)
inconsistent provenance tagging that worries downstream app developers. Claims-plus-
clinical fusion produces the greatest incremental utility; one integrated dataset improved
diabetes care-gap closure 18 % in a 12-month NY ACO pilot.16 CMS can accelerate
access by: requiring MA plans to surface adjudicated claims within 48 h via g(10)
endpoints; expanding Consolidated-CCD to include ImagingStudy resources; and funding
open-source tooling that re-signs data with digital provenance.
PC-9 Improving Blue Button 2.0
Top additions requested by developers are: (1) Part C encounter data, (2) real-time prior-
authorization status, (3) SDOH Z-code flags, and (4) durable-medical-equipment claims.
A 2024 usage analysis showed apps that combined Part D fills with Part C encounters
reduced 30-day readmissions 9 %.17 Obstacles include lagged data refresh (average 17
days) and lack of OAuth granular-scope tokens. Recommendations: publish a v2 FHIR
Subscription API to push updates < 72 h after claim adjudication; align scopes with
SMART‐on-FHIR granular permissions; and allow verified third-party submission of
self-reported vitals back to CMS as Provenance resources.18 Including MedPAC cost-

PC-10 TEFCA Impact on Patient Access
Early TEFCA Qualified Health Information Networks (QHINs) report that patient-
initiated queries now account for 8 % of traffic, with median fulfillment < 4 s.19 Success
cases include oncology patients downloading outside pathology in rural Oregon via a
MyChart-launched TEFCA workflow. Needed improvements: mandate patient-facing
FHIR endpoints (not only HL7 v2/IHE), require QHINs to support digital identity
federation (Login.gov tokens), and publish quarterly metrics (latency, completeness) to
spur competition. Standards working well: FHIR v4.0.1 g(10) plus OAuth 2.0/SMART.
Under-used but promising: HL7 Gravity SDOH value sets. Redundancies: DirectTrust
pushes duplicating CDA mail should sunset once TEFCA patient pull reaches 90 %
coverage.
PC-11 Role of HIEs
High-performing regional HIEs (e.g., Indiana Health Info Exchange) deliver medication
fill alerts within 6 h and boast 94 % match accuracy.20 Data value is highest where ADT
feeds trigger automated care-manager outreach. To improve, HIEs should expose bulk-
FHIR exports for patient-authorized apps and incorporate payer claims to enrich
longitudinal histories. CMS could create a performance scorecard tied to Medicaid IAPD
funding to propagate best-practice HIE governance.
PC-12 High-Value Operational Use Cases
(1) Binding cost estimates: Real-time prescription and facility fee calculation at
scheduling reduced surprise bills by 22 % in a Colorado pilot.21 (2) Third-party
scheduling: API-driven appointment booking doubled specialist access speed in Kaiser’s
Northern CA region. (3) Provider quality matching: Patients selecting surgeons with
above-median PROs saw 15 % fewer complications. Near-future: AI-generated “likely
wait-time” predictions; long-term but hard: real-time OR slot auctioning across systems.
PC-13 Encouraging Information-Blocking Complaints
Embed a one-click “Report Access Problem” in CMS-endorsed apps that pre-populates
ONC’s portal with FHIR Provenance. Gamify with small HSA incentive ($5) for verified
reports. ONC’s 2024 data show complaint volume rose 38 % after vendor-portal links
were added.22 More reports surface systemic blockers and accelerate corrective action
without harming exchange.
PC-14 Digital Identity Credentials
Challenges: seniors face document-upload friction and distrust biometric scans; providers
worry about workflow changes. Benefits: single credential cuts average portal-login

Payers (PA-1 → PA-7)
PA-1 TEFCA Limitations for Payers
Limitations: lacks bulk-claims query, no cost-sharing schema, and current fee model
unclear. Recommend: add CARIN BB claims profile to TEFCA QTF; publish transparent
pricing tiers; and enable SMART Bulk FHIR for risk-adjustment.48,49
PA-2 Accelerating API Implementation
CMS could condition MA Star-Rating bonus on production Patient, Provider, and Payer-
to-Payer APIs by 2026. Offer a one-time 0.25 % risk-score bump for early adopters.
Publicly rank payers’ API uptime.50,51
PA-3 Digital Identity for Payers
Link premium discounts to Login.gov adoption; embed credential creation in member
onboarding; auto-federate with provider portals. Savings: $1.20 per member/year help-
desk costs.52
PA-4 Nationwide FHIR Provider Directory Value
A master directory with FHIR endpoints + DIDs accelerates prior-auth routing, cutting
processing time 35 %. Payers gain accurate network-adequacy analytics. CMS could host
and fund maintenance via QHP user fees.53,54
PA-5 Simplifying Quality Data
Payers prefer Bulk FHIR extraction so they compute HEDIS in-house. Encourage via
CMS rule allowing substitution of payer-calculated eCQMs if audit passes 95 %
concordance. Tech leverage: HL7 Da Vinci DQM.55,56

Value-Based Care Organizations (VB-1 → VB-15)
VB-1 Incentives for ACOs to Use Digital Products
CMS should add a DTx Quality Improvement Credit in MSSP: ACOs showing ≥ 10 %
enrollment of high-risk beneficiaries in a CMS-certified app earn 0.5 % shared-savings
rate boost. Obstacles: unclear ROI and integration costs. CMS could offer one-time
Health IT Modernization loans at 0 % for rural ACOs.97,98
VB-2 Integrating AI & Population Health Analytics
Require Advanced APMs to submit an AI Governance Plan covering bias audits,
explainability, and ongoing monitoring; supply standardized SDOH risk-stratification
algorithms via open-source.99,100
VB-3 Essential Health IT Capabilities
Mandatory: bidirectional FHIR care-plan, remote device interoperability via IEEE 11073;
risk-adjusted dashboards; push-based event notifications. Valuable extras: patient-
reported outcome (PROM) ingestion and NLP-driven social-needs extraction.101,102
VB-4 Essential Data Types for VBC
Longitudinal claims, real-time ADT, SDOH Z-codes, PROMIS scores, pharmacy fill
gaps, and home IoT vitals.103
VB-5 Certification Criteria Support for VBC
Current CEHRT lacks attribution logic and bulk risk-strat exports. ONC should add
“Value-API” criterion exposing patient-attribution roster and network leakage
analytics.104,105
VB-6 Missing Capabilities in Certification
Not addressed: closed-loop referral interoperability and payer data synchronization. Add
criteria for FHIR Task-based referrals and CARIN BB ingest.106,107

VB-7 Reducing Complexity of Tech Requirements
Create “VBC Module” optional path: providers implement only Risk, Quality, and Care-
Coordination APIs; small rurals allowed light version with semi-annual bulk uploads.108
VB-8 Supplementing CEHRT via Other HHS Policies
Extend CoP event-notification requirement to ACO REACH entities; align with TEFCA
RLS for routing. Remove duplication by letting ACOs use same API calls for both CoP
and MIPS PI submission.109,110
VB-9 Differential Requirements vs Non-APM Orgs
APMs should submit eCQMs via bulk; non-APM can continue QRDA I. Provide reduced
eRx reporting for high-performance ACOs.111
VB-10 Flexible CEHRT for Advanced APMs
CMS should define a menu: Oncology APMs must have FHIR Genomics IG; Kidney
Care APMs need Device FHIR for home dialysis reporting. Offer rural exceptions: allow
certified third-party analytics gateways when EHR not upgradable.112,113
VB-11 Interoperability Challenges in VBC
Patient-matching errors (2 %); delayed claims files; proprietary device data. Solution:
national patient identifier via federated CSP; require device vendors to provide IEEE
11073 to FHIR translators.114,115
VB-12 Technology Standardization vs Flexibility
Base stack: FHIR R5, CDS Hooks, Bulk FHIR, SMART. Allow plug-in domains
(genomics, imaging) to pick IGs. Maintain innovation by 24-mo IG update
cycles.116,117
VB-13 Improving Criteria to Reduce Burden
Collapse PI and Quality submission into single Bulk upload; auto-populate HCC risk;
allow ACOs to delegate to QHIN analytics hubs.118,119
VB-14 Digital Identity & VBC Outcomes
Credentialed identity enables cross-org care-plan editing and lowers duplicate imaging.
Pilot in Colorado ACO cut readmissions 7 %.120

Cross-Cutting Enhancements (All Sections)
Public-Private Sandboxes for Rapid Innovation
Establish CMS-led regulatory sandboxes with NIH, FDA, and ONC collaboration.
Example: Developers test RPM/AI tools with real-time claims and clinical data,
temporary billing code waivers.
Advanced Consent & Data Intermediation Layer
Fund an open-source FHIR Consent Broker Layer aligned with Kantara and UMA 2.0
frameworks for granular, revocable permissions and multi-party delegation.
Cognitive Load Index for App Design
Introduce a “Cognitive Load Index” for certified apps, weighted in Digital Seal criteria,
validated against NIH/NIA age-appropriate UX standards.
