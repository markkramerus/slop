PC-7. If CMS were to collect real-world data on digital health products' impact on health
outcomes and related costs once they are released into the market, what would be the
best means of doing so?
To accurately assess the real-world impact of digital health products (DHPs) on beneficiary outcomes and
costs, CMS must expand its data strategy beyond traditional provider-reported and claims-based
sources. While provider-generated data such as claims and quality reports are foundational, they can be
limited by reporting biases and conflicts of interest, particularly when reporting outcomes for services
they provided themselves. To ensure objectivity, CMS must incorporate independent data sources,
including beneficiary-reported outcomes and consumer-generated data, alongside rigorous data
management and analytics.
Traditional sources, such as Medicare claims, MIPS, and Hospital Compare, offer insights into utilization
and clinical performance but may not fully capture the actual effectiveness of digital health technologies.
Providers, incentivized by reimbursement models, may unintentionally overstate the benefits of digital
interventions. As CMS evaluates tools like remote monitoring devices and AI-enabled diagnostics, it must
validate provider-reported outcomes with independent evidence to guide policy and payment decisions.
A promising strategy involves incorporating health data from consumer-facing platforms. Wearable
devices and mobile apps from Apple, Fitbit, Google, etc. generate continuous biometric data, such as
heart rate, activity, and sleep metrics, that can offer real-time insight into health outcomes. CMS could
incorporate voluntary beneficiary data-sharing agreements or pilot programs to securely integrate such
data with claims and EHRs. In parallel, CMS should engage digital health vendors used by ACOs and
Medicare Advantage plans to access device data collected through care protocols.
Most critically, CMS must center its assessments on patient-reported outcomes. Beneficiary-reported
measures offer direct insight into quality of life, functional improvement, and satisfaction. CMS should
implement scalable tools, such as secure digital surveys and mobile apps, to collect this data at regular
intervals (e.g., 30, 90, 180 days post-intervention), enabling longitudinal analysis of DHP effectiveness.
Stratified sampling would allow CMS to assess specific populations and programs, including chronic
condition management and digital therapeutics.
To manage this diverse data ecosystem, CMS must invest in a robust data infrastructure. Data from
claims, providers, devices, and patients must be harmonized using standard models (e.g., FHIR, OMOP)
and supported by automated quality controls, privacy safeguards, and clear data stewardship protocols.
Governance must ensure secure compliance with HIPAA, CMS IS2P, and federal data protection
standards.
CMS will require experienced partners to build, integrate, and analyze this data ecosystem. Cogent
People brings over a decade of experience delivering data ingestion, management, analytics, and
governance solutions for CMS programs, including MIDAS, MIDCAP, CCIIO, and FMOA. We specialize in
large-scale data integration, data quality assurance, and data reporting that power decision-making
across CMS. With proven expertise in data strategy, system architecture, and federal health programs,
Cogent People is uniquely qualified to support CMS in evaluating the real-world impact of digital health
products on beneficiary outcomes and program performance.

PC-8. In your experience, what health data is readily available and valuable to patients or
their caregivers or both?
Beneficiary and Caregiver Health Data Access:
Beneficiaries and caregivers increasingly depend on health data to manage conditions, coordinate care,
and make informed decisions. The most readily accessible and useful data today includes lab results,
medication lists, immunization records, allergies, and visit summaries, typically available through EHR-
linked patient portals or FHIR-based APIs like Blue Button 2.0. These data support medication adherence,
chronic condition management, and basic patient engagement.
However, critical gaps remain. Patients often lack access to complete claims data, clinical notes,
operative reports, and discharge summaries, which are frequently siloed, unstructured, or hidden
behind proprietary systems. Real-time data, such as provider availability, wait times, or personalized cost
estimates, is generally absent. Additionally, important non-clinical factors like social determinants of
health (e.g., housing, transportation, air quality) are inconsistently collected and rarely shared with
patients, limiting their ability to manage care comprehensively.
Beyond clinical and claims data, high-value sources include pharmacy benefit data (e.g., medication costs
and coverage), patient-generated health data from wearables and apps, and public health records like
immunization registries and exposure alerts. These sources are essential to drive patient empowerment,
personalized care, and informed navigation of the healthcare system.
To fully support beneficiaries, CMS should consolidate these fragmented datasets into a single, user-
friendly platform that provides a comprehensive, real-time view of a patient’s health. The current
ecosystem of disconnected portals and inconsistent formats places an undue burden on patients. A
centralized, integrated solution would improve engagement, decision-making, and self-advocacy.
Provider and Technical Stakeholder Perspectives on Data Integration and Interoperability
From the provider and systems integrator perspective, seamless data exchange remains hindered by
persistent challenges. Clinical data is fragmented across EHR platforms, with inconsistent terminology,
documentation standards, and incomplete fields, undermining data quality and limiting the potential of
health analytics and research.
Consent and privacy regulations, such as HIPAA and 42 CFR Part 2, further complicate data sharing.
Robust consent management and dynamic privacy controls are not yet universally implemented, making
compliant, patient-centered exchange difficult. At the same time, providers and developers face high
technical burdens in managing disparate EHR interfaces, proprietary APIs, and secure data pipelines,
which demand specialized expertise and sustained investment.
The current ecosystem also lacks infrastructure to support emerging data types, such as patient-
generated health data and environmental exposure data. While essential for personalized and
population health strategies, these sources lack consistent integration standards.
Despite these barriers, opportunities are emerging. Broader adoption of HL7 FHIR APIs, implementation
of TEFCA, and alignment with data models like USCDI and OMOP can enable more consistent and secure

PC-11. How are health information exchanges (HIEs) currently helping to advance patient
access to health information in the real world?
HIEs play a pivotal role in improving clinical data access by securely aggregating and sharing lab results,
medication histories, and encounter summaries across healthcare organizations. This supports care
coordination, reduces redundant testing, and enables timely decision-making, especially for patients
with complex conditions who engage with multiple providers. However, HIE effectiveness is often
constrained by uneven provider participation, inconsistent data formats, and lack of real-time exchange
capabilities. Many HIEs face sustainability challenges due to fragmented funding and continue to operate
on outdated technical infrastructures. Interoperability gaps across HIEs, vendors, and national networks,
along with complex consent and privacy regulations, further hinder timely and reliable data exchange.
Moreover, patient access remains limited, as few HIEs offer user-friendly tools that empower individuals
to view or manage their health information.
Addressing these challenges requires greater standardization, investment in modern infrastructure, and
expanded stakeholder participation. HIEs must adopt national interoperability standards, such as HL7
FHIR and USCDI, and implement systems that enable real-time data exchange, quality monitoring, and
scalable governance. Enhanced patient-facing features, including mobile apps and web portals, are
essential to increasing individual engagement. Encouraging participation from both providers and payers
through policy or incentive mechanisms will be critical to achieving data completeness and continuity of
care.
Looking ahead, a more scalable approach lies in building a national, standards-based, cloud-native data
ecosystem. Operated under frameworks like TEFCA and powered by FHIR APIs, such a platform would
support secure, real-time data exchange across all care settings. It would integrate a broader range of
data types, including clinical, claims, social determinants of health (SDOH), and patient-generated health
data (PGHD), to create comprehensive, longitudinal patient records. A national system would also
incorporate federated provider and FHIR endpoint directories, enabling dynamic data discovery and
trusted routing, while offering scalable tools for quality reporting and decision support. Robust
governance and interoperable consent frameworks are necessary to ensure patient privacy and trust.
Unlike many regional HIEs, this scalable architecture would support continuous, nationwide data

PR-3. How important is it for healthcare delivery and interoperability in urban and rural areas
that all data in an EHR system be accessible for exchange, regardless of storage format
(for example, scanned documents, faxed records, lab results, free text notes, structured
data fields)? Please address all of the following:
From Cogent People’s perspective, ensuring that all data within an Electronic Health Record (EHR),
regardless of format is accessible for exchange is not only a matter of interoperability, but a foundational
requirement for comprehensive data integration, data management, and data governance. Access to
diverse data types, structured fields, scanned documents, lab results, faxed records, and free-text notes,
is essential to achieving a unified, high-quality data ecosystem that enables consistent ingestion and
interpretation across healthcare systems. However, the current reality is fragmented: disparate data
formats, inconsistent standards, and legacy system constraints impede seamless integration. Rural
providers are often at a disadvantage, operating with limited infrastructure that makes digitization of
unstructured formats particularly burdensome, while urban systems face data volume and infrastructure
complexity challenges that strain interoperability solutions.
The inability to standardize and integrate all available health data undermines not only data sharing, but
also the quality and reliability of downstream analytics and reporting. Without high-quality, consistently
structured data; providers, payers, and public health agencies face challenges in generating actionable
insights, performing population health analysis, or tracking care outcomes. These issues degrade
decision-making, increase administrative burdens, and weaken the impact of health interventions. From
Cogent People’s experience supporting CMS Marketplace systems, we found that in some cases,
achieving meaningful integration requires moving beyond transport-level API exchange, and toward
robust data normalization pipelines and event-based processing models that allow data to be cleansed,
transformed, and mapped to common semantic standards before integration.
Technical barriers remain substantial. Many legacy systems store clinical content in static or proprietary
formats that do not align with HL7 FHIR or USCDI data classes. Moreover, unstructured data lacks the
metadata needed for effective indexing or search. Integrating such content into data platforms requires
advanced tooling, such as Natural Language Processing (NLP), Optical Character Recognition (OCR), and
standardized metadata tagging, each of which must be governed under rigorous data quality

VB-4. What are the essential data types needed for successful participation in value-based
care arrangements?
Successful participation in value-based care arrangements requires access to comprehensive, high-
quality data across several essential types:
1. Clinical Data: Includes patient diagnoses, lab results, medication histories, procedures, and
encounter notes. This data is critical for risk stratification, care management, and clinical quality
measurement.
2. Claims Data: Contains billing information, service utilization, and payment records. Claims data
supports cost analysis, utilization management, and outcome measurement.
3. Patient-Reported Outcomes: Data on patient health status, functional status, and satisfaction
collected directly from patients, which helps assess care effectiveness and quality from the
patient perspective.
4. Payment Data: Detailed information on reimbursements, incentives, shared savings, and
penalties. Payment data is essential for tracking financial flows, aligning incentives, and
evaluating the economic impact of value-based care.
5. Social Determinants of Health (SDOH): Information on factors such as housing, income,
education, and access to food or transportation, which impact health outcomes and care needs.
6. Care Coordination Data: Includes referral patterns, care team communications, and transitions
of care information necessary to manage patient journeys and reduce duplicative or fragmented
care.
7. Quality and Performance Metrics: Standardized measures related to clinical outcomes, patient
safety, and process adherence used to evaluate provider performance against value-based care
goals.
8. Financial Data: Cost and reimbursement data to assess spending relative to outcomes, enabling
value analysis and incentive calculations.
9. Behavioral Health Data: Information related to mental health diagnoses and treatments, as
integrated behavioral health is often a critical component in value-based care.
Together, these data types enable providers and payers to monitor performance, identify gaps, and
improve both clinical and financial outcomes in value-based care models.

This includes clearly defined data ownership, access policies, and data-use agreements that support
secure and compliant exchange of clinical, claims, pharmacy, and patient-generated health data.
Effective governance enables consistent population attribution, longitudinal tracking, and timely quality
reporting, core functions in any APM environment.
In parallel, APM organizations must prioritize data modernization to support real-time analytics and
scalable insights. This requires investment in cloud-native data platforms capable of ingesting,
normalizing, and transforming diverse datasets using standards. Automated data pipelines with
embedded identity resolution and data quality checks ensure consistency and traceability across
systems. These modern platforms enable organizations to merge EHR, claims, social determinants of
health (SDOH), and patient-reported data to generate actionable intelligence that supports proactive,
coordinated care.
Interoperability is another foundational requirement for APM success. While non-APM organizations
may rely solely on internal systems, APM participants must maintain bi-directional data exchange
capabilities across multiple networks and vendors. This includes publishing and consuming FHIR APIs,
integrating with TEFCA-aligned QHINs, and maintaining connections with state and regional Health
Information Exchanges (HIEs). Seamless data sharing across care settings allows providers to close care
gaps, reduce duplicative services, and improve the accuracy of performance measurement and risk
stratification.
Data quality is equally critical. APM organizations must implement continuous data quality assurance
processes, including validation rules, anomaly detection, and reconciliation across clinical and billing
systems. Inaccuracies in coding, documentation, or patient attribution can directly affect financial
reconciliation, shared savings distribution, and clinical performance scores. A strong data quality
program ensures that analytics are trusted, timely, and reflective of true care delivery patterns.
Finally, advanced analytics and reporting capabilities are essential to enable data-driven decision-making
and support clinical and operational excellence. APM organizations must leverage predictive analytics,
risk scoring, and real-time dashboards to empower care teams with insights that guide interventions,
reduce unnecessary utilization, and drive performance against CMS-defined metrics. These tools should
support automated quality reporting, benchmark comparisons, and care gap closure strategies that
improve outcomes while containing costs. Unlike non-APM entities, which may only report
retrospectively, APM organizations must sustain a proactive, intelligence-driven approach to care
management, powered by sophisticated, integrated data systems.
