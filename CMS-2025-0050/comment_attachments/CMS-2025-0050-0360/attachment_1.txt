Respondents to this RFI highlighted a desire for health management and care navigation apps that help understand conditions, coordinate care, and prompt next steps . Today’s apps often fall short on features and interoperability; for example, patients struggle to gather all their health information in one place and to know what actions to take . Data Access and Integration: A seamless, patient -centric digital ecosystem requires that patients and caregivers can easily access all of their health data in one place , without managing dozens of portals . Despite policy advances (HIPAA’s right of access, 21st Century Cures Act, and CMS Interoperability rules), the reality is that many patients still face fragmented records and cumbersome processes . CMS’s Blue Button 2.0 API made Medicare claims and coverage data available via FHIR®, spawning apps that let beneficiaries view claims history and out -of-pocket spending . However, Blue Button 2.0 currently offers only basic demographics, coverage, and Part A/B/D claims data . We recommend expanding Blue Button 2.0 to include additional data valuable for care management – for example, incorporating chronic condition indicators, lab results from claims (e.g. test codes and values when available), prior authorization or DME utilization data, and linking immunization or screening records. Developers have indicated that richer datasets would enable more useful patient -facing tools . CMS should also explore integrating Medicare Advantage and Medicaid data into a unified patient access API, so beneficiaries who transition between programs or have multiple coverage types see their information in one place. To handle the influx of data, CMS can leverage the United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) standard – a common set of data elements for nationwide exchange – ensuring that new data fields are standardized and developer -friendly. Another pillar of integration is harnessing Health Information Exchanges (HIEs) and TEFCA for patient access. The Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) has launched a nationwide network -of-networks to enable sharing of clinical data across disparate HIEs . Early progress is promising – as of 2024, thousands of organizations and over 14 million documents have been exchanged via TEFCA’s initial Qualified Health Information Networks . TEFCA now even supports Individual Access Services , meaning patients (or apps on their behalf) can retrieve health records through this network, not just providers . CMS should promote patient -facing uses of TEFCA by coordinating with ONC to certify or endorse apps that serve as patient gateways to TEFCA data. For example, an approved app could, with one login, pull a patient’s records from all TEFCA -connected providers – offering a longitudinal health record. This would fulfill the vision of a one-stop patient portal. HIEs can also play a role : many regional HIEs already aggregate data from hospitals, labs, and clinics; CMS could encourage HIEs to offer consumer access solutions or integrate with Medicare’s Blue Button data . To advance this, CMS might require that HIEs participating in Medicare value -based programs enable patient access as a condition of participation, or provide grant funding to HIEs developing patient/caregiver portals. Respondents noted that data from HIEs, when available, is valuable but can vary in accuracy and usability . CMS could work with ONC on data quality standards for HIE -shared data (e.g. reconciling duplicate records, ensuring timely updates) to boost trust in these sources. Hand in hand with identity is privacy and information blocking enforcement . CMS and ONC have made it clear that patients should be able to access their electronic health information without interference. Yet, awareness of how to report information blocking is low among the public. CMS should conduct outreach (through Medicare handbooks, patient advocates, etc.) to encourage patients and caregivers to report information blocking when they encounter providers or IT developers improperly restricting access . A simple, well -publicized online complaint portal (building on ONC’s existing portal) and protections against retaliation would empower consumers to speak up. More reports, if investigated by ONC, could spotlight problem areas and ultimately deter information blocking practices . Providers (PR) Encouraging Digital Health Adoption by Providers: Frontline healthcare providers are critical to integrating digital health solutions into patient care, yet many remain cautious. CMS can play a pivotal role in incentivizing providers to leverage approved digital products in clinical practice. For example, CMS might incorporate use of certified care management apps or remote monitoring tools into value -based payment models or MIPS improvement activities. In rural areas and smaller practices, lack of resources and awareness are key obstacles . We recommend that CMS expand technical assistance and grant programs to support adoption of digital health in underserved settings – similar to past ONC extension centers for EHRs. This could include template workflows for using patient apps (e.g. how to refer a patient to a vetted diabetes management app and monitor progress) and cybersecurity best practices for third -party integrations. Providers also need clarity on their responsibilities when recommending digital tools . CMS, in partnership with medical boards, should issue guidance delineating providers’ liability and oversight duties : for instance, if a clinician suggests an app, they should inform patients about data privacy and disclaim that the app is not a medical device unless FDA -approved. Providers should at minimum ensure any recommended app has appropriate evidence or certification (perhaps tied to a CMS - approved list as noted above). When providers do use digital tools in care, they should share relevant information with patients – e.g. explaining how an AI -driven decision support reached a recommendation. This transparency is vital to maintain trust in AI in healthcare. Streamlining Clinical Workflows and Reducing Burden: One of the biggest barriers providers face is the burden of data entry and quality reporting , which diverts time from patient care. Digital health solutions must reduce, not add to, provider workload. We urge CMS to focus policy on integration of apps into EHR workflows . For instance, apps that require separate logins or duplicate data entry discourage provider use. The adoption of standards like SMART on FHIR is key here. SMART on FHIR allows third -party apps to launch within the EHR interface and access data via secure FHIR APIs, using OAuth2 and OpenID Connect for authentication (pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov ). CMS should continue to enforce the 21st Century Cures Act requirement that certified EHRs support SMART on FHIR (the “standardized API” criterion) . Additionally, CMS could require EHR vendors to simplify the app onboarding process . Today, many hospitals face lengthy procedures to enable an app in their EHR, even if the app meets technical standards. Through conditions of participation or certification incentives, CMS/ONC could push for a more turnkey “app store” model for healthcare – where providers can plug in approved apps with minimal IT hassle. Interoperability and Data Exchange: Providers are increasingly expected to exchange data with other providers, patients, and payers. CMS has promoted FHIR API use cases in its regulations – e.g., patient access APIs, provider directory APIs, payer -to-payer exchange for care continuity. We encourage providers to adopt these and more. In fact, many leading health systems already support multiple FHIR APIs (for individual and bulk data) either directly or via intermediaries . However, smaller practices may not be aware or have not tapped into these capabilities. CMS should require EHR vendors (via ONC certification) to provide implementation toolkits to their provider clients for each of these standard APIs, explaining use cases like automating patient intake forms or enabling population health queries. Moreover, bulk FHIR data export is a game - changer for reducing manual reporting burden. We strongly support CMS’s exploration of using Bulk FHIR for quality reporting . Instead of each provider calculating quality measures and submitting through cumbersome processes, providers could simply export raw patient data (de - identified or as allowed) and let CMS perform the analytics centrally . Early tests of this approach are promising – an AI -based platform by ConcertAI showed that automating data retrieval from EHR and into research systems cut staff time significantly , reducing screening time per patient from ~41 minutes to ~12 minutes with no loss of accuracy . By relieving providers of double - documentation and manual uploads, such approaches let clinicians focus on care. We recommend CMS pilot a voluntary program where providers allow CMS to pull quality data via FHIR APIs (with proper privacy safeguards) and compare the effort and accuracy against current reporting. This could demonstrate savings in clinician hours and eventually be scaled to all of Medicare. Another way to assist providers is by aligning interoperability efforts with their existing quality workflows . For example, many providers already participate in registries or use electronic case reporting for public health. CMS could design interoperability requirements so that one action serves multiple purposes. If a provider sends standardized data to a clinical registry for patient care feedback, perhaps that same feed could satisfy a CMS quality measure submission, killing two birds with one stone . The key is to consolidate requirements and reuse data flows. We propose CMS convene a workgroup with providers, EHR developers, and measure stewards to identify overlaps between interoperability tasks and quality reporting tasks, aiming to simplify the total ask on providers .

CMS can amplify ONC’s reporting portal among providers and assure whistleblower -type protections. Payers (PA) Advancing Interoperability in Payer Systems: Health plans (including Medicare Advantage organizations and Medicaid plans) hold vast data that could improve care continuity if shared appropriately. CMS has led by example with initiatives like Blue Button 2.0 for claims and the Data at the Point of Care API (which allowed providers to retrieve Medicare FFS claims for their patients) . We recommend CMS continue to push payers to implement and utilize open APIs for patient and provider access. Specifically, CMS should expand regulatory requirements (from the CMS Interoperability and Patient Access final rule) to all Medicare Advantage and Qualified Health Plans to not only maintain a Patient Access API but also a Provider Access API and a Payer -to- Payer exchange API for seamless data exchange . Payers should make claims, encounter, and formulary data available in real time to members and treating providers. Moreover, CMS might consider hosting a sandbox or gateway that simplifies how providers query multiple payers’ data – essentially a “universal payer API” endpoint that routes requests to the right plan, potentially via TEFCA or a centralized directory of payer FHIR endpoints. Digital Identity and Security (Technical Perspectives): From a technology vendor or HIE perspective, implementing digital identity solutions raises practical questions. The consensus is that modern API ecosystems need strong, federated identity for security and convenience . Requiring digital identity credentials (such as NIST IAL2 verified) for data access could enhance cybersecurity by reducing weak passwords and tying access to verified individuals . Using OpenID Connect (OIDC) as the standard protocol for identity federation is advisable – it builds on OAuth2 and is widely adopted, meaning many developers and products already support it. Mandating OIDC across Medicare APIs and networks would ensure compatibility and ease integration . Potential downsides that tech stakeholders note include the overhead of managing these identity services and ensuring equitable access (some patients without smartphones or photo IDs might struggle to obtain digital credentials). To mitigate that, multiple options (government -provided login like Login.gov, as well as private like ID.me or state ID services) should be accepted to give choice . Overall, technology vendors favor clear, uniform standards – we recommend CMS/ONC formally adopt OpenID Connect for any patient or provider -facing authentication in CMS programs. This could be done via rulemaking or guidance that any API requiring login must support OIDC with a set of certified Identity Providers. This approach echoes how SMART on FHIR already relies on OIDC for authentication tokens , and expanding it will strengthen the trust framework. Open Standards vs. Proprietary APIs: Many EHR vendors and data companies historically offered proprietary APIs, which fragmented integration efforts. CMS should firmly encourage use of open, standards -based APIs . One lever is through certification and payment incentives: CMS could require that, to participate in Medicare (as an ACO, EHR for Meaningful Use, etc.), any data exchange must use standards unless an exception is justified. Another idea is a compliance scorecard published by CMS or ONC that shows which vendors use proprietary interfaces versus standardized FHIR for various functions – essentially public shaming to drive conformity. The benefits of open APIs (lower integration cost, vendor -agnostic apps, easier data aggregation) far outweigh the effort of standardization. For instance, had lab systems universally adopted LOINC and FHIR, the industry would have been better positioned to aggregate COVID -19 data quickly. CMS should coordinate with ONC to expand required API capabilities in certification (e.g., not just patient data APIs but scheduling APIs, or bulk data APIs for all vendors). If a vendor must choose between maintaining a custom API or using FHIR that meets CMS policy, they will lean towards the latter to avoid losing customers or certifications. Additionally, CMS should consider the sustainability of network infrastructure . Operating national networks or directories has costs; policies might be needed to ensure that smaller HIEs or new entrants aren’t shut out due to high fees. A possible model is a tiered fee structure or federal support for core interoperability utilities to ensure broad participation . Another consideration is rationalizing overlapping networks and standards . Many tech stakeholders are concerned about multiple parallel channels (e.g., direct messaging, FHIR point - to-point, various HIE frameworks, and now TEFCA) . Redundancy can waste resources and confuse implementers. CMS, with ONC, could convene a summit to map out which standards/channels best fit which use cases and where consolidation might help. For instance, if TEFCA can handle certain queries, perhaps CMS could retire or de -emphasize older mechanisms (like certain direct trust exchanges) to focus industry effort. Conversely, if some innovative protocol emerges (like a secure RESTful push for event notifications), CMS can highlight that as preferred. The end goal should be an “API unification” where a handful of well -defined interoperable services cover most needs, reducing the need to implement dozens of interfaces . USCDI and Beyond – Data Content Standards: The USCDI provides a core set of data elements, and it has grown over time (now including more social and behavioral data, for example). Tech developers generally appreciate USCDI’s role but also report limitations. Many find that USCDI does not yet encompass all data they need – for example, genomic data, advanced imaging, or certain patient -reported outcomes are not fully represented. CMS asked whether adding more elements to USCDI would help or create scope creep . We suggest a balanced approach: incrementally expand USCDI in areas that directly impact care coordination and quality, but accompany expansions with implementation guidance to avoid confusion. High -value additions could include: device data (e.g. blood glucose readings), more granular mental health data, and data relevant to post -acute care. On the other hand, USCDI might not need to include everything if other methods can supply it. The RFI intriguingly mentions if a less structured but more comprehensive format would be preferred given improved language processing . This could hint at using NLP on clinical notes (essentially exchanging free text plus metadata). There is a place for that: not all clinical insight fits into discrete fields. A possible recommendation is for CMS/ONC to pilot a hybrid data exchange : sending core USCDI discrete data plus an unstructured summary (like a Continuity of Care Document or even a plain text clinical note) to ensure nothing is lost. With modern AI, the receiver could extract needed info from the text, addressing gaps in discrete data coverage . This aligns with vendor feedback that while structured data is ideal, it’s better to receive something (even free text) than nothing for a given concept. The RFI asks if certification should evolve to prioritize API -enabled capabilities over specific feature functions . We agree that certification should increasingly test for interoperability performance – i.e., can the system share and receive data effectively – rather than just internal features. For instance, an EHR’s ability to allow bulk export of all data (including notes and images) via API is more impactful for system interoperability than whether it has an internal report printing module. Similarly, certification should ensure EHI (Electronic Health Information) export capabilities move toward standardization. Data Exchange Networks and Compliance: Many networks (HIEs, vendor networks, QHINs) exist, and their connectivity varies. The RFI probes how networks use FHIR today, how many endpoints they connect, etc., to understand the landscape . One trend is the shift from older HL7 v2 and IHE - based exchange to FHIR -based query models. CMS should support network efforts to modernize with FHIR . For example, CMS could fund grants for HIEs to add FHIR API layers on top of their networks, making them more accessible to FHIR apps and national exchange. On network scalability, an open question is the tradeoff between point -to-point integrations vs. shared networks . The proliferation of direct FHIR connections (each app connecting to each provider’s API) can be hard to manage; conversely, a large network like TEFCA concentrates the connections but can be bureaucratic. A hybrid likely works: encourage point -to-point for simple bilateral data sharing (patient to single provider) and use networks for broad queries (patient to all providers). CMS can help by ensuring policies align : e.g., a provider fulfilling a patient’s access request could do so either by enabling a third -party app (point -to-point) or via an Individual Access Services QHIN (network), and both should satisfy compliance. Clear guidance on such equivalence can help avoid redundant implementations . Regarding information blocking from the tech vendor side , ONC handles certification and potential penalties for health IT developers. But CMS can complement that by requiring transparent reporting of any interfaces or practices that restrict interoperability . Tech vendors reported various forms of information blocking, from exorbitant fees for interfaces to non - responsiveness to integration requests . CMS could, for instance, mandate that certified EHRs annually attest to their connectivity (how many API calls served, how many third -party apps connected, etc.). This data could flag if a vendor is making it unreasonably difficult for outside apps to connect (an indicator of possible blocking). We also support CMS and ONC examining if certain actor categories (perhaps some specialty HIEs or third -party intermediaries) fall through the cracks of current enforcement and need new disincentives .

Value -Based Care Organizations (VB) Digital Health Adoption in Value -Based Care: Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs) and other Alternative Payment Models (APMs) have strong motivation to keep patients healthy and out of the hospital. Digital health tools can be a force -multiplier in these efforts by enabling proactive, continuous care. Yet, many ACOs underutilize tools like remote monitoring, AI risk stratification, or patient engagement apps. To encourage adoption, CMS should incorporate technology use into APM incentive structures . For example, offer a modest shared savings bonus or advanced payment to ACOs that deploy approved digital health management tools to a certain percentage of their high -risk patients . CMS could also relax certain reporting requirements for ACOs that show they are using real -time data tools effectively – essentially trading paperwork for proven tech -enabled management. Current obstacles include upfront costs, uncertain ROI, and integration challenges. CMS’s new $15 million initiative with Massive Bio, recognized under the Cancer Moonshot, illustrates a path: by investing in AI -driven trial matching and remote pre - screening hubs, it aims to reach 50,000+ new patients and scale to 250,000 by 2027 . This “hub - and-spoke” model, combining AI analytics with decentralized trial participation, is funded because it promises improved outcomes and equity . CMS could create a Digital Health Adoption Program granting similar seed funds or implementation support to ACOs for telehealth, RPM, or AI tools that target Medicare’s biggest cost drivers (heart failure, diabetes, etc.). The return on investment can be high: as one study showed, a remote monitoring program in a Medicare population reduced hospitalizations by 59%, saving an estimated $12 million in a couple of years . If ACOs see that kind of tangible benefit, they will eagerly adopt these technologies. Another lever is integrating key tech capabilities into APM requirements or scoring . CMS asks how AI, analytics, care coordination, etc., can be better integrated into APMs . One idea: in ACO quality measures or in the Innovation Center’s model requirements, include measures of digital engagement (e.g., percentage of patients on remote monitoring, or having electronic care plans). Alternatively, CMS could require an ACO’s care coordination strategy to explicitly address how they use data analytics and patient -facing tech – making it part of the application and evaluation for programs like the Shared Savings Program. Essential Health IT Capabilities for Value -Based Care: Successful value -based care relies on effective population health management . Certain IT capabilities are now practically required: care planning tools that span settings, event notifications for transitions of care, patient matching across providers, data normalization for combining EHR data, and easy access to claims and cost data . CMS should ensure that these capabilities are either provided or facilitated for ACOs. For example, CMS could extend its Beneficiary Claims Data API (as used in CPC+ or other models) so that any ACO can retrieve near -real-time claims updates on their assigned beneficiaries. This helps identify care outside the network or gaps. Additionally, patient event notifications (like ADT alerts) are so critical that CMS already mandated hospitals share them (CoPs) . Addressing those gaps (maybe via a CMS -sponsored intermediary or requiring participation in a state HIE) would close the loop. Massive Bio’s model underscores some innovative capabilities that could translate to broader value -based care: their use of genomic data and AI to stratify patients for trials is akin to advanced risk stratification in population health . CMS can foster this by sharing more data (as above) and by clarifying that using predictive analytics is encouraged and fits under permissible uses of data. Another key capability is patient engagement and empowerment tools – e.g., patient portals or apps that encourage medication adherence, symptom reporting, etc. ACOs may struggle to get patients using them. CMS could partner with patient advocacy groups to create template engagement materials for ACOs to give to patients, highlighting the value of these digital tools in managing their health. Data Needs for VBC: Data drives value -based success. APM entities need a wide array of data: not just medical claims, but pharmacy, social determinants, behavioral health, and often functional status data. CMS should ask ACOs each year what data they lack. Likely answers include real-time notification of healthcare use (ER visits, urgent care), Medicaid data for their dual -eligibles, and data from outside providers. Efforts like combining Medicare and Medicaid data in the QRS (quality reporting system) are helpful, but more integration is needed. We recommend CMS accelerate projects like the CMS Data at the Point of Care pilot – expanding it so that any value - based provider can query CMS for claims histories of their patients. Also, provide bulk data extracts of targeted populations to ACOs (e.g., all dual -eligible patients’ Medicare and Medicaid claims in one file). Interoperability is sometimes harder in VBC because these organizations span multiple providers and EHR systems. This is why data standardization and easy exchange is so important. ACOs often set up data warehouses to collect from members; CMS can ease this by continuing to refine standards like FHIR Bulk Data and standard Quality Improvement Core (QI -Core) profiles for common metrics. Standardizing the data that flows in APMs will reduce custom integration costs. Certification and Standards to Support VBC: The ONC Certification Program today doesn’t explicitly address whether an EHR is “good for ACOs,” but perhaps it should. The RFI asks if current certification criteria support value -based care delivery and what more is needed . One gap is that certification has focused on individual patient record exchange and specific criteria (like transitions of care docs), but value -based care needs population -level and analytics capabilities . For instance, it would help if certified EHRs were required to have a bulk export feature (for panel management) – which, in fact, the Cures Act API criteria implicitly encourages. Also, data quality and completeness standards in certification would help ACOs: if an EHR is certified, we should trust that it captures key data like problem lists, medications, etc., in structured form that can be exported. Perhaps certification could include a “VBC readiness” test scenario : e.g., demonstrate that a provider can extract all diabetic patients and their last A1c values via the system’s API. If not, that’s a gap to fix.

Reducing Complexity and Preserving Flexibility: Value -based programs involve diverse types of organizations, from small physician -led ACOs to large integrated systems. Tech requirements must be right -sized. CMS has wisely allowed some flexibility (e.g., smaller ACOs may not have the same resources as bigger ones). We should preserve that by perhaps offering multiple pathways to meet a requirement . For example, if an ACO is required to notify patients’ primary care docs of hospital admissions, a large system might do it via an integrated EHR notification, whereas a small practice ACO might use a service from their HIE. CMS can set the outcome -oriented requirement (ensure 90% of admissions result in notification) but let the org choose tech solutions. CMS also references the Physician Fee Schedule 2024 rule allowing CEHRT (Certified EHR Technology) requirements in advanced APMs to be more flexible based on practice area . We support tailoring tech requirements: for instance, an oncology -focused model might need EHRs to handle genomic data, whereas a primary care model might emphasize e -prescribing and referral exchange. CMS should consult with each APM cohort to identify specific needs and then possibly provide a list of acceptable alternative tech solutions . If a small or rural provider cannot implement a full EHR module, maybe a smaller certified app that plugs in could suffice for compliance – this could lower the barrier to entry for resource -constrained participants . We must ensure flexibility doesn’t equal lack of interoperability though; any alternative should still adhere to core data standards. Overcoming Interoperability Challenges in VBC: Many ACOs report issues like difficulty matching patients across different EHRs, lag in data reporting (claims often arrive months later), and incompatibility between systems. A specific challenge is that post -acute facilities (SNFs, home health) and behavioral health providers often are outside the main data streams. CMS can aid by expanding programs like event notification requirements to more settings (as hinted in the RFI about hospital CoP notifications vs. APM participants receiving them ). If a patient is in an ACO, any hospital admission notification should reach the ACO care team, not just a community provider. Perhaps CMS could use its authority to require that ACOs (or their participants) be included in the notification routing (this might be achieved via the provider directory concept – an ACO could register as an interested party for certain patients). Another approach: CMS could stand up a centralized ACO data hub that all ACOs can plug into. For example, Medicare could push claims or utilize a shared FHIR repository where each ACO can see their patients’ Medicare utilization in near -real-time. This would mitigate the need for each ACO building custom data pipelines.

We urge CMS to take bold but thoughtful actions: fund what works, regulate where needed to ensure fair data access, and always center policy on improving patient care and health equity. With these steps, the vision of a seamless, secure, and innovative health ecosystem for all Medicare beneficiaries is within reach.
