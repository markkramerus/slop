To better support public sector and community innovation and whole-person care, we recommend expanding the Blue Button 2.0 API to include behavioral health, health-related social needs (HRSN), Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS)/waiver services, and dual-eligible context, with robust consent frameworks and transparent data source metadata. PC-10. How is the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement™ (TEFCA™) currently helping to advance patient access to health information in the real world? While TEFCA holds promise for advancing widespread patient and provider access to health information, this potential remains largely unrealized in practice, and adoption has been limited. Despite substantial federal investment, actual exchange volumes remain limited. Although TEFCA's early-stage implementation contributes to current limitations, the primary barrier lies in patient identity verification challenges and a lack of adoption of non-Treatment use cases that fundamentally undermine the framework's value proposition. The Core Challenge: Authentication Without Compromising User Experience Many providers and EHR vendors express legitimate concerns about granting patient access to information via TEFCA without robust authentication tied to known healthcare provider credentials. The prevailing workaround (using TEFCA for record location while requiring patients to authenticate separately to each identified organization via SMART on FHIR) significantly diminishes TEFCA's intended value for patient access by creating friction that defeats the purpose of a unified framework. Current Solutions Fall Short Alternative approaches that focus on requiring patient access service providers to validate patient identity to rigorous standards before facilitating record requests address only half the challenge. Even with high confidence that an incoming request from "John Smith" authentically originates from the claimed individual, organizations still face the critical challenge of accurately matching that verified request to the correct "John Smith" within their systems—a problem that high-assurance identity verification alone cannot solve. These issues result in higher-than-expected error rates and poor provider and patient experience. 
4 A Path Forward: Integrated Identity Solutions with Security Considerations One promising approach involves linking patients' EHR records to verified identities from established providers such as CLEAR, ID.me, or other Kantara-certified identity assurance services, then leveraging these same verified identities for patient access requests. However, this approach requires careful consideration of security limitations inherent in identity providers themselves. Identity-based attacks are increasingly common and particularly challenging to detect when valid user credentials have been compromised, and an adversary is masquerading as that user. Zero trust architecture, while valuable, is not a silver bullet for these security challenges, as it still relies on the fundamental integrity of identity verification systems that can be compromised through sophisticated attack vectors. Moreover, widespread adoption faces significant implementation challenges, including the need for both health systems and EHR vendors to support these processes, creating multiple points of friction that hinder patient adoption. PC-11. How are health information exchanges (HIEs) currently helping to advance patient access to health information in the real world? Well-coordinated HIEs are demonstrably transforming healthcare delivery by simplifying care coordination through better communication and real-time data sharing. For example, a patient's comprehensive medical history can be accessed during emergency visits or specialist consultations, leading to safer, more informed clinical decisions. With accurate visibility into a patient's medical history, providers can avoid ordering duplicate tests and procedures by accessing recent labs, imaging, and treatment notes. Ultimately, health and healthcare are local activities, and local context, partnerships, and data are critical for meeting the “last mile” of data exchange and health improvement. Proven Clinical and Economic Benefits Research consistently demonstrates HIE's tangible impact on patient outcomes and healthcare efficiency. Studies show that hospitals participating in an HIE significantly reduced 30-day readmission rates, compared with those that don't participate, with researchers attributing these results to better access to patient information across different healthcare organizations, enabling more informed decision-making that facilitates care coordination. Additionally, hospitals utilizing HIEs had more timely access to critical patient data, resulting in decreased intensive care unit (ICU) admissions. Cost savings are equally compelling. HIE was associated with a reduction of total costs of care, lab test costs, imaging test costs, and overall measures of return on investment. Systematic reviews confirm that low-quality evidence generally finds that HIE reduces duplicative laboratory and radiology testing, emergency department costs, hospital admissions, and improves public health reporting, ambulatory quality of care. 
5 Population Health and Public Health Surveillance When aggregated with other sources, HIE data proves valuable for identifying health trends, managing chronic disease populations, and supporting public health surveillance. The potential benefits of HIE on healthcare have fostered its implementation and adoption in the United States, with particular strength in facilitating population health management initiatives. National Infrastructure and Scale The national member organization Civitas Networks for Health represents 63 regional and statewide HIEs covering 45 states and the District of Columbia, with a shared mission of working with local communities to achieve improved health outcomes at a lower cost. HIE members work to improve care coordination and care management across healthcare systems and communities, enable more informed clinical decisions, and reduce hospital readmissions and other preventable expenditures such as unnecessary or duplicative tests and procedures. These state and regional organizations provide the “last mile” of connectivity, feeding and enhancing the data exchange available through national health information networks. Most state and regional HIEs already connect to national networks and align with interoperability frameworks. Limitations in Patient-Centered Access However, significant challenges remain in maximizing HIE's potential for patient access. While HIEs in general (national and state/regional) have successfully demonstrated value in business-to-business healthcare information exchange between providers, they have been primarily designed around provider-to-provider workflows rather than direct patient access. The same patient identity verification and authentication challenges that have limited TEFCA's patient access capabilities also affect state and local HIEs. While the infrastructure exists to support improved care coordination and clinical decision-making among healthcare organizations, broader patient-directed access capabilities require continued development to fully realize HIE's promise of truly patient-centered health information availability. 3. 6 C. Providers 1. Digital Health Apps PR-2. What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective utilization of the most useful and innovative applications for physician workflows, such as quality measurement reporting, clinical documentation, and billing tasks? How could these obstacles be mitigated? Technical Integration and Workflow Fragmentation The primary obstacle to innovative physician workflow applications lies in the complexity of integrating with existing EHR systems and resulting workflow fragmentation. A multi-center study published in the Journal of the American Medical Informatics Association found eight-fold differences in clicks required for identical tasks across healthcare systems, with error rates for basic functions like medication ordering ranging from zero to thirty percen¹. This variability is part of a broader usability crisis: a national study of 5,197 physicians found EHRs scored 45.9 out of 100 on standardized usability measures - an "F" grade compared to Google Search (93) or Microsoft Excel (57). Poor EHR design creates cascading barriers to innovation: developers must navigate fragmented, vendor-specific integration points while accommodating workflows that vary dramatically even within the same healthcare system. Each 1-point improvement in EHR usability correlates with 3% lower physician burnout odds, demonstrating how technical design failures directly impact both innovation adoption and clinician wellbeing. Current EHR systems often lack standardized APIs for third-party applications, forcing developers to create custom integrations for each vendor platform. This fragmentation prevents scalable deployment of innovative solutions and increases development costs significantly. Additionally, many healthcare organizations struggle with inconsistent data formats and limited interoperability between systems, making it difficult for innovative applications to access the information needed to streamline workflows. Innovaccer has more than a decade of experience integrating with multiple complex data sources for the healthcare enterprise, including EHRs and other systems of records across the enterprise. Mitigation strategies include accelerating adoption of standardized FHIR-based APIs with comprehensive implementation guidance, encouraging EHR vendors to develop more accessible developer programs with reduced integration barriers, and supporting healthcare organizations in adopting workflow optimization tools that can identify and address inefficient EHR usage patterns within their systems. Documentation Burden and Productivity Paradoxes A significant barrier to workflow innovation adoption stems from the paradox that many efficiency-improving applications can reduce measurable productivity metrics in fee-for-service environments. Physicians now spend up to twice as much time interacting with EHR systems as they do with patients, with recent data showing physicians spent 28.4 more minutes daily in EHRs in 2023 compared to 2022—an 8% year-over-year increase. Nearly 69% of primary care 
7 physicians report that most EHR clerical tasks they complete do not require physician-level training. Innovative applications that automate documentation, streamline coding, or reduce administrative tasks, including with AI copilots and agentic AI, such as the tools Innovaccer offers, may paradoxically reduce billable encounters or documentation volume in ways that negatively impact current quality metrics or productivity measurements. This creates disincentives for adoption even when these tools improve actual patient care efficiency and physician satisfaction. VBC adoption continues to grow, as reported by the National Association of ACOs and Innovaccer, but vast areas of the US healthcare system still operate under fee-for-service arrangements that don’t offer a clear path to pay for technology innovation without adding billing codes. Mitigation strategies include developing quality metrics that account for efficiency gains and care coordination improvements rather than purely volume or process-based measures, creating demonstration projects that measure patient outcomes and physician satisfaction. 2. Digital Identity PR-9. How might CMS encourage providers to accept digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, ID.me, Login.gov) from patients and their partners instead of proprietary logins that need to be tracked for each provider relationship? Current Challenges and CMS' Role Currently, many providers rely on proprietary patient portals and logins that create unnecessary complexity for patients and administrative burdens for providers. While digital identity technologies are not perfect, the current fragmented approach is demonstrably less secure and efficient. CMS can serve as a catalyst by addressing technical and operational barriers while balancing patient privacy with improved access needs. Technical and Implementation Support Providers need assistance integrating third-party identity verification systems into existing EHRs and patient portals. CMS can drive this by updating interoperability requirements under ONC's Health IT Certification Program to include support for federated identity protocols such as OpenID Connect and OAuth2. Additionally, CMS should provide implementation guides, pilot programs, and case studies such as the Department of Veterans Affairs' successful use of ID.me to help providers navigate the transition. 
10 Trust Framework and Legal Clarity CMS should collaborate with NIST and ASTP/ONC to define appropriate identity assurance levels for healthcare and establish a formal trust framework for credential providers. Creating legal safe harbors for providers who use CMS-approved identity services would reduce organizational hesitancy around adoption and liability concerns. Privacy and Equity Considerations Digital identity systems must follow privacy-by-design principles, including HIPAA and HITECH Act compliance, while enabling granular consent management. Recognizing unequal access to digital tools, CMS should support equitable solutions such as in-person identity verification at trusted locations including pharmacies and community health centers, multilingual interfaces, and Section 508 accessibility compliance. Broader Digital Health Integration Standardized digital identity credentials can enable access not only to clinical portals but also to wellness apps, wearables, and digital therapeutics, supporting data portability and patient empowerment in alignment with TEFCA goals. Patient education campaigns explaining the benefits of reusable digital identity credentials are critical for building public trust and digital literacy. CMS can modernize patient identity management in healthcare by standardizing trusted digital credentials through technical guidance, trust frameworks, and emphasis on privacy and equity. This approach would reduce fragmentation, simplify patient access, and support broader adoption of digital health innovations. PR-10. Regarding digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs): Challenges and Benefits for Providers Digital identity credentials meeting NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 standards offer significant benefits by reducing reliance on multiple proprietary logins that are costly to maintain and burdensome for patients and staff. Information Blocking (General Response for PR-12, 13, and 14) Current State and Needed Evolution While current information blocking regulations have begun to unlock electronic health information, the healthcare ecosystem increasingly requires bidirectional data exchange capabilities. As noted in recent enforcement activities, behavior rather than technology remains the primary impediment to effective health information sharing. Expanding Information Blocking to Include CRUD Operations The information blocking framework should evolve to encompass full CRUD (Create, Read, Update, Delete) capabilities within APIs. Current regulations focus primarily on data retrieval (Read operations), but modern healthcare workflows require comprehensive data interaction capabilities across systems. This includes scenarios such as: 
12 ● Primary care providers, patients, or caregivers correcting faulty data (Update operations) ● Third-party applications creating new care plans or medication adherence records (Create operations) ● Specialists contributing consultation notes back to primary care records (Create operations) ● Patients or caregivers adding patient-generated health data (Create operations) ● Care teams updating patient status or care plan modifications (Update operations) Restricting CRUD access when technically feasible could constitute a form of information blocking that limits care coordination and patient engagement just as significantly as read-only restrictions. The framework should recognize that effective interoperability requires bidirectional data exchange supporting all CRUD operations necessary for comprehensive care management. Provider Engagement and Complaint Reporting Encouraging providers to submit information blocking complaints requires clear education about what constitutes blocking behavior across all CRUD operations, streamlined reporting processes, and demonstrated follow-through on investigations. Increased reporting would help identify systemic barriers to comprehensive data access, enabling more targeted policy responses. Rather than mandating complaint submission, CMS should focus on creating safe reporting environments and ensuring transparency in complaint resolution processes that address both read and write access limitations. Data Exchange TD-15. Regarding bulk FHIR APIs: Please see our responses in PR-5 for additional details on the weakness of the current state of bulk FHIR implementation. Improved Use Cases and Data Flow Increased use of bulk FHIR would enable interoperable population health use cases at scale, addressing current limitations in population health interoperability. Non-bulk FHIR is inherently patient-specific and cannot efficiently handle dynamic cohorts needed for population health management. Current bulk FHIR implementations also lack the ability for consumers to fetch only "updated" data, requiring full dataset refreshes that create unnecessary data transfer overhead and limit real-time population health monitoring capabilities. Properly implemented bulk FHIR would support the rapid iteration and measurement cycles essential for modern healthcare innovation, enabling weekly or monthly population health assessments rather than annual reporting cycles. This aligns with the need to move beyond traditional constraints that have limited healthcare technology innovation. Potential Disadvantages and Implementation Challenges The primary disadvantage stems from architectural decisions made by EHR vendors. Most EHRs operate with separate application and reporting data layers, having built their patient access FHIR APIs on top of the application layer due to low data volumes. When implementing bulk FHIR, vendors face two options: building on their existing FHIR infrastructure or rebuilding to utilize their reporting data layer. Most EHRs have chosen the first approach because it's cheaper to build, but this creates significant operational risks when running bulk FHIR against production resources. Consequently, many EHRs have artificially limited bulk FHIR to very small cohorts—often 
14 around 1,000 patients—which defeats the purpose of bulk access for population health use cases. Technical Architecture Recommendations EHRs should build bulk FHIR capabilities against their reporting data layers to handle larger patient volumes safely and efficiently. This architectural approach would separate bulk operations from production clinical workflows while enabling the scale necessary for meaningful population health analysis. Governance Framework Considerations While building bulk FHIR capabilities against reporting layers is essential, EHR vendors have legitimate concerns about allowing unfettered external access even to reporting systems. A governance framework establishing enforceable operational requirements and limitations will be necessary to balance access with system stability and security. Although TEFCA could theoretically provide this governance, the bulk FHIR use case doesn't align well with TEFCA's QHIN-oriented model. A lighter-weight, focused governance forum specifically designed for bulk FHIR operations would likely be more effective (such as an existing industry collaboration that has recently formed) and should be convened separately to address the unique technical and operational requirements of population-scale data access. TD-16. What are the tradeoffs of maintaining point-to-point models vs. shared network infrastructure? Network Models and Scalable Participation Current rules generally encourage scalable network participation, but the framework for understanding network models should be more nuanced than a simple point-to-point versus shared infrastructure dichotomy. There are four distinct approaches to health information exchange: ● Pure Point-to-Point: Direct connections between two systems that are technically and contractually independent of any other connections. ● Governance-Only Network: Point-to-point connections that are technically independent but share contractual governance with similar connections (example: Carequality). ● Network with Shared Infrastructure: Connections route through a central hub while still being sent and received by individual participant systems, with shared contractual governance supporting network components (example: CommonWell). ● Full Data Replication: Participating systems feed data to centralized network structures that provide access to other participants through query-based or proactive routing (example: many state/regional HIEs). Key Tradeoffs The primary tradeoff of shared network models is the lack of real-time, synchronous data exchange capabilities. Current networks require lengthy data reconciliation processes that limit 
15 their effectiveness for time-sensitive clinical workflows. This conflicts with the need for rapid iteration and real-time measurement capabilities essential for modern healthcare innovation. Alignment Improvements Point-to-point models would be more workable with enhanced ASTP/ONC requirements governing how EHRs must accept data via APIs. Current limitations include insufficient API support for essential functions like clinical note creation, or excessive fees when API support does exist. These barriers prevent the bidirectional data exchange necessary for comprehensive care coordination. Optimal Framework Selection Each approach may be optimal in different circumstances. The middle approaches—governance-only networks and networks with shared infrastructure—offer important alternatives to the extremes of millions of individual point-to-point connections or full data replication. Where stakeholder needs and system capabilities are met through governance-only or shared infrastructure approaches, costs can be significantly reduced relative to full data replication models while maintaining necessary flexibility and real-time capabilities. The key is ensuring that regulatory frameworks support multiple models rather than forcing artificial constraints that limit innovation and efficiency in health information exchange. TD-17. Given operational costs, what role should CMS or ASTP/ONC or both have in ensuring viability of healthcare data sharing networks, including enough supply and demand, that results in usage and outcomes? Policy-Driven Network Selection, Not Network Promotion Setting aside legislative requirements that led to TEFCA's creation, CMS and ASTP/ONC's role should not be to promote or support specific data sharing networks as policy objectives in their own right. Rather, where policy objectives require information sharing among healthcare stakeholders, agencies should determine which of the four network approaches from our response to TD-16 (pure point-to-point, governance-only networks, networks with shared infrastructure, or full data replication) best fits the specific policy circumstances and goals. Ecosystem Solutions Aligned with Policy Goals It is appropriate for CMS to prioritize ecosystem solutions that effectively meet its policy objectives, provided this approach doesn't completely exclude other market-chosen solutions that satisfy regulatory requirements. This balanced approach fosters innovation while ensuring policy goals are achieved through the most effective means available. Practical Application: Prior Authorization APIs CMS has established payer API requirements for prior authorization, with policy goals including greater transparency and reduced administrative costs. However, these APIs currently require point-to-point connections, which may limit actual adoption by consumers and providers despite payers meeting their regulatory obligations. To facilitate adoption and advance the original policy objectives, CMS may find that some network approach becomes necessary. 
16 In this specific example, a full data replication model would be unnecessarily complex and costly, while a governance framework—possibly enhanced with network-level service infrastructure—would likely be sufficient to achieve transparency and cost reduction goals. CMS could appropriately incentivize participation in such a network solely to promote the underlying policy objective, rather than to promote network participation as an end in itself. Balanced Regulatory Framework This approach allows CMS to leverage ecosystem efficiencies where they serve policy goals while maintaining regulatory flexibility. Organizations should remain free to choose alternative approaches that meet the same regulatory requirements, ensuring that market innovation isn't stifled by overly prescriptive network mandates. The key is aligning network selection with specific policy outcomes rather than promoting particular network models for their own sake. Operational Efficiency and Policy Outcomes By focusing on policy outcomes rather than network promotion, CMS can achieve operational efficiency while supporting the diverse innovation ecosystem that drives healthcare technology advancement. This approach recognizes that different policy objectives may require different network approaches, and that regulatory frameworks should remain flexible enough to adapt to evolving technology capabilities and market solutions. F. Value-Based Care Organizations 1. Digital Health Adoption VB-1. What incentives could encourage APMs such as accountable care organizations (ACOs) or participants in Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) to leverage digital health management and care navigation products more often and more effectively with their patients? What are the current obstacles preventing broader digital product adoption for patients in ACOs? Current Adoption Barriers 2025 research from the National Association of Accountable Care Organizations (NAACOS) and Innovaccer reveals significant obstacles to digital health adoption among ACOs. Financial risk represents the top barrier for 87% of respondents, followed by provider readiness (80%), lack of interoperability (75%), and high technology costs (67%). These findings demonstrate that despite 70% expressing optimism about AI's role in enabling predictive analytics and scaling value-based care strategies, ACOs face substantial implementation challenges. EHR Structural Limitations A fundamental obstacle is the limited capability of electronic health records to support proactive, personalized patient engagement.
