Why This Is a Systemic Issue • These measures: o Don't allow patient refusal as a valid denominator exclusion o Use “gap closure” logic enforced by payers, not clinicians • Even documented informed refusal (e.g., “Patient refuses statin due to side effects”) does not protect the provider or plan from a quality penalty • Patients remain in the denominator and count against compliance scores Consequences • Providers experience ethical conflict and pressure to override patient preference • Plans , care delivery organizations, managed care contracts often run automated outreach, “gap” reports, and incentive programs to push providers harder • Vulnerable populations (e.g., women, immigrant communities, people with prior trauma or vaccine hesitancy) may be over -targeted • Providers are burned out, and patients lose trust The hospital transition -of-care measures (e.g., those tracking follow -up visits, medication reconciliation, or outreach within 7 –30 days of discharge) often don’t account for patient refusal — meaning: • If a patient declines follow -up care, doesn’t answer calls, or refuses to schedule a visit, • The provider or hospital may still be penalized for “failure to transition care appropriately,” • Even when every effort was made on the clinician’s end. CMS should formally recognize that patient empowerment includes the right to refuse preventive screenings and treatments , and that such refusals when properly documented should serve as valid denominator exclusions in quality measurement logic. Member Attribution Logic versus Actual Provider Access to the Patient CMS should allow providers and health plans to flag beneficiaries for removal from attribution or quality measure denominators when multiple documented outreach attempts (calls, letters, portal messages) are unsuccessful , or when patients refuse care, relocate , or experience access barriers. Structured FHIR -based reporting of outreach and refusal data should be incorporated into attribution logic and digital quality measure calculations to create fairness and transparency in value -based care models. Providers should not be penalized in quality scores or star ratings for patients they cannot reach, especially when they are attributed retroactively and outreach efforts are clearly documented. A FHIR -based, structured outreach and contact status reportin g mechanism should be introduced to enable transparency and fairness across value -based programs. This would create parity between actual patient engagement capacity and quality accountability, and relieve provider burden in underserved or high -risk populations. 
Many experienced physicians, including those deeply committed to quality care, have chosen early retirement or opted out of value -based contracts. A major factor has been the disconnect between rigid quality logic and the real -world dynamics of patient behavior, access, and care refusal. CMS must modernize its attribution and quality measurement rules to recognize unsuccessful outreach, documented refusals, and access barriers as valid exclusions. This change would help retain experienced clinicians, reduce burnout, reduce burden, and preserve trust in value -based care models. While CMS explores the role of patients, providers, technology vendors, and value -based care models, there is little clarity on how patient refusals of remote monitoring or preventive services should impact provider performance scores. This raises concerns about fairness in compliance and quality programs. I recommend CMS develop guidance that acknowledges patient choice in quality scoring systems.
