Introduction
CareMessage is a nonprofit leveraging technology to improve health outcomes for low-income
patient populations. At CareMessage, we commend CMS and ASTP/ONC for issuing this RFI with
the shared goals of improving chronic disease prevention/management, expanding access to care,
and supporting the transition to value-based care.
We at CareMessage rely on interoperability and standards-based technologies to develop
solutions that improve patient outcomes at a population level. Requirements such as USCDI and
FHIR Bulk Data Export are critical to making our technology available to patients and providers in a
simple, cost-effective way. As an example, please consider our Automated Gaps-in-Care Journeys
for Safety-Net Organizations[0Ņéä, which automates SMS outreach to at-risk patients on behalf of
providers. This solution relies on FHIR and Bulk FHIR to trigger targeted outreach to patients,
providing education on chronic conditions and increasing access to care.
As we expand our support for additional CEHRT FHIR platforms and support additional care gap
use cases, reliable access to standardized data will be critical for us and others aiming to keep
patients healthier. Unfortunately, only one out of several CEHRT Bulk FHIR implementations we
have tested has met our requirements in data availability and reliability.
Despite the huge potential of FHIR Bulk Data Export to be a transformative technology for
population health use cases, the current reality is that most implementations are saddled with
limitations, preventing widespread adoption. We therefore urge CMS and ASPT/ONC to strengthen
guidance and enforcement so FHIR Bulk Export can become the efficient, high-impact data
platform patients and providers need to achieve better health outcomes.

Require reporting for data monitoring of real-world Bulk FHIR implementation
performance
For export jobs that do complete, they can take days to finish for a modest amount of resources
exported. Not only does this lag time prevent many use cases from being possible, but it adds
confusion and uncertainty when combined with the fact that jobs can become ŌĆ£stuck╦« or fail
eventually. That uncertainty is leading to wasted efforts of many engineers across the industry as
well as providers who are burdened with escalating issues with bulk FHIR implementers. Providers
are sitting on calls with other stakeholders to assist with debugging issues with CEHRT Bulk FHIR
technology, painstakingly investigating problems that should have been prevented by the
certification process.
Inferno is a good start for certifying data presence and security features. However, for FHIR Bulk
Data Export it is too easy to achieve certification without the software system working across the
board in a production context.
As such, we strongly urge the ASTP/ONC to monitor and regulate the real world performance of
Bulk FHIR to ensure population health apps can deliver value for healthcare providers and their
patients.

Require Bulk FHIR implementations adhere to a specific implementation of
rate-limiting
Every FHIR Bulk Data server is a shared resource: patients, providers, technology vendors, and
other stakeholders may all depend on it. Safeguards are therefore needed so that one client╩╝s
behavior cannot degrade service for everyone else. Because a server╩╝s internal job queue is

Require reduction in unnecessary provider involvement in Bulk FHIR setup
Once a provider agrees to share their patient population via Bulk FHIR with a tech vendor, the steps
required to enable seamless data flow should be minimal and low-burden for the provider.
Certified Health IT ŅéüCEHRTŅéé systems should be required to have FHIR Bulk Data Export capabilities
enabled by default. CEHRT should not be allowed to:
ŌŚÅ Require providers to install additional software
ŌŚÅ Require providers to contact the EHR vendor to initiate installation
ŌŚÅ Require providers to manage the lifecycle or runtime of any supporting systems
In addition, CEHRT should not require providers to manually configure patient groups before export
is possible, nor should they require a manual backfill process to access historical data. All USCDI
resources for all patients should be available by default, without manual intervention from the
provider or the CEHRT vendor.
Require more explicit documentation for certified APIs
Certified Health IT should be required to clearly label optional enhancements and API features that
are not mandated by ONC regulation as ŌĆ£uncertified╦« or ŌĆ£pre-certification,╦« even if those features
appear in the HL7 specification or a future/draft version of USCDI. This distinction is critical for
ensuring implementers understand which parts of an API are governed by certification
requirements and which are provided on a best-effort basis.

For example, if a CEHRT vendor exposes FHIR APIs outside of USCDIŌĆösuch as the Communication
resourceŌĆöthose should be explicitly marked as uncertified. The Communication resource is not
currently included in any version of USCDI nor is it required by the US Core Implementation Guide,
so it qualifies as an optional enhancement. If a client begins to rely on that resource (e.g., to
retrieve patient-facing messages or care coordination notes) and the API is later removed or
breaks, there is no regulatory recourse. This stands in contrast to required resources like
Observation, where certified systems are expected to ensure reliable access and vendors are held
accountable under ASTP/ONC certification criteria.
Developers and providers should not be expected to interpret regulatory frameworks just to
determine which FHIR endpoints can be safely relied upon. Documentation should clearly
distinguish between ŌĆ£certified╦« and ŌĆ£uncertified╦« APIs, allowing implementers to immediately
understand which resources are covered under certification and which are not. This transparency
reduces integration risk, avoids confusion, and helps maintain trust between health IT developers
and implementers.
Additionally, CEHRT should be prohibited from implementing hidden or undocumented features in
their FHIR or Bulk FHIR servers, even if those features conform to future versions of the HL7
specification. If such features are implemented, they should be explicitly documented and clearly
labeled as uncertified.
As described by Hyrum╩╝s Law Ņéā2Ņéä, ŌĆ£With a sufficient number of users of an API, it does not matter
what you promise in the contract: all observable behaviors of your system will be depended upon
by somebody.╦« In other words, once functionality is exposedŌĆöeven unofficiallyŌĆöusers will come to
rely on it. If that functionality degrades or is removed without warning, it creates significant

necessary to prevent these issues and ensure predictable behavior across certified health IT
systems.
