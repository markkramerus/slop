Proposal: Certified Developer Access Track for Plan and Eligibility Data (HPMS + MARx/BEQ) To meaningfully stimulate innovation in digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries, CMS should establish a Certified Developer Access Track — a structured framework that enables compliant third-party developers to access critical CMS plan and eligibility data, with guardrails to ensure accuracy, security, and consumer protection. Currently, the most valuable CMS datasets — including HPMS plan benefit packages and MARx-based eligibility indicators — are only accessible to a limited set of entities under plan sponsor agreements. This restricts the ability of independent technology vendors to power accurate, compliant, and scalable digital tools that can improve plan choice, cost transparency, and health decision-making for beneficiaries and their caregivers. We propose CMS develop a two-tiered access program : Tier 1: Structured Plan Data Access from HPMS CMS would publish a developer-facing version of plan benefit data, matching what is displayed on Plan Finder. This structured dataset would include: 
5 ● Plan premiums, benefit structures, and cost-sharing details ● Supplemental benefits ● Service areas and eligibility rules ● Provider and pharmacy directories ● Star ratings and key metadata This data would be made available via API or downloadable flat files in a standardized schema. Importantly, it would be aligned with HPMS submissions and updated on a CMS-defined schedule (e.g., September 15 for AEP readiness). To qualify, developers would need to: ● Obtain HITRUST CSF or SOC 2 Type II certification ● Disclose intended use cases ● Agree to CMS rules around display accuracy and benefit description standards ● Submit to periodic CMS audits or UI/UX reviews Tier 2: Read-Only Eligibility Access via MARx/BEQ APIs To complement plan data, developers should also have limited access to real-time eligibility indicators drawn from MARx or the BEQ system. This would include: ● Medicare Part A/B status ● Dual eligibility and Medicaid level (e.g., QMB, SLMB ● Low-Income Subsidy LIS flags ● Current plan enrollment and SEP triggers ● Enrollment effective dates and disenrollment lookbacks Access would be read-only and scoped strictly to pre-enrollment decision support — ensuring developers cannot directly initiate or modify enrollments without being contracted or certified under other programs (e.g., EDE.

Proposal: A CMS-Certified Enrollment API for Third-Party Technology Platforms To unlock meaningful innovation in digital plan navigation and enrollment, CMS should create a Standardized Enrollment API that allows certified third-party developers to submit enrollments on behalf of brokers and consumers in a secure, compliant, and auditable manner. 
7 Currently, most broker-assisted enrollments in Medicare Advantage or Part D plans are funneled through a limited set of proprietary platforms. These middleware systems: ● Support only a subset of plans based on commercial arrangements ● Introduce licensing and transaction-based fees for both plans and brokers ● Constrain the ability of smaller or regional plans to gain visibility ● Prevent technology developers from building alternative tools that improve broker efficiency or consumer experience ● Limit CMSʼs visibility into the broker ecosystem and real-time Agent of Record tracking This dynamic restricts choice, raises costs, and disincentivizes innovation in enrollment tooling — especially for startups and regional health plans seeking to modernize consumer experiences. Short-Term Recommendation: Launch a CMS-Certified Enrollment API Track CMS should create a certification pathway , similar to the Enhanced Direct Enrollment EDE process used in the ACA Marketplace, that allows third-party technology vendors to build compliant enrollment experiences powered by a CMS-maintained API. Key Features of the API: ● Submission of enrollment applications directly to CMS or plan sponsor systems ● Enrollment status and error code reporting (modeled after MARx Transaction Reply Codes) ● Eligibility window validation and SEP flagging ● Secure Agent of Record tracking ● Support for application save-and-resume workflows and real-time plan selection 
8 Developer Certification Requirements: ● HITRUST CSF or SOC 2 Type II security certification ● CMS-approved display logic, disclaimers, and plan parity rules ● UI/UX review of the end-user experience to prevent plan steering or deceptive enrollment ● Logging and audit support for CMS and MA plan oversight Collaborative Content Oversight: Plan-Developer Co-Review Model To further reduce the operational burden on CMS, we recommend introducing a plan-developer co-review model . Under this model: ● Certified developers work directly with participating MA/PDP plans to validate content accuracy and compliance (e.g., benefits display, disclaimers, suppression rules). ● CMS provides a standardized validation checklist and metadata schema to ensure consistency. ● Each validation session is logged and attested to by both the plan and developer, with artifacts made available for CMS spot-audits. ● A CMS-hosted Developer & Plan Portal could manage certification artifacts, checklist uploads, and dispute resolution. This shared responsibility model enables faster iteration, encourages accountability, and allows CMS to focus on structural oversight rather than every planʼs front-end rendering details. Data & API Access Conclusion By empowering developers to build secure, compliant enrollment pathways via CMS-certified APIs, this proposal lowers barriers to entry, fosters competition, and reduces consumer reliance on opaque or commercially exclusive enrollment platforms — while keeping CMS in control of the compliance framework.

It also lacks many consumer-relevant attributes such as accessibility accommodations, telehealth access, or scheduling pathways. Technical Standards and Certification TD-4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs over proprietary APIs? CMS has made strides in releasing public APIs like Blue Button 2.0 and BEQ, but adoption remains limited because these APIs are often fragmented, narrowly scoped, or tied to proprietary or plan-specific access requirements. To encourage adoption of open, standards-based APIs, CMS should commit to a core technical stack across all beneficiary data systems: ● HL7 FHIR for structured health data and plan directories ● OpenID Connect and OAuth 2.0 for identity and access management ● SMART on FHIR profiles for secure, app-based workflows These standards are already in use within portions of CMS (e.g., Blue Button), but should be extended to other key APIs like BEQ, MARx, HPMS, and enrollment. In addition, CMS should: ● Publish transparent onboarding requirements and schemas for each API 
17 ● Offer sandbox environments and test harnesses to speed up development ● Reduce reliance on non-public access rules that require plan sponsorships or opaque approvals ● Clearly distinguish clinical use cases from plan selection, navigation, and eligibility APIs , which have distinct value propositions and user workflows Aligning across these open standards will reduce the cost of integration for developers, increase innovation, and help CMS better monitor and govern data access consistently. TD-5. How could a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints improve access to health information for patients, providers, and payers? Who should publish such a directory, and should users bear a cost? A standardized, nationwide directory of FHIR endpoints would meaningfully improve access to health information by supporting automated, secure connectivity across stakeholders . Today, endpoint discovery is fragmented, incomplete, and often manual — limiting the effectiveness of patient-facing apps, data aggregators, and provider tools. We recommend that CMS — potentially through ONC or in partnership with TEFCA-designated entities — maintain a federally published directory of FHIR endpoints , accessible via API and updated through attestation or certification channels. Such a directory would support: ● Patients , by allowing consumer-facing apps to discover the correct endpoint to retrieve or share their data across providers, plans, and health systems ● Brokers and counselors , by integrating accurate data sources into plan navigation tools — especially for identifying in-network providers who are actively accepting patients ● Payers , by enabling better data interoperability across systems and reducing ambiguity in data sharing responsibilities Importantly, this endpoint directory should complement — not replace — other provider directory efforts, such as those proposed in TD2(d), which focus on network participation, availability, and accessibility attributes. 
18 We believe such a national directory should be publicly maintained and free to access , at least for read-only operations. CMS could explore cost recovery models for advanced search features or analytics dashboards, but baseline directory access should be openly available to enable innovation and patient choice. 4. Data Exchange TD-12. Should CMS endorse non-CMS data sources and networks, and if so, what criteria or metrics should CMS consider? Yes, CMS should endorse certain non-CMS data sources and networks — particularly those that provide high-value data not currently available through federal APIs. This includes: ● Health plan-sponsored APIs (e.g., provider directories, supplemental benefits, real-time formulary tools) ● Data aggregators with verified provider affiliation, appointment availability, or facility access metadata ● SDOH networks that compile community-based support services ● Pharmacy benefit platforms with real-time pricing and inventory signals Rather than CMS “certifyingˮ these sources outright, we recommend a federated trust model based on: ● Annual third-party audit or attestation of data accuracy and update frequency ● Transparent metadata standards , including when the data was last refreshed and how it was collected ● CMS-published criteria for acceptable sources , including interoperability formats (e.g., HL7 FHIR, NCPDP SCRIPT ● Inclusion in a CMS-maintained interoperability registry , which developers and plans can reference when selecting compliant data sources Endorsement should not create liability for CMS but rather set a high bar for participation in the digital Medicare ecosystem , while allowing innovation to flourish around CMS infrastructure. 
19 TD-13. What new opportunities and advancements could emerge with APIs providing access to the entirety of a patient's electronic health information (EHI)? Access to a patientʼs complete electronic health information EHI via standardized APIs — as supported by the 21st Century Cures Act — would create substantial innovation opportunities across plan navigation, care management, and consumer support. Key advancements could include: ● Personalized plan selection : Using structured clinical data (e.g., diagnoses, conditions, medications) to help match beneficiaries to Special Needs Plans SNPs, SSBCI-qualified benefits, or provider networks aligned with their care. ● Seamless onboarding and guided workflows : Auto-populating plan comparison tools, health risk assessments, or supplemental benefit eligibility forms. ● Care coordination and outreach automation : Triggering care transitions, medication adherence prompts, or targeted outreach based on discharges, referrals, or risk signals. ● Outcome benchmarking : Enabling developers and plans to assess whether health outcomes align with program intent or benefit design. ● Data portability and health equity : Supporting community-based organizations and caregivers in delivering context-aware support for underserved or high-risk individuals — assuming consent is captured. These use cases are powerful even outside clinical settings, provided access is secure and beneficiary-directed. a. What are the primary obstacles to this? Several barriers limit the potential of EHI-based APIs for innovation: ● Access restrictions : Many EHI systems restrict access to clinical actors or organizations with NPIs, excluding digital tools focused on navigation, enrollment, or care planning. ● Ambiguity in permitted use : Non-HIPAA-covered apps face unclear guidance about what they can or cannot do with EHI — especially for plan-related features. ● Fragmented implementation : EHR vendors vary widely in how they implement USCDI-based APIs, making integration inconsistent. 
20 ● Authentication complexity : Consumers must often manage credentials for multiple portals (e.g., MyChart, MyMedicare, Login.gov), hindering consent and OAuth-based access. TD-19. Regarding price transparency implementation: a. What are current shortcomings in content, format, delivery, and timeliness? Despite recent regulatory progress, price transparency data remains fragmented, inconsistent, and often unusable for consumers and third-party tools. Specific shortcomings include: ● Inconsistent file formats : Hospitals and payers use varying schemas and naming conventions, making machine-readable file ingestion unreliable. ● Lack of standardization across pharmacies and provider types — especially in how negotiated rates are expressed and which CPT/HCPCS codes are included. ● Limited real-time pricing : Most price files reflect static, scheduled pricing and donʼt account for formulary tiers, step edits, or pharmacy-level pricing fluctuations. ● Poor discoverability : Many transparency files are buried on hospital websites or payer portals, often without an index or metadata. ● Missing consumer context : Files rarely include cost-sharing implications (e.g., deductible status, drug tier level), which makes them less actionable. b. Which workflows would benefit most from functional price transparency? ● Medication pricing and pharmacy shopping : Consumers would benefit from knowing which pharmacy offers the lowest out-of-pocket cost for a given drug at the point of sale. ● Procedure-based care comparison : Price-aware consumers could compare imaging centers, outpatient surgeries, or labs across providers and facilities. ● Plan selection support : Accurate, location-specific cost estimates could be used by brokers and tech platforms to guide consumers toward financially suitable plans. 
21 ● Provider referrals : PCPs and care navigators could select higher-value referral targets when equipped with local pricing insights. c. What improvements would be most valuable for patients, providers, or payers, including CMS? ● Standardized schema : Mandate a CMS-defined JSON or FHIR-based schema for hospital and payer machine-readable files. ● Provider-level calculators : Require payers to offer a tool that allows consumers to estimate costs by procedure a n d provider. ● Real-time prescription pricing : Establish a minimum standard for pharmacy benefit managers PBMs to publish real-time formulary-based pricing for covered drugs. ● Consumer-level integration : Allow third-party developers to access these tools via API to embed pricing transparency directly in plan comparison, provider lookup, or care navigation platforms. For CMS, these improvements would: ● Support more accurate plan comparisons ● Increase public accountability ● Lower beneficiary confusion and downstream call volume d. What would further motivate solution development? ● RFP transparency reform : As noted in our TD1 and TD2 responses, many technology vendors are unable to participate in CMS-related innovation because RFPs are not publicly released or are narrowly distributed. ● Public incentives for integration : CMS could reward plans or vendors that implement transparency APIs aligned to patient choice workflows (e.g., point-of-sale Rx pricing tools). ● Data quality certification : Establish a “CMS Verifiedˮ seal or audit process for vendors that consistently deliver accurate, up-to-date pricing data in approved formats. 
22 Innovation is currently hampered not by a lack of interest, but by inconsistent access, unclear incentives, and limited integration opportunities. Clarifying expectations and making pricing data interoperable would unlock the next generation of cost-aware consumer tools. CMS Strategic Role CMS should standardize and expand data sets available to consumers, particularly concerning supplemental benefits, dental coverage, and drug pricing. Facilitating stronger collaboration between CMS and external health data providers will enhance consumer experience. CMS should also promote robust API access and interoperability standards, simplifying processes and reducing consumer friction. Implementation of direct enrollment APIs will significantly enhance consumer convenience, supported by provider-assisted, compliant plan selection integrations. Expected Consumer Impact and Outcomes Proposed innovations will shorten sales cycles, simplify enrollment processes, and enhance consumer satisfaction through improved clarity and ease of plan selection. Broker productivity will increase with better technological support, positively impacting consumer navigation. Provider-assisted tools will improve consumer-provider interactions, fostering better healthcare outcomes. Metrics will clearly demonstrate improvements in consumer engagement, broker efficiency, and market effectiveness.
