1. Introduction and Organizational Background
The SCAN Foundation (TSF) appreciates the opportunity to respond to the Centers for Medicare
& Medicaid Services (CMS) Request for Information (RFI) on the health technology ecosystem.
The SCAN Foundation focuses on programs and investments that help all older adults,
particularly those from underrepresented communities, age well at home and in the community.
We envision a society where all of us can age well with purpose, in the location that we choose.
We recognize that technology plays an important role in supporting older adults’ ability to thrive
in community, particularly those with chronic conditions and who need long-term services and
supports.
TSF believes that policy development, including policies governing health technology, should be
grounded in the voices and experiences of those the policy impacts on a day-to-day basis. In
order to support health and aging policymakers’ ability to more easily access older adults’ lived
experiences, TSF, in partnership with the Public Policy Lab, developed The People Say
(thepeoplesay.org), a national platform that collects and shares the authentic stories of older
adults and caregivers. The People Say contains thousands of qualitative data units from over 100
older adults across the country, all of which are tagged and coded to policy-relevant topics that
allow a user to quickly find a story that humanizes a unique policy or program. Examples include
transportation needs, social connection, and the use of technology in aging care. The People Say
itself is a tech-enabled research tool that TSF hopes CMS and other policymaking entities
leverage as they design technology policy on behalf of Medicare beneficiaries and other older
adults.
The platform also includes some specific feedback from interviewees on how technology shows
up in their day-to-day lives. For example, one older adult from rural Iowa shared, “I don’t do
anything online. I have never sent one text... I didn’t get a cell phone until two years ago... I
can’t afford a cell phone. Yours cost about a thousand dollars. I finally found one for like $80.”
An older adult from Texas remarked, “All I do with my phone is text, take pictures, and play
bingo. That’s all I do with my phone”, further explaining that she does not even attempt to
address any of her health care decisions if they require her to log on to the computer. These
honest perspectives, while not necessarily representative of all older adults, provide rich context
to how some aging individuals engage with the kinds of technology this RFI seeks more detailed
technical information on. They should also temper expectations that all older adults are

comfortable enough with certain tech-enabled health innovations that merely offering new tools
will be enough to spur meaningful adoption and use.
2. Responses to Key Topics
Current Technology Landscape & Barriers
Health technology adoption continues to expand, offering convenience, improved care
coordination, and timely interventions for chronic illnesses. However, many older adults
encounter persistent barriers that limit their ability to benefit from these innovations fully. These
barriers are vividly illustrated through the lived experiences collected via The People Say
platform.
Some older adults lack digital literacy and confidence when interacting with technology. As one
provider shared, “I think that one of the factors is the internet... many don’t know how to use it to
navigate insurance or benefits.” Even among those with devices, some use them only for basic
functions, such as texting or playing games, and do not feel equipped to access healthcare portals
or virtual appointments. An older adult in rural Ohio commented, “I don’t know how to do any of
that. I have a nice cell phone. I have a computer, but I don’t get on it because I’m afraid
somebody will steal my information.” This might make it harder for older adults to access the
technologies that are being used by providers to assist them in their care plans.
In addition, the affordability of technologies remains a pressing concern. The same provider
interviewed for The People Say reported that older adults have difficulty obtaining even basic
health monitoring tools: “I see that many come in asking for the blood pressure machine... they
want to know if their blood pressure is high or low, but they can’t afford it or don’t know where
to get it.”
Internet connectivity is another major issue, especially in rural areas. As one rural Iowan
explained, “Internet accessibility has always been a big issue... There's still people that... can't...
get Mediacom to provide service for the street they live on.” We know that without consistent
high-speed internet, technologies like telehealth and remote monitoring are out of reach for
many.
These realities underscore the importance of building systems that are inclusive and accessible,
with support for digital training, affordable devices, and investment in infrastructure. The People
Say continues to be a valuable tool for understanding and tracking these evolving challenges
through firsthand accounts that can support CMS’ more nuanced understanding of how older
adults engage with technology.
3. Conclusion
Older adults are not a monolithic group. Geographic, economic, cultural, and linguistic
differences all shape how technology is perceived and used.

For digital health solutions to work effectively, they must offer flexible, culturally relevant, and
low-barrier pathways to engagement. Lived experiences shared on The People Say offer
important insights into these intersecting barriers and can inform meaningful responses.
We commend CMS for initiating this RFI and for its commitment to building a more effective
and inclusive health technology ecosystem. As CMS explores new strategies, the voices and
experiences of older adults should remain central to the process. Lived experience is not an
anecdotal addition—it is essential data.
The SCAN Foundation remains committed to sharing insights from our work, including data and
stories from The People Say platform, and to elevating the perspectives of older adults as this
important dialogue continues.
