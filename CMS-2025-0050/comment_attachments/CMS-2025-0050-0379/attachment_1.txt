Dear Administrator Oz and Assistant Secretary Keane,
We greatly appreciate the opportunity to respond to the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS)
Request for Information CMS-0042-NC regarding the Health Technology Ecosystem (RFI). Friends of HL7 is
a non-profit organization devoted to raising awareness of the benefits of interoperability and the role of
Health Level Seven International (HL7) in advancing standards to support interoperability and information
sharing to improve healthcare delivery, reduce costs, empower patients, and improve health outcomes.
We commend your leadership in gaining public input on the actions needed to fully realize the benefits that
digital health products—accompanied by data availability for all stakeholders—will have, not only on
Medicare beneficiaries, but all Americans. We also greatly appreciate your continued support for HL7,
which, for more than 30 years, has served as the premier global authority on healthcare interoperability
standards, with members representing healthcare providers, government agencies, payers, manufacturers,
technology developers and innovators, consultants, and patients from more than 50 countries. HL7 carries
out this role as a not-for-profit, neutral, ANSI-accredited standards development organization dedicated to
providing a comprehensive framework and related standards and implementation support for exchanging,
integrating, sharing, and retrieving electronic health information. HL7 recognizes the essential role of
patients and caregivers in shaping a health technology ecosystem that meets their needs.
As an established vendor-neutral forum that has helped move the entire industry to greater interoperability,
HL7 provides both CMS and the Office of the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy/Office of the
National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ASTP/ONC) with unique value through its
collaborative approach to standards development. HL7's Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR)
standard has become the foundation for modern healthcare interoperability, endorsed by federal
regulations including the CMS Interoperability and Patient Access Final Rule, the ONC Cures Act Final Rule,
the ONC Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability Final Rule, and the CMS Interoperability and Prior
Authorization Final Rule. HL7's diverse membership ensures that standards development balances the
needs of all stakeholders, including patients, while prioritizing patient care and data accessibility. This
vendor-neutral, multi-stakeholder approach is essential as the Administration seeks to advance
interoperability across the complex healthcare ecosystem without favoring any particular commercial
interests or technological approaches.

1. Answers to TD-5 & TD-14, “Ensuring Proper FHIR Endpoint Publishing and Use of FHIR APIs”
- A Coalition Approach
In response to question TD-5 related to a nationwide directory of FHIR endpoints, the publication of FHIR
endpoints is crucial to improving access to health information, in that it enables data users to find and
request information from providers’ technology platforms effectively and efficiently. To fully realize the
goals of interoperability and increase networks’ use of FHIRs as described in question TD-14, additional
capabilities—beyond publication—are needed. To further advance interoperability and data access and
use, more robust FHIR endpoint services are needed to ensure that APIs can be accessed and utilized
effectively.
HL7, as a vendor-neutral forum, proposes to partner with the federal government and lead a public-private
sector coalition of stakeholders, to not only collect and publish FHIR endpoints, but also to address the
many challenges described above, including inconsistent endpoint publishing practices, inability to search
for an endpoint by provider, lack of standardized discovery mechanisms, limited visibility into endpoint
capabilities, and unreliable endpoint functionality.
This coalition would build on ONC’s investments to date, and include the development of a more
comprehensive implementation guide to FHIR endpoint publishing and monitoring and the creation of
standardized endpoint publishing requirements specifying essential metadata about each endpoint's
capabilities, authentication requirements, and supported resources. The coalition would establish and
operate a centralized, searchable registry of FHIR endpoints, enabling automated discovery, while
developing and implementing tools to validate endpoint metadata accuracy and completeness.
The endpoint directory would complement provider directories, recognizing that endpoints are tied to
technology platforms used by practices, hospitals, and other provider organizations, not individual
providers. The endpoint directory and related services would also complement current governance and
trust frameworks, offering a complementary technical implementation layer that operationalizes endpoint
access in real-world digital workflows. Additionally, through the combined expertise of public and private
sector partners, the coalition would implement monitoring systems to track endpoint availability and
performance, ensuring published endpoints exist and function reliably in production environments.
Beyond fundamental discovery, this public-private partnership would develop standardized testing
protocols that verify endpoints function according to their published capabilities, create tools that validate
real-world API interactions to identify operational issues, establish performance benchmarks for response
times, data completeness, and error handling, and implement continuous monitoring solutions that alert
stakeholders to degraded endpoint performance. By working in concert with other existing frameworks,
HL7’s coalition approach would transform the current fragmented landscape into a cohesive ecosystem
where endpoints can be reliably discovered, assessed for capability, and integrated into workflows with
minimal friction, significantly enhancing existing network participation and data exchange.
For example, HL7’s leadership in defining FHIR endpoint metadata, capability assertions, and testing
criteria would enable current networks and their participants to integrate this directory service to enhance
discovery and access.

2: Answers to TD-9 & TD-14, “Prioritizing FHIR API Conformance Testing to Increase Use”
Recording your questions related to certification of health IT in TD-9 and increasing networks’ use of FHIR
APIs in TD-14, establishing a conformance or certification program will play a key role in accelerating the
uptake and effective and efficient use of APIs to support interoperability. Migrating away from a Health IT
Certification Program that focuses solely on EHR functionality to one that prioritizes the exchange of data
across systems—through the certification of operational APIs—is greatly needed to facilitate
interoperability and seamless and secure information sharing as envisioned by HHS, private sector
stakeholders, and patients.
HL7, as a vendor-neutral forum, proposes to partner with the federal government to develop and operate a
FHIR API voluntary certification program. Through this new program, HL7 would establish clear, objective
standards for FHIR API implementation that address both technical conformance as well as real-world
interoperability needs. The program would provide clear implementation targets for healthcare
organizations and technology vendors, with the goal of reducing implementation costs and accelerating
interoperability.
Rather than each organization developing its own interpretation of standards and regulations, this HL7 FHIR
API certification program would establish common understanding and implementation approaches. This
standardization will reduce the technical debt associated with custom implementations, lower integration
costs, and allow organizations to focus resources on improving care delivery and supporting patients,
rather than solving interoperability challenges.

3: Answers to PC-13 & TD-18, “Remedies for Reducing Information Blocking”
We appreciate HHS’ inclusion of question TD-18 related to information blocking within the RFI which we
hope will spur the sharing of information by patients, providers, and technology innovators about the
prevalence and nature of information blocking practices. Still, because all RFI responses are being made
publicly available, many organizations and individuals may not come forward due to fear of various
repercussions associated with their reporting.
As a neutral forum with a long history of engaging stakeholders and gaining consensus on standards and
related solutions, HL7 proposes to take several steps to understand the prevalence and nature of
information blocking, the negative impact that information blocking has on health outcomes, healthcare
costs, and the health and wellbeing of individuals and therefore the benefits of mitigation. This information
can help educate healthcare stakeholders about the actions they may be taking to unintentionally or
intentionally engage in information blocking, while also informing future federal policymaking. Housing
these efforts within a neutral body with a long history of promoting fairness, building trust, and facilitating
consensus will help to address the fear of information blocking reporting that many stakeholders within the
healthcare system experience.
The following is a series of alternative steps that HL7 could take to help address the information blocking
problem.
1. HL7 will lead the development and fielding of an anonymized survey of healthcare providers,
payers, technology developers and innovators, researchers, public health agencies, and patient
advocacy organizations to gain a better understanding of (1) the prevalence of information
blocking, (2) the nature of policies or practices that inhibit access, and (3) the impact that lack of
data access has on patient care, outcomes, and healthcare costs.
2. HL7 will also collaborate on the conduct of periodic interviews with both patients and health care
organizations, including providers, payers, technology developers and innovators, researchers, and
public health agencies as well as additional research, to further understanding of the nature of the
problem, as well as the actions that could be taken to reduce the level of information blocking and
improve data access and use.
3. HL7 will develop, publish, and widely disseminate a report that summarizes its findings, raising
awareness of the prevalence of the problem, the policies and practices that exacerbate the
problem, and proposed federal policies, actions, and other solutions to improve data access and
use.
In addition, should CMS desire, HL7 could leverage the FHIR endpoint directory and—as an extension to
conformance testing—HL7 would, under appropriate privacy and confidentiality agreements, establish
access to each FHIR endpoint, gathering metadata (for example, the process for requesting access) and
providing evidence for full end-to-end accessibility.

Benefits of Implementation
The approaches recommended in this letter will benefit the entire U.S. healthcare system. By ensuring that
FHIR endpoints are properly published, discoverable, and functional, and assuring that APIs are accessible
without special effort, these initiatives will reduce the technical barriers that currently impede information
sharing between providers, payers, public health agencies, and patients.
These recommendations will reduce administrative burden and accelerate value-based and patient-
centered care. Ultimately this progress will empower Medicare beneficiaries through easier access to their
data and use of digital tools that let them access, manage, and understand their health data.
Health systems will be able to confidently invest in API technologies that carry certification, knowing they
will support the organization's interoperability goals without unexpected limitations. Implementation of
these recommendations will reduce uncertainty and development costs. With consistent, well-
documented FHIR endpoints, developers will be able to create new applications and services that address
unmet needs in healthcare delivery, population health management, and patient engagement. Startup
companies and innovators will be able to develop new healthcare applications with greater confidence that
they can access the data needed to deliver value to users. The innovation ecosystem can continuously
improve healthcare quality while reducing costs through more efficient information exchange and reduced
administrative burden.
These recommendations will enhance clinicians’ ability to deliver evidence-based, coordinated care by
ensuring timely access to critical patient information. Primary care providers, specialists, and emergency
physicians will benefit from automated notifications and seamless access to records, improving follow-up,
reducing readmissions, and minimizing errors. Reliable APIs will reduce time spent searching across
systems, allowing clinicians to focus more on patient care. Overall, these improvements will lower cognitive
and administrative burdens, reduce burnout, and support better clinical decision-making and outcomes.
From a patient perspective, implementation of these recommendations will address a major frustration:
the inability to easily access and share personal health information. With improved data sharing, patients
will be able to seamlessly transfer their medical history when changing providers, ensure emergency
clinicians have life-saving information, and reduce the burden of coordinating care among primary care
providers and multiple specialists. This will prevent delays, duplicate tests, and out-of-pocket costs, while
empowering patients to take a more active role in their care
For the healthcare system as a whole, these recommendations will drive innovation and efficiency by
creating a foundation for advanced analytics, population health management, and precision medicine.
Health systems will be able to implement sophisticated care management programs that identify high-risk
patients across multiple care settings and coordinate interventions to prevent complications or
hospitalizations. Healthcare researchers will be able to more easily identify potential participants for
clinical trials based on specific medical conditions or characteristics, accelerating medical advances.
These system-wide benefits will translate to reduced healthcare costs, improved population health
outcomes, and a more resilient healthcare infrastructure capable of responding to emerging health
challenges.

In Closing
Thank you for the opportunity to share our insights and recommendations. The initiatives proposed in this
letter directly address several of the challenges identified in the RFI.
By developing and implementing approaches to ensure proper FHIR endpoint publishing, conformance of
APIs with established standards, and reductions in information blocking, HL7 can help provide HHS and
the broader healthcare community with essential tools to advance interoperability.
These complementary initiatives leverage HL7's established position as a vendor-neutral standards
development organization with deep expertise in healthcare interoperability. By working collaboratively with
the federal government and a broad coalition of public and private sector stakeholders, HL7 can lead
coalition efforts that develop and implement solutions that address immediate interoperability challenges,
while establishing a foundation for continued progress toward a fully interoperable healthcare ecosystem.
We welcome the opportunity to discuss these proposals further with CMS and ASTP/ONC and collaborate
on their implementation. Through these public-private partnerships, we can create a health technology
ecosystem that supports improved care delivery, enhanced patient outcomes, and more efficient
healthcare operations through seamless information exchange in the years to come.
