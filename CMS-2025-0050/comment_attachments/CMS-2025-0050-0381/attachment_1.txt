We are encouraged with the focus on the needs of caregivers as key members of the health technology ecosystem and our comments on the RFI’s specific questions are outlined below. Section B. Patients and Caregivers. 1. Patient Needs PC-1. What health management or care navigation apps would help you understand and manage your (or your loved ones) health needs, as well as the actions you should take? a. What are the top things you would like to be able to do for your or your loved ones' health that can be enabled by digital health products? RCI has never endorsed any particular app, however, the caregivers that we engage with do have specific functions they look for with digital products for their caregiving needs. One of the top needs is a shareable interface that allows the entire care team – beyond the primary family caregiver – to stay informed and engaged. This helps reduce caregiver burden by streamlining communication and coordination. 
 Caregivers also emphasize the importance of using a shareable interface to communicate with the professional care team, ensuring everyone stays informed about the care recipient’s condition and any emerging health concerns. Additional useful features include: listing all emergency contacts, outlining emergency/disaster plans, and integrating RCI’s Caregiver Profiles to better understand the caregiver’s experience and transition. These profiles offer a structured way to assess where a caregiver is in their journey – whether they are new to caregiving, in an intensive phase, or transitioning out of the role. By capturing this information, systems can tailor support services, anticipate needs, and reduce caregiver burnout. Embedding Caregiver Profiles into digital tools can help care teams better coordinate care while aligning with efforts to personalize and improve caregiver support at scale. b. If you had a personal assistant to support your health needs, what are the top things you would ask them to help with? In your response, please consider tasks that could be supported or facilitated by software solutions in the future. Our caregivers shared with their program coaches that they often find it difficult and time-consuming to schedule doctor and lab appointments, as well as to track lab results. This challenge is especially pronounced for those caring for multiple individuals and/or managing full-time professional responsibilities. An app designed to streamline these tasks would be a valuable tool for supporting caregivers. Also, apps that enable caregivers to monitor vital signs or symptoms remotely would be especially valuable for those who do not live in the same household with the patients (or long-distance caregivers). Such technology can provide timely alerts for health changes or emergencies, allowing caregivers to respond quickly and ensure their loved ones receive appropriate care. 
 PC-2. Do you have easy access to your own and all your loved ones' health information in one location (for example, in a single patient portal or another software system)? c. Were there particular data types, such as x-rays or specific test results, that were unavailable? What are the obstacles to accessing your own or your loved ones' complete health information electronically and using it for managing health conditions or finding the best care (for example, limitations in functionality, user friendliness, or access to basic technology infrastructure)? Understanding test results, treatment plans/steps, medication usage and side effects can be challenging, especially for individuals whose primary language is not English. Apps that can conduct accurate translation – from medical jargon to plain language, and from English to other languages – would be very beneficial. PC-4. What features are missing from apps you use or that you are aware of today? a. What apps should exist but do not yet? Why do you believe they do not exist yet? Rural families often face limited broadband access or weak phone signals. Therefore, apps designed to function without internet connection or cellular service would be especially beneficial for rural caregivers and their families. These apps can use satellites for tracking and communication even without a phone signal. They also often utilize the phone’s built-in GPS or pre-downloaded content (e.g., Kindle and YouTube allow downloading content for offline reading or viewing). PC-5. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in these digital health products? a. What role, if any, should CMS have in reviewing or approving digital health products on the basis of their efficacy, quality or impact or both on health outcomes (not approving in the sense of a coverage determination)? What criteria should be used if there is a review process? What technology 
 solutions, policy changes, or program design changes can increase patient and caregiver adoption of digital health products (for example, enhancements to data access, reimbursement adjustments, or new beneficiary communications)? Given the profound impact caregiving has on individuals’ physical, emotional, and financial well-being, CMS should play a proactive role in supporting the development and adoption of digital health tools that benefit both patients and caregivers. While not functioning as a product approval body, CMS can help set standards that prioritize efficacy, quality and impact on outcomes- particularly for tools that address caregiver needs, care coordination, and population health. One foundational step would be to incorporate a standard caregiver identification question into all Medicare and Medicaid annual wellness visits and related digital tools (e.g., patient-facing check-in apps). This simple addition could serve as a systemic flag to identify caregivers early, connect them to appropriate resources, and generate critical data on the scale and nature of caregiving within the Medicare and Medicaid populations. To increase patient and caregiver adoption of digital health products, CMS should consider policy and programmatic changes, such as: ● Reimbursement alignment for tools that reduce burden on caregivers, improve health literacy, or demonstrate improved outcomes. ● Technology solutions that reduce friction – such as platforms that minimize repetitive data entry and automatically deliver curated, condition-specific resources to the caregiver’s email or portal, based on the care recipient’s diagnosis and the caregiver’s role. ● Enhanced data access policies that allow caregivers (with appropriate permissions) to view and manage relevant health information across care settings – supporting the continuity of care and reducing confusion or duplication. ● Clear and proactive communication strategies that include outreach to family caregivers, promoting digital emotional wellness tools, physical health resources, and decision-making support. 
 By centering caregivers in digital innovation efforts and leveraging CMS’s reach, we can address caregiver burnout, improve care coordination, and ultimately drive better outcomes for both patients and those who support them. b. What changes would enable timely access to high quality CMS and provider generated data on patients? Develop data platforms that provide data summaries and explanations in multiple languages and plain language formats, especially for non-English-speaking populations. While advances in technology have made more data available for patients and caregivers, it remains challenging and often overwhelming to locate the specific data or resources needed for particular purposes. Therefore, we should develop apps that are tailored for caregivers and use smart filtering and artificial intelligence (AI) to deliver relevant, personalized information and resources based on their unique caregiver situations. The primary purpose of RCI’s caregiver Data Platform is to create centralized, easy-to-navigate platforms that aggregate trusted data and resources from public and local sources, reducing the need to search multiple websites. We would welcome an opportunity to work more closely with CMS to deploy our evidence-based caregiver solutions. PC-6. What features are most important to make digital health products accessible and easy to use for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers, particularly those with limited prior experience using digital tools and services? To ensure digital health products are accessible and easy to use for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers – especially those with limited digital experience – it is essential to prioritize features that reduce barriers, foster trust, and support mental well-being. From the mental health and caregiving advocacy lens, the following features are critical: 1. Plain Language and Clear Communication ● Use of Plain Language: All content, including instructions, labels, and notifications, should be written at a 6th-grade reading level or 
 lower. Avoid medical jargon or technical terms unless clearly defined. ● Visual Aids and Icons: Incorporate universally recognizable icons and simple visuals to reinforce understanding. ● Multilingual Support: Offer content in multiple languages commonly spoken by Medicare beneficiaries and their caregivers. 2. Accessibility for Varying Abilities ○ Compliance with WCAG Standards: Ensure the platform meets or exceeds Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)3 to support users with visual, auditory, cognitive, and motor impairments. ○ Customizable Interfaces: Allow users to adjust text size, contrast, and audio settings to suit their needs. ○ Voice Navigation and Screen Reader Compatibility: Integrate voice commands and ensure full compatibility with screen readers and other assistive technologies. ○ Low-Tech Compatibility: Design for use on older devices and with low-bandwidth internet connections, which are common among underserved populations. 3. Support for Mental Health and Emotional Well-Being ○ Non-Stigmatizing Design: Use sensitive, appropriate, culturally competent language, and non-clinical terminology to reduce anxiety and stigma around mental health. ○ Privacy and Trust: Clearly communicate how personal data is protected. Trust is especially important for users managing mental health conditions. ○ Integrated Mental Health Resources: Provide easy access to mental health support, such as crisis lines, caregiver support groups, and educational materials. 4. Robust User Support and Training ○ Live and On-Demand Help: Offer phone-based and live chat support with trained representatives who understand the needs of older adults and caregivers. 3 https://www.w3.org/WAI/standards-guidelines/wcag/ 
 ○ Step-by-Step Tutorials: Include video and interactive guides that walk users through key tasks, such as scheduling appointments or accessing test results. ○ Caregiver Access and Proxy Features: Enable caregivers to manage health information and appointments on behalf of beneficiaries, with appropriate consent and privacy controls. 5. User-Centered Design and Testing ○ Co-Design with Target Users: Involve Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers--especially those with limited digital literacy--in the design and testing process. ○ Iterative Feedback Loops: Continuously gather and incorporate feedback to improve usability and accessibility. 2. Data Access and Integration PC-8. In your experience, what health data is readily available and valuable to patients or their caregivers or both? c. What specific opportunities and challenges exist to improve accessibility, interoperability and integration of clinical data from different sources to enable more meaningful clinical research and generation of actionable evidence? A key challenge in improving data accessibility and interoperability is ensuring that critical health information follows the patient – especially when a family caregiver is involved. Caregivers frequently act as a bridge between multiple providers, such as primary care physicians, specialists, and community-based services. However, they often lack easy access to the critical data needed to support continuity of care. An integrated, user-friendly interface that gives authorized caregivers access to relevant health information would reduce duplication, improve decision-making, and ease burden of care coordination. Such systems should prioritize seamless data sharing across providers, while ensuring appropriate privacy protections. In addition, better data integration would allow caregivers to connect with community-based supports – such as local Area Agencies on Aging (AAAs) – 
 and to develop tools like emergency preparedness plans that require access to accurate, up-to-date health information. These improvements not only support caregiving but also generate more robust, real-world evidence that can inform meaningful clinical research and policy decisions. PC-9. Given that the Blue Button 2.0 API only includes basic patient demographic, Medicare coverage, and claims data (Part A, B, D), what additional CMS data sources do developers view as most valuable for inclusion in the API to enable more useful digital products for patients and caretakers? c. Is there non-CMS data that should be included in the API? In looking at what data is available, one of the standout things that should be available in apps is the designation of the caregiver. Being able to have a declaration of ‘’this is my caregiver’’ can increase the quality of care and allows the designated caregiver to be appropriately supported. Caregivers are often the ones who ensure that care continues effectively after a hospital discharge or medical appointment – they are the engine that keeps the care journey moving forward. Officially designating a caregiver within health systems and digital tools is a critical first step toward fully recognizing their role. This formal recognition helps bring caregiving into the mainstream, which in turn can drive greater investment in resources, support, and visibility for caregivers across the healthcare system. PC-11. How are health information exchanges (HIEs) currently helping to advance patient access to health information in the real world? a. How valuable, available, and accurate do you find the data they share to be? Data sharing across multiple sources is highly valuable. It enables both caregivers and healthcare providers to have a more complete, real-time picture of the care recipient’s health status. When all members of the care team – across settings and specialties – have access to the same information, it improves care, reduces the risk of preventable adverse health events, and supports timely, informed decision-making. 
 PC-12. What are the most valuable operational health data use cases for patients and caregivers that, if addressed, would create more efficient care navigation or eliminate barriers to competition among providers or both? One of the most valuable operational health data use cases for patients and caregivers is real-time access to practical, decision-making information—such as what services are covered under their health plan, what their out-of-pocket costs will be, and which local facilities offer specialized services like CT scans, including direct contact information for scheduling. This type of transparency reduces confusion, speeds up care navigation, and helps caregivers avoid delays or duplicative efforts. For caregivers juggling multiple responsibilities, having easy access to this information can significantly reduce stress and free up time to focus on work, family, or their own health—ultimately improving overall care efficiency and outcomes. Improving access to these data elements also supports greater competition among providers by enabling patients and caregivers to make more informed, value-driven choices. Conclusion Caregivers are essential to the healthcare system, yet remain underrecognized in data systems, care coordination, and policy design. By integrating caregiver identification into digital tools, improving access to relevant health information, and reducing operational burdens through improved data transparency, CMS can play a pivotal role in strengthening the caregiving infrastructure. Elevating caregiving as a core consideration in digital health strategy is both a practical and necessary step toward a more person- and family- centered care system. We commend your leadership and stand ready to support the Administration’s efforts through continued dialogue and data-driven input and welcome the opportunity to work with CMS and ASTP/ONC to help identify solutions that will better equip caregivers and patients to improve their health care.
