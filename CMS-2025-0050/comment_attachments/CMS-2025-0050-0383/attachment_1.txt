Patients and Caregivers
PC-8. What health data is readily available and valuable to patients or caregivers?
The type of health data readily available and valuable to patients and caregivers largely depends on the use
case. In general, individuals typically have access to basic demographic information, diagnoses, and—when
systems are well-integrated—treatment plans or prescription records. However, referral information is often
omitted, and care history is typically fragmented unless all providers use the same electronic health record
(EHR) system. Even then, immunization records and other preventive care data may not be accessible.
For data to be truly valuable, it must be understandable to the patient or caregiver. EMI’s work on the AHRQ
eCare Plan project underscored the importance of translating diagnoses and other clinical details into plain
language that is culturally and linguistically appropriate. Without these adaptations, even available data can
be functionally inaccessible. eCare Plan Project URL: https://www.ahrq.gov/ecareplan/index.html
In addition to clinical data, human services data—such as eligibility for nutrition assistance, housing status,
or care plans created outside of clinical settings (e.g., schools or foster systems)—are highly valuable but
rarely available or integrated. EMI's work supporting ACF and federal partners revealed that patients often
must repeatedly share the same story to access multiple benefits, increasing stigma and trauma.
Incorporating these human services elements into the CMS interoperability agenda would support whole-
person care and ease the burden on families.
a. What data is valuable but hard to access?
• Care coordination: Referral history, care team contact lists, and provider-to-provider
communications
• Chronic disease management: Longitudinal vital sign trends, lab values over time, and
remote monitoring data
• Caregiving support: Advance directives, medication schedules, shared care plan access,
and proxy permissions
Even when technically available, this data is often displayed in clinical or coded formats that are
difficult to interpret—particularly for individuals with limited health literacy or for whom English is
not a first language. There is a widespread lack of culturally and linguistically accessible formats,
which reduces the utility of health data for decision-making and care navigation.
c. What are the integration challenges?
Critical mechanisms to enable data sharing—such as proxy logins, agreements, governance
structures, role-based access controls or permissions, and shared infrastructure—exist but are
significantly underutilized. Patients and caregivers need more consistent, user-friendly, and
privacy-conscious ways to engage with their data across different health information systems and
care settings.
PC-10. How is TEFCA helping with access?
The Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) focuses primarily on
provider and payer data sharing. However, its design enables interoperability that improves
patient access indirectly—by ensuring that patients' care teams are aligned and fully informed,
reducing duplicative testing and improving timely treatment.
2

While TEFCA primarily addresses provider and payer exchange, expanding its scope or
integration points to include CBOs and human service agencies would enhance patient access.
EMI supports efforts to align TEFCA infrastructure with evolving standards for human services
data exchange, such as those developed by HL7’s HSS Workgroup and the Gravity Project.
PC-11. How are Health Information Exchanges (HIEs) helping with access?
HIEs are inconsistent with their support for application programming interfaces (APIs) and
exchange of usable data, largely due to inconsistent funding which also impacts the development
of meaningful guidance on implementation. In EMI's experience working with a large health
system, we’ve learned that the information the health system retrieved from the local HIE was
limited and often in an unusable format for clinical care.
PC-13. How can CMS encourage information blocking complaints? What would be the impact?
CMS should simplify and streamline the complaint reporting process. A clearer and easier
interface—especially for patients and caregivers—would increase reporting and promote
enforcement of information access policies.
Providers
PR-1. How can CMS encourage provider use of approved digital products?
Providers will adopt tools that are trustworthy, reinforce evidence-based practices, seamlessly
integrated, and reduce workload instead of adding to it—including their workflow. CMS should
invest in digital tools and APIs that support workflows outside traditional EHR systems—including
systems used by human service providers, community care hubs, and social care networks.
EMI’s experience across states (e.g., MO, CA, NY) highlights that providers are more likely to
adopt digital tools when aligned with reimbursement models, consent policies, and when those
tools support interoperability with non-clinical entities.
a. Obstacles include:
In EMI’s experience working with providers, some of the most common obstacles include:
• Disruption to workflow
• Concerns about liability
• Limited interoperability with electronic health records (EHRs)
• Absence of standardized vetting mechanisms
c. Responsibilities when recommending products?
When providers recommend or deploy digital health applications, it is critical to set clear
expectations for their use, especially tools designed for patients or caregivers. Providers and
developers must ensure users understand that these tools are not substitutes for clinical
judgment or medical care.
EMI applies standard best practices by including clear disclaimers in all consumer- or caregiver-
facing apps to reinforce appropriate use. For example, in the eCare Plan project, the following
disclaimer was included:
Disclaimer

constitute medical advice or professional services. The information provided
should not be used for diagnosing or treating a health problem or disease, and
those seeking personal medical advice should consult with a licensed physician.

PR-3. How important is full EHR data access?
Beyond EHR access, bidirectional data sharing with human services platforms is critical. EMI’s digital health
and standards implementation work underscores the need for CMS to support write-back functionality and
standardized exchange formats across systems that may not be EHRs but are crucial for care coordination
and enrollment in safety net programs.
a. Challenges:
EHR data can be unstructured or inconsistent due to the volume and other challenges that
healthcare and social care settings face with documentation or administrative tasks. Clinical notes
may also contain sensitive or contradictory information, requiring thoughtful parsing by other care
providers or the patient to ensure accurate interpretation.
c. Technical barriers:
In EMI’s experience working with healthcare systems, community-based organizations and
clinics, and public health agencies, a key barrier is limited write-back functionality for many
systems. This acts as a significant barrier because it hinders bidirectional data flow necessary for
closed-loop referrals, real-time care planning, and data exchange for the purpose of providing
whole person-centered care.
PR-8. How can CMS simplify clinical quality reporting?
CMS can simplify clinical quality reporting within EHRs while also encouraging real-time updates
in the following ways:
• Support the integration of chronic disease data sources (data streams, repositories or
registries, information systems)
• Promote alignment with evidence-based prevention
• Reduce administrative burden through automation and application of digital tools
• Focus on critical use cases within chronic disease prevention and value-based innovation
that address the highest impact and priorities (i.e. based on severity, cost, mortality data).
• Responsibly and transparently explore the application of tech solutions like avatars,
digital twins, and AI to reduce clinician and care provider burden.
• Emphasizing the importance of community partnerships as essential to whole-person
care to encourage buy-in and strengthen referral networks.
EMI recommends CMS build on lessons from human services data integration efforts and
streamline quality reporting by aligning data element requirements with other federal agencies.
Cross-agency coordination, including consistent identifiers and shared vocabularies, would
reduce duplication and enhance outcome measurement for individuals served across health and
human services sectors.

Payers
PA-3. How to encourage digital identity adoption?
To encourage digital identity adoption, EMI’s experience includes working with organizations to
establish clear standards for:
• Individual consent and identity ownership
• Caregiver permissions aligned with state and federal privacy laws
• Core data elements and purpose-of-use rules
• Interoperability mechanisms (e.g., QR codes, APIs) to embed digital ID into existing
health IT systems
Adoption of digital identity tools should include thoughtful rollout strategies and technical
guidance for developers, healthcare providers, and users of these tools. EMI recommends CMS
promote adoption of consent and identity mechanisms that work across sectors. Successful
models like Maryland’s CRISP Consent Tool show the value of cross-program, real-time consent
infrastructure. Digital identity tools must allow for multi-agency access and honor nuanced legal
constructs like minor consent and guardianship common in human services programs.
PA-5. How can payers help simplify provider quality data responsibilities?
Payers can simplify provider data responsibilities by:
• Promoting real-time, EHR-powered extraction of clinical quality data
• Investing in next generation reporting tools
• Aligning data collection with provider workflows to reduce duplication and fatigue
Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks
TD-1. What steps can CMS take to stimulate developer interest?
• Provide implementation guides tied to real-world use cases, including but not limited to
housing, child welfare, and nutrition services—and incentivize vendors to extend APIs
and apps to these contexts. EMI has developed such cross-domain use cases through
the Connecting for Better Health initiative.
• Create tipsheets and certification checklists informed by ASTP/ONC expertise
• Support communities of practice to reduce onboarding friction for developers
• Fund pilots to expand and validate HL7 eLTSS and other care plan standards for children
and youth served in non-clinical settings, such as school-based programs, foster care
systems, or juvenile justice.
TD-6. What interoperability functions does TEFCA uniquely provide?
EMI recommends that CMS explore how TEFCA can support secure, consented exchange with human
services data systems. Many CBOs and human service providers are outside the traditional TEFCA design
yet serve the same individuals and families as health systems.
TD-7. Has USCDI improved interoperability?
Yes, the United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) has enhanced consistency in data
exchange. However, continued expansion is needed to fully support behavioral health, social
care, and patient-generated health data. EMI proposes a glide path to support this, including
governance, business models, and alignment with existing taxonomies like 211 LA and
LOINC®/SNOMED® mappings.

Value-Based Care Organizations
VB-1. Incentives for APMs to use digital products?
APMs should be incentivized to use digital tools that bridge clinical and human service domains. EMI
recommends CMS tie incentive payments to capabilities like person-centered care planning, eligibility
verification, and closed-loop referral tracking across sectors. CMS should support person-centered
enrollment systems that prevent re-traumatization by enabling the reuse of previously collected data (with
consent). Standards-based care plans and narrative data sharing should be incentivized to support continuity
across programs.
VB-2. How can AI, analytics, usability, etc., be integrated into APM requirements?
Artificial Intelligence (AI), including Large Language Models (LLMs), can:
• Integrate data from EHRs, wearables, and public health sources
• Power virtual assistants and chatbots for patient communication
• Translate coding systems for cross-system compatibility (e.g., ICD-10 ↔ SNOMED CT)
• Use predictive analytics to model risk, optimize resources, and inform interventions
• Automate administrative tasks, allowing providers to focus on clinical priorities
Natural Language Processing (NLP) also enables structured analysis of clinical notes, expanding
the usability and reach of critical health information across platforms.
