HEALTHCARE DATA INTEROPERABILITY POLICY ANALYSIS
At Cognitive, we have witnessed the profound disconnect between the promise and reality of
healthcare data interoperability, a gap that reflects not just technical challenges but also
fundamental misalignments in how we approach healthcare transformation. Despite more than a
decade of federal investment and regulatory mandates, the vision of seamless, patient-centered
health information exchange remains frustratingly elusive, trapped between ambitious policy
frameworks and the messy realities of healthcare delivery, where competing priorities, resource
constraints, and entrenched workflows create implementation barriers that no amount of regulatory
language can overcome.
Introduction
The pursuit of healthcare data interoperability has become both our industry's greatest opportunity
and its most persistent source of frustration, representing a complex challenge that extends far
beyond technical specifications and touches every aspect of how we deliver, coordinate, and
experience healthcare in America. Cognitive has learned that interoperability isn't just a technical
issue, but a fundamentally a human problem disguised as a technical challenge. The most
sophisticated FHIR APIs and blockchain solutions mean nothing if they do not translate into better
experiences for patients navigating complex health conditions or clinicians trying to deliver
coordinated care.
Current Policy Goals and Objectives. Federal agencies have articulated remarkably
comprehensive visions for healthcare data interoperability that address every stakeholder's needs
on paper, yet these policy objectives often exist in isolation from the messy realities of healthcare
delivery, where the disconnect between policy aspiration and operational reality has created a
landscape where technical compliance frequently substitutes for meaningful progress. The most
troubling aspect we see is the tendency to treat interoperability as a destination rather than a
journey, with regulations specifying technical requirements and compliance timelines while
providing limited guidance on navigating the complex organizational, financial, and cultural
changes necessary to achieve meaningful interoperability.
Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) Strategic Framework. CMS has established
a comprehensive strategic framework that recognizes interoperability as fundamental to healthcare
transformation, promoting secure health information exchange to support better decision-making
and system efficiency with their vision of "a secure, connected health care system that empowers
patients and their providers" reflecting sophisticated understanding of interoperability as an
enabler of broader healthcare goals rather than an end in itself. However, the gap between CMS's
strategic vision and practical implementation reveals the limitations of top-down policy
approaches, where the agency's emphasis on FHIR APIs as the technical foundation, while
technically sound, can become an obstacle when organizations focus on compliance rather than
improving healthcare delivery and patient outcomes.
Office of the National Coordinator (ONC) Regulatory Initiatives. The Office of the National
Coordinator has pursued interoperability primarily through regulatory mechanisms stemming from
the 21st Century Cures Act, focusing on empowering patients with medical data access and
addressing information blocking through mandatory compliance requirements that represent a

significant shift from voluntary adoption to regulatory enforcement. What concerns us at Cognitive
about ONC's regulatory approach is its emphasis on preventing bad behavior rather than
incentivizing good outcomes. While information blocking regulations are necessary, they don't
adequately address the more common challenges of technical limitations, resource constraints, and

Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Comprehensive Data Strategy. HHS has
developed a comprehensive data strategy for 2023-2028 that extends beyond healthcare-specific
interoperability to encompass broader HHS data integration, identifying five critical priority areas
including cultivating data talent, fostering data sharing, and enabling whole-person care delivery,
with explicit connections to Cancer Moonshot initiative demonstrating how data interoperability
serves broader public health objectives. The challenge we see at Cognitive is that while this
comprehensive approach is conceptually sound, it requires levels of coordination and data
integration that current healthcare IT infrastructure and organizational capabilities cannot support,
suggesting that the strategy's identification of preparedness and incident response as anchor use
cases reflects recognition that crisis situations often reveal both the potential and the limitations of
our current interoperability capabilities.
Policy Gaps and Shortcomings. The most significant policy gaps we observe at Cognitive stem
from the disconnect between regulatory aspirations and implementation realities across diverse
healthcare settings, where federal policies work reasonably well for large, well-resourced
organizations but create compliance burdens without corresponding benefits for smaller practices,
specialty providers, and safety-net organizations. Perhaps more concerning is the framework's
limited attention to user experience and patient engagement, where regulations specify technical
requirements but provide minimal guidance on making these capabilities meaningful and
accessible to patients, as evidenced by consumer research revealing that over half of healthcare
consumers find accessing medical records "stressful and time consuming”.
Implementation Disparities Across Healthcare Settings. The reality of interoperability
implementation reveals stark disparities that undermine the vision of universal health information
exchange, with only 67% of psychiatric hospitals achieving certified electronic health record
(EHR) adoption compared to 86% of general acute care hospitals, while rural healthcare settings
and long-term care facilities face particularly acute challenges that current policy frameworks
inadequately address. These implementation disparities often reflect deeper organizational and
financial challenges that technical solutions alone cannot address, creating a fragmented healthcare
landscape where patients moving between different care settings encounter information silos that
compromise care coordination and safety.
Patient Access Barriers and User Experience Challenges. Consumer research reveals a
troubling disconnect between regulatory requirements for patient data access and actual patient
experiences. As stated above, over half of healthcare consumers reporting that accessing medical
records is "stressful and time consuming," while more than 40% express hesitation to switch
providers for fear of losing access to records and data. The fundamental design limitations of
patient portal systems, the primary mechanism for consumer health data access, reveal how
technical compliance can do little for user experience, with half of study participants reporting that
portals "aren't user friendly" and 63% experiencing problems with getting historical records.

Fragmentation in Health Information Exchange Participation. The voluntary nature of health
information exchange participation has created a patchwork of connectivity that fundamentally
limits the effectiveness of interoperability policies, with different HIE models operating with
varying technical standards, governance structures, and participation requirements that create a
fragmented landscape where even providers with certified EHR systems may not be able to
exchange data with all relevant care partners. The technical heterogeneity created by EMR vendor
implementations compounds these connectivity challenges, with hundreds of EMR systems each
having their own technical specifications, clinical terminologies, and unique customizations that
prevent true interoperability and data sharing across systems.
Technology Infrastructure Requirements and Standards. The technology infrastructure
requirements embedded in current interoperability policies represent both significant
achievements and persistent limitations in our approach to healthcare data exchange, with
regulations such as the HTI-1 final rule establishing unprecedented transparency requirements for
artificial intelligence while revealing the reactive nature of healthcare IT policy that struggles to
keep pace with technological innovation. At Cognitive, we've learned that successful technology
implementation requires not just technical specifications but also adequate time, training, and
support to help organizations navigate complex transitions without compromising care delivery or
operational stability.
HTI-1 Final Rule: Algorithmic Transparency and Certification Updates. The HTI-1 final rule
represents a significant advancement in addressing the emerging intersection of artificial
intelligence and healthcare interoperability, establishing unprecedented transparency requirements
for AI and predictive algorithms embedded in certified health information technology that
recognize the need for accountability mechanisms enabling clinical judgment and patient
advocacy. However, the challenge we see at Cognitive is that transparency requirements alone
may not be sufficient to address the complex ethical and practical challenges raised by healthcare
AI, as healthcare organizations need guidance, training, and support to effectively evaluate and
implement these technologies in ways that improve rather than complicate clinical workflows.
HTI-2 Final Rule: TEFCA Implementation and Regulatory Framework. The HTI-2 final rule
establishes the regulatory foundation for the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common
Agreement (TEFCA), creating new federal regulations that provide greater transparency and
implement provisions to support reliability, privacy, security, and trust within TEFCA,
representing a significant step toward standardized, nationwide health information exchange. The
rule's creation of the TEFCA Manner Exception represents a significant policy development that
could either facilitate or constrain data sharing depending on implementation, as it allows
healthcare providers and developers to limit fulfillment of health information requests to only
through TEFCA, which aims to create standardized pathways but raises concerns about potentially
limiting patient choice.
FHIR API Implementation and Standards Evolution. The widespread adoption of FHIR APIs
represents the most significant technical advancement in healthcare interoperability policy
implementation, with CMS regulations requiring payers to implement Patient Access APIs,
Provider Directory APIs, and Payer-to-Payer APIs using FHIR standards, which enable a
fundamental shift from proprietary data exchange methods to standardized, patient-controlled
access mechanisms. However, FHIR implementation has revealed significant challenges related

Impact on Value-Based Care and Patient-Focused Access. The transition to value-based care
models requires comprehensive patient data integration that extends beyond traditional clinical
information to encompass social determinants of health, care coordination activities, and outcome
measurements, with interoperability serving as "the backbone of successful value-based care
initiatives," yet current implementations often fall short of providing the comprehensive data
integration necessary for effective value-based care management. Cognitive has observed how the
fragmented nature of current health information systems creates particular challenges for value-
based care organizations serving complex patient populations, where the requirement to integrate
data from multiple sources often requires manual data reconciliation processes that are both time-
consuming and error-prone.
Value-Based Care Integration Challenges. Value-based care models require a level of data
integration and care coordination that current interoperability implementations struggle to support
effectively, extending beyond technical connectivity to encompass data quality, clinical workflow
integration, and outcome measurement capabilities that necessitate sophisticated health IT
infrastructure and organizational capabilities. The emphasis on API-based data access, while
providing technical connectivity, doesn't automatically address the care coordination challenges
that are central to value-based care success, creating a gap between technical data availability and
practical care delivery integration that continues to limit the effectiveness of these models.
Patient Access Implementation Gaps. Despite regulatory requirements for patient data access,
implementation has revealed significant limitations in both technical functionality and user
experience that undermine the vision of patient-centered care, with the MyHealthEData initiative's
goal of giving "every American control of their medical data" remaining largely unrealized in
practical healthcare delivery. The challenge is compounded by patient access to health
information, which often does not extend to the complete longitudinal health record that would be
most valuable for patient decision-making and care coordination. Many portal systems provide
access only to information generated within specific healthcare organizations, rather than
comprehensive health histories.
Persistent EMR and Data Sharing Challenges. Electronic Medical Record systems continue to
present fundamental architectural barriers to effective data sharing despite compliance with
interoperability standards, with the proliferation of EMR customizations and proprietary
extensions creating a landscape where hundreds of systems each have their own technical
specifications and unique customizations that prevent true interoperability. The focus on EMR
adoption rather than interoperability during the initial HITECH Act implementation established a
foundation of systems not designed with comprehensive data sharing as a primary objective, while
data quality and standardization issues represent persistent challenges that technical standards
alone cannot address.
Technical Architecture Limitations. The technical architecture limitations of current EMR
systems reflect fundamental design decisions made during an era when interoperability was not a

Policy Framework Inadequacies. Current regulatory frameworks, although comprehensive in
scope, contain significant gaps that limit their effectiveness in addressing real-world
interoperability challenges. Information blocking regulations primarily focus on intentional data
withholding but do not adequately address technical limitations, resource constraints, or workflow
incompatibilities that may effectively prevent data sharing. The regulatory emphasis on
standardized APIs and technical specifications has not been accompanied by sufficient attention
to implementation support, user education, and workflow integration, creating a gap between
technical compliance and operational effectiveness that continues to limit the practical impact of
interoperability policies.
Patient Experience Frustrations. Patient experiences with healthcare data access reveal
persistent frustrations that indicate fundamental limitations in current interoperability
implementations, with consumers reporting that accessing medical records remains "stressful and
time consuming" while many experience repeated testing or cancelled appointments due to lack of
access to test results. Patients often find themselves serving as the primary mechanism for
information transfer between providers, undermining the vision of seamless electronic health
information (EHI) exchange, while patient portal systems fail to present information in ways that
support decision-making and care management, with medical terminology and fragmented
information presentation creating barriers to meaningful engagement.
Current Federal and Industry Position on Patient-Controlled Health Records vs. Distributed
Model. The federal government and healthcare industry maintain complex positions regarding
patient control versus distributed health record models, with policy rhetoric emphasizing patient
empowerment while practical implementation reinforces organizational custody of patient health
information, as exemplified by the MyHealthEData initiative promising patient control while
maintaining structures that keep authoritative records within provider systems. Current federal
regulations focus primarily on providing patient access to copies of health information rather than
enabling patients to maintain authoritative health records, treating patients as consumers rather
than active participants in health record creation and maintenance, while industry resistance stems
from liability concerns, regulatory compliance requirements, and business model considerations.
Industry resistance to patient-controlled health records stems from multiple factors including
liability concerns, regulatory compliance requirements, and business model considerations that
create financial incentives for organizations to maintain control over patient data as a competitive
advantage.
Rationale for Provider-Controlled Records. Healthcare organizations justify maintaining
control over authoritative health records based on arguments related to clinical responsibility, data
integrity, and regulatory compliance that reflect legitimate concerns about patient safety and care
quality, with providers arguing they bear ultimate responsibility for clinical decisions requiring
control over supporting health information. Regulatory compliance requirements create practical

barriers to patient-controlled health records, as healthcare organizations must meet numerous

Emerging Technologies: Blockchain and Self-Sovereign Identity. Blockchain technology
presents promising solutions to fundamental challenges limiting patient-controlled health records,
particularly related to data integrity, security, and interoperability, with blockchain-based systems

verification and comprehensive audit trails. Self-sovereign identity technologies offer
complementary capabilities that could address authentication, authorization, and consent
management challenges, enabling individuals to store and manage health data in secure digital
wallets with granular control over access permissions, though these technologies also present
significant implementation challenges, including energy consumption concerns and the need for
new technical infrastructure.
Extended Episodes of Care Models. Extended episodes of care models represent a fundamental
shift from traditional fee-for-service approaches to comprehensive value assessment
encompassing all healthcare actors and factors contributing to patient outcomes, requiring
sophisticated data integration capabilities that extend far beyond current interoperability
implementations to capture social determinants of health, care coordination activities, and long-
term outcome measurements. Current interoperability standards provide some foundation for these
models but fall short of the comprehensive data integration required, while policy frameworks
must address attribution challenges, outcome measurement standardization, and risk adjustment
methodologies that account for the complexity of factors influencing health outcomes.
Technical and Policy Requirements for Comprehensive Care Valuation. Extended episodes
of care models require sophisticated data integration capabilities that extend far beyond current
interoperability implementations to capture social determinants of health, care coordination
activities, patient behaviors, and long-term outcome measurements, with technical infrastructure
needs including real-time data integration from multiple sources, predictive analytics capabilities,
and outcome tracking systems operating across extended time periods. Policy frameworks for
extended episodes must address attribution challenges, outcome measurement standardization, and
risk adjustment methodologies accounting for the complexity of factors influencing health
outcomes, requiring new regulatory frameworks that can accommodate multi-sector care
coordination complexity while maintaining accountability for outcomes and costs.
Innovation Requirements for Comprehensive Care Coordination. The implementation of
extended episodes of care models requires technological innovations that extend beyond current
health IT capabilities to encompass care coordination, patient engagement, and outcome prediction
tools. Artificial intelligence and machine learning technologies are essential for processing vast
amounts of data and identifying care optimization patterns. Blockchain and distributed ledger
technologies could play a crucial role by providing secure, auditable mechanisms for tracking care
contributions, outcome attribution, and payment distribution across multiple organizations and
time periods. Meanwhile, patient engagement technologies and self-sovereign identity solutions
enable patients to maintain control over their health information while participating in complex
care coordination activities.

Conclusion. The analysis of current healthcare data interoperability policies reveals a complex
landscape of ambitious objectives, significant achievements, and persistent challenges that
continue to limit the realization of patient-centered, interoperable healthcare delivery, with
fundamental gaps remaining between technical compliance and meaningful healthcare
transformation despite comprehensive federal policy frameworks and substantial regulatory
requirements. We believe that future progress will require more fundamental changes to healthcare
delivery models, including greater adoption of patient-controlled health records enabled by
blockchain and self-sovereign identity technologies, comprehensive care coordination systems
integrating social determinants of health, and extended episodes of care models aligning incentives
with comprehensive patient outcomes—innovations requiring not only technical advancement but
also new regulatory frameworks, incentive structures, payment models, and organizational
approaches that truly center patient needs and preferences in healthcare delivery and data
management.

manage your (or your loved ones) health needs, as well as the actions you should take?
a. What are the top things you would like to be able to do for your or your loved ones’
health that can be enabled by digital health products?
b. If you had a personal assistant to support your health needs, what are the top things

Patients and caregivers often struggle with fragmented, inaccessible health data spread across
multiple portals and systems. A truly effective, national, digital health services platform would
unify all medical records, from lab results and imaging studies to specialist notes and medication
histories, into a single, intuitive interface that is directly accessible by the patient at no cost. This
platform should be capable of both on-demand and automatic syncing with every healthcare
provider’s system, eliminating the need for patients to manually request or upload records. For
example, after a hospital discharge, the app would proactively aggregate rehab plans, new
prescriptions, and follow-up instructions into a timeline, flagging critical steps such as "Schedule
cardiac rehab within 14 days" or "Check potassium levels weekly." Data received from non-self-
systems should be labeled as such and meta data for each received data element should have its
complete provenance documented. Once current EMR vendors have the technical ability to
distinguish “self-generated” data from third-party data, patients must be allowed to enter their own
data or data that is currently not well documented in any existing EMR, to include, OTC
medications, vitamins, health goals, advanced directives, etc.
A top patient request is context-aware medication management and automated reconciliation.
Current apps offer basic reminders, but users want systems that explain “why” a drug matters
("This beta-blocker lowers your heart attack risk by 30%"), warn about conflicts ("Avoid
grapefruit with this statin"), and auto-refill prescriptions by analyzing usage patterns. For
caregivers managing complex regimens, dose-by-dose tracking with AI-driven alerts (e.g.,
"Missed insulin dose detected via connected pen") could prevent dangerous oversights.
Patients also desire intelligent care coordination tools. Imagine a "health assistant" that books
appointments based on provider availability, insurance coverage, and care gaps (e.g., "You’re due
for a mammogram—here are 3 in-network clinics with Saturday slots"). For loved ones, shared
task boards could assign responsibilities such as "Uncle Jim: Drive to chemo every Tuesday" or
"Sister Maya: Monitor Dad’s glucose readings." The app would facilitate secure communication
with care teams, translating medical jargon into plain language (e.g., "Your LDL is 120—let’s
discuss lowering it below 100").
Proactive health guardianship is another unmet need. Advanced apps could analyze wearable data,
EHR trends, and SDOH factors (e.g., local air quality) to predict risks. A parent might receive:
"Asthma alert: Pollen levels spiking tomorrow. Pack the inhaler for school and avoid outdoor
recess." For seniors, fall detection sensors could auto-alert family and 911 while displaying critical
data to EMTs (e.g., "Allergies: Penicillin; Current medications: Warfarin").
If given a "health assistant," patients would prioritize crisis-ready functionality. During
emergencies, the app should instantly share living wills, medication lists, and provider contacts

PC-4. What features are missing from apps you use or that you are aware of today?
a. What apps should exist but do not yet? Why do you believe they do not exist yet?
b. What set of workflows do you believe CMS is uniquely positioned to offer?
A critical gap in current digital health tools is the absence of truly unified patient portals that
transcend institutional boundaries. While apps such as MyChart allow access to records within a
single health system, they fail to aggregate data from Medicare, Medicaid, private insurers, and
specialty providers into a cohesive timeline. Patients managing chronic conditions often spend
hours reconciling conflicting medication lists from different EHRs, while caregivers struggle to
track appointments across unconnected provider portals. This fragmentation persists because EHR
vendors prioritize loyalty to health systems over patient needs, maintaining proprietary data
formats that resist integration. CMS is uniquely positioned to mandate a Federal Health Data
Aggregation Standard, requiring all federally funded providers to contribute real-time updates to
a government-hosted patient portal using FHIR R4.0.1. By leveraging its authority under the 21st
Century Cures Act, CMS could compel vendors such as Epic and Cerner to auto-populate this
portal with structured clinical data, claims histories, and SDOH indicators, without charging
patients for access. Recognizing that achieving such a goal would be a multi-year process, CMS
should mandate that that all data be semantically constrained using publicly accessible
vocabularies. Vendors currently unable to deliver completely structured data, such as notes, should
be required to provide context appropriate wrappers around the unstructured data to facilitate post-
receipt NLP and processing.
Another glaring omission is the lack of AI-powered care navigation tools that contextualize health
data for lay users. Existing apps might show lab results but rarely explain their implications (e.g.,
“Your A1C of 8.2 indicates poorly controlled diabetes—here’s how to adjust your diet”) or
automate next steps (e.g., scheduling retinal exams when glucose levels rise). These tools don’t
exist because developers face insurmountable barriers accessing comprehensive, real-time patient
data across disparate EHRs. CMS could solve this by expanding its Blue Button 2.0 API into a
National Health Data Utility, ingesting Medicare/Medicaid claims, provider-generated EHR data,
and wearable metrics into a secure repository that, again, patients would have no-cost access to.
Third-party apps that meet strict federal certification criteria (e.g., non-commercial use,
accessibility compliance) could then query this utility through standardized APIs, enabling
features such as predictive hypoglycemia alerts for diabetics or fall-risk assessments for seniors,
all while maintaining patient privacy through NIST IAL2 credentialing.
CMS should also pioneer crisis-ready health profiles that automatically share critical data during
emergencies. Current apps rely on patients to manually input allergies, medications, and advance

PC-7. If CMS were to collect real-world data on digital health products’ impact on health
outcomes and related costs once they are released into the market, what would be the
best means of doing so?
To systematically capture real-world data (RWD) on digital health products’ outcomes and costs,
CMS must leverage its regulatory authority to mandate a National Digital Health Evidence
Platform, integrated with the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA)
and Qualified Health Information Networks (QHINs). This platform would serve as a centralized
hub, requiring all certified digital health tools regardless of vendor to automatically report de-
identified patient outcomes, utilization patterns, and cost data via FHIR R4.0.1 APIs. By
embedding this requirement into TEFCA’s governance framework, CMS can ensure that every
interaction with a digital health product (e.g., medication adherence alerts, telehealth visits, remote
monitoring alerts) generates structured data streams accessible to researchers, policymakers, and
even patient’s themselves where appropriate.
The platform would aggregate three core data types:
• Clinical Outcomes: Pulled directly from EHRs via QHINs, including changes in biometrics
(e.g., HbA1c reductions), hospital readmissions, and adverse events.
• Patient-Reported Data: Captured through CMS-certified apps using standardized
PROMIS® measures, with voice-to-text functionality for low-literacy users.
• Cost/Utilization Metrics: Tied to Medicare claims via an expanded Blue Button 3.0 API,
tracking downstream savings from reduced ER visits or avoided complications.
