Using Professional Credentials Exchange’s expertise in secure sharing of practitioner data, we offer the following recommendations in response to RFI question PA-4: What would be the value to payers of a nationwide provider directory that included FHIR endpoints and used digital identity credentials? 1. Move beyond digital identity to include digital credentials. The true value of a nationwide provider directory lies not simply in its breadth or inclusion of FHIR endpoints, but in the trustworthiness, completeness, and ongoing verification of its data. A functional provider directory that serves as a single source of provider information requires a continuously verified representation of what a provider is authorized and qualified to d o, including licensure, privileges, specialties, network participation, and location data. While Login.gov, ID.me, and similar identi ty platforms confirm who is logging in, a directory built on verified digital credentials enables CMS to achieve broader interoperability goals. CMS should require the adoption and exchange of digitally signed, primary source verified credentials as a foun dational layer of any national provider directory initiative —ensuring that directories not only identify who a provider is but reliably represent what that provider is authorized and qualified to do. 2. Build directories on source -verified data, not self -attested updates. Directory errors are not caused by missing FHIR endpoints. They result from disconnected, outdated, or self - reported attributes that cannot be easily reconciled across organizations. Solving this requires a network of authoritative data issu ers—not a single centralized database —and a way to distribute verified data programmatically.
