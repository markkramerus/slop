I submit this response as a practicing emergency physician who sees daily the downstream e Ưects of fragmented health information and the ongoing barriers that prevent both patients and providers from fully realizing the promise of interoperability and patient access. Despite signiﬁcant regulatory progress, the real-world experience of patients and frontline clinicians still reﬂects profound gaps in data availability, data sharing, and care coordination. These gaps directly a Ưect clinical decision -making, patient safety, and system e Ưiciency across the full spectrum of care. I will focus my comments on the continuing clinical challenges that, in my experience, remain unresolved, and how CMS could further advance patient access, digital health, and care coordination by addressing these real-world pain points. Comments: PC-1 / PC-2: Patients Cannot Reliably Assemble a Usable Health Record  Patients presenting to the emergency department often arrive with only partial knowledge of their medical history, medications, procedures, or recent labs.  This is particularly problematic for medically complex patients, those with multiple specialists, recent hospitalizations, or those transitioning between care settings.  Even tech-savvy patients struggle to access multiple patient portals, retrieve records, or consolidate information into a coherent summary.  A uniﬁed record that aggregates data across systems would materially improve safety, help eliminate redundant testing, reduce unnecessary admissions and support better informed decision-making. PR-3: Incomplete Data Formats Limit Provider Utility  Even when data is technically "available," much of it is locked in PDFs, scanned documents, or proprietary formats not easily accessible in real-time through HIEs, PHRs or other similar services.  In emergent situations, time-sensitive access to accurate medication lists, allergies, recent imaging, or specialty consults is often unavailable or delayed.  True interoperability must include all data types, not just structured EHR ﬁelds. PR-4 / PR-7: Patient-Contributed Data Should Be Easier to Ingest 
 Many families attempt to assemble their own records to support emergency care, but we lack simple workﬂows to ingest those records on arrival.  In my experience, family- and patient-generated records can often be the most up- to-date and clinically relevant source of information, especially given the disconnected nature of d iƯering EMRs that often fail to maintain accurate, current lists of medications, hospitalizations, procedures, or active medical problems. PC-12 / PR-2: Digital Health Can Simplify Administrative Burden  The intake process for many care settings is still reliant on repetitive, duplicative paperwork that patients are required to complete over and over.  Digital tools that auto-populate intake forms based on patient-generated health data could reduce administrative burden, increase accuracy, and improve e Ưiciency across multiple healthcare settings. VB-3 / VB-4 / VB-11: Better Data Access is Central to VBC Success  Many of the avoidable admissions I see are linked to gaps in outpatient care coordination, fragmented specialist follow-up, missed care transitions or just plain unavailable/incomplete information.  Enabling patients and their care teams to access complete longitudinal records and allowing them to share tha t information e Ưiciently would directly support population health management, risk stratiﬁcation, and proactive care, reducing unnecessary ED visits, admissions, and costs.  Complex high-touch patients often experience the worst fragmentation and would beneﬁt most from personal aggregation tools. PC-5 / TD-17: CMS Should Encourage Innovation in Personal Health Records  The current ecosystem heavily favors large EHR vendors and payers who control data ﬂows.  Independent personal health record platforms, which serve the patient directly, should be incentivized and integrated into CMS policy frameworks to complement system-based data exchange e Ưorts.  CMS could support this innovation through grants, demonstration projects, and explicit recognition of patient-controlled aggregation tools as valid interoperability participants. PC-14 / PR-9: Simpliﬁed Digital Identity Credentials Are Critical 
 The proliferation of multiple logins across providers creates a barrier both for patients and for care teams trying to access shared data.  A trusted federal credential (e.g., Login.gov) could simplify patient access while maintaining security standards. Conclusion From the front lines of clinical care, I can attest that we continue to see avoidable harm, delays, and ine Ưiciencies directly attributable to fragmented health data. The core regulatory framework of TEFCA, FHIR, and CEHRT provides the necessary technical foundation, but the real-world clinical experience remains far behind. CMS has the opportunity to:  Embrace patient-centered aggregation platforms as a complement to system-based data sharing.  Expand data access beyond claims into broader clinical records.  Simplify ingestion of patient-generated health records into clinical workﬂows.  Support widespread adoption of single digital identity credentials.  Reduce administrative burden through smarter digital intake tools. Ultimately, improving patient access is not simply a data-sharing problem, it is a care quality problem. CMS leadership can help ensure that the policies intended to empower patients actually deliver meaningful results at the bedside. Thank you for your consideration.
