With Certify as the backbone, individual health plans would
be able to access and contribute to a continuously verified
national provider directory via open APIs. This shared
infrastructure would eliminate the need for each plan to
maintain its own fragmented, siloed system, and would
replace costly, redundant workflows with a real-time,
trusted source of provider truth.
Key benefits to payers include:
Lower administrative costs: Payers today invest significant
resources in collecting, verifying, and maintaining provider
data, both for compliance and operational use. By plugging
into Certify’s infrastructure, these efforts are streamlined,
and duplicative verification processes are reduced or
eliminated entirely.
Reduction in out-of-network claims: Inaccurate provider
directories are a major driver of unintended out-of-network
care. With a real-time national directory that accurately
reflects plan participation at the location and provider level,
patients are routed correctly, and costly surprises are
avoided.
Fewer rejected claims and denials: Many claim rejections

stem from simple mismatches in provider data, such as an
outdated TIN, NPI, or practice affiliation. Certify ensures all
this data is up to date and consistent across the payer
ecosystem, reducing friction and improving first-pass claim
acceptance.
Reduction in downstream waste: Clean provider data
upstream means fewer issues downstream, including fewer
member grievances, less time spent on appeals, reduced
call center volume, and better member satisfaction overall.
When layered with a digital identity credentialing system,
this infrastructure becomes even more powerful. Providers
can verify their own information through authenticated
channels, ensuring that the data submitted is accurate,
attributed, and audit-ready. This significantly reduces the
risk of fraud, enhances trust in the data, and provides
payers with a much clearer chain of custody for every
update.
In short, the combination of Certify’s real-time
infrastructure, accessible via APIs, and a verified digital
identity credentialing layer creates a scalable, accurate,
and secure foundation for managing provider data
nationally. For payers, this means reduced costs, improved
operational efficiency, better compliance, and a more
trusted experience for both providers and members.

Governance and Access
The directory itself should be governed through a
public-private collaboration. CMS or ONC could define the

data standards, interoperability rules, and governance
principles, while infrastructure and directory operations
should be managed by a technically capable partner.
Certify is well positioned to serve in this role, already
managing real-time provider data with robust lineage,
survivorship logic, and cross-stakeholder configurability.
Cost Structure
Basic access—particularly for patients, non-profit entities,
and regulatory use cases—should be free and open. For
commercial users, high-volume integrations, or
value-added services, a tiered pricing model tied to actual
system cost drivers would support the sustainability of the
infrastructure while still prioritizing equitable access.
Importantly, the operational cost of maintaining this national
directory would be more than offset by the cost savings it
enables—through reduced administrative overhead, fewer
denied or misrouted claims, lower fraud risk, and improved
patient and provider satisfaction. In this way, the directory
would ultimately pay for itself.
In short, a nationwide directory of FHIR endpoints, built on
Certify’s infrastructure, would not only improve access to
health information but also reduce administrative friction,
eliminate duplication, and increase trust in the healthcare
experience. It would serve as a shared foundation for
innovation, efficiency, and better outcomes across the
entire healthcare ecosystem.

VB- What are the essential data types Successful value-based care (VBC) arrangements require
4 needed for successful a broad ecosystem of accurate, actionable data. While
participation in value-based care much of the focus is often on clinical outcomes and cost
data, these models fundamentally rely on one foundational
arrangements?
layer: accurate, real-time provider data. Without it, patient
attribution breaks down, quality measurement is flawed,
care coordination falters, and incentives become
misaligned.
From Certify’s perspective as the provider data
infrastructure powering the next generation of digital health
tools and operations, the following data types are essential
to value-based care success:
Verified provider identity and credentials - Accurate NPI,
taxonomy, licensure, and board certifications are necessary
to validate that the provider is qualified and eligible to
participate in a given model. This data also supports
downstream integration with credentialing, contracting, and
compliance systems.
Provider affiliations and practice locations - Understanding
which groups, facilities, and networks a provider is affiliated
with, and at which locations they actively see patients, is
critical for accurate patient attribution, care coordination,
and reimbursement under value-based arrangements.
Network participation data - Knowing which health plans
and products a provider participates in, and under what
terms, ensures patients are routed correctly and that
attribution models reflect the real-world delivery landscape.
Demographic and cultural competency attributes -

Language proficiency, ethnicity, and cultural training or
focus areas enable more personalized and equitable
patient-provider matching, which is especially important for
closing gaps in care and addressing social determinants of
health under VBC models.
Historical data lineage and change tracking - To support
auditability, compliance, and performance tracking,
value-based care programs need visibility into when and
how provider data changes, across systems and over time.
Certify’s infrastructure provides this through automated
delta tracking, survivorship logic, and end-to-end data
lineage.
When these provider data elements are made available
through a real-time, API-driven infrastructure like Certify,
they serve as the backbone for a wide range of
value-based workflows—including accurate patient
attribution, risk adjustment, network adequacy validation,
quality measure assignment, and care navigation.
In short, clinical and claims data may power the engine of
value-based care, but it’s accurate provider data that lays
the track. Without it, VBC programs are built on unreliable
foundations. Certify is purpose-built to ensure this
foundation is solid, scalable, and accessible across the
healthcare ecosystem.
