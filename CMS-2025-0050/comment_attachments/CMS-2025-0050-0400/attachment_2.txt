Dates and materials subject to change. 2 Patients and Caregivers Patient needs PC-1 What health management or care navigation apps would help you understand and manage your (and your loved ones) health needs, as well as actions you should take? People and caregivers struggle daily with the fragmented healthcare system interacting with multiple patient portals, insurance websites, out-of-date provider directories, lack of transparency in healthcare costs, confusion about their health plan coverage/benefits, and stand-alone digital solutions that don’t talk to each other. The proliferation of smartphone apps and on-demand services across every industry has raised the bar for what a “good” experience should look like in healthcare. Particularly after the pandemic accelerated telehealth and digital health usage, patients are now demanding personalized support available 24/7. Surveys consistently show that nearly 76% of consumers want more control over healthcare costs, and 91% find transparency tools helpful (htt1) in managing their care. Further, 61% of consumers consider online appointment scheduling extremely or very important, and 77% prefer to complete pre-visit forms digitally (htt2), which are clear indicators that people desire and will use tools that empower them to self-serve and navigate care on their own terms. There is a clear need for an easy-to-use unifying navigation layer that can sit on top of these disparate systems and provide a one-stop shop experience to help people live a healthier, happier life. This need is not theoretical; it’s measurable. The U.S. healthcare system wastes an estimated $760 to $935 billion annually (htt3), with a significant portion attributed to administrative complexity, inefficient care coordination, and preventable service duplication. Over 89% of Americans (htt4) search for health information online before consulting a provider, reflecting a massive need for trusted, accessible tools to answer health questions and guide next steps. Even something as basic as finding an in-network provider remains a challenge because as much as 30% of provider directory data is inaccurate or outdated (htt5), leading to delays in care and poor consumer experiences. Moreover, patients often have no way to verify whether a specific provider, at a specific location, is covered under their plan, or what that the visit or procedure will cost. These ambiguities can prevent care entirely.
