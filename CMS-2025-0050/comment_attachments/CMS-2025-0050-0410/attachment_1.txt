As a healthcare quality leader, I’ve had the privilege and challenge of spearheading
digital health transformation and witnessing up close the opportunities for improvement.
I sincerely applaud CMS for initiating this RFI regarding the health technology
ecosystem. I submit these reflections to help guide CMS and ONC toward a future
where digital tools are truly care-enabling.
1) To truly advance quality and safety in healthcare, we must move beyond static,
retrospective measures and instead incorporate the lived experiences of patients as
active contributors to improvement. Current tools like HCAHPS, while valuable, offer
a limited and often delayed view of care experiences, missing key dimensions such
as emotional safety, real-time responsiveness, and other nuances of care that are
necessary to really improve care. Patients are more than survey respondents - they
are experts in how care feels and functions across settings. CMS should support
infrastructure that enables continuous, bidirectional feedback loops - integrating
patient stories, PROMs (Patient-Reported Outcome Measures), PREMs (Patient-
Reported Experience Measures), and digital feedback channels into the core of
quality measurement. Embedding patient voice will not only improve outcomes for all
Americans, it will rebuild trust in care.
Policy recommendation:
• Develop a mechanism to collect beneficiaries experiences and outcomes through
multiple channels (online, phone/text, etc). Such a mechanism is vital to
understanding/addressing care gaps and identifying brights spots (and learning
why they are bright spots). Such a tool could inform initiatives across HHS.
2) Patients deserve real control and understanding of their health data, as well as a
prominent voice that shapes healthcare quality. Currently, patients often struggle to
navigate multiple portals just to access labs or visit summaries. A truly person-
centered ecosystem should provide access to the full spectrum of data that
influences health, including prior authorization status, social needs, and benefits
information. Expansion of Blue Button to include these elements could enable a new
generation of apps that help people manage their care - not just when they’re sick,
but every day. Furthermore,
Policy recommendations:

• Expand Blue Button application programming interfaces (APIs) to include
Medicare Advantage encounter data, prior authorization status, and community
referrals.
• Support Bulk FHIR export for patients switching providers or apps, ensuring
portability.
3) For providers, technology should reduce friction -not introduce more. Promising
digital tools often cannot have the impact they should due to barriers in
implementation specific to electronic health records. CMS could support better
alignment by requiring modular, standards-based APIs, promoting ingestion of
community and wearable data, and ensuring clinical utility is prioritized in
certification criteria.
Policy recommendations:
• Mandate EHRs support modular, plug-and-play APIs for external tools, including
SMART-on-FHIR launch contexts
• Require ingestion and export of patient-generated health data from wearables,
home devices, and community sources via FHIR Device and Observation
resources.
• Include support for FHIR Document Reference and Optical Character
Recognition of scanned legacy files to reduce paper-based silos.
• Incentivize clinical decision support that integrate real-time data into provider
workflows (e.g., maternal early warning systems, risk flags).
4) Many brilliant startups wither due to opaque hospital integration costs or prohibitive
certification requirements. CMS has an opportunity to change this. A national open
marketplace for certified digital health tools - paired with technical assistance for
early-stage developers - would be a meaningful step toward democratizing access to
innovation. Additionally, monitoring and publishing common interoperability “pain
points” would bring needed transparency to the developer experience and promote
trust across the ecosystem.
Policy recommendations:
• Create a CMS-led open marketplace of certified digital tools with transparency
around integration capabilities and TEFCA compliance.
• Establish a developer technical assistance program for early-stage or under-
resourced vendors serving high-need populations.
• Mandate vendor reporting of integration fees and timelines as a condition of
certification.

• Publicly track and publish patterns of interoperability blocking, similar to the HHS
Transparency in Coverage rule’s approach.
As someone inspired by MAHA’s mission to elevate innovation that promotes health
and transparency, I would be honored to support this work more directly. The future of
care should be personal, proactive, and digitally fluent - and I would be delighted to
contribute to shaping that future.
