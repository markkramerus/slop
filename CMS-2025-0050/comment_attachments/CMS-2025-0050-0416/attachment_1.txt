PC-8. In your experience, what health data is readily available and valuable to patients or their
caregivers or both?
• PC-8 Response:
o While a substantial volume of patient health data is technically accessible to patients and
caregivers, it remains fragmented across providers, payers, and legacy systems. Information from
multiple providers is rarely considered holistically—each provider tends to focus on the data
they directly collect or observe, with limited integration of prior records or external inputs. This
creates a fractured clinical picture. As a result, the patient—who typically lacks medical
training—is often placed in the untenable position of reconciling conflicting information, filling
in gaps and making critical decisions based on incomplete or inconsistent inputs. For instance, a
cardiologist may recommend a treatment plan without full visibility into recent neurological
evaluations or medication changes made by a primary care physician. Each provider sees only a
slice of the patient's health story, and the patient is left to assemble the full narrative. To move
beyond this fragmented state, health data must be transformed from isolated “data points” into
“integrated knowledge”. This requires a shift toward modern health information representation
platforms—specifically, ontology-governed personal clinical knowledge graphs. These
systems organize multimodal data (clinical notes, labs, medications, imaging, patient-reported
outcomes, etc.) into structured, contextual formats that can be analyzed holistically.
For example, consider a Medicare beneficiary managing diabetes, hypertension, and mild
cognitive impairment. Today, her glucose logs are stored in a mobile app, her medication history
sits in her pharmacy record, and her recent neurologist notes exist only in scanned PDFs. No
provider sees the full picture, and the patient must navigate her care with limited insight. With a
unified knowledge graph, however, this information could be consolidated, semantically aligned,
and queried using AI. A prompt like “What treatment adjustments have improved A1C for

c. What specific opportunities and challenges exist to improve accessibility, interoperability, and
integration of clinical data from different sources to enable more meaningful clinical research and
generation of actionable evidence?
• PC-8c Response:
o Improving accessibility, interoperability, and integration of clinical data across systems requires
a foundational shift in how we structure and interpret health information. Much of today’s health
data exists in structurally interoperable formats—such as standardized HL7 messages or FHIR
resources—but lacks the semantic coherence necessary for systems to truly understand and
reason over it. This leads to major challenges when multiple systems attempt to integrate data for
meaningful clinical use. The core issue is not just how data is exchanged, but how it is
interpreted. Different providers may use different terminology, omit contextual metadata, or
represent the same condition in incompatible formats. Consequently, integrating data from
multiple sources often results in duplication, ambiguity, or even contradiction—making it
difficult to support advanced clinical decision-making, research, or patient self-management.
What is needed is a semantic layer that unifies these fragmented inputs and translates raw data
into a coherent, machine-readable model. Ontology-governed personal clinical knowledge
graphs offer such a structure. By organizing patient data as interconnected concepts and
relationships—rather than flat lists or free-text notes—these systems enable real-time reasoning,
cross-domain queries, and intelligent summarization.
For example, in a value-based care setting, a patient’s medication adherence data (from a
pharmacy), glucose readings (from a device), and recent discharge notes (from a hospital EHR)
might all be available—but siloed. A knowledge graph can harmonize these data streams,
surface interactions (e.g., medication changes impacting glucose control), and answer context-

rich questions like: “Has this patient’s cognitive function declined in conjunction with a new
medication regime?” This is the difference between data being stored and data being used. The
opportunity lies in elevating structured health records into operational knowledge systems that
support timely, context-aware, and personalized insights for all stakeholders—patients,
providers, researchers, and policymakers.
To realize this opportunity, CMS and partners must:
§ Incentivize adoption of semantic data representation standards and architectures.
§ Align certification requirements with semantic interoperability—not just transport
protocols.
§ Support APIs and platforms that expose longitudinal patient models rather than static
records.
§ Support the development of provider-centric applications that are based on fully
integrated knowledge graphs that incorporate the breadth of the patient’s clinical
experience and eliminate clinical bias from that provider’s direct experience.
§ Support the development of patient-centric applications to provide an actionable
framework for decision making that is based on wholistic information that is presented in
a way that enables the patient to make effective decisions in a fragmented clinical
environment
§ Prioritize open ontologies and modular knowledge graph frameworks that can evolve
with clinical evidence.
Ultimately, this is not just an IT transformation—it is a CARE TRANSFORMATION.
Enabling meaningful integration of clinical data will unlock insights, reduce duplication, identify
patterns, and empower all parties to make better, faster decisions at scale.

• VB-1 Response:
o Digital health management must fundamentally start with optimal data integration,

EHR’s) impose structural constraints, they do not define logical rules or knowledge. Meaning is
not inherent in a (EHR) database — it must be provided by ontologies, reasoning engines, and
knowledge management systems. Consider the difference between a graph database, a knowledge
base, and a knowledge management system (KMS):
§ A graph database stores relationships explicitly but does not infer new ones.
§ A knowledge base contains structured knowledge with defined ontologies (semantics) and
reasoning rules.
§ A knowledge management system (KMS) integrates various sources, facilitates reasoning,
and supports decision-making by capturing and processing knowledge dynamically.
In a knowledge management system (KMS):
§ The system integrates multiple knowledge sources.
§ It processes updates dynamically, ensuring that derived insights are maintained over time.
