Dear Secretary Kennedy and Assistant Secretary Keane,
On behalf of the Commonwealth of Massachusetts' Executive Office of Health and
Human Services (EOHHS), I am writing to offer comments on Request for Information
(RFI); Health Technology Ecosystem 1 issued jointly by the Centers for Medicare &
Medicaid Services (CMS) and the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy / Office of
the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ASTP/ONC), on May 16,
2025. EOHHS appreciates the opportunity to provide input on the RFI and more
generally the dedication of the agencies to soliciting and considering input from the
various stakeholders impacted by these issues.
Introduction
EOHHS is the largest Secretariat in the Massachusetts state government, and our
efforts are focused on the health, resilience, and independence of the one in four
1 2025-08701.pdf

residents of the Commonwealth we serve. EOHHS provides access to medical and
behavioral health care, substance use disorder treatment, long term services and
supports, and nutritional and financial benefits to individuals and families with low
incomes. EOHHS is also the single state agency responsible for the administration of
the Commonwealth's Medicaid and Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP)
programs, collectively referred to as MassHealth. MassHealth provides comprehensive
health care coverage for over two million Massachusetts residents, including 40% of our
children and 60% of our residents with disabilities. MassHealth’s mission is to improve
the health outcomes of our diverse members and their families by providing access to
integrated health care services that sustainably and equitably promote health, well-
being, independence, and quality of life. The Massachusetts Health Information
Highway (Mass HIway) is the state-designated Health Information Exchange (HIE)
operating under EOHHS. The Mass HIway has evolved from a provider of foundational
services (such as Direct Messaging and the Public Health Reporting Gateway) into a
state-level policy and coordination framework that supports the development and
integration of interoperable health data services. Our work is grounded in regulatory
alignment, stakeholder engagement, and a commitment to advancing digital equity
across the healthcare ecosystem.
In response to the RFI, EOHHS offers targeted comments on the role of HIEs in
supporting interoperability, patient access, and digital innovation, as facilitators of
policy-aligned infrastructure and enablers of data exchange in underserved sectors.
A. Patients and Caregivers
PC–11. How are health information exchanges (HIEs) currently helping to advance
patient access to health information in the real world?
a. Value, Availability, and Accuracy: The Mass HIway facilitates secure clinical data
exchange across Massachusetts among hospitals, behavioral health providers, long-
term services and supports (LTSS), home health agencies, and ambulatory providers.
While not directly patient-facing, these transactions lay a foundation for coordinated,
patient-centered care and reduce duplicative services. As the health ecosystem shifts
toward consumer-mediated exchange, the Mass HIway is positioned to support policy
frameworks and coordination efforts that enable integration with patient-authorized apps
and digital health tools, by working with certified vendors, ensuring alignment on
consent and identity verification standards, and fostering participation by under-
connected providers.
b. Recommendations: CMS should recognize and support HIEs as policy and
convening entities that can bridge patient and provider facing capabilities. Funding
mechanisms should allow HIEs to act as shared service enablers for Fast Healthcare
Interoperability Resources (FHIR)-based patient access Application Programming
Interface (API)s, personal health record integration, and identity verification tools for
patient-authorized exchange, in support of Medicare and Medicaid populations, rural or
behavioral health providers, and other sectors with low digital capacity.

c. High-Performing Models: Massachusetts’ combination of the HIway Connection:
Requirement and the Statewide Event Notification Service (ENS) Framework
exemplifies a successful policy-technology hybrid model. Rather than aggregating data
such as Admit, Discharge and Transfer (ADT)s, the Statewide ENS consolidates ADT
distribution under a centralized governance structure. Acute care hospitals, and soon,
psychiatric hospitals, are required to send ADT messages to at least one certified ENS
vendor. Certified vendors then reflect and share ADTs with each other, enabling
subscribing recipients to receive notifications for their patient panels through a single
connection point. CMS should promote similar policy-aligned frameworks to streamline
delivery of ENS and other health information services while preserving vendor diversity
and market choice.
d. Ongoing Role of HIEs: HIEs remain essential for supporting local interoperability
among providers who are underserved or excluded from national networks. Their role
as trusted, regionally governed intermediaries is vital in reaching providers that may not
participate directly in the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement
(TEFCA) or commercial networks. While TEFCA is designed to accommodate HIEs as
participants or sub-participants under Qualified Health Information Networks (QHINs), in
practice, many regional and state HIEs face technical, operational, and financial barriers
to participation.
Notably, TEFCA participation is voluntary, and the current framework does not include
dedicated federal funding or clearly defined roles to enable or sustain HIE involvement,
especially for those serving Medicaid, LTSS, and behavioral health sectors. These
providers often lack the internal capacity or resources to engage in national exchange
independently. Without formal incentives or policy mechanisms, there is a risk that
existing digital disparities will be exacerbated.
CMS should establish formal roles, guidance, and dedicated funding pathways to
support HIE participation in TEFCA, either directly or through sub-participant
arrangements. Doing so would ensure broader representation of underserved providers
and help operationalize CMS goals for equity, access, and data-driven care
coordination across the full healthcare continuum.
Enabling Patient-Centered Innovation Through HIE Policy and Infrastructure
As previously stated, while the Mass HIway does not currently provide direct patient-
facing capabilities, we strongly support CMS’s vision to empower Medicare and
Medicaid beneficiaries through expanded access to digital health tools. To help achieve
this vision, CMS should provide funding and policy support to enable regional HIEs like
the Mass HIway to serve as coordinating and enabling infrastructure for patient-directed
exchange. Specifically, HIEs can help by:
• Facilitating integration with secure, standards-based applications that aggregate
data from multiple providers and payers, including through TEFCA and FHIR-
based APIs.

• Supporting the deployment of patient-authorized data access services by
certified vendors, with shared infrastructure for consent management and identity
verification.
• Acting as trusted intermediaries for patient data access in underserved sectors
such as behavioral health, LTSS, and home health, where individual
organizations may lack the technical capacity to implement these solutions
independently.
Additionally, the Mass HIway provides policy-backed infrastructure that supports use
cases relevant to patient-centered innovation, including:
• Coordinated delivery of ADT-based event notifications (via the Statewide ENS
Framework) to enable timely follow-up, care transitions, and home-based care
planning.
• Enabling secure, standards-based messaging between community-based
providers and hospitals to support integrated care.
CMS should recognize HIEs as essential convening and policy partners in the
development of the digital health ecosystem envisioned in the RFI, particularly to
address interoperability gaps in complex, community-based care settings where
patients face the most barriers to access.
B. Data Access and Integration
PC–10. How is TEFCA currently helping to advance patient access?
As TEFCA is still early in its implementation, its impact on patient access remains
limited, especially for providers not yet connected to a QHIN. However, its potential to
enable nationwide, standards-based patient access will depend heavily on how well it
integrates with existing state and regional infrastructure.
The Mass HIway is not a QHIN but is well positioned to serve as a facilitator of sub-
participation, enabling smaller or resource-constrained providers, especially in the
Medicaid, LTSS, and behavioral health sectors, to gain access to TEFCA-based
exchange. In this intermediary role, the Mass HIway could support coordinated consent
management, help validate identity and organize shared services to make TEFCA
participation feasible for underserved segments of the healthcare market.
To that end, CMS should:
• Provide implementation funding and matching incentives to help state HIEs serve
as TEFCA on-ramps for non-QHIN providers.
• Clarify and standardize policies around patient authentication, consent capture,
and identity verification across TEFCA participants to promote interoperability
and trust.

• Enable HIEs to participate in or support patient-authorized longitudinal data
aggregation, even for providers who are not directly TEFCA-connected.
Standards such as SMART on FHIR (Substitutable Medical Applications and Reusable
Technologies on Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) and Bulk FHIR (Flat FHIR)
offer scalable, patient-centered approaches to digital access and should be increasingly
prioritized for modern integration needs. At the same time, IHE standards such as XCA
(Cross-Community Access) and XDS (Cross-Enterprise Document Sharing) continue to
play an important role in many state and enterprise networks, including those supporting
public health and clinical document exchange. CMS should encourage hybrid models
that allow legacy and modern standards to coexist, while promoting convergence
toward API-driven, real-time workflows where feasible.
Finally, while TEFCA represents a critical national framework, CMS should continue to
support robust state and regional alternatives. States like Massachusetts that have
established policy frameworks and operational networks should not be penalized for
pursuing interoperable, standards-aligned pathways outside of or adjacent to TEFCA. In
fact, these networks should be recognized as essential building blocks in the broader
ecosystem of nationwide exchange.
C. Providers
PR–2. What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective utilization
of the most useful and innovative applications for physician workflows, such as quality
measurement reporting, clinical documentation, and billing tasks? How could these
obstacles be mitigated?
One of the primary barriers to actionable health data exchange is the quality of patient
data entered into provider source systems (EHR, EMR, case management systems).
While USCDI is an advance in standardizing data, Medicare and Medicaid programs still
struggle with the quality of data received from the provider community. This impacts
real-time transactions such as Event Notification Systems, asynchronous transactions
such as patient claims data (as required by the Interoperability and Prior Authorization
final rules), and federal systems such as the Transformed Medicaid Statistical System.
Starting a conversation around a system of provider and vendor guidance, incentives,
and penalties focusing on data quality in addition to the establishment of standardized
transaction process (FHIR, TEFCA, Direct Messaging, ENS) would enable the
healthcare industry to overcome a longstanding barrier to healthcare data exchange.
PR–6. Is TEFCA currently helping to advance provider access?
a. Examples: As TEFCA is still early in its implementation phase, most Massachusetts
providers are not yet experiencing tangible improvements in data access. While a few
national QHINs have gone live, the majority of local providers are not yet connected to
these networks, and many key use cases, such as referral coordination or transitions of
care, remain reliant on state-level solutions like Direct Messaging and Event

Notifications. However, TEFCA’s potential to facilitate query-based access to out-of-
state records could help address longstanding gaps in interstate interoperability.
b. Recommendations: CMS should encourage QHINs to prioritize provider-facing use
cases that improve clinical workflows, such as referral summaries, prior encounter
retrieval, care coordination tools, and medication reconciliation. Additionally, CMS
should offer financial support to defray the costs of onboarding providers, including
small, rural, behavioral health, and safety-net organizations that may lack the resources
to participate directly in TEFCA.
c. Other Options: State HIEs remain critical infrastructure for provider data access and
for use cases not yet fully supported through TEFCA. HIEs are often more responsive to
local regulatory contexts, provider networks, and care delivery models. CMS should
ensure that state exchanges can participate in TEFCA as sub-participants or trusted
intermediaries and should offer clear technical and policy guidance to enable this
pathway.
d. Redundancies: The proliferation of overlapping networks, including national
exchange frameworks, regional HIEs, vendor-led ecosystems, and payer networks, can
create confusion and inefficiencies for providers. CMS should promote alignment and
convergence around TEFCA-compatible APIs, while preserving the flexibility of local
solutions that meet the needs of specific communities or care settings. Reducing
duplicative participation burdens through unified consent models, common endpoints, or
shared certification criteria would help lower barriers to effective exchange.
PR–7. What strategies can CMS implement to support providers in making high-quality,
timely, and comprehensive healthcare data available for interoperability in the digital
product ecosystem? How can the burden of increasing data availability and sharing be
mitigated for providers? Are there ways that workflows or metrics that providers are
already motivated to optimize for that could be reused for, or combined with, efforts
needed to support interoperability?
Recent CMS and industry efforts have largely focused on promoting FHIR Standards
which support real-time clinical and administrative data exchange. Asynchronous data
exchange standards such as Direct Messaging (Direct Secure Messaging » DirectTrust
) have largely been left unaddressed even though a significant portion of healthcare
data exchange is conducted using asynchronous batch transactions such as the Direct
Messaging Standard. The use cases supported by asynchronous data exchange form
the backbone of a number of administrative and clinical data flows that are not easily
supported by the FHIR Standard transactions. Massachusetts suggests that CMS add
asynchronous data transfer transactions to its standardization efforts.
D. Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks
TD–6. What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform?

TEFCA establishes a common, federally endorsed legal and governance framework
that enables trusted exchange across independently governed networks. Its “network-
of-networks” model, facilitated through designated Qualified Health Information
Networks (QHINs), provides a scalable way to standardize identity assurance,
operational practices, and access rights across national participants. TEFCA's most
unique function is its role in enabling cross-network interoperability, bridging otherwise
siloed systems operated by vendors, payers, and regional networks. For HIEs like the
Mass HIway, TEFCA offers a potential backbone to scale interconnectivity beyond the
state. However, realizing this potential will depend on sufficient support for sub-
participant engagement, including funding and technical assistance.
TD–7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange, and what
are its limitations?
United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) has improved the consistency and
clarity of data elements exchanged across systems, especially in support of national
programs and certified APIs. However, its current scope remains too limited to meet the
needs of value-based care, behavioral health, LTSS, and social care coordination.
Notably, elements such as social determinants of health (SDOH), behavioral health
screening results, and referrals to community-based organizations are not sufficiently
represented. The USCDI+ program shows promise for addressing these gaps, and
CMS should encourage and fund stakeholder engagement in expanding use case-
specific datasets. HIEs can support implementation of extended USCDI+ domains by
helping normalize and exchange new data classes across diverse providers.
TD–8. What are the most effective certification criteria and standards under the ONC
Health IT Certification Program?
The FHIR API and SMART on FHIR criteria have been especially effective in
standardizing access pathways for data sharing and application integration. These
should continue to be core elements of certification. Future iterations of the certification
program should expand to include:
• Bi-directional exchange capabilities for event notifications, referrals, and care
plans.
• Consent and authorization services that enable patient-mediated exchange
across organizational boundaries.
• Support for EHI Export and real-time data access via APIs, particularly for
longitudinal data.
• Digital identity verification standards that facilitate scalable patient and provider
access to health information across platforms.
E. Value-Based Care Organizations
VB–3. What are essential health IT capabilities for value-based care arrangements?
Essential capabilities to support value-based care (VBC) include:

• Real-time Event Notification Services based on ADT data to track care transitions
and support timely intervention.
• Bi-directional care coordination platforms that enable sharing of care plans,
assessments, social needs, and provider notes across organizations.
• Data normalization and aggregation services to harmonize information from
multiple EHR systems and data sources.
• Analytics tools that support risk stratification, identification of care gaps,
performance measurement, social determinants tracking, and risk adjustment of
capitation rates.
• Robust and dynamic data visualization capacity.
• Platforms that support closed-loop referrals to providers of various services in a
pre-defined catalog or resource database, whether they be specialty physical
health services, behavioral health services, long-term supports or services, or
services to address health-related social needs, such that the referral-receiving
entities are able to share with the entities whether services were provided and
the impact of those services. Ideally, such platforms can support integration with
eligibility/enrollment systems to streamline service eligibility checking prior to
service delivery.
o To support development of a robust and interoperable ecosystem
for these closed-loop referral platforms, ASTP/ONC should
consider establishing a certification framework for these platforms,
like ONC’s Health IT Certification Program
• Platforms that allow providers to understand referral-receiving capacity of
“downstream” organizations (e.g., skilled nursing facilities, inpatient behavioral
health facilities) and to allow those downstream organizations to “claim” available
referrals, in support of efforts to reduce the amount of time in higher-cost settings
spent waiting to transition to lower-cost settings.
In Massachusetts, the Statewide ENS Framework, administered through a policy
directive, facilitates consistent and scalable ADT sharing across acute care hospitals
and certified vendors. The Mass HIway coordinates this framework as a statewide
policy lever to improve care continuity, especially for Medicaid and safety-net
populations. This approach supports value-based arrangements by reducing
fragmentation, enabling earlier interventions, and providing a shared technical and
operational foundation for providers.
CMS should support similar state-level policy-aligned infrastructure models, including
ENS frameworks and shared data services that help providers participate in VBC
without duplicative investment.
VB–4. What are the essential data types needed for successful participation in value-
based care?
The following data types are critical for successful implementation of VBC programs:

• ADT messages with timestamps, facility identifiers, and patient encounter
metadata.
• Structured care plans and assessments, especially those aligned with chronic
care, behavioral health, and home-based services.
• Claims-based cost and utilization data, including encounter history and
procedure-level granularity.
• Pharmacy and laboratory data, including fills, results, and turnaround times.
• SDOH indicators, referral status, and care team directories including contact
information for community-based organizations.
The Mass HIway can support the collection and distribution of many of these data types
through its ENS framework, Direct Messaging infrastructure, and Public Health
Reporting Gateway, as well as through future coordination efforts around statewide data
integration and normalization.
Conclusion
EOHHS appreciates the leadership of CMS and the ASTP/ONC in advancing a more
connected, equitable, and patient-centered digital health ecosystem. As Massachusetts’
state-designated Health Information Exchange, the Mass HIway is uniquely positioned
to serve not only as a provider of core HIE services, but as a policy and coordination
framework that enables trusted data exchange across diverse care settings. We look
forward to continued collaboration with CMS, ASTP/ONC, and our national and regional
partners to expand data access, support TEFCA implementation, enable value-based
care, and ensure that the benefits of digital health transformation reach all communities,
including those historically underserved by existing infrastructure.
