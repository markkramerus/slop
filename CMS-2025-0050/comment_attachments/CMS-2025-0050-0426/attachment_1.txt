5Gratitude for the Vision We thank the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) for issuing this important Request for Information and for inviting the healthcare community to offer insight into how we might accelerate progress toward a truly value-based, person- centered, and outcomes-driven healthcare system. The timing of this request could not be more critical—or more promising. We are at a historic inflection point where policy vision, stakeholder will, and digital capabilities can finally converge to deliver the transformation we have long envisioned. Now is the best time in our nation’s healthcare journey to take a holistic approach that integrates design thinking, shared accountability, and cross-stakeholder alignment across both policy and technology ecosystems. Rather than isolated reform efforts, we must organize complementary and scalable systems of care that are co- designed, co-governed, and co-measured across payers, providers, community partners, and most importantly, patients and caregivers. At the heart of this transformation are local health ecosystems—the foundational building blocks of real, measurable change. Whether city, county, or state, these ecosystems share many characteristics but must also be treated as unique. Each deserves a tailored blend of aligned incentives, shared accountability, and operational autonomy to succeed. By acting locally and learning iteratively, CMS can catalyze scalable and sustainable outcomes nationally. Executive Summary: Technology as a Catalyst for Patient Engagement and Improved Outcomes in a More Efficient and Effective US Healthcare Ecosystem The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) and the Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ONC), through this Request for Information (CMS-2025-0050-0001), have clearly signaled a strategic pivot: from viewing interoperability and digital infrastructure as regulatory requirements, to recognizing them as core enablers of patient engagement, care navigation, and improved health outcomes. At the heart of this RFI lies an urgent and important question: How can we leverage scalable, standards-based technology to empower Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers to make informed decisions and achieve better health— while reducing friction and inefficiencies across the ecosystem? Our response embraces that vision. We believe the future of healthcare lies in a stakeholder-aligned, efficient and effective, information-driven, interoperable, patient-centered digital ecosystem, where every touchpoint—clinical, operational, or administrative—is designed to enhance the experience of care, improve access and use of the right information at the right time, and reduce disparities in outcomes.
6Alignment – The Foundational Accelerator The U.S. healthcare system is highly fragmented and structurally misaligned across stakeholders, and this fragmentation is a core reason why many of the goals of value-based care, equity, and patient-centered innovation remain elusive despite decades of policy initiatives and technology investments. Adding more technology without first achieving stakeholder and operational alignment risks worsening fragmentation, increasing burden, and missing the very goals the technology is meant to advance—namely, better outcomes, greater equity, and lower cost. Before adding more tech, we must align the people, workflows, policies, and incentives that technology is supposed to serve. What Do We Mean by Fragmentation and Misalignment? 1. Fragmentation Across Stakeholders Stakeholder Group Fragmentation Examples ProvidersOperate on disparate EHR systems, with siloed care delivery and documentation PayersUse proprietary rules for prior auth, quality, and risk; limited transparency across plans Patients/CaregiversMust navigate multiple portals, plans, providers, and apps to manage their care Technology VendorsCompete on closed systems and non-interoperable solutions Public Health & Community ServicesDisconnected from clinical workflows, despite clear relevance to outcomes 2. Misalignment of Incentives Volume vs. Value: Many providers are still reimbursed under fee-for-service, while payers attempt to drive value-based models—resulting in conflicting operational incentives. Risk vs. Reward: Providers often bear the documentation and care coordination burden without receiving proportional rewards.
7Data Ownership vs. Data Use: Patients often lack clear access to or control of their data, even though it’s critical for engagement and self-management. Innovation vs. Reimbursement: Digital health and AI tools may improve outcomes but struggle to scale without a direct reimbursement pathway or VBC tie-in. Why This Matters for the CMS RFI and Broader Reform Efforts CMS’s RFI implicitly acknowledges this fragmentation by: Asking how to simplify access to data across payer and provider lines. Seeking input on digital identity to avoid multiple logins and fractured patient experiences. Exploring bulk FHIR APIs and TEFCA as foundations to stitch together a more coherent data infrastructure. Emphasizing care navigation and patient engagement—functions made difficult precisely because of system fragmentation. CMS can lead systemic alignment through: 1. Practical Cross-Stakeholder Interoperability Standards Local Deployments Required use of Da Vinci, Gravity, and TEFCA frameworks by all CMS-participating plans and providers. Endorsement of shared FHIR infrastructure for real-time patient data exchange. 2. Payment Model Integration CMS aligning risk adjustment, Stars, and quality metrics with digital reporting standards, to harmonize incentives. Incentives for providers that implement closed-loop workflows with performance analytics or efficiency and effectiveness digital tools not only collect but also act appropriately on data. 3. Shared Patient Engagement Infrastructure Fund, endorse and incentivize public-private partnerships to build, broadly deploy and utilize shared mobile platforms with digitally-enabled patient navigators that work across health plans and systems. 4. State and City-Based Pilots to Model Alignment Encourage geographic alignment pilots and rollouts (including aligned High-Needs ACO + Medicaid Managed Care + Hospital at Home) to prove what whole-system coordination looks like when incentives and tools are shared, used and measured.
8Conclusion Yes, our healthcare system is fragmented and misaligned—and this is not just a policy observation; it's a lack-of-design problem that digital infrastructure with aligned incentives can begin to fix. This RFI is a pivotal opportunity to: Examine the fragmentation and its root causes clearly, Propose alignment strategies, And support infrastructure that enables and supports shared responsibility with accountability and rewards for outcomes—across every stakeholder in the ecosystem. Specifically, we recommend CMS focus its investments and regulatory guidance in the following priority areas: 1. Alignment Across Local Stakeholders Thoughtful and structured projects that kickoff and define the current state, desired state and clear steps and incremental value roadmaps to improved multi-stakeholder value based care aligned operations. Ensure that the needs of patients, care-givers, care teams, align with and are addressed by the goals and measures of risk scoring, quality measures and appropriate cost and resource utilization efficiently delivered by local health automated digital ecosystems. 2. Supportive Patient and Caregiver Experience Mobile-first, secure tools that integrate medical records, care plans, scheduling, and educational resources in a single platform. Real-time updates and two-way communication between care teams and patients. 3. Local Digital Tools with Services that Reduce Friction Technologies that align with Da Vinci, Gravity, and TEFCA frameworks to automate quality reporting, close care gaps, and streamline prior authorization—all while supporting the patient experience. 4. Data Integration that Feed Analytics to Drive Outcomes Incorporating patient-reported outcomes, SDOH data, and remote monitoring directly into care team workflows and quality analytics. Making this data actionable in the moment—whether through clinical decision support, automated follow-ups, or personalized engagement prompts.
95. Scalable Infrastructure and Trust Frameworks Adoption of digital identity solutions to reduce login burden, increase security, and support cross-organizational care coordination. Investment in scalable FHIR-based APIs and Bulk Data strategies to enable real-time learning health systems. In summary, we believe CMS has a unique opportunity to lead a transformational shift: to move from data compliance to information-enabled care, from fragmented tools to orchestrated digital experiences, and from retrospective quality measurement to real- time engagement that delivers measurable results. We are confident that with thoughtful guidance, strategic partnerships, and a relentless focus on the patient and caregiver experience, CMS can realize its vision of a truly equitable, efficient, and outcomes-driven digital health ecosystem. Greatest Areas of Opportunity to Facilitate VBC Performance 1. Enhanced Interoperability & Data Access Standardized APIs and Bulk FHIR: Accelerating adoption of Bulk FHIR capabilities will enable providers, payers, and value- based care organizations to access comprehensive patient-level data sets quickly and reliably, improving population health management. Encouraging standardized API implementation across all stakeholders can streamline data exchange, reducing administrative burdens. Nationwide Directory of FHIR Endpoints: A centralized provider directory with validated FHIR endpoints could significantly improve efficiency, reducing the complexity and costs associated with managing multiple, disparate data connections. 2. Advanced Digital Identity Solutions Unified Identity Management: Implementing robust, interoperable digital identity solutions (e.g., Login.gov, ID.me, CLEAR) can reduce fragmentation by enabling seamless access to patient data across multiple providers and payers. This directly reduces friction for patients, providers, and payers, improving care coordination and patient engagement, critical components of successful VBC. 3. Comprehensive, Actionable Data Integration Expanding USCDI and TEFCA Utilization:
10Broadening the United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) to include richer, structured clinical data would support more sophisticated analytics, real-time decision support, and better management of care transitions. Leveraging TEFCA more strategically to support interoperability use cases, such as event notifications and timely data exchange, would significantly enhance patient care management capabilities. Full EHI Accessibility: Enabling APIs to access the entirety of Electronic Health Information (EHI) beyond current minimal datasets will support comprehensive analytics and predictive modeling required for proactive population health strategies. 4. Simplified Quality Measurement and Reporting Unified Quality Reporting Framework: Creating a standardized, consolidated approach to clinical quality data submissions using Bulk FHIR APIs could drastically reduce provider administrative burdens. This would allow providers to reallocate resources toward care improvement rather than data reporting, incentivizing adoption of VBC arrangements. 5. Leveraging Advanced Analytics and AI Population Health Analytics and AI-driven Care Coordination: Encouraging broader integration of advanced analytics, predictive modeling, risk stratification, and AI-driven insights into VBC programs could drive significant improvements in care outcomes. CMS support for specific advanced technology capabilities as part of certification requirements can drive consistent adoption and effective usage at scale. 6. Enhanced Patient and Caregiver Engagement Digital Health Management Applications: Promoting CMS-endorsed or reviewed digital health products tailored specifically for Medicare beneficiaries can empower patients, improve health literacy, and strengthen patient-provider engagement. Enhanced engagement will directly contribute to improved outcomes and performance metrics under VBC models. 7. Refined ONC Certification Requirements for Value-Based Care Targeted Certification Criteria: Revising ONC Health IT Certification to prioritize value realization from interoperability, population health management tools, and digital quality measurement capabilities directly 
11tied to VBC performance metrics will align technology investments with VBC success criteria. Flexible Technology Requirements for APMs: Allowing flexible, use-case-specific certification requirements based on clinical practice areas and the resource constraints of smaller, rural providers can support broad-based adoption without compromising care quality. Strategic Recommendations for Maximizing Impact: Focus on Achievable and Measurable Goals with Cross-stakeholder Process and Systems Alignment first, this will create a success and operational execution framework to guide and coordinate actives for continuous incremental progress and value achievement. Focus on interoperability and data standardization next, as foundational infrastructure changes will create broad ripple effects. Prioritize digital identity to streamline patient-provider-payer interactions, significantly impacting engagement and operational efficiency. Streamline and consolidate reporting requirements via simplified technology solutions to reduce the burden on providers and accelerate adoption. Integrate advanced analytics and AI-driven tools specifically designed to enhance risk management and care coordination to boost clinical outcomes rapidly while reducing Care Team burden. Engage stakeholders early and consistently, ensuring VBC solutions meet practical operational needs and receive active buy-in from providers, payers, and patients. By strategically prioritizing these areas, CMS and ASTP/ONC can meaningfully accelerate Value-Based Care adoption with value realization, delivering better health outcomes and improved healthcare economics nationwide. Alignment of Stakeholders, Processes and Supporting Systems Why Prioritize Alignment First? Reduced Friction and Waste: Operational alignment reduces duplication, errors, administrative burdens, and delays in critical workflows. Faster Realization of Value-Based Outcomes: Clearer and cleaner operational collaboration accelerates the benefits from accurate risk reporting, Stars improvement, and MSSP savings. Improved Data Quality and Analytics:
12Standardized data and streamlined processes significantly enhance predictive modeling and population health analytics. Higher Provider and Payer Engagement: Smooth operational integration fosters buy-in, compliance, and proactive participation from both sides. Alignment between payer and provider operations and systems is foundational to successfully realizing incentives around accurate risk reporting, CMS Stars performance, and MSSP cost controls. Before substantial improvements in outcomes can be consistently achieved, this alignment must be prioritized. Here’s a structured approach to factoring operational and systems alignment into your overall strategy: Step-by-Step Framework to Align Payer and Provider Operations and Systems: Step 1: Establish Shared Objectives & Vision Mutually Agreed Outcomes: Risk-score accuracy, quality metrics (Stars), cost controls (MSSP), patient experience. Alignment on Incentives: Clearly defined financial and quality-based incentives for both payers and providers. Jointly Defined KPIs and Metrics: Co-create and agree upon a set of operational, clinical, and financial metrics. Step 2: Current-State Assessment & Gap Analysis Systems Inventory and Data Mapping: Comprehensive mapping of payer and provider systems, interfaces, data sources, and existing standards (FHIR, X12, HL7). Operational Workflow Assessment: Identify friction points, redundancy, and inefficiencies in payer-provider workflows. Gap Analysis: Clearly document where interoperability, data integration, and operational collaboration gaps exist.
13Step 3: Leverage and Expand FHIR Accelerator Initiatives Implement Da Vinci Standards: Leverage Da Vinci’s clinical data exchange (CDex), gaps-in-care management, risk adjustment (RA), and prior authorization (PAS, CRD, DTR) standards to simplify provider- payer data exchanges. Integrate Gravity SDOH Standards: Standardized collection and exchange of social determinants data to accurately reflect patient risk and inform quality initiatives. Step 4: Establish Shared Infrastructure & Data Frameworks Common Data Governance and Standards: Adopt or co-create data dictionaries, terminologies, and interoperability standards. Joint Data Platform Approach: Consider cloud-based, neutral infrastructure (e.g., Microsoft Azure, AWS HealthLake, Google Cloud Healthcare) to facilitate secure, governed data exchange and analytics. Unified Provider Directory and Identity Management: Common FHIR endpoint directory; standardized patient identity management (e.g., CLEAR, ID.me). Step 5: Operational Workflow Optimization Process Reengineering: Joint payer-provider teams redesign workflows, especially for risk coding, prior authorization, and clinical documentation submission. Automated and Real-time Data Exchanges: Automation of routine processes (e.g., prior auth, risk adjustment submissions, quality reporting) via APIs. Closed-loop Feedback Systems: Real-time or near-real-time feedback loops from payers to providers to quickly identify and resolve data-quality issues or coding gaps. Step 6: Pilot and Demonstrate Value Targeted Pilot Projects: Start with controlled pilots around specific value-based arrangements, demonstrating measurable operational and clinical improvement.
14Rapid Learning and Iterative Improvement: Use pilot learnings to refine infrastructure, workflow, and data exchanges before full-scale implementation. Step 7: Strategic Incentive Alignment Alignment of Payment and Operational Incentives: Co-develop contracts and shared savings arrangements incentivizing the alignment and data-sharing behavior that achieves desired operational improvements. Transparent Performance Visibility: Jointly accessible dashboards to track real-time operational, quality, and financial metrics, enabling proactive management and accountability. Step 8: Scale and Sustain Joint Governance Structure: Ongoing joint operational and technical governance committees to maintain alignment, set priorities, and resolve conflicts. Continuous Improvement Culture: Establish a continuous feedback mechanism with stakeholders to keep improving alignment, outcomes, and systems integration. Expansion of Proven Methods: Scale successful pilots and practices to broader provider networks and payer organizations. Think National While Acting Local Considering the complexity of healthcare ecosystems, regulatory changes, and interoperability demands, it would be significantly more effective to implement progressive changes via a phased, city- or state-based approach, with a common due date as the endpoint. Here’s the detailed rationale: Rationale for City/State-Based Phased Implementation: 1. Manageable Complexity Local Customization: Healthcare delivery and payer-provider dynamics vary widely by geography. City/state-level implementations allow localized adjustments based on specific market needs, resources, and maturity. Reduced Risk: Breaking implementation into manageable geographic segments allows iterative learning, reducing overall systemic risk and enabling corrections before broader rollout. 2. Incremental Learning & Improvement Rapid Feedback Loops: City/state pilots provide opportunities to quickly test and learn from implementation methods, refine technology standards (e.g., Gravity, Da Vinci), and identify operational barriers. Progressive Refinement: Best practices, templates, and guidelines developed from initial pilots can streamline subsequent implementations, enhancing efficiency and effectiveness. 3. Resource Allocation & Support Focused Resources: Limited technology resources (skilled implementers, system integrators, interoperability specialists) can be effectively deployed, avoiding dilution across a simultaneous nationwide rollout. Improved Vendor Coordination: Healthcare IT vendors and consultants can better support incremental rollouts rather than spreading thinly across a national "big bang" event. 4. Stakeholder Engagement and Adoption Local Leadership Buy-in: Localized implementation encourages active engagement of city/state-based provider networks, regional payers, and governmental agencies, which promotes buy-in and accountability.
19Cultural Readiness: City-based rollouts can better address local cultural norms and workflows, fostering smoother adoption and sustainability. 5. Scalability & Repeatability Proven Implementation Model: Successfully completed city/state-level implementations can serve as proof-points and reference models, creating repeatable playbooks that significantly streamline later rollouts. Gradual Capacity Building: The phased approach allows gradual training and capacity building, enhancing overall system readiness and reducing disruption to daily healthcare operations. 6. Policy and Compliance Flexibility State-Level Regulations & Policies: Health regulations vary significantly between states. City/state implementations provide flexibility to incorporate local regulatory variations and ensure compliance. Adaptability: Allows faster adaptation to state Medicaid variations, waivers (e.g., CalAIM, NYS 1115), and local policies, preventing costly misalignments with federal regulations. Recommended Implementation Roadmap: Phase 1: Early Adopter Cities/States Select diverse, strategically valuable cities/states.
