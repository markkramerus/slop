PR-3: Importance of Full EHR Data Accessibility
▪ Current challenges in accessing different data formats:
o Fragmentation of data types: Scanned documents, faxes, free-text notes,
and structured fields often reside in siloed modules within EHR systems
or are stored in proprietary formats, preventing unified access
o Legacy infrastructure: Many health IT systems lack support for modern
data standards (e.g., FHIR), limiting seamless integration and extraction
of diverse data formats
o Inconsistent metadata and provenance: Without unified tracking, it's
difficult to reconcile the origin, version, and context of data, especially
from external or historical sources
▪ Impact on patient care quality:
o Incomplete clinical picture: Clinicians may miss critical insights (e.g.,
allergies noted in scanned intake forms or historical trends in free-text
progress notes), leading to diagnostic errors or suboptimal treatment.
o Delays in care coordination: Difficulty accessing full data hinders timely
handoffs and comprehensive treatment planning across providers,
disproportionately impacting rural and underserved communities
▪ Technical barriers to full data accessibility:
o Incompatible data representations: Data stored in non-interoperable
formats (PDFs, images, proprietary schemas) require advanced parsing or
manual entry, increasing errors and costs
o Lack of standardization: Absence of universally enforced schemas across
systems makes real-time data exchange complex
o Scalability constraints: Traditional architectures struggle to support real-
time access and traceability at scale across distributed care networks
▪ Cost or privacy implications of making all data formats interoperable:
o Cost: Converting legacy data formats into interoperable structures
involves upfront investment in data transformation tools and staff
training. However, long-term benefits include reduced duplication,
streamlined audits, and improved billing accuracy
o Privacy: Ensuring HIPAA and FISMA compliance when exposing historical,
unstructured data (e.g., scanned notes) necessitates robust access
controls, encryption, and traceability mechanisms like those in code626’s
framework626 platform
▪ Priority level compared to other interoperability needs:
o High Priority: True interoperability mandates not only exchange of
structured clinical fields but all patient-relevant data, including
unstructured and scanned records. In both urban and rural settings,
especially during disconnected or degraded environments,
comprehensive data access is foundational for care continuity
▪ Recommendation: Adoption of a data framework like 626’s that ensure lossless,
traceable, and format-agnostic data movement will play a pivotal role in meeting
CMS' objectives for a unified digital health ecosystem. These tools bridge the

technical and operational gaps that currently fragment patient data across care
landscapes

o Implement consistent OAuth2-based patient authentication and consent

TD-3: Digital Identity Implementation
▪ Challenges
o User burden and access barriers: Patients with low digital literacy or
limited access to technology may struggle to obtain and use IAL2/AAL2
credentials
o Integration complexity: EHRs, health apps, and provider portals must
adapt to federated identity workflows, requiring engineering and
compliance resources
o Trust alignment: Differing levels of assurance (LOA) across credential
providers may hinder cross-system interoperability
▪ Benefits
o Enhanced security and fraud prevention: Strong digital identity reduces
identity theft, unauthorized access, and medical record mismatches
o Streamlined user experience: Enables single sign-on (SSO) across systems,
including government and third-party health services
o Trust in data exchange: Verifiable identities improve confidence in cross-
network patient matching and record sharing
▪ Impact of Requiring IAL2/AAL2 Credentials
o Significantly improves resistance to phishing, credential stuffing, and
session hijacking
o Supports Zero Trust principles and compliance with EO 14028 and NIST
SP 800-63-3 guidelines
o Enhances record linking accuracy across payers, providers, and apps by
tying data to verified identities
o May delay adoption among smaller providers or vulnerable populations
without support mechanisms

▪ Impact of Mandatory Use of OpenID Connect (OIDC)
o Standardizes identity workflows: Ensures all third-party apps and portals
follow a common, secure, interoperable protocol
o Facilitates consent and audit: OIDC scopes and tokens improve control
over data sharing, supporting TEFCA and HIPAA compliance
o Accelerates innovation: Reduces vendor-specific barriers, allowing digital
health startups and public tools to integrate easily
▪ Recommendation: CMS and ONC should encourage adoption of OIDC-compliant,
IAL2/AAL2 digital identities with incentives and technical assistance, while
maintaining fallback options to avoid exclusion of underserved populations.
Digital identity (ID.me, Login.gov) is already integrated into the code626 FHIR
auth gateway. We support IAL2/AAL2 credentialing out of the box
