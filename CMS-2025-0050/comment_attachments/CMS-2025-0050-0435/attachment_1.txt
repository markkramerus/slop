Responses to Key RFI Questions
Patient and Caregivers
PC-5. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in
these digital health products?
Role:
CMS plays a critical role in evaluating digital health products for efficacy, quality, and health
outcomes—not as a gatekeeper for coverage, but as the standard-setter to accelerate
innovation. As the gold standard for clinical and policy guidance, CMS influences both
commercial payer policies and how technology companies design and implement their products.
A CMS review process could provide validation and signal alignment with national health
priorities, encouraging greater adoption and trust among providers, payers, and patients.

Criteria:
● Interoperability across CMS, provider systems, and patient-facing tools
● Demonstrated impact on health outcomes (quality measures, patient-reported outcomes)
● Input and usability for both patients and caregivers
● Equity of access and inclusivity across patient populations
● Data security and compliance with federal and state privacy standards
Adoption Enhancement Recommendations:
● Clear, centralized federal guidance on reimbursement for digital health products
● Transparent communication loops between CMS, state agencies, and providers
● Real-time access to policy changes and rate adjustments
● User-friendly digital tools and dashboards for patients and caregivers
● Consistent educational initiatives to build digital literacy and trust
These steps would promote transparency, alignment, and trust, leading to better patient
engagement and more consistent outcomes.
Changes to enable timely access to high quality CMS and provider generated data on
patients
● Implementation of real-time adjudication systems that collect data at the point of care
● Centralized, role-based data dashboards accessible to CMS, enrolled providers,
patients, and payers
● Standardized data-sharing protocols and service-level agreements across states,
providers, and systems
● Mandated interoperability and data portability between CMS platforms and provider
EHRs
● Incentives for timely, accurate data entry and exchange
● Expansion of value-based care agreements that reward data transparency and
outcomes
These changes would reduce fragmentation, accelerate informed decision-making, and
enhance care coordination across the healthcare ecosystem.
PC-7. If CMS were to collect real-world data on digital health products' impact on health
outcomes and related costs once they are released into the market, what would be the
best means of doing so?
CMS should establish a centralized, interoperable data infrastructure to serve as a single source
of truth for digital health impact measurement. Key steps include:
● Standardized data schema: Require vendors to report using common standards (e.g.,
FHIR, USCDI) to enable consistent and comparable outcomes reporting.

● Centralized clearinghouse: Contract with or create a neutral third-party vendor to
collect, validate, and analyze data across platforms.
○ This includes data that has not historically been accessible; e.g., prior
authorizations, credentialing etc.
● Utilization-based reporting: Track real-world usage and outcomes using standardized
metrics and formats, enabling predictive analysis at scale
● Full-loop interoperability: Ensure seamless integration with EHRs, claims systems,
and patient-facing tools.
● Bidirectional feedback loops: Share insights with vendors and providers to support
ongoing product improvement and clinical integration.
As a first step, CMS should audit the current system to identify where interoperability breaks
down, incorporating provider, patient, and vendor feedback. The findings should inform updated
standards, safeguards, and accountability measures to reduce errors, waste, and fraud while
improving patient outcomes.
PC-8. In your experience, what health data is readily available and valuable to patients or
their caregivers or both?
Basic clinical visit summaries and EOBs are typically available post adjudication, but often lack
context or clarity for patients and caregivers to act on meaningfully in a timely manner
Credentialing and eligibility data are extremely valuable but fragmented—often siloed across
multiple systems, even within the same payer (e.g., eligibility vs. claims). This makes real-time
access and synthesis difficult. Other hard-to-access but high-value data includes:
● Accurate provider directories and enrollment data
● Total cost of care and service-level pricing
● Outcome metrics and status of corrective action required
● Provider availability and eligibility verification
● Prior authorization information
Specific sources that would be valuable:
● Eligibility and enrollment data: Enables real-time understanding of benefits and helps
prevent care disruptions.
● Prior authorization and credentialing timelines: Standardizes access expectations,
Reduces administrative burden and prevents delays in access to care.
● Remediation and compliance data: Identifies quality gaps and supports ongoing
improvement.
Opportunities and challenges:
The main challenge is a lack of interoperability and standardization across systems. Even when
APIs exist, data is often incomplete, outdated, or hard to integrate with.

However, there is a significant opportunity for CMS to partner with vendors already working to
unify these systems into user-friendly, actionable dashboards—combining monetary and clinical
data to support real-time decision-making and policy alignment. Vendors like us are
well-positioned to support CMS as a conduit for clean, integrated, and accessible
data—leveraging existing infrastructure such as TEFCA to empower patients, caregivers, and
providers alike.

PR-7. What strategies can CMS implement to support providers in making high-quality,
timely, and comprehensive healthcare data available for interoperability in the digital
product ecosystem? How can the burden of increasing data availability and sharing be
mitigated for providers? Are there ways that workflows or metrics that providers are
already motivated to optimize for that could be reused for, or combined with, efforts
needed to support interoperability?
CMS should establish a centralized, “source of truth” grounded in standardized metrics, clear
definitions, and actionable data-sharing guidelines. To minimize provider burden, interoperability
requirements must align closely with existing clinical and administrative workflows—particularly
within EHRs, scheduling, billing, and compliance systems providers already rely on.
Rather than creating parallel reporting structures, CMS can:
● Incentivize reuse of existing data points already captured for value-based care, quality
reporting, and billing, reducing redundant documentation.
● Support vendor integrations that streamline data exchange within native workflows,
leveraging API-first infrastructure to enable seamless connections across digital health
tools.
● Simplify compliance through uniform service-level agreements and shared standards
across payers, creating predictable and consistent requirements.
● Offer implementation funding and technical assistance, especially targeting small or rural
providers, to ensure broad participation and equity.
● Encourage ecosystem-wide participation to prevent data silos and promote
comprehensive, high-quality datasets.
By embedding data sharing into systems providers already optimize for—such as
reimbursement, authorization, and care coordination—CMS can expand timely, high-quality
healthcare data availability while reducing administrative overhead. This approach accelerates
smarter, data-driven care, supports transparency, advances equity, and improves patient
outcomes (predictive analysis)
E. Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks
TD-1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to
stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries
and caregivers?
Short-Term Steps (Next 2 Years):
● Provide financial incentives to vendors focused on Medicare/Medicaid populations

● Streamline and clarify integration pathways to reduce friction with CMS systems.
● Shorten decision and development cycles to support faster iteration and innovation with
vendors
● Expand access to data and encourage real-time, data-driven feedback loops with the
private sector
Longer-Term Steps:
● Transition certification requirements from traditional EHR systems toward API-based
approaches where real-time care and patient interactions occur, fostering innovation at
the point of care rather than solely within the system of record.
● Support and promote open-source APIs to lower barriers to entry and standardize
technology development across stakeholders.
● Build and maintain a centralized digital infrastructure that supports modular innovation
and interoperability across the care ecosystem.
TD-2. Regarding CMS Data, to stimulate developer interest—
Credentialing and prior authorization data would be most valuable; today, these are locked
behind individual MCOs which makes it difficult to build a unified solution.
TD-4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available
APIs over proprietary APIs?
By providing more guidance on which open frameworks to adopt and directly incentivizing them
(e.g., by demonstrating that they allow claims to be paid faster), there could be rapid adoption of
these frameworks. Today, there are too many competing frameworks with insufficient guidance
as to which to adopt.
TD-12. Should CMS endorse non-CMS data sources and networks, and if so, what criteria
or metrics should CMS consider?
Yes. Ideally, this would happen in phases that allow for testing; i.e., rather than set up criteria at
the start, allow for a phased approach that allows for competing data sources and networks to
prove value to the healthcare system. In doing so, the criteria + metrics can become clear over
time as certain networks are able to prove more value than others.
Conclusion (which recaps your main argument and lists your recommendations
again)
The CMS plays a critical role in fostering innovation in the US healthcare system. It can drive
significant change in two parts:
● Establishing frameworks around information sharing to allow newer entrants to engage,
especially considering the amount of technological innovation occurring

● Set up programs to allow private sector players to rapidly test new technological
solutions in phased approaches (e.g., real time adjudication, real time credentialing etc.)
to allow for a more data informed process to innovation; today’s RFI approach narrows
the potential impact to only what can be conceived within the CMS, while private vendors
may have access to technology that would allow for more innovative, wide ranging
approaches that can be rapidly derisked. This would allow the CMS to take a more
holistic approach to evaluating what policy and technology should be implemented
