Founded in 2006 out of the Stanford University’s Biodesign Center, iRhythm Technologies, Inc., is a digital health and diagnostics company that specializes in outpatient (ambulatory) monitoring for cardiac conditions, focused on arrhythmias and heart rhythm disorders. We participate in Medicare and Medicaid as an independent diagnostic testing facility (IDTF). iRhythm has a set of home-based patch cardiac monitoring devices, combined with cloud-based analytics, FDA-cleared artificial intelligence algorithms, and several hundred qualified cardiac technicians who review the data. iRhythm’s hardware, software, and services can provide continuous, uninterrupted monitoring for up to 14 days to identify arrhythmias, such as atrial fibrillation, which is common in Medicare beneficiaries, that can increase the risk of cardiovascular events, stroke death, hospitalization, and health care utilization if untreated. We agree that the health and well-being of Americans will benefit from greater and more effective deployment of technology to allow patients to make informed decisions that helps them live healthier lives. Specifically, digital health products play an outsized role as the use of technology in everyday lives of patients continues to evolve and grow. We appreciate the interest of HHS on addressing issues of improving patient access to data-drive health tools, advancing interoperability, improving patient experience, and enhancing clinical workflow — as these are critical to ensure safe and effective adoption of these technologies. We believe there is a tremendous opportunity to make improvements in data interoperability and digital infrastructure, and the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS)HHS is well positioned to advance initiatives to achieve such progress and increase patient access to digital tools and capabilities. The policy framework that your agency has worked to effectively implement over the last decade has created significant opportunities to modernize and improve the flow of health information between patients, providers, and payers. This has enabled a digital technology evolution that has helped to improve patient outcomes, increased access to care, and reduced costs, but more can be done to improve patient adoption and fully leverage the capabilities of innovative digital health technologies. We hope that our experience and perspective may be valuable in developing an overarching policy framework. Overview of iRhythm’s Products and Services 
For almost 20 years and since the passing of the HITECH Act in 2009, iRhythm has been addressing these issues, starting with both the use of AI, and making data digital available across patients and health care systems. We have a mature patient-centered user experience with technology, and seamless EHR integration, conforming to federal interoperability standards. Prior to the availability of iRhythm’s products, detection of cardiac rhythm disorders typically required seeking brick-and-mortar care to receive a 10-second ECG, emergency room or overnight admission to have 24 to 48 hours of continuous telemetry monitoring, or prescription of a 48-hour Holter monitor that included multiple wires, a large recorder and battery back, and had to be fitted and adjusted in person by a hospital ECG technician. iRhythm developed the first patch-based continuous ECG monitor that can provide continuous, uninterrupted recording of an ECG. The data is analyzed by AI, reviewed by qualified cardiac technician’s at iRhythm’s IDTF, and provided to physicians who do the interpretation and review. Our over 150-plus peer-reviewed publications have shown that iRhythm’s monitoring hardware and services provider improved detection of arrhythmias than all other categories of monitors. More recently, we have shown that in a full sample of Medicare fee-for-service beneficiaries, iRhythm’s long-term continuous monitoring (LTCM) product, compared to other types of cardiac monitors, was associated with increased adjusted odds arrhythmia diagnosis yield, lower adjusted odds of retesting. LTCM was also associated with reduced risk of emergency room visits and hospitalization and lower overall incremental health care resource utilization1. During the pandemic, iRhythm accelerated its efforts and transformed its care model to enable home-based diagnostics and care. If the prescribing physician and patient prefer, then our cardiac monitoring products can be mailed directly to the patient. Patients are guided through self-application, either with the printed instructions for use (English and Spanish) or via our optional MyZio patient app for iOS and Android. The patient app is used as an adjunct to our medical device, and includes device delivery tracking, symptom notifications, and patient education and care instructions. The app is among the top 50 medical apps downloaded and has a 4.7 out of 5-star rating in the App store. Our studies have shown that patient compliance is improved through the use of these interventions. iRhythm’s diagnostic services have also shown promise in value-based care and risk-sharing models. The diagnosis of atrial fibrillation (AF) has been shown to be an inflection point for health care cost and utilization in Medicare beneficiaries. In a Medicare Part A and Part B analysis of 228,295 patients with newly diagnosed atrial fibrillation, 41% of patients had a cardiovascular hospitalization within the first year following diagnosis of AF.2 Of these, 21% were for AF, 19% for heart failure, and 7.7% were for stroke or transient ischemic attack. Prior studies have shown that there are 600,000 adults with undiagnosed AF in the U.S., most of whom are age ≥ 653. The incremental cost burden of undiagnosed AF is $3.1 billion4. IRhythm has successfully developed programs for targeted population health management to identify undiagnosed arrhythmias, such as atrial fibrillation, in high-risk populations. Our technology solutions and tools allow population health management dashboarding by site of care, hospital, health system, and even by payor. Recommendations for Realizing the Potential of Digital Health Alignment on Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) and Medicare Reimbursement The American Medical Association’s Digital Medicine Payment Advisory Group is developing a new category of CPT codes for the algorithmic analysis of clinically relevant patient data. To be clear, these efforts do not address reimbursement levels, which are under the Center for Medicare & Medicaid Services’ (CMS) jurisdiction. To fully unlock the potential of digital health, we believe CMS must ensure services are appropriately priced within the Physician Fee Schedule, with attention to fiscal responsibility to both taxpayers and the Medicare Trust Fund. Healthcare spending grew 7.5% in 2023, reaching $4.9 trillion and accounting for 17.6% of GDP.5 This financial trajectory is unsustainable. When properly deployed, certain digital health technologies can streamline care, reduce costs and utilization, and improve patient outcomes. CMS should encourage adoption of proven technologies through supportive payment policies, while disincentivizing outdated, unproven or wasteful procedures. Financial incentives have a powerful role in healthcare transformation, and CMS has a meaningful opportunity to use them to influence spending patterns and care quality. Strengthening Today’s Digital Health Ecosystem Broadly defined, the digital health has grown from approximately $80 billion in 2014 to $550 billion in 2024. While not all services and devices are or should be covered by federal health programs, iRhythm’s experience as a leader in wearable cardiac monitoring underscores both the promise and the challenges of this evolving ecosystem. iRhythm has benefited from the FDA’s Breakthrough Devices Program and digital health frameworks, which have supported innovation and timely clearance of novel diagnostics. CMS has complemented these efforts by reimbursing digital and tech-enabled services, fueling continued growth. However, better alignment between regulatory and reimbursement frameworks is essential. Real-world evidence (RWE) from routine clinical use demonstrates how digital tools perform in diverse settings and supports value- based decisions. Advancing Polices to Strengthen Engagement with Digital Health Products Policies that promote engagement with digital health tools must emphasize accessibility, caregiver involvement, provider integration, and appropriate reimbursement. Expanding reimbursement pathways will improve affordability, while intuitive user interfaces will enhance patient engagement. Caregivers play a critical role in remote monitoring. Policies should support secure data access for caregivers and offer educational tools to help them understand reports and intervene when necessary. Ensuring robust privacy protections and secure collaboration among patients, caregivers, and providers will further enhance the effectiveness of these tools. Home enrollment models allow patients to receive and apply diagnostic devices themselves, overcoming barriers such as travel limitations and appointment backlogs. These models facilitate earlier diagnoses and better adherence while reducing system burden. To foster trust in home-based diagnostics, CMS could establish standardized communication guidelines so providers can clearly explain the clinical utility, safety, and FDA clearance status of these tools. For providers, streamlined EHR integration and automated reporting of arrhythmia detection can minimize administrative burden and enhance clinical decision-making. Implementation of home enrollment models for diagnostic devices, combined with automated EHR physician alerts for significant findings, can help ensure appropriate treatment – such as timely anticoagulation for patients with atrial fibrillation – by enhancing clinical oversight and closing care gaps. CEHRT certification should align with remote monitoring workflows, recognizing Zio’s contribution to cardiovascular care quality metrics and value-based programs. Policymakers should also incentivize the adoption of evidence-backed technologies through performance-based measures, ensuring that high-performing digital health tools improve patient engagement, provider efficiency, quality metrics, and overall healthcare outcomes. By implementing these strategies, it can support more accessible, integrated, and patient-centered approach to digital cardiac monitoring, driving both clinical and economic improvements in healthcare. Feedback on HHS Requirements for Technology Use in Value-Based Care iRhythm supports HHS’s efforts to advance the use of technology in value-based care arrangements and acknowledges the foundational role that ONC plays in promoting interoperability. We believe, however, there are opportunities to strengthen the alignment between current requirements and the tailored needs of digital diagnostic technologies. Current ONC-certified systems primarily focus on EHR-focused workflows, which may not incorporate diagnostic platforms like long-term continuous cardiac monitoring. As a result, providers may miss diagnostic links between disease states, such as the relationship between arrythmias and certain chronic diseases. In turn, this could lead to delayed or higher-cost care. 
As described above, the economic burden of managing undiagnosed and untreated arrythmias is substantial, especially in diabetic and respiratory-compromised patient populations. While screening strategies have generally targeted patients with cardiovascular and vascular risk factors, we have learned that there may be a substantial proportion of undiagnosed AF in patients with diabetes and respiratory conditions, such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. Early detection through ECG monitoring can prevent serious events such as stroke and heart failure. Claims-based research is now quantifying the arrhythmia burden among Medicare beneficiaries with chronic conditions and revealing disparities in access to ECG monitoring. Patients who go unmonitored are more likely to be diagnosed during costly acute events, such as hospitalizations. Meanwhile, machine learning tools are helping EHRs identify high-risk patients, enabling timely, outpatient interventions. As CMS expands digital diagnostics under Medicare, we urge recognition of the full value provided by advanced, home-based cardiac monitoring technologies. Digital Technologies and the Diagnostic Journey Equally important is what follows diagnosis. We believe that AI-driven identification and timely monitoring must be coupled with scalable, patient-centered care pathways that proactively manage diagnosed conditions and avoid costly downstream events. By partnering with virtual cardiology platforms, iRhythm helps ensure appropriate post-diagnosis care that is cost-effective and clinically sound. From a diagnostic perspective, early detection is key. Historically, arrythmia diagnosis has typically been confined to brick-and-mortar settings, which can be costlier for both the patient and the service that is provided. For example, a patient may be called-in to undergo a diagnostic procedure using a Holter monitor or another type of device that is more burdensome yet diagnostically inferior. Technological advances that rely, in part, on AI, have freed the patient and the provider from traditional healthcare settings. FDA-cleared devices like iRhythm’s Zio monitor can be applied at home and return heartbeat data as effectively as if applied in a physician’s office. Recent guidelines reflect these advances. in 2024, the American College of Cardiology’s Solution Set Oversight Committee specifically recommended a minimum of 14 days of cardiac monitoring for post-stroke patients. They have downgraded the recommendation of using implantable cardiac monitors in all patients after stroke, as these are both expensive and have not been shown in randomized trials to decrease the risk of recurrent stroke. Further, the European Society of Cardiology and the European Stroke Organisation issued a Class IIa recommendation for atrial fibrillation screening, including 14-day ECG patches as a preferred tool.7 There is a clear and growing body of clinical evidence in support of screening for undiagnosed atrial fibrillation and other types of arrythmias. Despite this growing evidence, some guideline organizations lag. The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force concluded in 2022 that there was insufficient evidence to support routine AFib screening in asymptomatic adults over 50—a stance that overlooks how timely diagnosis can reduce healthcare costs and adverse outcomes. Based on more recent evidence, international professional societies have advocated the systematic evaluation of patients age ≥ 65 for arrhythmia screening. Alternative Payment Models (APMs) CMS's APMs aim to improve care quality while containing costs. Integration of certified health IT is essential for supporting value-based care (VBC). However, current policies do not always recognize the value of digital diagnostics like Zio within APMs, limiting providers' ability to optimize patient care through continuous cardiac monitoring. To better align technology policy with APM goals, CMS should expand CEHRT criteria to support remote monitoring tools. Zio data should be considered a valid metric under programs such as MIPS and ACO REACH. Enhancing data-sharing standards would ensure that providers can efficiently utilize structured, real-time insights from Zio for care coordination and outcome tracking. Additionally, reducing reimbursement uncertainty for digital health tools in value-based arrangements would reinforce their role in preventive care and cost reduction. By prioritizing proven technologies, CMS can improve outcomes and financial sustainability across APMs while advancing digital innovation. Streamlining CEHRT to Support Value-Based Care and Competition CMS should revise CEHRT and Promoting Interoperability criteria to accelerate the adoption of remote cardiac monitoring, strengthen value-based care, and reduce provider burden. Embracing FHIR/API standards, third-party device integration, and remote monitoring workflows will enhance the efficiency and effectiveness of VBC models. These improvements will reduce administrative burdens for providers, ensure compliance with regulatory requirements, and enable data-driven insights through structured diagnostic information, ultimately improving patient care. Improved CEHRT functionality will also boost patient choice and competition. Automated tracking of cardiac metrics should be built into CEHRT to support ACO and CIN reporting requirements. Additional tools, such as patient portals and apps like MyZio, should be fully integrated with EHRs to maximize patient engagement and remote monitoring adherence. These updates will ensure that CEHRT supports modern cardiac care while aligning with broader policy goals to improve access, affordability, and outcomes.
