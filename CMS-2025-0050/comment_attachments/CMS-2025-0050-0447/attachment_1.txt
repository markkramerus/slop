ABOUT TYPE 1 DIABETES (T1D) Type 1 diabetes (T1D) is a chronic autoimmune disease that can strike suddenly and, without proper management, can be life-threatening. It occurs when the immune system attacks and destroys insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas, leading to lifelong dependence on externally administered insulin. It is estimated that 9.5 million people live today with T1D throughout the globe. A recent study estimated that nearly 2 million Americans currently live with T1D - nearly 600,000 enrolled in Medicare. 1 People with T1D rely on insulin therapy, delivered through multiple daily injections or insulin pumps to survive. However, insulin is not a cure as it is demanding and allows for little margin for error to avoid imprecise treatment. Too much insulin can cause hypoglycemia, leading to confusion, seizures, unconsciousness, or even death. Too little insulin, especially over time, can result in serious complications such as kidney failure, vision loss, cardiovascular disease, nerve damage, and life- threatening diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). Despite rigorous routines, blood glucose levels often fluctuate unpredictably, making daily life with T1D both physically and emotionally exhausting. On average, people diagnosed with T1D have an 11-year shorter life expectancy with a global average of 24-life years lost.2 Mortality rates for those living with T1D are 3- 18 times higher than the general population.3 The constant burden of T1D through relentless management contributes to high rates of diabetes distress and burnout, with many reporting that T1D negatively impacts their ability to plan for the future, 1 Smith, R., Eisenberg, S., Turner-Phifer, A., LeGrand, J., Pincus, S., Omer, Y., Wang, F., & Pyenson, B. These tools provide real-time data and more precise insulin delivery, easing some of the daily burden. However, they do not eliminate the disease’s challenges. As the global incidence of T1D continues to rise, there is an urgent need not only for better access to existing therapies but also for the development of breakthrough treatments that go beyond insulin replacement. Promising treatments that can modify the course of the disease and preserve or restore beta cell function would ultimately lead to a world without T1D. We envision a future with permanent disruption in disease progression, restoration of insulin independence, and the ability for those impacted by T1D to live indistinguishable lives from those without. ABOUT BREAKTHROUGH T1D As the leading global type 1 diabetes research and advocacy organization, Breakthrough T1D helps make everyday life with type 1 diabetes better while driving toward cures. We do this by investing in the most promising research, advocating for progress by working with government to address issues that impact the T1D community, and helping educate and empower individuals facing this condition. At Breakthrough T1D, we view digital health products as encompassing a wide range of tools that support real-time diabetes management. This includes CGMs, insulin pumps, AID systems, algorithms that enable interoperability of devices, as well as the mobile applications that support data sharing, clinical oversight, and patient decision-making. These products are not ancillary—they are essential to daily life for people with T1D as they provide critical information and automation that reduce the daily burden of management, the risk of complications, hospitalizations, and long- term health consequences. We encourage CMS to re-evaluate the current Transitional Coverage for Emerging Technologies (TCET) pathway for ways to expand the types of devices and other digital tools that may be eligible, remove the restrictive cap on applicants, and seek ways to reduce the burden on manufacturers in order to expedite access to innovative therapies for Medicare beneficiaries. For individuals with T1D, delays in Medicare coverage for new technologies can lead to dangerous disruptions in care. Many beneficiaries age into Medicare already using digital tools—such AID/Closed Loop Systems (CLS) systems and CGMs—that they rely on to maintain daily safety and long-term health. When coverage is not yet available through Medicare, these individuals are forced to either pay out-of-pocket, switch to less effective tools, or go without. This can result in poor glucose control, increased risk of hospitalization, and emotional distress.6 An improved TCET could help ensure that people living with T1D have timely access to innovations that are already improving lives in other insured populations, while still allowing CMS to evaluate longer-term outcomes. (b)What changes would enable timely access to high-quality CMS and provider generated data on patients? To enable timely access to provider generated data, CMS should encourage greater interoperability between digital health apps and electronic health records (EHRs), ensuring that data can flow seamlessly across platforms used by patients and providers. What we know about devices and the community living with T1D is that seamless communication between technologies is not a luxury—it’s a necessity. People with T1D often rely on multiple interconnected devices, such as CGMs, insulin pumps, and health apps, to manage a condition that demands 24/7 attention. When these tools cannot communicate effectively, whether due to proprietary barriers, lack of standardization, or insufficient EHR integration, patients face fragmented care. For example, a Medicare beneficiary may use a CGM and an insulin pump, but without interoperability, their provider cannot view real-time data trends or make timely treatment adjustments. Likewise, caregivers may be unable to monitor or assist remotely, increasing the risk of undetected blood glucose spikes and emergency interventions. 4PR-1. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage providers, including those in rural areas, to leverage approved digital health products? (a)What are the current obstacles? Providers in rural areas face more pronounced and persistent challenges that hinder adoption of digital health tools.7 These include technology gaps in local health systems, such as a lack of interoperability between digital tools and EHRs. Additionally, inadequate cellular infrastructure can limit both patient and provider access to app-enabled tools, creating major barriers to effective digital care in these communities. To address these barriers, CMS should work with stakeholders to ensure equal access across geographies to covered items and services, particularly in Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) identified resource shortage areas. One current obstacle is the uncertainty around coverage for new digital tools, which discourages providers from investing in their use. An improved TCET pathway could reduce this uncertainty and offer providers more confidence in recommending innovative technologies. In addition, the administrative burden of navigating prior authorization (PA) processes further deters providers, particularly those in more rural areas with limited resources, from recommending or supporting digital health technologies. CMS can lessen this burden by simplifying PA workflows or establishing an automatic approval for digital tools that meet certain safety and efficacy thresholds, potentially aligned with a framework like TCET. TD-1. What short term (in the next 2 years), and longer-term steps can CMS take to stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers? BreakthroughT1D supports the TCET pathway as a meaningful step toward accelerating access to innovative medical technologies. For people living with chronic conditions like T1D, access to life-improving and potentially life-saving devices often lags behind FDA authorization due to delays in Medicare coverage. TCET helps bridge this gap and provides a clearer pathway to coverage. However, the current TCET program is limited to five devices annually, which constrains the pathway’s impact and creates uncertainty for developers and patients. We encourage CMS to expand the capacity of the TCET program so that more high- impact technologies, particularly those that serve large patient populations such as people living with T1D, can benefit from accelerated coverage consideration. Breakthrough T1D appreciates CMS’s leadership in advancing a more person- centered, technology-enabled Medicare system. We strongly support efforts to expand access to digital health tools that improve outcomes and quality of life for individuals with chronic conditions like T1D.
