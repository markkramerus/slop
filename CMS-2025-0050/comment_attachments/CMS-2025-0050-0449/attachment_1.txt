Introduction
While all topics addressed in this RFI are important, Firely USA Inc. has chosen to focus on those areas where, as
an HL7 FHIR-focused health technology vendor, we are best positioned to contribute meaningful insights and
advance progress.
In that spirit, we would like to highlight a key consideration that is woven throughout our responses but
deserves particular attention. We believe it is best addressed in a dedicated section to provide clarity and
context:
Intersection of Regulation and the Evolving HL7 FHIR Implementation Guide
Firely recognizes and supports CMS and ONC/ASTP’s efforts to establish a baseline version for FHIR
Implementation Guides (IGs). We also appreciate that, in prior rulemaking such as CMS-0057-F, CMS has
strategically referenced certain IGs as “strongly recommended,” signaling their importance without mandating
adoption before sufficient real-world maturity.
As HL7 and the broader FHIR community evolve these IGs, updates occur frequently to address newly identified
use cases, correct inconsistencies, and streamline interoperability by reducing redundancy across IGs. While this
evolution can sometimes result in breaking changes, in most cases these refinements enhance the IG’s goals and
usability in real-world implementations.
We acknowledge the Standards Advancement Process (SVAP) as an important mechanism employed by ONC to
manage this evolution while maintaining regulatory consistency. However, the annual cadence of SVAP updates
can lag behind the pace of innovation in the FHIR ecosystem. As a result, implementers, particularly those
aligned with rapidly changing IGs, may find themselves constrained by outdated regulatory references or limited
flexibility in certification.
The transition toward fully interoperable health data exchange is both necessary and aspirational. Still, it comes
with inevitable growing pains for vendors, providers, and payers alike. While we expect to soon enter the
stabilized period, as the ecosystem matures flexibility during this transitional phase is essential. Prematurely
locking in specific IG versions could stifle innovation and increase implementation burden.
Firely does not propose a singular solution to this challenge, as the strength of the FHIR movement lies in its
collaborative, community-driven nature. Instead, we respectfully recommend that CMS and ONC continue to
work closely with HL7, Da Vinci, and other Accelerators to explore sustainable paths forward.
To that end, we propose the following guiding principles:
• Continue to recommend, but not require, a specific IG version during early phases of implementation,
especially when an IG is undergoing rapid iteration.
• Adopt a modular certification approach that focuses on core FHIR API capabilities, rather than requiring
complete adherence to an entire IG, particularly where IGs include optional or situational elements.
• Allow for contextual flexibility in certification criteria, recognizing the diversity of EHR system
architectures and use cases across different healthcare stakeholders.
• Consider increasing the frequency or responsiveness of SVAP updates or exploring complementary
processes to keep pace with the FHIR community's development cycles.

Across these APIs, we support both individual FHIR queries and bulk data access (Flat FHIR/Bulk Data IG)

g. Bulk FHIR—Do you support Group ID-based access filtering for population-specific queries?

Standardizing these definitions would greatly enhance interoperability, reproducibility, and automation in

We support the use of CDS Hooks as part of the CMS-0057 Prior Authorization process to enable real-time,

TD-3. Regarding digital identity implementation:
b. How would requiring digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800–63–
3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs) impact cybersecurity and data exchange?
Requiring digital identity credentials significantly enhances cybersecurity by enabling stronger authentication
and reducing identity spoofing risks. However, if implemented in a fragmented manner, it could introduce
usability challenges for patients and partners. For example, supporting shared identity verification across
applications and enabling true single sign-on (SSO) experiences would promote ease of use and data liquidity.
Conversely, maintaining siloed identity models with multiple login requirements would hinder adoption and
fragment access across platforms. A coordinated, standards-based approach is essential to maximize security
benefits without compromising user experience.
c. What impact would mandatory use of the OpenID Connect identity protocol have?
OpenID is a clearly established standard that we fully support and recommend.
TD–4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs over proprietary
APIs?
• Mandate FHIR API compliance in ONC certification and tie reimbursement to API adoption.
• Penalize proprietary API use through reduced payments or audits.
• Build solid use cases for providers on top of these APIs that provide high-value, e.g. prior Auth, clinical
decision support.

TD–5. How could a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints improve access to health information for
patients, providers, and payers? Who should publish such a directory, and should users bear a cost?
Providers: Patient-facing apps often struggle to integrate with provider systems because FHIR endpoints are not
consistently discoverable, despite the g(10) requirements.
Payers: The challenge is even greater for payer endpoints, as there is currently no mandate for publishing or
maintaining them.
A centralized, publicly accessible directory, regularly validated for endpoint availability and accuracy, would
significantly reduce these barriers. CMS or a trusted neutral entity should publish and maintain the directory,
and access should remain free and as open as possible to encourage broad adoption and interoperability.
TD–6. What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform?
a. What existing alternatives should be considered?
CMS and ASTP/ONC could consider adopting the FAST Security Implementation Guide for Scalable Registration
with UDAP support as an intermediate step. This approach would enhance access to FHIR endpoints without the
operational and governance overhead associated with TEFCA’s SOPs.
Importantly, this would not eliminate the need for a trust framework. Rather, it would shift the decision-making
to individual payers and providers, who could determine which partners to trust and allow for registration,
maintaining flexibility while improving scalability.
TD–7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange and what are its limitations?
USCDI has had a significant positive impact on interoperability. It establishes a clear, technology-independent
baseline of data elements, which facilitates the mapping and implementation of concrete standards; such as
exposing proprietary data through FHIR. Additionally, it supports the use of interoperable ValueSets to codify
data that might otherwise remain uncoded, providing a pathway to standardize proprietary EHR values.
a. Does it contain the full extent of data elements you need?
Not entirely. As additional use cases and programs—such as Quality Improvement initiatives and USCDI+ Quality
(QI-Core) emerge, we continue to identify necessary data elements that are not yet part of the core USCDI.
b. If not, is it because of limitations in the definition of the USCDI format or the way it is utilized?
Neither. USCDI is a growing specification and its current limitations are expected as it evolves. Its expansion
reflects the natural progression of emerging interoperability use cases.
c. If so, would adding more data elements to USCDI add value or create scoping challenges? How could such
challenges be addressed?
While adding more data elements would provide value, it could also introduce scoping and implementation
challenges. Not all systems will need to support every data element. One solution would be to enable
certification to subsets of USCDI based on relevant use cases.
Firely supports categorizing and harmonizing USCDI with intersecting initiatives to clarify overlapping needs. This
"Venn diagram" approach can help stakeholders prioritize high-impact data elements. It would also assist the
FHIR community and ONC certification programs in implementing the kind of flexibility we advocated for in our
introduction; allowing tailored interoperability pathways while maintaining alignment with national standards.

d. Given improvements in language models, would you prefer a non-proprietary but less structured format
that might improve data coverage even if it requires more processing by the receiver?
Firely looks forward to how LLMs will improve interoperability and patient care. This question is use case
specific. While modern LLMs offer impressive capabilities for extracting meaning from free-text or semi-
structured inputs, we believe that the primary interchange format for health data should remain a well-
structured, standards-based representation.
Deterministic Processing
Structured formats enable reliable, repeatable parsing and validation. There’s zero ambiguity about
what “CodeableConcept” or “Observation.valueQuantity” means eliminating the variability inherent in
natural-language interpretation.
Regulatory & Audit Needs
Clinical and billing workflows often have strict audit, compliance, and traceability requirements.
Machine-readable structure with explicit data types (timestamps, codified values, references) supports
downstream use cases—quality measurement, clinical decision support, claims adjudication—without
ad hoc NLP quality checks.
Efficiency & Scale
Offloading heavy unstructured-to-structured transformation to each receiver would multiply cost and
complexity across the ecosystem. A common, pre-validated structured payload reduces redundant
processing and minimizes the risk of inconsistent interpretations.
A hybrid approach could work best, using FHIR as the backbone for structured, interoperable data exchange
while supplementing it with less structured formats for additional context.
TD–9. Regarding certification of health IT:
a. What are the benefits of redefining certification to prioritize API-enabled capabilities over software
functionality?
Focusing on API-enabled capabilities allows for greater flexibility, scalability, and interoperability. APIs can be
abstracted or implemented via “relied upon software,” reducing redundancy while still meeting certification
requirements. This enables modular solutions and fosters a more open ecosystem for innovation.
b. What would be the drawbacks?
Shifting focus away from software functionality could lead to gaps in usability or workflow integration if APIs
exist but are not well-supported in practice. There’s also a risk of inconsistencies in how data is surfaced if not
carefully aligned with underlying application logic.
c. How could ASTP/ONC revise health IT certification criteria to require APIs to consistently support
exchanging data from all aspects of the patient’s chart (e.g., faxed records, free text, discrete data)?
ONC could expand certification to include conformance testing for structured representations of traditionally
unstructured data. This might include requirements to:
• Tag and categorize unstructured content (e.g., scanned documents, narrative text) using FHIR standards
like DocumentReference and Observation.
• Support natural language processing (NLP) integration or structured wrappers to facilitate data
extraction and use.

d. What policy changes could CMS make to motivate providers to respond to API-based data requests with
best possible coverage and quality?
• Tie API responsiveness and data completeness to incentive programs (e.g., MIPS, ACOs).
• Encourage real-time or near-real-time responses rather than batching or delays.
• Provide reporting transparency around API performance, completeness, and error rates to drive

e. How could EHRs capable of bulk data transfer reduce provider burden for quality performance reporting?
What capabilities are needed? What concerns exist?
Bulk FHIR APIs reduce the need for manual queries and custom extracts. With daily or ongoing submissions:
• Providers offload the reporting burden.
• Data quality APIs can validate continuously.
• Costs decrease as point-in-time extracts are replaced by automated feeds.
However, concerns include:
• Validation complexity (FHIR data is often incomplete or invalid).
• Need for more granular filters to avoid overwhelming data volumes.
• Ensuring data relevance and timeliness across systems.
TD–10. For EHR and other developers subject to the ONC Health IT Certification Program, what further steps
should ASTP/ONC consider to implement the 21st Century Cures Act’s API condition of certification (42 U.S.C.
300jj–11(c)(5)(D)(iv)) that requires a developer’s APIs to allow health information to be accessed, exchanged,
and used without special effort, including providing access to all data elements of a patient’s electronic health
record to the extent permissible under applicable privacy laws?
To further implement the Cures Act API certification condition, Firely recommends ONC consider the following:
• Incentivize developers to support APIs that go beyond USCDI to include full EHI where feasible, using
FHIR as the preferred standard. As FHIR mappings mature, more of the full patient record can be made
available in a structured and computable way.
• Standardize EHI export/import through FHIR-based APIs to improve portability.
• Clarify “without special effort” by discouraging proprietary barriers and requiring clear, public API
documentation.
• Promote SMART app integration with streamlined workflows for app registration and approval.

export or ‘‘EHI export’’ for a single patient.... Now that this capability has been in production for over a year,

Firely commends ONC for taking the first step by requiring the §170.315(b)(10) export capability, even while

Over time, we believe this capability should evolve into a standardized API-based model, ideally using FHIR,

A phased approach, guided by maturity of FHIR mappings for broader EHI elements, would help strike a balance
between immediate utility and long-term interoperability.

TD–12. Should CMS endorse non-CMS data sources and networks, and if so, what criteria or metrics should
CMS consider?
Based on feedback from the FHIR community and our customer base, we believe maximum healthcare value is
achieved when providers, caregivers, and payers have seamless access to a comprehensive, unified record of
Electronic Health Information (EHI), enriched with relevant social, administrative, patient-generated, and other
appropriate data.
TD–13. What new opportunities and advancements could emerge with APIs providing access to the entirety of
a patient’s electronic health information (EHI)?
APIs that provide access to a patient’s full EHI, beyond the USCDI-defined elements, create opportunities for
deeper clinical decision support, improved care coordination, advanced analytics, and enhanced patient
engagement. Full EHI access could also accelerate innovation in areas such as personalized medicine, population
health management, and research by enabling richer, more context-aware datasets.
a. What are the primary obstacles to this?
Exporting a patient’s full EHI is not likely to be broadly useful unless the data is mostly structured using FHIR.
While unstructured data (e.g., PDFs, clinical notes, scanned images) will always be part of the healthcare
ecosystem, it would benefit significantly from being organized within FHIR resources using categorization and
metadata standards.
Without the structure and semantics that FHIR provides, we risk perpetuating the same interoperability
challenges we’ve faced for decades, namely, fragmented data in proprietary formats that are difficult for
systems to interpret and act on programmatically.
Additional obstacles include:
• Data quality and completeness variability across systems
• Performance and scalability concerns exporting large volumes of data
• Legal and compliance concerns around broad data sharing
• Consent and access management complexity
b. What are the primary tradeoffs between USCDI and full EHI, especially given more flexible data processing
capabilities today?
USCDI provides a carefully curated and standardized set of data elements that can be relied upon across use
cases and systems. It offers predictability, reduces implementation complexity, and facilitates certification and
regulatory alignment.
In contrast, full EHI offers broader access but introduces variability in data format, structure, and quality. While
modern computing and AI tools can increasingly handle unstructured or semi-structured data, this still requires
significant investment in normalization, interpretation, and governance.
Assuming that full EHI can eventually be expressed consistently in FHIR, the tradeoff shifts from technical
feasibility to operational complexity, balancing data usefulness with burden, privacy, and the risk of information
overload for both clinicians and patients.
