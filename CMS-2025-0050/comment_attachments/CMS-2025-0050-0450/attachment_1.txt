TD-6: What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform? Response : TEFCA’s unique value lies in its ability to enable network -to-network exchange of health information at a national scale. Rather than relying on individual, point -to-point integrations between healthcare organizations, TEFCA establishes a trusted framework that allows entire networks—such as health systems, payors, and data platforms —to connect through designated Qualified Health Information Networks (QHINs). This greatly simplifies the complexity of interoperability by allowing participants to connect once to a QHIN and gain access to a broad ecosystem of other connected entities. The result is more efficient, secure, and scalable data exchange that supports treatment, care coordina tion, payment, and individual access use cases. TEFCA reduces duplication of effort, lowers the cost of connectivity, and accelerates access to complete and accurate patient information —ultimately improving outcomes for patients, providers, and payors alik e. TD-6 a: Are there redundant standards, protocols or channels or both that should be consolidated? Response : Yes, there are redundant standards, protocols, and channels that should be considered for consolidation. To reduce complexity and promote broader adoption, CMS and related agencies should consider limiting the proliferation of additional data formats. For example, while the Coverage Requirements Discovery (CRD) API utilizes CDS Hooks and the Documentation Templates and Rules (DTR) API incorporates Cl inical Quality Language (CQL), these formats add technical overhead and complexity for implementers. A more streamlined and consistent approach —such as using simplified, well -structured JSON payloads —would make APIs easier to adopt, test, and scale. Consol idating toward fewer, widely supported standards would lower implementation costs, reduce variation, and encourage more uniform interoperability across the healthcare ecosystem. TD-9 b: What would be the drawbacks? Response : Redefining certification to prioritize API -enabled capabilities over software functionality presents some notable drawbacks, particularly for software companies focused on delivering robust health experience platforms such as member and provider portals. O ne key concern is that certification criteria heavily weighted toward APIs may undervalue the end -to-end user experience, including usability, accessibility, and engagement —areas where portal platforms excel. While APIs are essential for interoperability, they do not account for the design, workflow integration, and communication features that drive member and provider satisfaction. Additionally, certification that emphasizes APIs could shift limited developme nt resources toward compliance efforts rather than innovation in user -centric functionality. It may also deprioritize valuable platform features that aren't exposed through APIs but are critical to real -world adoption and daily use. Finally, the complexity and cost of maintaining certification solely focused on API capabilities could disadvantage vendors offering holistic experience solutions, especially if those APIs do not reflect the full depth or nuance of their integrated offerings. 
TD-9 c: How could ASTP/ONC revise health IT certification criteria to require APIs to consistently support exchanging data form all aspects of the patient's chart (for example, faxed records, free text, discrete data)? Response : No, ASTP/ONC should not revise certification criteria to require APIs to support exchanging data from all aspects of the patient chart. While the goal of comprehensive data exchange is important, mandating API -level access to elements like faxed records, f ree text, and other non -standard formats risks introducing unnecessary complexity and development burden. As noted previously, recent advancements in natural language processing (NLP) now allow systems to effectively extract structured insights from unstructured data, such as clinic al notes, denial letters, or patient surveys. These capabilities reduce the need for rigid, fully structured APIs in every case. Requiring APIs to support every data type —especially legacy formats —would increase implementation timelines, raise costs, and p otentially slow down innovation in other high -impact areas. Instead, certification should promote flexible, standards -based APIs for core data types, while allowing organizations to supplement with NLP -driven or document -based approaches for less structure d content. This balanced model encourages innovation without overregulating how all data must be exchanged. TD-12: Should CMS endorse non -CMS data sources and networks, and if so, what criteria or metrics should CMS consider? Response : While endorsing non -CMS data sources and networks may seem like a pathway to expanding access and accelerating innovation, there are several significant concerns that CMS should carefully consider before moving forward with such endorsements. First, data q uality and consistency remain a major risk. Non -CMS sources often lack standardized validation protocols, which could lead to in consistencies in data that undermine decision -making, reporting accuracy, or compliance efforts. Second, privacy and security concerns are heightened when dealing with entities that may not uniformly follow HIPAA or other federal protections —particularly w hen sensitive patient information is exchanged across less -regulated networks. Third, CMS endorsement could be interpreted as a guarantee of reliability or neutrality, creating reputational risk if the endorsed source fails to meet expectations or engages in unethical data practices. Additionally, endorsing select networks could unintentionally stifle innovation or reinforce market consolidation, especially if smaller or open -source initiatives are excluded. There's also the risk of interoperability misalig nment if endorsed networks rely on proprietary standards or diverge from national frameworks like TEFCA. For these reasons, CMS should proceed cautiously and avoid blanket endorsements unless strict criteria are in place to ensure transparency, accountabil ity, and alignment with national interoperability and data governance objectives. TD-15 b: What are the potential disadvantages of their use? Response : While Bulk FHIR APIs offer efficiency for large -scale data extraction, their use also presents several potential disadvantages. First, performance and infrastructure strain can be significant for both the data provider and recipient, particularly if bulk r equests are not well -scoped or optimized. Large data exports can lead to system slowdowns, timeout errors, or queuing issues—especially in environments not built to handle high -volume l oads. Second, there is a risk of overcollection of unnecessary data, which can increase storage costs, complicate downstream processing, and raise privacy concerns if more information is retrieved than is actually needed for a given use case. This is espec ially problematic when the requestor is unsure of the exact data needed, leading to "just in case" extractions. 
Third, data freshness and real -time utility may be limited in bulk exports. Since data is pulled in large batches, it may not reflect real -time updates or recent clinical activity, which can limit its usefulness in time -sensitive workflows. Lastly, the aut horization and access control requirements for Bulk FHIR are more complex, particularly when involving third -party applications or cross -organizational data sharing. Ensuring proper scoping and auditability of what was accessed —and why—can be a governance challenge. These disadvantages reinforce the need to apply Bulk FHIR APIs judiciously, focusing on well - defined, high -value use cases rather than using them as a default approach for data access. TD-16: What are the tradeoffs of maintaining point -to-point models vs. shared network infrastructure? Response : Maintaining point -to-point models offers greater control, customization, and direct accountability between trading partners, which can be especially beneficial in healthcare settings where data sharing requirements vary significantly by organization, geogr aphy, or use case. Point - to-point integrations allow for tailored implementations, more precise access controls, and direct relationships betwe en entities —factors that can improve performance, security, and support responsiveness. However, this model also comes with tradeoffs. As the number of connections grows, so does the complexity and maintenance burden, potentially leading to scalability cha llenges. In contrast, shared network infrastructure (such as QHINs under TEFCA) can simplify connectivity by acting as a hub—but may introduce less flexibility, higher dependency on centralized governance, and slower responsiveness to custom needs or innov ation. For organizations with well -established partnerships or specialized data exchange workflows — such as payors working with known providers or vendors —point -to-point models can offer a better balance of efficiency, security, and alignment with business processes, even if they require more effort to maintain. The key tradeoff is between scalability and control, and for many use cases, maintaining direct connections still provides the most practical and effective solution. This will naturally increase stakeholder interest and participation, particularly when paired with implementation support, testing i nfrastructure, and clear alignment with business and clinical outcomes. Additionally, establishing centralized directories for FHIR endpoints and standardizing registration/onboarding processes would further reduce friction and help unify access pat terns across the ecosystem. Ultimately, better alignment depends on a shift from compliance -driven checkboxes to value - driven design, where APIs support real -world use cases, improve operational efficiency, and promote consistent, interoperable data exchan ge across the healthcare continuum.
