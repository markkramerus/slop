Thank you for the opportunity to provide comments on the Health Technology Ecosystem RFI
(CMS-0042-NC) . Regard Technologies, Inc is a platform that uses proprietary algorithms to automatically
review patient data, identify the most common conditions, and present machine-generated notes and
reports for physicians. These evidence-backed notes save physicians time, help prevent missed diagnoses,
reduce coding queries, and improve documentation. We are pleased to offer perspectives from our
experience as a technology vendor.
Our comments focus primarily on sections B. Patients and Caregivers, C. Providers, E. Technology
Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks, and F. Value-Based Care Organizations
PC-3. Are you aware of health management, care navigation, or personal health record apps that
would be useful to Medicare beneficiaries and their caregivers?
Regard has the ability to synthesize all available patient data , surfacing relevant insights, recommending
both acute and chronic diagnoses, and suggesting care plans. The platform would function as an AI
co-pilot, assisting clinicians and Medicare beneficiaries by aggregating vast quantities of structured and
unstructured data, identifying potential safety concerns and missed diagnoses, and enabling accurate,
auditable documentation. Today this empowers clinicians to ensure every beneficiary gets
evidence-backed care. If deployed as a patient-facing tool, this capability could give beneficiaries and
caregivers a clear, actionable view of their conditions, risks, and care opportunities in a way that is
clinically relevant and easy to understand. This would empower patients to ask better questions, manage
chronic diseases more effectively, and coordinate care across multiple providers with confidence.
PC-4. What features are missing from apps you use or that you are aware of today?
Most personal health record apps today act as passive data repositories—they provide access to raw
clinical information, but they don’t explain it. What’s missing is clinical context and insight: What’s
important? What’s been missed? What should I watch for? Regard’s technology is built to answer those
questions by reviewing 100% of a patient’s chart and distilling it into the most relevant
insights—surfacing diagnoses, reconciling fragmented information, and identifying gaps in
documentation that matter for care. For Medicare beneficiaries—especially those with complex, chronic

conditions—this could turn a confusing data printout into an intelligent summary of their health, giving
them and their caregivers a practical tool to proactively manage care, catch conditions early, and shift
from today’s reactive sick care model to truly preventative care.
PC-12. What are the most valuable operational health data use cases for patients and caregivers that, if
addressed, would create more efficient care navigation or eliminate barriers to competition among
providers or both?
One of the most valuable—but underutilized—use cases for operational health data is using it not just to
track or summarize care, but to actually recommend diagnoses. Today, much of the data in a patient’s
electronic health record sits dormant—fragmented across labs, notes, histories, and medications. It’s
typically accessed for retrospective reporting or after-the-fact decision-making. But that model reinforces
a reactive system, where patients engage only after problems escalate.
Regard provides a proactive care platform that flips the traditional paradigm. The platform leverages AI
to synthesize 100% of a patient’s structured and unstructured data within the EHR, proactively surfacing
relevant insights, ensuring every diagnosis has supporting evidence to confirm its accuracy, suggesting
appropriate care plans, and helping to eliminate fraud and waste. It functions as an AI co-pilot, assisting
clinicians by identifying missed diagnoses, surfacing safety concerns, and ensuring every diagnosis is
backed by clinical evidence—all in real time.
If this capability were made accessible to patients and caregivers, it could radically improve care
navigation. Rather than sifting through disjointed records or waiting on a clinician to interpret what’s
already in the chart, patients would receive an searchable and intelligent summary of their health: likely
conditions, associated risks, and next-step recommendations. This would allow beneficiaries to catch
diseases earlier and make more informed decisions—whether it’s choosing a provider, seeking a second
opinion, or initiating preventive care. This improves access, accuracy, and outcomes.
PR-1. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage providers, including those in rural areas, to
leverage approved (see description in PC-5) digital health products for their patients?
CMS should establish new technology add-on payment (NTAP) to encourage or endorse technologies that
can systematically review 100% of a patient’s structured and unstructured data—ensuring nothing
clinically relevant is overlooked.
For rural providers, who often operate with limited staff and high patient-to-provider ratios, AI-based
platforms like Regard can function as clinical co-pilots. These tools can synthesize data from across the
EHRS and HIE —to surface missed diagnoses, recommend care plans, and flag safety concerns as a
provider opens the chart. With the support of NTAP, CMS could assure providers to ensure every
diagnosis has supporting evidence so that we know it is accurate, helping to eliminate fraud & waste and
catch diseases early to foster more preventative care instead.
PR-7. What strategies can CMS implement to support providers in making high-quality, timely, and
comprehensive healthcare data available for interoperability in the digital product ecosystem? How can
the burden of increasing data availability and sharing be mitigated for providers? Are there ways that

workflows or metrics that providers are already motivated to optimize for that could be reused for, or
combined with, efforts needed to support interoperability?
Providers are overwhelmed by the volume of clinical data, yet most only have time to review an estimated
3% of what’s available in the EHR. To mitigate this burden, they need tools that can intelligently process
and surface the most clinically relevant information
Regard’sanalyzes 100% of the structured and unstructured data in the EHR and HIEs, synthesizing it to
recommend diagnoses, flag missed conditions, and suggest care plans—all within the clinician’s existing
workflow. This not only enhances care quality and patient safety at the point of service but also generates
high-quality, evidence-backed, auditable documentation that is immediately ready for exchange.
By promoting adoption of platforms that improve documentation upstream, CMS can significantly reduce
the burden of downstream data mapping, reconciliation, and transmission. Rather than requiring
additional data entry, this approach supports interoperability by making diagnosis- and care plan–ready
documentation the default—accelerating data sharing, improving continuity of care, and easing the
administrative load on frontline providers.
TD-1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to stimulate
developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers?
Define and endorse AI technologies that measurably improve care and outcomes. This framework
would encourage developers to build tools that systematically review 100% of a patient’s structured
and unstructured data to drive toward better outcomes.
By setting clear expectations for clinical accuracy, auditability, and safety, NTAP approvals would
give developers a trusted pathway to align their innovations with CMS priorities.
VB-1. What incentives could encourage APMs such as accountable care organizations (ACOs) or
participants in Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) to leverage digital health management and
care navigation products more often and more effectively with their patients?
To drive adoption of digital health management and care navigation tools across APMs, CMS should
align incentives with technologies that help providers deliver more proactive, accurate, and coordinated
care—at scale. One approach is to reward the use of tools that not only engage patients but also close
clinical gaps by surfacing overlooked diagnoses, standardizing care recommendations, and guiding
providers toward early interventions. These can be conveyed by creating CPT codes to cover the use of
AI for the review of patient charts to ensure nothing is overlooked.
VB-2. How can key themes and technologies such as artificial intelligence, population
health analytics, risk stratification, care coordination, usability, quality measurement, and patient
engagement be better integrated into APM requirements?
One essential capability for value-based care is the ability to transform raw health data—especially
unstructured clinical documentation—into meaningful, actionable insights that can diagnose and support

earlier, more accurate intervention. Foundational to this is health IT that can systematically analyze a
patient’s full medical record and surface likely or missed diagnoses, supported by evidence already
present in EHRs and health information exchanges (HIEs).
These capabilities not only improve care quality but also generate accurate, auditable documentation that
can align with reimbursement models, reduce administrative burden, and help providers succeed under
value-based arrangements. Investing in infrastructure that supports these insights at scale will be
foundational for the next generation of clinical and operational performance.
In closing, we believe CMS should encourage the use of technology that helps providers deliver
higher-quality, lower-burden care that:
● Equips health care providers with better information about their patients to improve outcomes &
reduce burden on doctors
● Ensures every diagnosis has supporting evidence so that we know it is accurate, helping to
eliminate fraud & waste
● Catches diseases early to foster more preventative care instead of the current sick-care paradigm
This can be achieved by incentivizing health systems to adopt AI technologies that review 100% of
patient data and surface evidence-backed diagnoses. CMS should prioritize technology that fits
seamlessly into the provider workflow. Creating CPT codes or AI pathways for NTAP would support
these efforts.

and improve care for all patients. We welcome any further questions and look forward to ongoing
collaboration.
