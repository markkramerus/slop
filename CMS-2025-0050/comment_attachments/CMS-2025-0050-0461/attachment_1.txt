Note the RFI Summary lead-in, “Effective and responsible adoption of technology can empower
patients to make better decisions for their health and well-being”. We believe this Administration is
uniquely qualified to turn the tables on an HIT infrastructure currently subservient to everything big: big
government and big institutions, including big providers, big health plans, big vendors, big pharma...
“Patient-centric” and “patient empowerment” are contending buzzwords but are entirely peripheral
considerations to many of the most highly-promoted HIT infrastructure “solutions”. Individual clinicians
have also been brushed aside, with little attention to their pleas for focus on usability, efficacy and
burden reduction. Deserving they are, yet bearing little recognition, ever relegated to the role of small
cogs in a big wheel.
Although we didn’t have time to fully address every RFI question we worked to examine muddles and
mania in current HIT infrastructure and to offer worthy suggestions in our quest for “effective and
responsible adoption of technology [to] empower patients”.

PC-2 – Do you have easy access to your own and all your loved ones' health information in one location (for example, in a
single patient portal or another software system)?
• Only if there is a single provider for all care.
• Otherwise each provider has a separate portal - which could amount to a dozen or more per individual.
• Each portal has its own variant way to access, navigate, display and alert/notify.
• The need is for independent PHR and/or Health Record Bank accounts (not EHR portals), where an
individual can direct their health information be sent, e.g., after each encounter.
• This is the centered model = one patient one record - single source of truth.
• Obviates the need for schemes such as TEFCA where individual health information is scattered all over the
place in dissonant fragments.
PC-2a – If so, what are some examples of benefits it has provided?
• All health information for an individual is directed to a single logical location and dispersed, as needed and
authorized, from that same location.
• Provides a single point of access to, and full integration of, an individual's health information.
• Enables individual, not institutional, control of personal health information.
• Encourages providers to talk with the individual, not about the individual.

health information?

o Demographics, current contact information, next of kin

o Personal providers and caregivers: directory (location, contact information), past and present
• Curated (reconciled, integrated) lists:

PC-5a – What role, if any, should CMS have in reviewing or approving digital health products on the basis of their efficacy,
quality or impact or both on health outcomes (not approving in the sense of a coverage determination)? What criteria should
be used if there is a review process? What technology solutions, policy changes, or program design changes can increase
patient and caregiver adoption of digital health products (for example, enhancements to data access, reimbursement
adjustments, or new beneficiary communications)?
First consider statutory obligations...
HITECH Act (2009), Section 3001(c)(3)(A): “The National Coordinator [ONC] shall, in consultation with other
appropriate Federal agencies (including the National Institute of Standards and Technology), update the Federal
Health IT Strategic Plan... to include specific objectives, milestones, and metrics with respect to the following:
‘‘(i) The electronic exchange and use of health information and the enterprise integration of such information.
‘‘(ii) The utilization of an electronic health record for each person in the United States by 2014…”
• As described in the HITECH Act (sub-bullet (ii) above), it is incumbent on CMS/ASTP/ONC to develop
“specific objectives, milestones, and metrics with respect to… the utilization of an electronic health record
for each person in the United States”.
• HITECH does not say “an electronic health record for each provider”, yet a substantial fortune (>$45B USD)
has been spent in incentives for provider-based EHR systems, thus ensuring that “each person” in the
United States had a dozen or more separate electronic health records. This is equivalent to, and perhaps
worse than, having none.
• The assumption seems to be that person-centeredness will appear as an emergent phenomenon, without
the need for any actual constituent part that unambiguously represents the person in the overall picture.
21st Century Cures Act (2016), Section 4006(a), Encouraging Patient Access to Health Information
“The Secretary shall use existing authorities and take additional actions... with the goal of offering patients
access to their electronic health information in a single, longitudinal format that is easy to understand, secure,
and may be updated automatically...”
• Over extended time CMS/ASTP/ONC has done little to establish or promote a person-centered electronic
health record, inclusive of health information over time from all providers. This was the point of HITECH and
later the 21st Century Cures Act. Taking better care of providers and their records – if that’s what’s been
accomplished – has a tenuous connection at best to taking better care of patients.
PC-5b – What changes would enable timely access to high quality CMS and provider generated data on patients?
• Important to define "high quality data"... What are its characteristics, how is it measured, how is it ensured?
• Data quality, or lack thereof, plagues all end uses and all end users of health information today. Everyone
wants to rely on digitized data but we have vanishingly little upon which to base trust/assurance – other than
the virtue that it was captured, managed and rendered by software.

“The vision we seek to achieve is a system where individuals are at the center of their care and where providers
have the ability to securely access and use health information from different sources. A system where an

TEFCA – The Scatter Model
• TEFCA is a prime example of how the US Government has demanded, effectively enabled and enforced the
separation of individuals and their health information.
• TEFCA ensures individual health information scattered across multiple sources
o As diverse and disparate fragments
• TEFCA builds on a HIN of HINs. The very postulation of such an overarching framework/agreement
instantiates the combinatorial explosion of complexities between myriad institutional EHR/HIT systems each
with distinct conventions, policies and governance and methods, evidenced as disparate:
o Individual IDs, identity parameters
o Event, activity and healthcare procedure definitions
o Data definitions: structured and unstructured, data types, formats, precisions, content and reference
ranges, units of measure, code/value sets
o Content/context patterns and binding
o Metadata and provenance
o Mechanisms for authorization (permissions) and consent management
o Mechanisms for user authentication and access control
o Mechanisms for accountability
o Mechanisms for audit: events/triggers, logs, log content
----------
The Vanishing Individual
• HITECH, the 21st Century Cures Act and even TEFCA claim to aspire for each individual to have their own
record and be at the center of their own care
• With TEFCA...
o There is nowhere to point to that is that center.
o Individuals remain as passive parameters in the workings of institutional systems and the exchanges
between them.
o Individuals have no independent existence – they remain scattered and fragmented across institutions,
QHINs/HINs, systems and devices.

Individual PHR or HRB Account – Account and Data Exchange Functions
Account Setup
1. Patient or patient representative/coach establishes an individual account with trusted entity, such as an
independent Health Record Bank (HRB) or Personal Health Record (PHR) Entity
a. Patient receives:
i. A Unique ID
ii. An Account Access URL to their individual account (with modifiable password)
iii. A FHIR Endpoint (Push URL) where their health record entries can be sent (in batches conforming with
HL7 FHIR)
2. Patient shares the Push URL with their healthcare Providers
Account Deposits
3. Account “deposits” are made as persistent health record entries:
a. Originated by patients, their guardians or representatives
b. Originated by, then received from, external sources, such as patient healthcare providers (e.g., at the
conclusion of a patient encounter)
4. All “deposits” are visible to the individual patient
Account Withdrawals
5. Account “withdrawals” are copied from persistent health record entries:
a. Based on inquiries received from/transmitted to:
i. Covered entities legitimately operating under HIPAA provisions for Treatment, Payment and Operations
ii. Other entities authorized by the patient or their representative
• Patient Representatives, Health Coaches, Family Members, Friends...
• Clinical Trials/Researchers, Public Health, Marketing...
b. Based on “push” or “pull” model
c. Extracts may be constrained, as appropriate, to:
i. “Minimum necessary and “need to know” for intended purpose of access/use
ii. De-identified data
6. All ”withdrawals” are visible to the individual patient

Record Entry Convention
• From the HL7 FHIR Record Lifecycle Event Implementation Guide:
o Actions are taken to support patient health.
▪ Actions are taken in provision of healthcare to individuals.
▪ Actions are taken as the result of rules-based EHR[/HIT] System algorithms.
▪ Actors (i.e., patients, providers, users, systems) take Actions.
▪ Actions broadly encompass tasks, acts, procedures or services performed or provided.
o The EHR[/HIT] System captures Actions taken and creates corresponding Record Entries.
▪ Record Entries provide persistent evidence of Action occurrence, context, disposition, facts, findings
and observations.
▪ From the point of Record Entry origination to the end of its lifespan, the EHR[/HIT] System manages
each Entry consistent with and according to scope of practice, organizational policy, and
jurisdictional law.
▪ In support of individual health and in provision of healthcare to individuals, Actors perform Actions
and Actions have corresponding Entries in the EHR[/HIT] Record, (i.e., Action instances are
documented by Record Entry instances).
▪ Record Entries may be captured during the course of the Action or sometime thereafter.
▪ The Actor (author/source) of the Record Entry may be the same as an Actor performing the Action
or not…

o Actions have associated metadata (e.g., who, what, when, where, why, how, under what conditions, in
what context).
▪ The corresponding Record Entry captures this metadata along with other Action and Record Entry
related information.
o Each Record Entry also includes its own provenance metadata such as who (authoring Actor) and when
(documented).
▪ Record Entries may be encapsulated to bind Actor (individual, organization, and/or system)
signatures to data and metadata content and data/time of occurrence.
▪ Actions and related Record Entries capture a chronology of patient health and healthcare and also a
chronology of operations and services provided in/by a healthcare enterprise.
▪ Record Entries reflect changes in health information from the time it was created, to the time it was
amended, sent, received, etc. In this manner, each Record Entry serves as persistent evidence of
an Action taken, enabling providers to maintain comprehensive information that may be needed for
legal, [clinical,] business, and disclosure purposes.
▪ To satisfy these purposes, Record Entries must also be retained and persisted without alteration.
o Record Entries have both a lifecycle and a lifespan.
▪ Lifecycle Events include originate, retain, amend, verify, attest, access/view, de-identify,
transmit/receive, and more.
▪ Lifecycle Events occur at various points in a Record Entry lifespan, always starting with a point of
origination and retention (i.e., when the Entry is first created and stored).
o A Record Entry may have a pre and post Event state if content is modified.
▪ In this case, the original Record Entry is preserved (with signature binding) and a new Entry is
created (with new signature binding).
▪ A Record Entry contains data and metadata, in multiple formats, following various conventions and
standards. Included data may be tagged, and/or delimited, structured (concise, encoded,
computable), or unstructured (free form, non-computable).
▪ Data may be encoded as text, document, images, audio, waveforms, in ASCII, binary or other
encoding.
▪ [Record Entry content may encompass, correspond or be bound directly to FHIR resource(s) related
to Actions Taken.]
• To effectively manage health data/records longitudinally and continuously throughout the continuum of care,
needed is the Record Entry convention implemented across EHR/HIT systems and points of exchange.
These conventions are fully specified in the standards enumerated in response to PC-12 below.

Fax or Faux? Are We Really Ready to Replace the Fax in Health Information Sharing?
Fax → a facsimile; an exact copy, especially of a document; an image of a document made by electronic
scanning and transmitted as data by telecommunication links
Faux → artificial; not genuine or real; being an imitation of the genuine article
While there is widespread agreement with the objective to replace fax machine transmission/reproduction of
health records with “interoperability” between EHR/HIT systems, the question is whether the current set of
interoperability schemes are in fact competent to achieve/replace a basic fax function. Indeed there is one vital
characteristic where it remains superior, if not infallible... What goes in (source artifact) is identical to what
comes out (artifact produced by the fax). If not, these devices would be immediately placed out of service and
then repaired or replaced.
The design of today’s interoperability schemes has largely disregarded the requirement to reproduce (for
purposes of exchange and end use) an identical copy of the original (source) record, as an authentic
reproduction without alteration.
Today’s interoperability schemes are mostly comprised of “standardized” exchange artifacts, e.g., HL7 v2/v3
messages, CDA/C-CDA documents or FHIR resources. Use of these artifacts typically REQUIRES data content
transformation twice in the course of exchange: first at transmission, from source representation to exchange
artifact representation, then again on receipt, from the exchange artifact to receiver representation. Thus:
Source → Exchange → Receiver
Transformations often INTRODUCE alterations, omissions and unresolved errors in data content.
Transformations often LOSE OR DISJOIN attribution, authorship/signature, verification/attestation, provenance,
context, purpose of capture, consistency, comparability, units of measure and binding to conventions of coding,
classification and vocabulary systems.
The risks (to data integrity) introduced by data transformation – as described above – also represent major risks
to the integrity of the clinical process and most importantly, SERIOUS RISKS TO PATIENT SAFETY.
These risks also strike at core principles of trust (assurance) and truth (authenticity) which are essential to
achieve full interoperability and usability of, and confidence in, health information – for providers and individual
subjects of care alike.
Suggest that it’s time to fully assess and address serious risks to clinical integrity and patient safety posed by
health data/record management and exchange “solutions” that fail to maintain fidelity to source.
