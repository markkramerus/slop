Introduction
Cohere Health is a patient-centric company committed to streamlining the clinical decision-making
processes for health insurers and physicians, with the ultimate aim of improving patient care and health
outcomes. Launched in 2019, Cohere is transforming prior authorization–a process that presents
numerous financial, administrative, and clinical concerns for all stakeholders.
Our mission directly aligns with the Administration's policy to substantially reduce private expenditures
associated with Federal regulation compliance, particularly as it relates to the process of prior
authorization (PA), achieved through a focus on intelligent automation, interoperability, and the
implementation of evidence-based care pathways. Enclosed in our RFI response is an outline of how
we’re unleashing innovation and reducing burden in Medicare through a new approach to PA:
● Applying innovative technology to PA can provide needed oversight and transparency of
health insurance for Americans; minimize physician burnout; improve patients’ health
outcomes; and limit inappropriate care, waste, and abuse in our nation’s healthcare system.
● Using the latest developments in artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) responsibly
can surface real-time approvals for patient care, while ensuring care is never denied by
AI/technology. This approach, coupled with an improved peer-to-peer process between
physicians of the same specialty, will ensure patient safety, improve clinical outcomes, and
significantly optimize the healthcare system.
● Cohere has observed an 80 percent reduction in care delays, a 40 percent reduction in
healthcare providers time spent on authorizations, and a significant reduction in denials of
patient care using intelligent PA. This strategy is preferred by providers, as evidenced by our 93
percent provider satisfaction rating.
Our close work with Medicare Advantage (MA) plans, as well as Commercial and Managed Medicaid
plans, makes us a valuable thought partner to the agency. We have tested and optimized best practices
for PA that have the potential to truly transform the system. We would also be thrilled to help CMS pilot its
first-ever AI-driven PA solution within the Medicare Fee-for-Service (FFS) program, leveraging the
innovation afforded by the private sector. Adopting an approach for Medicare FFS that more closely
resembles the approach in MA would be an effective way for CMS to capture savings for the program,
identify inappropriateness and abuse patterns across the delivery system, and ensure patient safety and
appropriate clinical care. This will significantly reduce confusion and abrasion amongst providers who
submit for both MA and FFS. Cohere could identify a subset of codes to pilot this approach with the
Medicare Administrative Contractors (MACs).

1. Patient Needs
Intelligent PA is an important mechanism to ensure patients receive safe and appropriate medical care
without unnecessary delays. Patient safety is of utmost importance and care decisions must use
evidence-based criteria. For example, Cohere’s ML models can identify retropulsion (bone fragments
pushing into spinal canal) on an MRI report, which is a contraindication for a kyphoplasty (injected cement
into the vertebral body to treat a compression fracture). If the technology determines that the request is
not appropriate for auto-approval, it immediately pends the case for physician review.
Some treatments, such as spinal cord stimulators, are invasive and have unpredictable and variable
outcomes. It is vital to carefully look for all indications and ensure all clinical criteria are met before
rendering an approval. Cohere has found that spinal cord stimulators are grossly overused; only 50
percent of patients who receive a spinal cord stimulator have positive outcomes. This aligns with the
evidence that this should be a treatment of last resort for patients with spinal issues. If any gaps in the
clinical record indicate a patient may not meet all the criteria for appropriateness and effectiveness based
on the guidelines, an authorization requires clinician review.
Peer-to-peer consultations can be extremely beneficial for ensuring appropriate and safe clinical care, so
long as the conversation is held between physicians of the same specialty (or, ideally, sub-specialty).
Oftentimes, peer-to-peer reviews can be regarded as adversarial or burdensome for physicians, but there
is untapped potential in leveraging this process as more akin to second opinion/consults, which Medicare
regulations do not support today. Cohere encourages CMS to require that peer-to-peers take place
between physicians of the same specialty to improve patient safety. Leveraging peer-to-peer
consultations as a second opinion process would also ensure that the latest and greatest treatments,
clinical protocols, and evidence can be applied to patients, even in the most remote and rural settings, so
that no matter where a patient lives, they too receive access to evidence-based medicine and safe,
quality outcomes.
Cohere appreciated Dr. Oz’s comments on limiting the code set to reduce the burden on physicians. We
believe that the number of codes is not the relevant issue, however, and that the area for greater
opportunity and impact is around the scope of care appropriateness. We believe the best way to reduce
provider abrasion is to focus oversight on where in the code set inappropriate use most frequently occurs.
Cohere has the data that can help CMS optimize which codes should be on a PA list–codes that are and
are not inappropriately used–to help with list optimization.

● Prior Authorization Decision Data: Prior authorization data is some of the most valuable health

● Clinical Guidelines - Medical Necessity Criteria: Standardized, machine-readable versions of the

PC–11. How are health information exchanges (HIEs) currently helping to advance patient access to
health information in the real world?
● HIEs play a valuable role, particularly at the regional or state level, in aggregating and sharing
patient data among participating providers. This often improves information availability for
continuity of care during transitions and for emergency care. Some HIEs offer patient portals, or
contribute data to larger patient-facing platforms, thereby improving patient access to their
records.
a. How valuable, available, and accurate do you find the data they share to be?
● Valuable: Generally valuable for care coordination, especially for longitudinal views within a
specific region or health system.
● Availability: Varies significantly by HIE. Some have broad participation, others are more limited.
● Accuracy: Generally considered accurate for the data they ingest, but completeness can be an
issue if not all relevant providers participate.
● Limitations: The data may not always be real-time, comprehensive across all care settings (e.g.,
fragmented from different HIEs or non-participating entities).
b. What changes would you suggest?
● Increased Interoperability *between* HIEs: Ensure seamless data exchange across different
HIEs, perhaps leveraging TEFCA.
● Standardization of Data Elements and Quality: Mandate adherence to USCDI and other
standards to improve data consistency.
● Integration of Prior Authorization Data: Encourage HIEs to incorporate structured PA requests,
decisions, and clinical criteria into their data exchange.
● Enhanced Patient-facing Capabilities: Support HIEs in developing more robust and user-friendly
patient portals or APIs for patient access.

● Models that demonstrate strong governance, high provider participation rates, robust data quality,

d. What is the ongoing role of HIEs amidst other entities facilitating data exchange and broader

● HIEs will continue to play a crucial role as regional or state-level aggregators and facilitators of

TEFCA, many HIEs are likely to evolve into or become participants within QHINs, leveraging their
existing infrastructure and trusted relationships. They will likely coexist and interconnect with
vendor HINs and private exchange networks, forming a multi-layered ecosystem for data flow.
Their role will be to ensure comprehensive local data coverage and support use cases specific to
their communities, while linking into the broader national framework.

PR–1. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage providers, including those in rural areas, to
leverage approved digital health products for their patients?
CMS and its partners can encourage provider adoption by:
● Demonstrate Value & ROI: Clearly articulate the tangible benefits for providers, such as
reductions in administrative burden and offer reimbursement for specific digital health services
that demonstrably improve care or reduce costs. Link digital health adoption to value-based care
models and quality performance measures.
● Streamlined Integration and Interoperability: Prioritize digital health solutions that offer seamless
integration with EHRs and clinical workflows, or have the opportunity to connect to existing
channels like the fax machine. Interoperability is key to ensuring advancements in technology can
modernize processes like prior authorization, however, today, most healthcare organizations,
especially smaller individual physicians in both urban and rural areas, are still using fax
machines. In fact, about 50 percent of all prior authorization submissions are non-electric (e.g.,
8

fax or phone). Fax conversion, which Cohere deploys, is one strategy that would ensure that,
regardless of the means of submission (e.g., phone, fax, portal), there is no “wrong door” for
submission.
● Education and Training: Provide comprehensive training and ongoing support for providers and
their staff on how to effectively use digital health tools.
● Address Interoperability Gaps: Aggressively enforce information blocking policies and mandate
standardized APIs (especially for Prior Authorization) to ensure that digital health products can
truly exchange data. Today, the legacy portal systems lack simple design and interactiveness to
connect with smaller systems, which is critical for adoption and true interoperability.
● Rural Support: Offer grants for rural providers to invest in necessary broadband infrastructure and
digital health technology, and provide technical assistance tailored to their unique needs.
a.What are the current obstacles?
● Administrative Burden: Perceived or actual additional work required to integrate and use new
digital tools.
● Lack of Interoperability: Siloed EHR systems and proprietary data formats prevent seamless data
flow.
● Cost of Adoption: Financial investment in new technologies, training, and infrastructure.
● Lack of Clear ROI: Difficulty in demonstrating a direct financial or clinical benefit that justifies the
investment.
● Digital Divide: Limited broadband access and technological expertise/investment in rural areas.
● Information Blocking: Practices by some legacy vendors that hinder access to or exchange of

● Trust and Efficacy Concerns: Skepticism regarding the effectiveness or security of unproven

PR–2. What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective utilization of the most
useful and innovative applications for physician workflows, such as quality measurement reporting,
clinical documentation, and billing tasks? How could these obstacles be mitigated?
● Obstacles:
○ Lack of Data Standardization: Inconsistent data formats and requirements make it difficult
to develop applications that work across multiple EHRs.
○ Proprietary APIs and Information Blocking: EHR vendors often use proprietary APIs or
engage in information blocking, hindering third-party developers from accessing and
utilizing clinical data. This forces developers to build point-to-point integrations,
increasing cost and complexity.
○ Fragmented Clinical Guidelines: Today, there is a duopoly in the clinical guideline industry
and providers face inconsistencies and confusion when submitting prior authorizations
and making key clinical decisions to render care.
○ Regulatory Complexity: A patchwork of state and federal regulations around data sharing,
privacy, and billing.
○ High Cost of Integration: Developing and maintaining integrations with numerous EHR
systems is expensive.
○ Usability Issues with Existing EHRs: Clunky interfaces and workflows in legacy EHRs
make it difficult to integrate new, innovative applications seamlessly.
○ Limited Access to Real-World Data: Difficulty in obtaining de-identified real-world data for
training and validating AI/ML models.
○ Unclear standards for AI governance: Creates confusion and mis-trust on the
appropriate use of AI within healthcare. Further, the patchwork nature of state level
regulations will greatly increase compliance burdens and limit the benefits of AI usage.
● Mitigation:
○ Strict Enforcement of Information Blocking Rules: CMS and ONC must vigorously
penalize information blocking practices to ensure true interoperability.
○ Mandate Standardized FHIR APIs: Require all certified EHRs to support a
comprehensive set of FHIR APIs, including those for Prior Authorization (e.g., Da Vinci
CRD/DTR/PAS), Patient Access, Provider Access, and Bulk FHIR.
○ National Guideline Clearinghouse: As Cohere previously suggested, CMS could issue an
RFP for a nationwide clearinghouse to standardize clinical guidelines in an open-source,
public-good framework. This would make guideline-based automation more feasible.

○ Incentivize EHR Modernization: Provide incentives for EHR vendors to adopt more open
architectures and developer-friendly platforms.
○ Cloud-Based Interoperability Platforms: Support the development of secure, cloud-based

○ Reduce Redundant Reporting: Consolidate quality reporting requirements and leverage

PR–3. How important is it for healthcare delivery and interoperability in urban and rural areas that all data
in an EHR system be accessible for exchange, regardless of storage format (for example, scanned
documents, faxed records, lab results, free text notes, structured data fields)? Please address all of the
following:
● It is critically important for healthcare delivery and interoperability that all data in an EHR system
be accessible for exchange, regardless of format. The current reality, where approximately 50%
of PA submissions are non-electronic, highlights this urgent need. This means that, despite best
efforts, interoperability with EMRs will not unlock all of the potential and it is critical that the
healthcare system finds new ways to migrate fax to simpler apps and portals. Across Cohere’s
book of business, we have nearly 90 percent portal adoption which is significantly higher than
national average, however, we find that it is critical to still support the 10 percent that don’t submit
via electronic means.
a. Current challenges in accessing different data formats.
● Scanned Documents/Faxes: These are essentially images, requiring manual review or complex
OCR/AI processing to extract usable data. This is time-consuming, prone to error, and a major
bottleneck for prior authorization. Fax conversion, which Cohere deploys, is one strategy that
would ensure that, regardless of the means of submission (e.g., phone, fax, portal), there is no
“wrong door” for submission.
● Free Text Notes:While rich in clinical detail, extracting structured, actionable information from free
text requires advanced Natural Language Processing (NLP) and AI, which many systems lack.
● Lack of Standardization: Even structured data fields can vary in terminology, coding, and
granularity across different EHRs, requiring significant mapping and normalization.
● Proprietary Data Models: Data stored in proprietary formats within EHRs is difficult to access and
translate without vendor-specific tools.
b. Impact on patient care quality.
● Care Delays:Inability to quickly access complete patient information, particularly from faxes and
scanned documents, directly leads to delays in diagnosis and treatment, as seen in the PA
process. Cohere's intelligent PA has reduced care delays by 80%.
● Medical Errors:Incomplete or fragmented patient data can lead to medication errors, redundant
tests, and inappropriate treatment decisions.
● Provider Burnout: Clinicians spend excessive time sifting through unorganized or inaccessible
data, contributing to frustration and burnout.
● Fragmented Care: Lack of a holistic patient view hinders effective care coordination across
different specialties and care settings.
c. Technical barriers to full data accessibility.
● Legacy Systems: Many legacy EHRs were not designed with interoperability in mind and lack
modern API capabilities and also often strategically block integration with smaller, newer
technologies as previously mentioned.
11

● Cost of Data Transformation: The expense and technical complexity of converting unstructured or
proprietary data into standardized, exchangeable formats.
● AI/NLP Maturity: While rapidly advancing, AI/NLP for accurate extraction from complex clinical
free text still requires significant development and validation.
● Lack of Common Language: No common "language" across all healthcare data systems for
consistent interpretation.
● Fragmented Clinical Guidelines: As previously mentioned, today there is a duopoly in the clinical

● Cost: Initial investment would be significant for EHR vendors and providers to upgrade systems,

PR–12. Should ASTP/ONC consider removing or revising any of the information blocking exceptions or
conditions within the exceptions (45 CFR part 171, subparts B through D) to further the access,
exchange, and use of electronic health information (EHI) and to promote market competition?
Yes. From Cohere Health's experience, particularly in the context of prior authorization and the challenges
of accessing complete clinical data, we believe ASTP/ONC should continuously review and consider
revising or refining information blocking exceptions, with a strong bias toward promoting access,
exchange, and use of EHI. The goal should be to remove any exceptions that are being exploited to
hinder legitimate data sharing for treatment, payment, and healthcare operations. In addition to patient
care challenges posed by information-blocking practices of legacy technology companies and
EMRs/EHRs, it also thwarts innovation in the healthcare delivery system, particularly among technology
startup organizations which is a detriment to the economy.

● Content and Manner Exception: Ensure that this exception is not used to limit the types of EHI
made available (e.g., intentionally excluding detailed imaging reports or prior authorization

are designed to be prohibitive barriers.
● Security and Privacy Exceptions: While essential, ensure these are not overly broadly interpreted
to justify blocking data that could be securely shared.
● Interference Exception: Ensure that legitimate competition or innovation is not being labeled as
"interference" to justify blocking.
Revising exceptions should focus on clarity, specificity, and preventing loopholes that allow actors to
avoid their obligations. The ultimate impact should be to promote a more competitive market for digital
health and technology companies by lowering the barrier to data access for innovators.

F. Value-Based Care Organizations
Prior authorization data and the insights gleaned from its deployment can be leveraged to develop metrics
concerning the appropriateness of care within total cost of care models, empowering CMS and payers to
effectively monitor and, as appropriate, reward or penalize physicians. For example, intelligent PA can
predict physician behavior and proactively recommend timely services (e.g., an authorization for surgery
could trigger the timely arrangement of home-based post-acute care or the delivery of durable medical
equipment [DME]), or suggest shifts in the site of service (e.g., from inpatient to outpatient/ambulatory
surgery centers when clinically appropriate), which are significantly more cost effective.
