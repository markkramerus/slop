1. Digital Health Applications and Provider Adoption (PR-1 to PR-4)
PR-1. Encouraging Provider Use of Digital Health Products
Houston Methodist strongly supports efforts to expand access to and adoption of approved digital
health products for providers. Remote Patient Monitoring (RPM) has evolved from a tool primarily
used in intensive care units (ICUs) to a widely adopted solution across acute care settings. RPM
offers continuous surveillance and early detection of changes in patient conditions. The newest
generation of wearable medical devices provide real-time, continuous vital sign data for all
hospitalized patients, enhancing clinical responsiveness.
In April 2023, Houston Methodist launched RPM initiative using the BioButton®2across all acute
care units. Initially deployed at our flagship hospital, the program has expanded to over 2,600 non-
ICU beds. The BioButton is a small, battery-operated device that sticks to the patient’s chest upon
admission, continuously monitoring heart rate, respiratory rate, and skin temperature. The data
feeds into a central dashboard, where AI-driven analytics identify concerning trends and notify the
care team in real time.
This program has already yielded improved patient outcomes, enhanced safety, and reduced the
workload for bedside nurses. Based on these results, we are exploring outpatient applications to
support post-discharge recovery. To enable broader adoption of RPM technologies, we urge CMS
and ONC to eliminate reimbursement barriers for RPM devices and promote integration of
standardized device data into EHR systems.
PR-1a. Current Obstacles
Despite their clinical value, RPM devices often lack adequate Medicare coverage, making them
unaffordable for many providers. While Houston Methodist covers the annual subscription cost for
the BioButton® without separate Medicare payment, most providers lack the financial flexibility to

adopt such technology at scale. Additionally, RPM implementation requires dedicated staff to
respond to alerts and manage technical issues—resources that many organizations cannot spare.
Compounding these challenges, current fee-for-service payment models prioritize time-based
billing over long-term disease control, misaligning incentives and undervaluing outcomes. To
address this, we urge CMS to:
• Eliminate arbitrary requirements such as the 16-day data threshold for RPM and Remote
Therapeutic Monitoring (RTM), which often lack clinical relevance and limit appropriate use.
• Adopt the American Medical Association’s CPT Panel recommendation to establish new
supply codes that differentiate between 2–15 days and 16+ days of device use and ensure
fair and sustainable reimbursement for both.
• Shift RPM reimbursement to reflect the full scope of care—device provisioning, clinical
integration, and patient outcomes—rather than minutes of monitoring or interaction time.
Since the introduction of RPM and RTM codes, reimbursement cuts under the Medicare Physician
Fee Schedule have jeopardized program sustainability, particularly for providers in underserved or
rural areas. RPM should be treated not as an optional add-on, but as an essential infrastructure for
managing chronic conditions, facilitating transitional care, and supporting early intervention. We
respectfully urge CMS to halt further payment reductions and ensure rates reflect the clinical and
operational demands of high-quality RPM services.
PR-1b and PR-1c. Information and Responsibilities When Using Digital Tools
Providers have a responsibility to ensure that patients understand the goals of any digital health
intervention, how their health data will be used, and the critical role they play in co-managing their
health. Houston Methodist informs every patient that is admitted about the use of the BioButton®
and the associated AI-algorithms used to monitor vital trends.
PR-2. Obstacles in Physician Workflows
Houston Methodist recognizes several key obstacles that hinder providers from developing,
deploying, and utilizing digital health tools including cost constraints, limitations on telehealth use,
and challenges related to reimbursement and coverage.
Addressing Cost Barriers
Cost remains a significant barrier to broader adoption of digital and AI-enabled tools, particularly
among smaller practices. To address this challenge, CMS should consider shifting away from time-
based reimbursement models and move towards rewarding improved clinical outcomes. Though
Houston Methodist and other large systems have been able to overcome this cost barrier, may
smaller practices and community-based providers cannot.

Support for Permanent Telehealth Flexibilities
While Houston Methodist supports CMS’s decision to allow direct supervision via telehealth
available through CY2025, we urge CMS to make this permanent across all scenarios. Clinical
teams are best positioned to determine when telehealth-based direct supervision is appropriate,
based on the specific needs and circumstances of their patients. Making this flexibility permanent
would support innovation and the sustainable integration of virtual care into clinical workflows.
Coverage and Reimbursement for Digital Health Tools
Uncertainty regarding the reimbursement of digital health tools is another notable barrier. CMS
should review coverage pathways and revise reimbursement frameworks to reflect the innovative
and diverse nature of digital health solutions and products. A lack of clear guidance about whether
and how digital solutions—such as predictive analytics platforms, decision support tools, and
behavior-modification technologies—can be reimbursed under Medicare or Medicaid leads to
underutilization of otherwise valuable care management tools.
Houston Methodist encourages CMS to offer specific guidance for providers and Medicare
Advantage plans regarding coverage of emerging technologies, including those aimed at non-
mental health indications (e.g., cardiology, pulmonology, neurology). Additionally, CMS should
consider permitting these tools to be reimbursed under a temporary transitional coverage
mechanism while more permanent policies are developed. To this end, CMS could pilot a one- to
two-year coverage window to allow new digital tools to gain experience, produce outcomes data,
and support the case for longer-term coverage. This would offer valuable flexibility and promote
innovation while preserving program integrity.
PR-3 and PR-4. Importance of EHR Data Accessibility and Third-Party App Access to
Administrative Workflows
Effective remote patient monitoring systems depend on clinicians having real-time access to
comprehensive health data and seamless integration into the Electronic Health Record (EHR). It’s
important that any new device or digital health tool that is designed to assist physician workflows
provide seamless data exchange between the device and the EHR system to support patient
monitoring.
Similarly, enabling third party applications to support administrative workflows requires more than
just access to patient data, it requires integration into full operational functions such as scheduling
and communication across the system. To facilitate this and make this easier for providers, CMS
and ONC should strengthen support for cross-platform functionality and enhance interoperability
between care outreach platforms and payer administrative systems.

2. Digital Health In Value-Based Care Models (VB-1 to VB-4)
VB-1. Incentives to Drive ACO/MSSP Adoption
One of the current challenges in value-based care (VBC) programs is the CMS requirement that
ACO’s must aggregate all data from various EHR systems for every patient, regardless of payer.
While this mandate aims to promote comprehensive care coordination, it places a considerable
burden on participating entities. Many small, rural, or resource-constrained organizations —as well
as independent primary care physicians and solo practitioners—are disproportionately impacted
due to the substantial investments required for data integration technology, IT infrastructure, and
training. These burdens can create a barrier to entry for physician practices that may otherwise be
well-positioned to participate in ACOs but lack the technical and financial capacity to comply.
The lack of interoperability among EHR platforms further compounds the issue, creating
inefficiencies and increasing administrative burdens. To mitigate these challenges, we recommend
that CMS provide targeted financial and technical support to both ACOs and affiliated providers to
build the necessary infrastructure for data integration. This could include grant funding or incentive
programs modeled after the HITECH Act to support investment in interoperable health IT systems,
staff training, and vendor coordination. In parallel, CMS should work with the Office of the National
Coordinator for Health IT (ONC) to enforce and accelerate the adoption of standardized
interoperability frameworks, such as FHIR-based APIs and USCDI-aligned data elements, across
certified EHR vendors. These standards are critical to enabling seamless data exchange across
platforms and reducing the complexity and cost of multi-payer data aggregation. Without such
federal coordination and vendor accountability, the burden of integration will continue to fall
disproportionately on ACOs, undermining the goals of value-based care and widening disparities in
participation.
VB-2. Integrating Technologies into APMs
HMCC’s strategy to generate savings focuses on preventing avoidable high-cost patient events by
using predictive analytics. These capabilities allow our team to identify and flag high-risk patients
and deploy case management, nurse outreach programs, or education initiatives to prevent
admission, readmission, or an unnecessary visit. Integrating predictive analytics and other digital
health technologies into Alternative Payment Model (APM) workflows beyond data collection is
crucial as predictive analytics or other AI would not be able be as effective in flagging non-
adherence.
However, the utility of such tools is limited when reimbursement fails to match the operational
realities of implementation. We encourage CMS to ensure digital monitoring tools that support APM
success are sufficiently reimbursed. CMS should consider reimbursement models that tie monthly
payments to outcomes or chronic condition control rates, as opposed to volume-based service

codes. CMS should also ensure that devices used in these models are eligible for coverage whether
they are classified under legacy benefit categories such as DME, or as incident-to supplies.
VB-3. Essential Health IT Capabilities
To support effective value-based care arrangements, CMS and ONC should prioritize platforms
capable of risk stratification as well as actionable care planning. These systems must help clinical
teams identify patients in need of additional services and guide interventions across the care
continuum.
VB-4. Essential Data Types
While the BioButton® offers continuous vital sign monitoring, identifying high-risk patients requires
additional data, including claims and encounter data, and social determinants of health (SDOH).
Access to this broader data enables our case managers and nurse outreach teams to deliver more
targeted interventions, improving care quality while reducing costs.
