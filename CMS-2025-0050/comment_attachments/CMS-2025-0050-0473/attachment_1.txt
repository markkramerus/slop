b. What changes would you suggest?
A number of organizations still leverage a “local policy exception” to decline TEFCA-based queries and

PC–11. How are health information exchanges (HIEs) currently helping to advance patient access to health
information in the real world?
a. How valuable, available, and accurate do you find the data they share to be?
In most cases, data surfaced through HIEs matches the quality of records obtained via direct interfaces
with source systems—labs, EHRs, and imaging repositories. The notable exception is patient matching:
without a consistent, industry-wide matching standard, stewardship quality varies widely. Some HIEs
employ dedicated data stewards and robust matching algorithms, while others rely on minimal rules,
resulting in mismatch rates exceeding 1%. Given the volume of daily patient interactions, even a 1% error
rate can lead to thousands of misattributed records each month.
b. What changes would you suggest?
To ensure consistent patient access and reduce fragmentation, we recommend CMS encourage and
potentially incentivize HIEs (or health data utility iterations) to formally join the TEFCA framework.
Participation would standardize identity proofing, consent handling, and query routing via Individual
Access Service Providers, enabling uniform rollout of patient-initiated access use cases across regions and
minimizing variability in technical implementation.
c. Are there particular examples of high-performing HIE models that you believe should be
propagated across markets?
While most HIE value remains inherently local—tailored to community needs—two organizations have
demonstrated scalable, sustainable growth beyond their initial service areas: CRISP (Maryland/DC) and
Contexture (formerly manifest in Indiana). Both have balanced financial viability with expanding provider
participation and technical robustness. Rather than applying their playbooks wholesale, other regions
should study their governance structures, funding models, and stakeholder engagement strategies to
inform locally adapted implementations.
d. What is the ongoing role of HIEs amidst other entities facilitating data exchange and broader
frameworks for data exchange (for example, vendor health information networks, TEFCA, private
exchange networks, etc.)?
HIEs serve a critical local purpose and should be incorporated into broader data-exchange
frameworks—particularly TEFCA—by acting as regional nodes within a unified trust architecture. This
integration allows HIEs to continue managing community-specific policies and patient relationships while
leveraging TEFCA’s standardized security, consent, and technical protocols for seamless interoperability at
scale.

PC–12. What are the most valuable operational health data use cases for patients and caregivers that, if
addressed, would create more efficient care navigation or eliminate barriers to competition among
providers or both?
Patients and caregivers need robust tools to find and engage the right providers by filtering on factors such
as location, service offerings (in-person vs. virtual), language, cultural competence, insurance coverage,
network participation, and real-time schedule availability. They also require seamless registration and
appointment booking workflows, as well as transparent, individualized cost estimates—both retail and
insurance-adjusted—so they can compare options and plan financially before receiving care.
b. What use cases are possible today?
Most provider and service information today appears on static websites, limiting discoverability and
comparability. Once enrolled, patients may access additional data through portal systems, but this model
restricts real-time visibility for those seeking new or alternative care options.
c. What should be possible in the near future?
Establishing patient- and caregiver-focused interoperability standards—enforced through HIT certification
and linked CMS payment incentives—would ensure consistent access to core functionalities such as
provider directories, scheduling availability, cost calculators, and quality metrics. A defined maturity model
and implementation roadmap could guide health IT vendors and care organizations through successive
feature rollouts in a transparent, technology-neutral manner that drives innovation and competition.
d. What would be very valuable but may be very hard to achieve?
Achieving full price and out-of-pocket cost transparency remains challenging due to fragmented payer
systems and complex benefit designs. Launching a certification program for payer IT platforms, supported
by CMS policy directives, would accelerate standardized pricing disclosures and empower patients with
clear, personalized cost information.

PC–14. Regarding digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800–63–3
IAL2/AAL2 credentialing service providers (CSP)):
a. What are the challenges today in getting patients/caregivers to sign up and use digital identity
credentials?
The primary barrier is cost: leveraging credentialing services like CLEAR or ID.me incurs transaction
fees—and sometimes subscription charges—which conflicts with CMS’s mandate that patient-access APIs
remain free. In practice, this forces many organizations to absorb identity-proofing expenses or revert to
basic username/password flows, perpetuating a fractured patient-access landscape. Without a sponsoring
entity willing to underwrite both the proofing and the secure data storage that follows, uptake among
patients and caregivers remains limited.
b. What could be the benefits to patients/caregivers if digital identity credentials were more widely
used?
If more patients and caregivers adopted IAL2/AAL2 credentials, they would enjoy seamless, one-time
sign-on across all certified portals and apps—eliminating repeated registrations and password resets—while
benefiting from stronger, multi-factor proofing that guards against unauthorized access. A single,
high-assurance credential would streamline aggregation of records from multiple providers into a unified
view, empowering users and their care teams to coordinate treatment more effectively. Improved security
and reduced friction would likely boost portal utilization and proactive health management, all while
cutting down on help-desk calls and manual identity-proofing workflows.
d. How would encouraging the use of CSPs improve access to health information?
Encouraging the use of credential service providers would strengthen trust in digital health data exchange
by ensuring that only properly vetted individuals can access sensitive information. With verified identity
credentials in place, stakeholders—including patients, providers, and payers—can have greater confidence
that data is being shared securely and with the appropriate person, which is foundational to expanding
access and reducing hesitancy around digital health interactions.
e. What role should CMS/payers, providers, and app developers have in driving adoption?
Payers—both CMS and commercial insurers—should lead by funding credentialing costs for their covered
populations, removing the financial hurdle for patients. Providers must prepare their front-line staff and
workflows to manage increased inquiry volumes, and leverage the patient’s own data to streamline intake

When critical unstructured data remains hidden, clinical decisions rely on incomplete information, driving
redundant testing, delaying treatment, and increasing the risk of adverse outcomes, particularly in
under-resourced rural settings.
c. Technical barriers to full data accessibility.
Transforming data into interoperable formats requires advanced solutions—like document indexing engines
and standardized metadata schemas—that many systems have not implemented, and vendor platform
fragmentation further impedes seamless exchange.
d. Cost or privacy implications of making all data formats interoperable.
Expanding interoperability demands substantial upfront investment in infrastructure upgrades, AI-powered
tools, and staff training, and broadening access heightens privacy and cybersecurity responsibilities;
however, the long-term return includes better care coordination, fewer duplicative services, and improved
outcomes.

TD–4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs over
proprietary APIs?
To drive adoption of open, standards-based APIs, CMS should go beyond simply mandating FHIR endpoints and
incorporate clear non-functional requirements—such as minimum performance and uptime standards—and require
a designated point of contact for API registration and support. Crucially, all specifications must be precisely defined
and streamlined to avoid creating a heavy compliance burden akin to Meaningful Use, ensuring that implementers
can focus on innovation rather than paperwork.
TD–5. How could a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints improve access to health information
for patients, providers, and payers? Who should publish such a directory, and should users bear a cost?
Creating a nationwide directory of FHIR endpoints—enriched with metadata that identifies each practice, health
plan, or facility—simplifies discovery for patients, providers, and payers by eliminating manual endpoint searches
and enabling automated integrations. CMS (or HHS), as the agency that mandates these APIs, should build and
maintain the directory. We recommend offering patient-facing API listings at no charge, while charging networks,
providers, and payers a nominal fee and requiring them to register, ensuring responsible use and visibility into who
accesses the directory.

we support the MATCH IT Act of 2025, which would define a uniform patient match rate, standardize key
demographic elements (to be folded into future USCDI releases), update certification requirements, and introduce
voluntary performance incentives.

TD–16. What are the tradeoffs of maintaining point-to-point models vs. shared network infrastructure?
a. Do current rules encourage scalable network participation?
The current rules do not sufficiently encourage scalable network participation. While APIs are necessary,
they are not enough on their own to enable interoperability at scale. Variability in how standards are
adopted and implemented makes point-to-point integration costly. A shared network model allows a
participant to connect once and exchange with their preferred clinical trading partners. It also enables the
network to provide common infrastructure services—such as directories, query orchestration, and
translation layers—which help accelerate onboarding and reduce time to value.
b. What changes would improve alignment (for example, API unification, reciprocal access)?
A recommended change would be to leverage TEFCA and accelerate the adoption of networked, or
brokered, FHIR.

TD–18. Information blocking:
a. Could you, as a technology vendor, provide examples for the types of practices you have
experienced that may constitute information blocking. Please include both situations of non
responsiveness as well as situations that may cause a failure or unusable response?
Incomplete or inaccurate patient-matching algorithms can return false negatives or positives, preventing
access to the correct record and undermining both usability and privacy. Variable configuration of C-CDA
documents—where some systems only expose a basic CCD and omit other document types—limits data
scope or floods recipients with irrelevant content, violating minimum-necessary principles. Finally,
inconsistent data formatting and mapping can render exchanged data unusable, delaying clinical
decision-making. ASTP/ONC and CMS should fund standardization of matching algorithms, tighten C-CDA
content requirements, and promote real-time data-quality tools to measure and improve exchange fidelity.
b. What additional policies could ASTP/ONC and CMS implement to further discourage healthcare
providers from engaging in information blocking practices?
Linking reimbursement to the adoption of modern exchange frameworks would create tangible incentives.
For example, modest bonus payments for providers who implement standardized FHIR-based
workflows—balanced by rate adjustments for non-adopters—would drive rapid uptake. This “carrot and
stick” approach, calibrated to remain budget-neutral, aligns financial rewards with interoperable behavior.
c. Are there specific categories of healthcare actors covered under the definition of information
blocking in section 3022(a)(1) of the Public Health Service Act (PHSA) that lack information
blocking disincentives?
Payers currently escape information-blocking scrutiny despite controlling vast data flows; extending
disincentives to payers would close this gap. Similarly, health-IT developers serving non-certified
settings—such as EMS, dental, long-term/post-acute care, behavioral health, and niche specialty
systems—operate outside ONC certification and avoid accountability. Mandating baseline interoperability
standards or certification requirements for these actors would ensure comprehensive coverage under
information-blocking rules.

One of our value-based care partners emphasized that stronger incentives are needed to accelerate digital health

Key obstacles cited include persistent fragmentation of patient data and interoperability gaps that result in

has led many providers to hesitate on new technology investments. Adoption of advanced analytics and AI remains
limited—only 18% of medical groups report leveraging these tools for VBC—while the complexity of moving into
risk-based contracts and the absence of clearly defined APMs for many specialties further compounds the challenge.

VB–12. What technology standardization would preserve program-specific flexibility while promoting
innovation in APM technology implementation?
A core set of standardized data elements and exchange protocols should be required to ensure consistency, while
allowing APMs to extend or customize modules to meet specific clinical or operational needs. Standardized—but
flexible—quality measures and reporting frameworks would support innovation while accommodating the diverse
requirements of different populations and specialties.
VB–13. What improvements to existing criteria and standards would better support value-based care
capabilities while reducing provider burden?
To reduce provider burden while strengthening value-based care capabilities, reporting requirements across
programs should be streamlined to eliminate duplication. Certification criteria should include baseline support for
automated data normalization, patient matching, and real-time event notifications. Broader support for
bi-directional exchange of clinical and claims data is also essential. Clear guidance and technical assistance should
be made available to help smaller providers meet interoperability standards without excessive complexity or cost.
VB–14. How could implementing digital identity credentials improve value-based care delivery and
outcomes?
Digital identity credentials support value-based care by enabling accurate patient and provider identification across
systems, reducing record mismatches. They simplify consent and access management, allowing care teams to
securely share information in real time—critical for coordinated care. These solutions also empower patients to
access and contribute to their own data, improving engagement and data quality. Greater trust and security help
accelerate data sharing and the adoption of analytics and population health tools.

To be effective, the directory should include key data elements such as:
● Organization and provider names
● Endpoint URLs and supported FHIR versions
● Available data types and services (e.g., clinical, claims, SDOH)

● Service status and uptime indicators
● Certification or compliance status (e.g., TEFCA participation)
