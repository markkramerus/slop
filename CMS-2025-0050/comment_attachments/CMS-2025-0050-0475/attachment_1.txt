US Agencies should adopt formal ontologies
Recommendation:
CMS should partner with NLM and ONC to lead a government-wide initiative to create formal
ontologies for healthcare, benefits, and eligibility. These ontologies would define shared
meaning across programs and agencies, enabling explainable AI, semantic interoperability,
smarter FHIR exchange, and future policy simulation. This initiative would build on NLM’s
work with SNOMED CT, LOINC, and UMLS, and create a standards-first foundation for
TEFCA, LLM safety, and semantic reasoning — with future optional support for graph
infrastructure like Neo4j.
Benefits:
1. Semantic Alignment Across Agencies Without Schema Lock-in
• Formal ontologies enable CMS, SSA, VA, and others to define domain-specific concepts
independently while aligning on shared upper-level concepts (e.g., Person, Benefit,
Encounter).
• This supports semantic interoperability despite differing database schemas—key for
TEFCA and whole-of-government data integration.
2. Leverages NLM’s Trusted Expertise in Health Data Semantics
• NLM’s stewardship of SNOMED CT, RxNorm, LOINC, and UMLS positions it well to
coordinate federal formal ontology development.
• A centralized branch (e.g., Health Data Standards Branch) could manage publishing,
harmonization, version control, and API services.
3. Supports Responsible AI and Large Language Model (LLM) Adoption
• Ontologies provide AI systems with a ground-truth semantic layer that anchors
predictions and conversational responses in well-defined concepts.
• This enhances explainability, traceability, and trust, especially for CMS’s beneficiary-
facing AI tools and decision support.
4. Enables Smarter Interoperability in TEFCA and FHIR Exchange

• Ontologies can support granular consent management (e.g., mental health, substance use
disorder), data segmentation, and context-driven routing decisions.
• FHIR natively supports integration with ontologies through CodeSystem, ValueSet, and
StructureDefinition resources.
5. Improves Program Evaluation and Policy Simulation
• CMS can model value-based care, ACO attribution, social risk factors, and eligibility
criteria using ontologies, enabling:
o Policy simulation
o AI-powered quality forecasting
o Alignment of plain-language notices via natural language processing
6. Enables Downstream Graph-Based Reasoning (Optional Future Phase)
• After establishing agency ontologies, CMS can adopt graph databases and semantic
reasoners (e.g., Neo4j, Stardog, SPARQL engines) for real-time inference.
• This phased approach respects existing infrastructure and promotes standards-first
adoption without immediate platform overhaul.
Suggested Partners:
• NLM – Core technical coordinator
• NIH/NIBIB – Research funding and ontology infrastructure pilots
• ASTP/ONC – Alignment with TEFCA and Interoperability Standards Advisory
• GAO, OMB, CDO Council – Oversight and policy support
• Academic Ontology Labs (leading universities)– Pilot collaborators
Academic QA would ensure:
• Logical consistency (using DL reasoners like HermiT or Pellet)
• Coverage and correctness (e.g., does the ontology represent all CMS benefit types
accurately?)
• Use-case driven testing (e.g., prior auth simulation, eligibility inference, coverage
groupings)
• Linking with standards (ICD-10-CM, CPT, HCPCS, FHIR value sets)
This protects CMS from the risk of flawed logic or misaligned classifications, especially in
high-stakes policy automation.

To strengthen data interoperability, program alignment, and responsible AI adoption, CMS
should support the development and use of formal ontologies across federal agencies. Formal
ontologies — structured using description logic and standards like OWL — allow programs such
as Medicare, Medicaid, the VA, SSA, and CDC to define their domain knowledge in a machine-
readable, interoperable format. These ontologies would complement existing code systems such
as SNOMED CT, LOINC, ICD-10, and CPT, and provide the logical foundation for modeling
benefit rules, eligibility definitions, consent frameworks, social risk factors, and policy
constraints. Unlike traditional flat value sets or rule-based systems, ontologies enable transparent
reasoning, cross-domain inference, and dynamic policy simulation — features increasingly
necessary for next-generation AI, quality measures, and beneficiary-facing digital tools.
CMS, in collaboration with the National Library of Medicine (NLM), ASTP/ONC, NIH, and
academic institutions, should lead a government-wide initiative to create, publish, and quality-
assure formal ontologies. These ontologies can be aligned with FHIR resources to support use
cases in TEFCA, such as granular data segmentation, eligibility adjudication, and semantically
enriched queries. Formal ontologies will not replace SNOMED CT or other terminologies, but
will complement them by defining relationships, constraints, and logic that support explainable,
AI-powered decision-making. With governance and oversight from trusted federal entities and
input from academic ontology labs, this initiative can ensure technical accuracy, transparency,
and interoperability across agencies — enabling the federal government to move from
exchanging data to sharing meaning.
