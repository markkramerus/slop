PA-2 Transitioning CMS’s Data at the Point of Care Pilot to General Availability
Credo supports broader deployment of APIs like Blue Button 2.0 and the Data at the Point of Care (DPC)
API. To accelerate adoption:
● CMS should require all Medicare Advantage (MA) plans to submit standardized claims data for
provider access through DPC or a similar API.
● CMS should incentivize plans and vendors to adopt a shared API framework for structured, real-
time data delivery. Combining Beneficiary Claims Data, DPC, and the Provider Access APIs under
the prior authorization final rule would lead to a unified approach to access Medicare claims
data across payers disparate systems.
● CMS should also consider including pre-adjudicated claims as part of the API feeds similar to
BDCA access. This would greatly accelerate value-based care organizations' ability to identify
and treat patients that are accessing care and need more support.
Currently, many providers support 20+ MA plans and must integrate inconsistent data streams with
varying timelines. For example, onboarding a new MA plan into a provider’s data environment can take
2–3 months and involve multiple engineering and data mapping resources. This complexity deters
participation in value-based arrangements.
TD-6: How to Improve TEFCA and National Data Exchange
Credo supports the continuation and expansion of TEFCA. To improve the value and efficiency of this
and other national data exchanges:
Create a national patient identifier. Despite decades of investment, national networks’ patient
matching methodologies are still insufficient. Sometimes, these errors can lead to the improper sharing
of protected health information (PHI). CMS should create a national patient identifier that solves this
issue. The risk of improper PHI sharing from faulty matches is far greater than privacy issues posed by a
national patient identifier. Due to the way patient matching is done through demographics and zip code
matching, we believe errors rates could be as high as 1%. This inappropriate sharing of PHI is a major
risk to patient privacy and needs to be fixed.
One potential approach is to extend the Blue Button infrastructure. Allowing for a public-private
partnership between government-funded plans and commercial plans to extend Blue Button so that
patients can register for a national patient identifier is one solution.
Improve data completeness in the network. While national exchange networks – including TEFCA –
are helpful, they are inefficient. Working with value-based care providers and analyzing their referral
feeds, we have found that only 50% of referrals to specialists are available on the national networks.
CMS should mandate that all participating EHRs and clinical systems support full access to patient data
when appropriate and requested by a provider. This would improve the completeness of the networks
and also would ensure both patients and providers have access to complete medical histories. Further,
CMS should enforce existing information blocking regulations, ensuring that vendors are not
inappropriately restricting access to data that could make a significant difference if it is able to be
accessed at the point of care.
Develop a standard, structured schema for FHIR. Many organizations that currently leverage FHIR
inappropriately or inconsistently implement it. The government should mandate a standard, structured

schema for FHIR data so providers and plans can easily combine patient information into a single,
unified EHR record with consistent naming conventions and data fields. We continuously see the
inappropriate use of FHIR and see missing data when CCDAs are converted into FHIR as well as CCDAs
being embedded inside of FHIR records.
Value-based care organizations want to leverage the capabilities of FHIR data exchange, but the existing
variation in data formats make it difficult to do so. CMS should create a specific schema for FHIR data
exchange, that maps how to translate CCDA records to FHIR. CMS should leverage TEFCA to create an
on-ramp for this schema.
