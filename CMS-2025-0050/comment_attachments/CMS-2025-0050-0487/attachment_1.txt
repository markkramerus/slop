burden for providers and payers and more meaningful engagement by patients.

PR-8. Data Exchange
If CMS can leverage direct bulk Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) data
exports from electronic health record (EHR) systems, it would significantly simplify the
process for clinical quality data exchange and reduce the burden on providers and
value-based care entities such as accountable care organizations (ACOs). However, it
is equally important to ensure that data accuracy and performance are transparently
reflected – ideally through a portal – to help avoid any year-end last-minute surprises.
PA-2. Information Blocking and TD-4. Technical Standards and Certification
As CMS seeks to encourage payers to accelerate implementation and utilization of
application programming interfaces (APIs) for patients, providers, and other payers, it
is important to ensure standards are not being implemented in a manner where they
are highly subject to interpretation, which could create “last mile” challenges. This
can be facilitated through shared testing / validation infrastructure and, for the most
critical APIs, through certification programs.
PA-4. Information Blocking and TD-5. Technical Standards and Certification
Centralizing and standardizing a single source of truth for provider information (as
through a national provider directory) would reduce the challenges with handling
what is by nature non-variable data but in practice faces variability in transactions
from multiple trading parties, with multiple specifications, in multiple different
formats and exchange methods defined by the trading parties.
PA-5. Information Blocking and TD-4. Technical Standards and Certification
CMS and ASTP/ONC could reduce the variability of access to EHR data through
introduction of the initially proposed (HTI-2 NPRM) EHR certification program
expansion into the electronic prior authorization API sets.

TD-16. Data Exchange
Data exchange should be either facilitated through a scaled centralized gateway
model or through standardized federated mechanisms. Use of either of these models
is critical to avoid introducing new specifications without addressing the current pain
points with point-to-point exchange models – where they are subject to
interpretation at the last mile, creating burden for building, configuring, validating,
and maintaining a broad set of point-to-point exchange mechanisms.
VB-1. Digital Health Adoption
Value-based care organizations taking significant performance-based risk have
strong, aligned incentives to drive value – better care at lower cost – for the patients
they serve. In this context, digital health management products and vendors are
evaluated based upon their ability to contribute to these goals; that is, their potential
to have a strong return on investment. Product adoption may lag where the
overarching financial incentives are weaker, where there is limited ROI potential, or
where reimbursement is lacking.
Another challenge for value-based care organizations is that patients most likely to
use digital products tend to be those with lower health risk / need and subsequent
expenditures. CMS and ASTP/ONC could increase their focus on provider and patient
outreach and education efforts to support digital product engagement across all
patient populations. Patients and caregivers might prefer simplicity, and fewer digital
health solutions that meaningfully help them navigate their care needs.
VB-2. Digital Health Adoption
To further integrate key themes and technologies such as artificial intelligence,
population health analytics, risk stratification, care coordination, usability, quality
measurement and patient engagement into alternative payment models (APMs),
CMS might consider applying a reimbursement model to support digital health
adoption, similar to the CMS Innovation Center’s GUIDE model which creates a per-
patient, per-month care management payment that replaces traditional fee-for-
service (FFS) payment for certain existing Medicare physician fee schedule services. In
the GUIDE model, these services include annual wellness visits, chronic and principal
care management, transitional care management, advance care planning, and
technology-based check-ins.
In general, Congress and CMS have significantly expanded Medicare fee-for-service
billing codes in recent years, which is well intentioned but may not be the best use of
federal dollars as the cost and quality impacts of billing for these services in a FFS
context is variable or unknown. Payment for these types of services may be better

VB-4. Digital Health Adoption
Essential data types needed for successful participation in value-based care
arrangements includes all files available from CMS currently plus patient phone
numbers needed for direct outreach (with the appropriate patient protections such as
data sharing opt out).
Access to timely admission, discharge, and transfer notification (ADT) feeds – both
from hospitals and skilled nursing facilities – is also needed to support value-based
care. While certain providers are required to provide ADT data under current
regulations, there remain significant gaps today. Value-based care organizations must
often work with multiple vendors to access this data, and mandated entities do not
always provide it to state health information exchanges (HIEs). CMS and ASTP/ONC
should broaden and enforce timely ADT data sharing as part of CMS Conditions of
Participation (CoP) and information blocking requirements to ensure all value-based
care organizations and their participating providers can consistently receive timely
ADT feeds – ideally through EHR systems and HIEs without requiring the use of third-
party vendors.
VB-11. Technical Standards, interoperability challenges in value-based care
The move to all-payer electronic clinical quality measure (eCQM) reporting for
Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) accountable care organizations (ACOs) is
burdensome and costly, as most ACOs have multiple EHR systems and even different
implementations across the same platforms. As these new methods of quality
reporting become mandatory per current regulations, the capabilities required to
implement them should be part of the base certified electronic health record
technology (CEHRT) definition. That is, health IT vendors should not be charging
providers extra fees – often charged by number of participating providers or
attributed patients – to turn on mandatory quality reporting functionalities.
While in theory all-payer eCQM reporting should be seamless in a world where we
have achieved interoperability, in practice ACO reporting now requires burdensome
collection, aggregation, and de-duplication of QRDA files into formats required for
successful reporting. Some EHRs cannot run QRDAs or are not supporting required
measures. Our Evolent Care Partners ACO has recommended that MSSP return to
reporting on a sample of Medicare ACO assigned patients – for whom we are taking
on accountability for costs and outcomes in our MSSP agreement with CMS – through

the CMS Web Interface. This method offers greater reliability for measuring Medicare
ACO quality performance and is significantly less burdensome. CMS should also

VB-14. Technical Standards
Implementing digital identity credentials could support value-based care delivery and
outcomes as well as health IT interoperability (e.g., less need for de-duplication).
However, credentials must reliably work for ALL beneficiaries to ensure there are no
adverse impacts for access to health care. There should be back-up methods, strong
patient privacy protections, and excellent consumer support, keeping in mind extra
supports needed for high-risk, high-need populations served within the Medicare and
Medicaid programs (e.g., dually eligible individuals).
VB-15. Technical Standards
A nationwide provider directory of Bulk FHIR endpoints has the potential to reduce
the technical burden of managing fragmented data, enable real-time access for
value-based care entities to support better patient outcomes, and enhance claims
data transparency. Such solutions must use aligned data definitions across payers to
reduce administrative complexity. Incorporating elements like security, identity
verification, and endpoint status will further strengthen the effectiveness of this
directory.
