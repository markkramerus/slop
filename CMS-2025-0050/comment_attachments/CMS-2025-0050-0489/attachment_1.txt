Patient Needs •Digital tools: web, mobile or other software applications, potentially leveraging sensors, wearables, or other hardware. •Digital health products: defined as digital tools that support individual health needs. •Health management applications: Digital tools that leverage patients' data and other information to support patients with health decision-making. •Care navigation applications: Digital tools that help patients identify, select, and access providers or auxiliary care services. •Personal health record apps: Software applications that collect and organize an individual's health records and provider encounter data for viewing, sharing, and usage in digital health products. PC-1. What health management or care navigation apps would help you understand and manage your (or your loved ones) health needs, as well as the actions you should take? a. What are the top things you would like to be able to do for your or your loved ones' health that can be enabled by digital health products? b. If you had a personal assistant to support your health needs, what are the top things you would ask them to help with? In your response, please consider tasks that could be supported or facilitated by software solutions in the future.Today, patients are often required to navigate several individual point solutions—each on separate platforms or apps—to manage their health and wellness. This fragmented experience creates unnecessary friction for both patients and caregivers. The problem is made worse by the lack of consistency among the various patient-facing tools, including payer apps, provider portals, and EHRs. A fully integrated digital health platform has the potential to transform this experience by consolidating the entire care journey—from finding providers to managing chronic conditions—into a seamless, user-friendly ecosystem. Such a solution would empower caregivers to manage multiple family member profiles within a single interface, supported by intelligent features that surface personalized insights and guide next best actions at both the individual and family level (e.g., coordinated scheduling, shared medical history, genomic 
insights). Ideal features would include the following, all supported by an intelligent digital agent that provides multilingual, cross-channel support (web, text, voice), that automates care navigation, data collection and entry, and other routine tasks: •Customized care pathways for chronic condition management and personalized health journeys (oncology, surgical recovery, etc.). •Longitudinal health tracking to monitor clinical indicators and overall health scores over time. •Clear, easy-to-understand guidance paired with proactive, omni-channel reminders to support treatment adherence. •Seamless ingestion of third-party health data, transformed into actionable insights and next steps. These interactions generate a legal medical record that needs to integrate seamlessly into the EHR. •Tailored Clinical Itineraries across primary, ancillary, and specialty care services—ensuring appropriate prerequisites have been met to maximize appointment and procedure outcomes. •Voice and text-based scheduling prompts, with timely reminders for appointments, screenings, health risk assessments, and follow-ups. These prompts can be initiated by the patient or proactively triggered by the digital assistant based on data in the medical record (e.g., referral order, health history, etc.)
It is essential that the experience be driven by a centralized data source to guarantee consistent value for end users as patients move between payor, provider, and other third-party applications. What are the obstacles to accessing your own or your loved ones' complete health information electronically and using it for managing health conditions or finding the best care (for example, limitations in functionality, user friendliness, or access to basic technology infrastructure)?A single comprehensive solution does not exist today. Even when clinical data is shared with patients, the responsibility to interpret that information—such as clinical notes or discharge instructions—often falls solely on them. The language used is rarely in lay terms, making it difficult for patients to understand what the information means or how to act on it. As a result, patients are expected to take the “next best step” in their care, despite often lacking the resources, access, or guidance needed to do so effectively. For instance, advising an overweight diabetic patient to simply "eat healthier" or "lose weight" is not enough. Without proper education on the relationship between food and health, or access to affordable, nutritious food—especially in the face of rising costs—such guidance is unlikely to lead to meaningful change. What many patients need is a clearer, more personalized understanding of their health. This includes seeing test results contextualized against a baseline that is relevant to them— recognizing that many clinical guidelines are not built on data from culturally diverse populations, nor do they account for 
cultural factors that may influence health baselines. Patients would benefit from clearly defined health goals, along with a personalized plan to achieve them. This should encompass lab results, medications and supplements, recommended screenings or risk assessments, and an overall health score that trends over time. Additionally, being able to view correlated health data from household members—such as weight or blood pressure—could provide valuable insights into shared environmental or behavioral factors impacting health. Patients should not need to know which application to use to access their information. Instead, they should be able to seamlessly access essential health and wellness data from any application or entry point they engage with throughout their care journey. PC-3. PR-2. What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective utilization of the most useful and innovative applications for physician workflows, such as quality measurement reporting, clinical documentation, and billing tasks? How could these obstacles be mitigated?Interoperability challenges between EMRs and digital tools, along with costly integration efforts due to vendor limitations, create significant barriers to efficient data sharing. Additionally, quality measures often rely on billing and claims data rather than clinical data, increasing administrative burdens for clinicians. To alleviate this, payors should be mandated to participate in Health Information Exchanges (HIEs) to capture clinical data directly from providers, reducing billing-related administrative tasks. Moreover, providers need sufficient time and training to effectively use innovative digital applications and understand their role in supporting clinical workflows. Adoption is often hindered by unclear or unfavorable reimbursement compared to traditional care. To overcome this, digital tools must be seamlessly integrated into daily 
workflows and designed with input from providers and clinical teams. Offering incentives and developing quality measures that reflect digital tools’ unique capabilities can further encourage adoption. Additionally, integrating CME courses tailored to these digital applications into required curricula will help ensure providers are competent and confident in their use. CMS can also assist with driving enhancements to support the physician workflow, particularly in providing more patient-centered care, and supporting their workflow to meet quality measures reporting, documentation, and billing tasks. While submitting data through structured data has shown progress, there are limitations in accuracy and reflection of the quality data being submitted. Many providers still document quality measures in unstructured formats such as clinical notes, imaging, or study results. As AI accuracy improves, CMS may consider partnering with developers to incorporate unstructured data into the quality measures reporting process. This enhancement could improve the accuracy of data, for example, by recognizing a normotensive blood pressure reading documented in clinical notes rather than just relying on structured data fields. Similarly, unstructured data may reveal quality measures like cancer screening and vaccinations that could be overlooked in structured data fields. Physicians and patients would need to be key participants in this process, with additional data access requests and agreements required. Finally, while value-based payment programs have been in place for years, barriers still limit organizations from effectively driving change. One limitation is aligning providers, patients, and communities through incentives to enhance engagement. Another limitation is the opportunity for multi-payor alignment to value-
based payment programs. The current structure incentivizes organizations based on performance regardless of their infrastructure. Organizations with fewer resources face greater challenges in meeting requirements and performance. An alternative incentive payment model could provide upfront investment to communities and healthcare organizations to enhance IT capabilities, reducing barriers faced by providers, patients, and communities. Aligning multi-payors on quality measure reporting should be explored further, especially as the Medicare Advantage program grows. Establishing a multi-payor database for all patients, especially Medicare and Medicare Advantage patients, could enhance the improvement work of value-based payment programs. PR-4.
