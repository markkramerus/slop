What short-term (next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers? Short-term: To stimulate developer interest in creating digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers, CMS can take several immediate steps. First, providing access to CMS sandbox environments, de-identified sample data sets, and standardized APIs would enable developers to design, test, and refine solutions that align with Medicare workflows and data structures. Offering small-scale pilot funding or innovation grants—especially for tools that address care navigation, preventive care, and chronic condition management—would help reduce risk for early-stage developers and encourage participation from smaller companies. CMS could also host developer challenges or public-private hackathons focused on solving high-priority problems, such as reducing avoidable hospitalizations or improving engagement with preventive services. Finally, reducing the burden to become a Federal Government vendor, especially for pilot projects, would encourage participation from smaller companies and eliminate the waste associated with “falling under” a Prime contractor. Long-term: Over time, CMS can foster a more robust digital health ecosystem by establishing a public certification pathway for CMS-aligned apps and digital tools. This would signal trust and legitimacy to both users and healthcare partners, similar to FDA digital health guidance or ONC certification programs. CMS can also create opportunities to embed certified tools directly into Medicare beneficiary-facing platforms, such as MyMedicare.gov or Medicare Advantage portals, ensuring these apps reach users at the point of need. To maintain momentum and quality, CMS could further incentivize developers through value-based care alignment—e.g., rewarding the use of certified digital tools that demonstrably close care gaps or improve outcomes.
