Dear Administrator Oz,
AdventHealth appreciates the opportunity to provide input on the Centers for Medicare and
Medicaid Services’ Request for Information, “Improving the Technology Experience for Medicare
Beneficiaries.” As a faith-based health care organization with over 50 hospitals, 2,000 care sites,
and more than 100,000 employees across nine states, we are committed to delivering whole-
person care that is equitable, seamless, and centered on the needs of patients.
We support CMS’ efforts to strengthen interoperability, modernize administrative processes, and
empower Medicare beneficiaries through better access to and integration of digital tools. To
that end, our comments focus on practical, incremental improvements that align with CMS’s
existing authorities, reduce administrative burden, and promote a more consistent, patient-
friendly health care experience. Specifically, we offer comments on:
• Modernizing and Standardizing Prior Authorization
• Aligning Policy with TEFCA and Interoperability Goals
• Leveraging Artificial Intelligence and Augmentation
• Supporting Equitable Technology Adoption
Modernizing and Standardizing Prior Authorization
The current prior authorization landscape remains one of the most significant administrative
and patient care challenges across the industry. While technological innovation has allowed for
partial augmentation, fragmentation and payer variability continue to delay care and increase
provider burden.
1

Challenges Identified:
• No single vendor offers a comprehensive solution. Thus, systems must “Frankenstein”
together 3–4 vendors to augment prior authorization across service lines.
• Payers have vastly different requirements, documentation standards, and timelines,
which leads to avoidable delays in patient care.
• Some health IT vendors are using AI to scrape charts and populate forms, but these
solutions are not universally accepted by payers.
Recommendations for CMS:
• Incentivize end-to-end prior authorization platforms through targeted funding or
pilot models.
• Mandate national standards for prior authorization, including:
o Uniform criteria for which services require authorization
o Shared “exempt services” list for low-risk procedures
o Consistent documentation expectations
• Encourage payer participation in standardized APIs and augmentation tools to
expedite approvals, reduce hospital stay durations, and alleviate rescheduling burdens.
Aligning Policy with TEFCA and Interoperability Goals
The success of the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) and
national interoperability frameworks depends on consistent, enforceable rules that promote
adoption without redundancy or risk exposure for providers.
Recommendations for CMS:
• Expand TEFCA-related regulatory exceptions, to acknowledging full TEFCA
participation satisfies the goal of the 21st Century Cures Act information sharing
requirements. If a request for electronic health information can be satisfied by the
requestor joining TEFCA, then such request is satisfied.
• Create safe harbors for providers demonstrating a good-faith effort to comply
with TEFCA and 21st Century Cures Act standards, reducing the threat of litigation
or regulatory penalties for technical violations. Technology advancements will always
outpace technological adoption and providers who demonstrate good-faith efforts to
comply should not be otherwise penalized.
2

• Clarify enforcement frameworks to reduce ambiguity and legal risk, particularly
as vendors and providers face increasing lawsuits involving alleged information-blocking
or tortious interference.
Leveraging Artificial Intelligence for Augmentation
AI shows promises to streamline through augmentation administrative processes such as
documentation and claims submissions. One vendor currently under consideration at our
organization uses AI to auto-populate payer forms by analyzing EHR data—a time-saving
innovation with potential to reduce documentation delays. Another vendor’s application
augments the document review for audits to enable our workforce to more timely process
materials.
Recommendations for CMS:
• Promote pilot programs that test and validate AI-based augmentation tools in
real-world clinical environments.
• Create regulatory flexibilities to permit AI-enabled clinical documentation in
payer reviews.
• Clarify standards around AI deployment in health care administration, focused
on safety, equity, and transparency without creating excessive new oversight
mechanisms.
We agree with CMS’ cautious, incremental approach and support a measured review of AI
regulatory oversight at this time.
Supporting Equitable Technology Adoption
While digitization increases efficiency, it also risks widening disparities if it is not implemented
with accessibility in mind. Older adults and underserved populations like rural communities
often face additional barriers when using digital portals or complex interface systems.
Recommendations for CMS:
• Set minimum usability standards for patient-facing tools, particularly focused on
the needs of older beneficiaries (e.g., large fonts, simplified navigation, phone support).
3

• Encourage caregiver proxy access and multi-lingual support on platforms to
ensure broader usability.
• Support digital literacy initiatives for Medicare beneficiaries, possibly through
partnerships with community health organizations and local aging networks.
Conclusion
The health care technology ecosystem is on the cusp of transformational change. However,
realizing that promise requires a thoughtful and collaborative approach. CMS is uniquely
positioned to accelerate progress by incentivizing best practices, setting clear national
standards, and fostering equitable innovation. AdventHealth is grateful for the opportunity to
contribute to this discussion and stands ready to partner with CMS to create a digital health
system that works better for everyone—especially the patients we serve. For additional
information, please contact Susana Molina Ramos, Director of Public Policy, at
Susana.MolinaRamos@AdventHealth.com.
