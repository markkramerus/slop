In your experience, what health data is readily available and valuable to patients, their caregivers, or both? Traditional clinical information , such as medications, lab results, and visit summaries, is generally available to patients and caregivers; however, access may not always be as seamless or centralized as needed. The information blocking regulation and assorted certification requirements have dramatically expanded the data that now flows from EHRs to patient portals and apps, but it is clear that there are still challenges presented by portals that patients log into separately and the fact that connections between EHRs and API -based data sources are still relatively limited in their uptake. PC-8a. What data is valuable, but hard for patients and caregivers, or app developers and other technical vendors, to access for appropriate and valuable use (for example, claims data, clinical data, encounter notes, operative reports, appointment schedule s, prices)? Non -medical factors that strongly influence health, such as food security, transportation access, and family support, are rarely captured in structured, exchangeable formats. Even when this data is collected by community -based organizations, it is not rout inely shared with patients or integrated into clinical systems, limiting its utility for care coordination and app developers. Additionally, challenges remain with the easy and cost -effective exchange of medical imaging. PC-8b. What specific sources, other than claims and clinical data, would be of highest value, and why? Patient -sourced data (such as reported symptoms, preferences, and home monitoring inputs) and data from community -based organizations (CBOs) addressing social needs would offer high value by providing critical context beyond traditional clinical and claims data. These sources can enhance personalized care, improve care planning, and support more effective interventions. As mentioned, medical imaging is one of the frustration areas for both providers and patients that should be addressed. When we achieve a state where images are more freely exchanged and available in real -time, it is likely to have a dramatic positive impa ct on efforts to lower costs across our health system. Since EHRs typically do not store medical images themselves —these are usually managed by PACS or Radiology Information Systems —focusing efforts on those imaging systems is likely to yield the greatest benefit. Even when images can be exchanged, there are still limitations on the exchange due to the file sizes required to retain the necessary resolution for the recipient. This is a difficult challenge to overcome, as larger files present both technical a nd cost issues.

PR-12. Should ASTP/ONC consider removing or revising any of the information -blocking exceptions or conditions within the exceptions (45 CFR part 171, subparts B through D) to further the access, exchange, and use of electronic health information (EHI) and promote market competition? The EHR Association supports maintaining key information blocking exceptions, particularly the infeasibility and manner exceptions, because they balance broad access to EHI with secure implementation. These exceptions allow actors to exchange data efficien tly without having to resort to expensive, proprietary methods. Keeping these flexibilities promotes standards -based exchange, fostering competition among digital health products and leveling the playing field, especially for those integrating with certifi ed EHRs. Narrowing these exceptions could create barriers to interoperability and hinder innovation for smaller developers and providers. Instead of revising or removing these exceptions, the EHR Association recommends continued ASTP/ONC guidance to ensure all stakeholders understand their application, advancing EHI access, and maintaining trust and feasibility across the healthcare delivery ecosystem. We suggest two key policy actions to strengthen the information blocking framework: 1. Update the timeline condition under the Infeasibility Exception to better align with real -world clinical and technical circumstances. As noted in our HTI-2 comments , the rigid ten -business -day requirement does not always account for the complexities of healthcare workflows and the resolution of legitimate infeasibilities. A more flexible, context -aware approach would better serve the goals of the regulation without u ndermining access. 2. Do not finalize the proposed “requestor preference” condition to the Manner Exception. As previously stated in our HTI -2 comments, introducing this condition could unintentionally override reasonable technical and operational decisions made by actors in fa vor of arbitrary or unsupported preferences. This could lead to increased burden, unnecessary costs, and confusion, especially where the requested method of exchange is technically unsupported, insecure, or impractical.

Equally important is the adoption of standardized methods for provider/patient attribution. As noted in the EHR Association’s comments on the 2026 IPPS rule , the absence of a universal attribution model undermines the reliability of performance measurement and complicates the use of shared, interoperable data. Alignment across CMS and private payers on attribution definitions would improve the accuracy of qua lity reporting and reduce administrative burden. PA-5a. The EHR Association has previously emphasized, including in our 2026 IPPS comments, that data utility is undermined without clear attribution and timely transparency. What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform? TEFCA’s unique value lies in its potential to standardize and scale nationwide health information exchange by establishing a clear governance framework, defining use cases, and maintaining consistent technical requirements across these use cases while bein g able to tailor these requirements to the specific needs of each use case. TD-7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange, and what are its limitations? The EHR Association views USCDI as a significant step forward in advancing interoperability and standardization across health IT systems. By defining broad, consensus -driven data classes and elements, USCDI establishes a common foundation for the exchange of clinically meaningful data via recognized standards such as HL7 FHIR and HL7 CDA (C -CDA). This has improved the consistency and usability of health information at the point of care and across care transitions. However, several limitations remain: ● Limited scope: USCDI does not yet cover all EHI, though this can be addressed by incrementally expanding its content. To increase USCDI’s scope efficiently and thoroughly, more modularity and stratification of certification criteria is required (see point below). ● Inconsistencies with HL7 standards: These create implementation challenges. Aligning USCDI more directly with relevant HL7 Implementation Guides would improve technical consistency.

TD-8. What are the most effective certification criteria and standards under the ONC Health IT Certification Program? Several certification criteria under the ONC Health IT Certification Program have had a meaningful impact on interoperability and trust: ● Privacy and Security (P&S) criteria have been foundational, providing a consistent baseline for safeguarding health information and serving as the closest operational proxy to HIPAA Security Rule certification. ● HL7 CDA C -CDA -based criteria and direct messaging played a critical role in advancing early interoperability. However, as the industry shifts away from document -based exchange, the program can begin focusing on modernizing these criteria toward HL7 FHIR -based API exchange. ● Public health criteria, especially for immunization and electronic laboratory reporting, have supported a scalable reporting infrastructure. However, their effectiveness is limited by the lack of reciprocal obligations on public health agencies (PHAs) to a dhere to the same standards, which leads to fragmentation and unnecessary work to connect. Migration of existing, mostly HL7 v2 -based, APIs to HL7 FHIR -based APIs should be considered on a use -case basis. Those in need of more comprehensive clinical data, such as Syndromic Surveillance, should migrate sooner. In contrast, those already migrating should have adequate transition time (e.g., eCR using both HL7 CDA and HL7 FHIR -based eICR reports). Others could continue to progress for the foreseeable future, i ncluding more minor updates, using HL7 v2 -based APIs. New use cases, such as queries by public health to providers through TEFCA or advancing operational reporting (like bed capacity, hospital respiratory data, and operational data for emergency situations ), or reciprocal data beyond the data currently shared from public health to providers and consumers, are well -suited to start with HL7 FHIR -based APIs. ● In recent years, HL7 FHIR -based API criteria, including patient and population services, have been among the most impactful, supporting ecosystem -wide data access, app development, and alignment with CMS programs. The EHR Association encourages ONC to continue refining the certification program to support modern, scalable, and bidirectional interoperability, with increased accountability across all participants in the data exchange ecosystem. TD-9a. What are the benefits of redefining certification to prioritize API -enabled capabilities over software functionality? 
 17 Redefining certification to emphasize API -enabled capabilities, especially HL7 FHIR -based APIs, is the right direction for modernizing interoperability. Prioritizing HL7 FHIR -based APIs over IHE Document Exchange APIs as a direction enables more flexible a nd efficient data sharing, allowing developers to design systems that meet their customers’ specific needs , covering the spectrum of sharing individual data, datasets, and still documents . However, certain non-API-specific criteria, such as functionality - based privacy and security, remain critical and should be preserved even as certification evolves. TD-9b. What would be the drawbacks? A narrow focus on HL7 FHIR -based APIs may be limiting. For example, HL7 v2 APIs remain highly effective for many public health workflows, and there’s little benefit in forcing migration away from proven infrastructure without a clear ROI. Moreover, certifi cation scope must be considered holistically: replacing several low -burden attestation -only criteria with a single complex API requirement (especially tied to immature standards) can lead to a greater net burden and unnecessary expenditure. Certification reforms must consider cost, maturity, and implementation feasibility, not just the number of criteria. TD-9c. How could ASTP/ONC revise health IT certification criteria to require APIs to consistently support exchanging data from all aspects of the patient's chart (for example, faxed records, free text, discrete data)? HL7 FHIR -based APIs already support much of the unstructured data referenced, such as faxed or scanned documents and free -text content, through the DocumentReference resource. Expanding USCDI to more explicitly include these data types and establishing cle arer expectations for supporting unstructured content would help advance interoperability. However, it is essential to remember that for interoperability to be effective, the data must be meaningful and relevant. While unstructured data can be valuable, applying standards and structure whenever possible enhances its usability and value . We should focus not only on enabling exchange but also on improving data quality and standardization before promoting the exchange of all available information indiscriminately. TD-9d. What policy changes could CMS make so providers are motivated to respond to API - based data requests with best possible coverage and quality of data? The use of and responses to API -based data requests offer clear value, but given the amount of staff work and training required, provider adoption depends on meaningful incentives. While Information Blocking regulations create a compliance framework, there is little tangible reward today for providers who deliver high -quality, comprehensive API responses. CMS could strengthen this through i ncentive alignment —for example, rewarding automated case reporting via eCR to replace manual submission burdens. Ultimately, policy strategies should be co -developed with providers, who are best positioned to advise on how to align API engagement with clin ical workflows and resource constraints. TD-9e. As of January 1, 2024, many health IT developers with products certified through the ONC Health IT Certification Program are required to include the capability to perform an electronic health information export or “EHI export” for a single patient a s well as for patient populations (45 CFR 170.315(b)(10)). Such health IT developers are also required to publicly describe the format of the EHI export. Notably, how EHI export was accomplished was left entirely to the health IT developer. Now that this c apability has been in production for over a year, CMS and ASTP/ONC seek input on the following: TD-11a. Should this capability be revised to specify standardized API requirements for EHI export? Yes, the EHI Export capability should be revised to include standardized API requirements. In its current form, codified at 45 CFR § 170.315(b)(10), EHI Export has not met its intended goals of simplifying patient access to their complete electronic health information or streamlining provider transitions between health IT systems. There are numerous variables contributing to this, but chief amongst them is the lack of defined, commonly accepted standards for both data format and delivery mechanisms. While developers are required to document their export specifications publicly , healthcare data remains highly complex and difficult to interpret consistently without a shared, well -understood standard, regardless of how detailed and thorough a developer’s format specification documentation is. This is especially true for population -level exports, where the volume and variability of data make it unrealistic to assume a seamless, low -burden export and import process. Even with thorough documentation, significant human intervention is typically req uired to process such exports meaningfully. The EHR Association recommends re -scoping the EHI Export criterion to focus not on “all EHI” but instead on all USCDI data and reference guidance on how to support EHI Export using HL7 FHIR Bulk Data that includes an organized USCDI data set. We suggest th at EHI Export be repurposed considering these realities and scope to a HL7 FHIR -based data set that can be shared using HL7 FHIR Bulk Data Access for a single or multiple patients that contains all USCDI scoped data as expressed in HL7 FHIR US Core/ That c an grow not only based on HL7 FHIR US Core expansion (which is only a limited data set) but on an identified combination of HL7 FHIR US Core and profiles from implementation guides reflecting specialized data such as full laboratory results, care plans, an d other full data sets. Any data not available in FHIR would inherently require more effort by the receiver and should not be expected to flow seamlessly under a standardized export. Additionally, it should be clarified that EHI Export is not appropriate as a standalone solution for health IT-to-health IT migration, which often requires tailored workflows and complex mappings beyond a universal dataset. In effect, EHI Export should be repositioned as a FHIR Bulk Data use case with a 
 19 defined implementation guide, rather than an open -ended export of all possible EHI. Repurposing the EHI Export into an HL7 FHIR Bulk Data Export -based criterion will also allow for the removal of the bulk data requirements from 45 CFR § 170.315(g)(10), consolidating bulk data capabilities into a single, modular requirement that can be me t by the appropriate health IT best suited to support such bulk queries. We recognize the concerns regarding EHR performance for certain bulk data volumes when implemented. A modular approach enables health IT to support the requirements, which could involve the EHR for specific bulk data use cases while not necessitating it for others, thus facilitating the optimal use of the technology necessary for these scenarios. Further more, it promotes the advancement of use case -specific implementation guides that address specific requirements and capabilities, including relevant Service Level Agreement (SLA) measures where necessary. TD-11b. Are there specific workflow aspects that could be improved? The challenges associated with EHI Export are not rooted in workflow execution. In fact, the workflow for initiating and performing an export should remain at the discretion of the developer, as it will always work most effectively when aligned with how ea ch product is designed and used in practice. Standardizing workflow details across developers would create unnecessary rigidity and complexity without adding meaningful value. The focus of certification should remain on ensuring the capability exists and f unctions reliably, rather than dictating the internal steps or user interfaces used to perform the export. TD-11c. Should CMS consider policy changes to support this capability's use? EHI Export capabilities are triggered by specific, infrequent use cases, such as a patient requesting a computable copy of their data or a provider transitioning to a new EHR. Therefore, CMS should not mandate their use. If existing standardized exchange m echanisms (such as FHIR or C -CDA) fulfill the vast majority of real -world needs more effectively, there is no benefit in requiring the use of EHI Export for its own sake. Instead, CMS should support the broader adoption of standards -based exchanges (e.g., HL7 FHIR and HL7 CDA), which already satisfy most interoperability needs. For exceptional cases requiring more comprehensive datasets, custom approaches remain viable until USCDI or FHIR profiles are mature enough to support “all EHI” use cases fully . TD-13. What are the primary tradeoffs between USCDI and full EHI, especially given more flexible data processing capabilities today? The primary trade -off between USCDI and full EHI lies in striking a balance between value and complexity. USCDI defines a structured, consensus -driven subset of health data that is standardized, broadly applicable, and readily interoperable. In contrast, the full scope of EHI encompasses vast and often unstructured or proprietary data elements tha t vary widely across systems and use cases. While it is true that some of this data may offer unique insights about the patient or meet edge -case needs, it often l acks standardization, making it more challenging to exchange, interpret, and use effectively. Processing full EHI requires more sophisticated tools, resources on both sides of the exchange, and safeguards, which can introduce cost, workflow burdens, and risks of inaccurate interpretation of patient data without a clear value proposition for most stakeholders. Therefore, prioritizing the continued development and adoption of standards, while incrementally expanding USCDI and providing flexible pathways to ac cess additional EHI when justified, may be the most pragmatic and effective approach. TD-15a. How would increased use of bulk FHIR improve use cases and data flow? The increased use of HL7 FHIR Bulk Data Export has the potential to significantly improve data flow and enable advanced use cases, such as digital quality measurement, population health analytics, and more efficient payer -provider data exchange. It provides a standardized, scalable approach for extracting large datasets, particularly useful when aggregating information across patient populations. However, realizing this promise depends on greater adoption, maturity of purpose -built implementation guides, and availability of robust testing tools. As noted in our comments on the FY2026 IPPS proposed rule, EHRs are certified to support HL7 FHIR Bulk Data Access; however, the limited deployment by providers and other stakeholders has hindered real -world experien ce. To bridge this gap, CMS could support targeted incentives and collaborative pilot programs that enable providers and developers to implement Bulk FHIR in specific, meaningful use cases —ultimately demonstrating its value and building industry confidence . For example, HRSA’s UDS+ initiative aimed to receive data from any FHIR -enabled system , including EHRs and specialty systems, such as dental software. While promising in theory, the reality proved more complex. HRSA’s requirement for de -identified data conflicted with its expectation to receive multiple data submissions and reconcile them into a unified patient -level view. Without ident ifiers, accurate deduplication was impossible. The burden of aggregation was ultimately placed back on the submitting provi der. This example highlights how the benefits of FHIR can be lost without aligned infrastructure, standardized deduplication strategies, and consistent support across health IT systems.

TD-19b. Which workflows would benefit most from functional price transparency? Functional price transparency has the potential to improve several key workflows across the healthcare continuum. One of the most impactful is medication prescribing. When providers can access real -time cost information and coverage details at the point of care, they can offer patients lower -cost alternatives, such as generic drugs or covered formulary options, based on individual insurance and pharmacy pricing. This supports more informed decision -making and greatly increases the likelihood that a patient will fill their prescription and adhere to it. In turn, this improves clinical outcomes and reduces downstream costs tied to medication non -adherence. Without functional price transparency, providers are often unaware that a prescribed treatment may be unaffordable for the patient, which can result in abandoned prescriptions, unanticipated out -of-pocket expenses, or follow -up calls to change therapy, con tributing to post -visit administrative burden. Providers are also penalized in value -based care models when patient cost barriers impede adherence, 
 22 despite having no control over drug pricing or formulary coverage. Functional transparency can directly reduce these gaps. This potential exists in other treatment areas, of course —virtually any diagnostic process that can impose significant costs on patients but is often prescribed without clear fees beforehand. VB-3. What are essential health IT capabilities for value -based care arrangements? Essential health IT capabilities for value -based care must support care coordination, performance measurement, patient engagement, and data -driven decision -making. A foundational need is standardization and consistency across CMS programs, particularly in quality measurement and reporting . Many value -based organizations operate across multiple EHR platforms and vendors, which complicates data normalization, mapping, and aggregation.
