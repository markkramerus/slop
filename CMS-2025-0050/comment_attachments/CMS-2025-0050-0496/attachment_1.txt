This section is intended for all stakeholders to provide input on questions as they relate to use
cases and workflows that involve technology vendors, data providers, and networks. While we

1. Ecosystem
TD–1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to
stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries
and caregivers?
ResolveSoft, a Women-Owned Small Business (WOSB), recommends that CMS stimulate
developer interest by offering grant programs, technical challenges, and procurement pathways
that prioritize inclusive small businesses. Specifically, we encourage CMS to expand the use of
WOSB set-aside contract vehicles and sole source opportunities, which significantly reduce the
administrative burden for small firms and accelerate time to value. Additionally, we advocate
streamlined RFP processes that prioritize oral presentations, solutioning sessions, and past
performance discussions over document-heavy submissions. These approaches foster meaningful
engagement and allow WOSBs to demonstrate technical value and mission alignment early in
the procurement lifecycle.
ResolveSoft is an ISO 9001:2015, 20000-1, and 27001 certified company, specializing in
transforming digital offerings and delivering operations and maintenance support. We
understand the critical missions of agencies such as the Department of Health and Human
Services (HHS), including the Administration for Strategic Preparedness and Response (ASPR),
Medical Reserve Corps (MRC), National Institutes of Health (NIH), Administration for Children
and Families (ACF), Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), and the Assistant
Secretary for Financial Resources (ASFR). ResolveSoft delivers full life-cycle Agile, Salesforce,
and DevOps/DevSecOps transformations, with scalable capabilities in automation, data
collection, and web application development.
Short-term actions CMS could take include expanding access to testing environments,
simplifying vendor onboarding, and reducing barriers to access APIs and data sets. In addition,
CMS can encourage WOSB participation and rapid solution development by offering incentives
for building digital health tools, expanding access to real-time and de-identified datasets, and
streamlining governance and compliance reviews. Reducing red tape and clarifying decision-
making pathways to move digital products into production environments would directly benefit
smaller, agile vendors like ResolveSoft.
Long-term strategies should include the establishment of dedicated innovation accelerators that
provide mentorship, infrastructure, and access to CMS datasets for WOSBs and other small
businesses. CMS could also support pre-qualified vendor pools for rapid tasking and foster
collaborative development programs with built-in funding for pilot deployments and user
research. Additional investment in open-source tooling, interoperable APIs, and reusable digital
components will create a more equitable development landscape and lower technical barriers for
participation in future CMS initiatives. Further, CMS should consider long-term programmatic
investments that sustain innovation pipeline such as challenge grants, milestone-based funding,
and technical assistance centers dedicated to supporting small and disadvantaged businesses.

ResolveSoft believes that to supplement the Blue Button 2.0 API and increase its value for
digital health applications, several additional datasets would be highly beneficial. These datasets
would fill key gaps in clinical context, social determinants of health, and real-time care
coordination:
1. Medicare Advantage (Part C) Claims Data
Why it matters: Blue Button 2.0 currently excludes Medicare Advantage claims, which cover
~50% of Medicare beneficiaries. Including this would provide a complete view of utilization,
particularly for managed care.
Use case: Applications could compare traditional Medicare with Advantage plans, optimize
benefit selections, and support whole-patient analytics.
2. Clinical EHR Data (C-CDA, FHIR Clinical Resources)
Why it matters: Claims data shows what was billed, not what actually happened in care. EHR
data provides richer clinical details such as vitals, lab results, and clinical notes.
Use case: Improves chronic disease management, risk stratification, and transitions of care.
3. Social Determinants of Health (SDOH)
Why it matters: Health outcomes are deeply influenced by non-clinical factors like housing,
transportation, food access, and social isolation.
Use case: Enhances predictive models, identifies at-risk populations, and allows for tailored
interventions and referrals to community-based services.
4. Patient-Reported Outcomes (PROs)
Why it matters: Claims and EHRs miss the patient’s voice, how they feel, function, and recover.
Use case: Enables holistic evaluation of care quality and effectiveness, especially in behavioral
health and post-acute care.
5. Real-Time Admission, Discharge, Transfer (ADT) Feeds
Why it matters: Blue Button data has a lag. Real-time ADT alerts improve care coordination,
reduce remissions, and support post-discharge follow-up.
Use case: Enables providers or care managers to intervene immediately after ER visits or
inpatient stays.
6. Behavioral Health Data
Why it matters: Mental health and substance use treatment data are often fragmented or
excluded. Integration is critical for whole-person care.
Use case: Combines physical and behavioral health data to better support patients with
comorbid conditions.

2. Digital Identity
TD–3. Regarding digital identity implementation:
a. What are the challenges and benefits?
ResolveSoft believes that challenges include technological access for underserved populations,
difficulty using digital identity tools, and hesitancy around sharing personal information online.
Many individuals—particularly older adults, rural residents, and low-income populations—face
barriers such as limited internet access, lack of smartphones, or unfamiliarity with multi-step
digital verification processes (e.g., facial recognition, document uploads). Even when access is
available, users often struggle to complete identity verification successfully, leading to
frustration and abandonment.
Additionally, there is widespread hesitancy to provide sensitive personal information over the
internet, especially to unfamiliar third-party identity providers. Concerns about data misuse,
identity theft, and lack of transparency in how information is stored or shared are common
among Medicare beneficiaries.
A further challenge is the redundant requirement to verify identity across multiple health
systems, applications, and government portals. This fragmented approach increases burden and
reduces usability.
One potential solution is to implement a federated digital identity model, where a beneficiary
completes identity verification once through a trusted platform (e.g., Login.gov or a NIST 800-
63-3 compliant provider), and then use that verified identity to access multiple CMS-approved
services and health systems. This “verify once, use everywhere” approach would reduce
redundancy, streamline access, and improve user trust.
Benefits of digital identity include enhanced security, reduced fraud, and more personalized
digital services. When implemented effectively, a secure digital identity reduces the risk of
unauthorized access and identity-based fraud (e.g., fraudulent claims, impersonation). It also

TD–7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange and what are
its limitations?
a. Does it contain the full extent of data elements you need?
USCDI has improved interoperability by standardizing core data elements across healthcare
systems, enabling more consistent and accessible exchange of patient information. It has
facilitated better coordination of care, improved data quality, and enhanced support for clinical
decision-making, especially in primary care and acute care settings.
However, gaps remain particularly in behavioral health and social care data—which limits the
ability of providers and digital tools to deliver whole-person care. For example, USCDI currently
includes limited structured fields for behavioral health conditions, treatment plans, or therapy
notes. Critical data such as psychiatric evaluations, substance use history, and mental health
crisis events are often stored in unstructured formats or siloed in non-integrated systems, making
it difficult to exchange or act on that information consistently.
In social care, while USCDI Version 3 includes some Social Determinants of Health (SDOH)
elements, such as housing and food insecurity, it lacks comprehensive coverage of care
coordination activities, social service referrals, program enrollment status, and outcomes of
community-based interventions. These data points are essential for addressing non-clinical
factors that drive health disparities and influence outcomes—particularly for Medicare and
Medicaid populations.
The lack of standardized, interoperable data in these domains creates blind spots for care teams,
researchers, and policymakers. Without this information, risk assessments may be incomplete,
interventions may be mistimed or misaligned, and value-based care models may fall short of
addressing patient needs.
To close these gaps, CMS and ONC should work with behavioral health providers, social service
organizations, and advocacy groups to expand USCDI to include more structured behavioral
health data (e.g., DSM-5 conditions, care plans, medication adherence) and social care elements
(e.g., resource utilization, referral resolution, caregiver support). This expansion would enable
more equitable, coordinated, and outcomes-driven healthcare delivery across settings.

d. Given improvements in language models, would you prefer a non- proprietary but less

Language model-based inputs could significantly increase data coverage and completeness
particularly for unstructured or under-documented domains such as behavioral health, social
care, and patient-reported outcomes. These AI-driven tools can extract meaningful data from
free-text clinical notes, patient narratives, and other non-standardized inputs, helping to populate
structured fields that are missing or inconsistently captured in EHRs and claims systems.
For example, natural language processing (NLP) and large language models (LLMs) can identify
medication adherence issues, functional status, psychosocial risk factors, or care coordination
notes embedded in provider documentation. This capability would be especially valuable for
supplementing gaps in USCDI-aligned datasets without requiring extensive workflow changes or
manual abstraction by clinicians.
However, the use of language models in healthcare data pipelines must be accompanied by
strong governance and guardrails to ensure safety, accuracy, privacy, and fairness. Key
considerations include:
Data provenance: Ensuring outputs from language models are traceable, transparent, and clearly
marked as AI-generated or AI-extracted.
Validation and accuracy: Establishing model performance benchmarks for healthcare-specific
domains and use cases, with clinician review or approval loops where appropriate.
Bias mitigation: Monitoring and adjusting for disparities in how language models interpret data
from underrepresented populations or non-standard language patterns.
Privacy and compliance: Enforcing HIPAA compliance and preventing unintended exposure of
protected health information (PHI) during training, inference, or storage.
When implemented responsibly, language models can serve as powerful tools for enhancing
interoperability by transforming previously inaccessible or underutilized text into actionable,
structured data elements. This would enrich datasets used for care coordination, quality
reporting, risk adjustment, and research—especially in areas where standardized coding has
historically lagged.
CMS and ONC could further support this innovation by developing guidance for AI-augmented
data capture, encouraging pilot programs, and including AI-extracted fields as extensions to
USCDI under defined use cases and validation thresholds.

RFI Response
ResolveSoft recommends emphasizing testable API conformance, integration with clinical
decision support (CDS) tools, and ease-of-use in real-world provider workflows as key pillars of
an effective certification framework under the ONC Health IT Certification Program.
First, testable API conformance ensures that health IT systems not only claim to support
interoperability standards (such as FHIR or SMART on FHIR), but actually implement them
correctly, consistently, and securely. Too often, “certified” APIs vary in implementation, leading
to costly custom integrations and interoperability gaps. CMS and ONC should enforce rigorous,
automated testing protocols—preferably through publicly available tools—to verify that APIs
can exchange structured data reliably across vendors and use cases. Certification should include
real-world use tests, not just laboratory validation.
Second, certification should promote seamless integration with clinical decision support (CDS)
tools. These tools are essential for translating raw data into actionable insights at the point of
care—helping clinicians make better decisions and improving outcomes. Certified systems
should demonstrate the ability to ingest standardized data from external sources and trigger CDS
alerts or care recommendations in line with clinical guidelines. APIs should support bi-
directional data exchange so that CDS platforms can both receive and contribute to patient
records.
Finally, ease-of-use in live provider workflows is critical. Certified systems must go beyond
technical compliance to support real-world usability. This includes minimizing disruption to
clinical tasks, ensuring intuitive user interfaces, reducing alert fatigue, and enabling patient-
centered views of data. Certification criteria should incorporate human-centered design
principles and usability testing, especially for modules used in high-volume care environments
such as primary care, emergency rooms, and long-term care facilities.
Together, these priorities ensure that certification is not just a checkbox exercise but a
meaningful signal of functional, secure, and user-friendly technology that supports
interoperability, improves care delivery, and reduces administrative burden.
TD–9. Regarding certification of health IT:
a. What are the benefits of redefining certification to prioritize API-enabled capabilities
over software functionality?
An API-first certification model would promote real-time interoperability by shifting the focus of
health IT validation from static software functions to dynamic, standards-based data exchange
capabilities. Rather than simply certifying whether a system includes a certain feature (e.g., a
medication list or clinical note viewer), API-first certification ensures that data behind those
features can be accessed, shared, and used across systems through modular, well-defined, and
consistently implemented interfaces.
This approach aligns with how modern digital ecosystems operate—relying on application
programming interfaces (APIs) to power real-time connectivity, automation, and user-centered
design. By making APIs the foundation of certification, CMS and ONC would promote
interoperability not just in theory, but in practical, measurable ways. Systems that pass
certification would need to demonstrate the ability to:
Enable external access to key clinical and administrative data elements (e.g., problems,
medications, encounters, labs, SDOH data, etc.) using FHIR or equivalent open standards.
Support bi-directional data exchange, allowing outside tools—such as care coordination
platforms, third-party apps, and analytics engines—to both read and write data securely.

c. How could ASTP/ONC revise health IT certification criteria to require APIs to

Certification should require read/write API access across both structured and unstructured chart
data to fully enable interoperability and support real-world clinical and administrative use cases.
While structured data—such as medications, labs, diagnoses, and procedures—forms the
backbone of interoperability today, much of the most valuable clinical information still resides in
unstructured formats, including physician notes, care plans, discharge summaries, scanned

Limiting certification to read-only access or structured fields restricts the ability of external
applications, care teams, and health data networks to participate in the full life cycle of health
information exchange.
For example: Read-only access prevents tools from writing back updated medication lists,
allergy data, or patient-generated observations—hindering collaborative care models.
Exclusion of unstructured data means decision support tools, research platforms, and AI-driven
analytics solutions cannot access a large portion of clinically relevant insights, such as narrative
notes documenting social risk factors, mental health assessments, or end-of-life preferences.
Requiring read/write capabilities across both data types would ensure that certified systems can
act as true interoperability platforms—not just data silos with limited visibility or editing
functionality. This would also enable:
Enhanced care coordination: Tools could update patient charts with care team communications
or community referrals.
Closed-loop workflows: External apps could contribute structured inputs back into the record
(e.g., screening results, eConsent, patient-reported outcomes).

TD–11. As of January 1, 2024, many health IT developers with products certified through
the ONC Health IT Certification Program are required to include the capability to
perform an electronic health information export or ‘‘EHI export’’ for a single patient as
well as for patient populations (45 CFR 170.315(b)(10)). Such health IT developers are also
required to publicly describe the format of the EHI export.
Notably, how EHI export was accomplished was left entirely to the health IT developer. Now
that this capability has been in production for over a year, CMS and ASTP/ONC seek input on
the following:
a. Should this capability be revised to specify standardized API requirements for EHI
export?
CMS should standardize API formats for Electronic Health Information (EHI) export to promote
consistency, usability, and interoperability across the healthcare ecosystem. Currently, the EHI
export requirement under the ONC Health IT Certification Program allows health IT developers
broad discretion in how they implement this capability. As a result, different vendors have
adopted varying data formats, export packaging methods, metadata structures, and delivery
mechanisms.
This lack of uniformity creates significant challenges for data recipients—such as patients,
providers, researchers, payers, and third-party app developers—who must build custom solutions
to interpret each vendor's output. Without standardized formats, exported data can be incomplete,
poorly structured, or difficult to parse and integrate into downstream workflows.

b. What are the primary tradeoffs between USCDI and full EHI, especially given more

USCDI provides a well-defined, structured foundation for data interoperability, but full
Electronic Health Information (EHI) allows for the clinical nuance and context that are often
essential to delivering whole-person care. While USCDI helps standardize the most common and
critical data elements—such as medications, allergies, immunizations, and problems it does not
encompass the full breadth and complexity of a patient's health record.
For example, behavioral health assessments, social needs documentation, care coordination
notes, provider-to-provider correspondence, and patient narratives are often stored in
unstructured or non-standard formats that fall outside the USCDI scope. These components may
lack standard vocabularies or consistent capture methods but can contain critical insights into a
patient's care needs, preferences, and lived experience.

By contrast, full EHI, as defined by the 21st Century Cures Act, includes all electronic Protected

TD–19. Regarding price transparency implementation:
a. What are current shortcomings in content, format, delivery, and timeliness?
Despite recent regulatory progress, many hospital and payer price transparency files remain
technically compliant but practically unusable. Pricing data is often published in large, flat
files—such as CSVs or JSON documents—with inconsistent formatting, lack of standardized
coding (e.g., HCPCS, CPT), or ambiguous service descriptions. These files are difficult to parse
and interpret, especially for patients and third-party application developers without technical
expertise.
Additionally, data is not updated frequently enough to reflect real-time pricing changes, coverage
updates, or negotiated rate fluctuations. This stale or incomplete data undermines trust, creates
confusion, and limits the ability of patients and providers to make informed decisions based on
accurate cost projections. Users are left with a fragmented view of what a service will actually
cost them, leading to surprise billing and diminished consumer engagement.

actively planning care and making cost-related decisions. Two of the most critical use cases are:

To modernize and scale price transparency, CMS should promote the development and adoption
