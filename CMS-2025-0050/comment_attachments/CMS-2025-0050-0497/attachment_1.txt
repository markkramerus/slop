1.) TD-2 : Regarding CMS Data, to stimulate developer interest, we recommend the
following enhancements to CMS APIs:
A unified and standardized approach to provider verification, credentialing, and data
updates would significantly improve accuracy and reduce burden. Provider identifiers (e.g.
NPI, TIN, CCN) and specialty types are important keys for matching and filtering data.
Establishing more consistent and standardized updates in these areas would improve the
reliability of data linkages across systems. Additionally, because providers may register
under multiple specialties or bill Medicare and other payers through different group
affiliations, it's essential to support the ability to access and reconcile more than just the
first matching record.
A consolidated approach to claims data would provide more consistent results and better
refresh schedules. For instance, JOS Group’s work on a CMMI Data Fusion Initiative
demonstrated how a ‘one stop shop’ approach to claim data could resolve issues like
beneficiary identity resolution differences between systems (such as the Bene Link Key
upstream and Medi-Medi in IDR or Bene ID resolution in CCW). On other projects for CMS,

2.) TD-7 : While USCDI has improved interoperability, several limitations still exist. The
length of lead time between announcement/finalization of the standard and the adoption
process creates a significant lag between emerging needs and formal inclusion, and the
current process does not accommodate rapid healthcare data evolution.
FHIR is an open-source platform with frequent updates and multiple versions, which
makes it challenging to keep up with changes. The documentation is often hard to
navigate, and it can be difficult to find the information you need.

It's also not guaranteed that FHIR is widely adopted across the industry. Integrating it with
major EHR systems like Epic and Cerner presents additional difficulties due to vendor lock-
in and proprietary architectures.
The way relationships are modeled in FHIR depends on the contributors, and those
interpretations don’t always align with CMS’s needs. At one point in the past, CMS
attempted to create a single Health Care Logical Model to cover multiple domains, but this
led to conflicting designs across systems and vendors.
A centralized governing body within CMS that leads the development of domain-specific
healthcare models from the top down could provide consistency and greatly improve
interoperability across systems.
While it is difficult to capture every field needed for each edge case, it's not just about
adding more fields as much as it is about needing to understand the data holistically.
Collaborating with interoperability platform contributors enables bi-directional feedback,
ensuring that standards and solutions evolve to meet CMS's specific needs. Each
component and data system has unique requirements, so working together across the
agency is essential to present a unified approach.
For example, on a past project, we partnered with three different Application Development
Organizations (ADOs) and the Office of Inspector General (OIG) to define what constitutes
a Telehealth claim. We encountered conflicting definitions from OEDA, CM, and OIG. By
collaborating across teams, the JOS Group was able to develop a unified definition using a
combination of place of service, HCPCS codes, and HCPCS modifier codes.
Regarding the question on language models, we would absolutely prefer a non-proprietary
but less structured format if it improves data coverage, even if it requires more processing.
Proprietary models often increase costs for agencies and developers, as any modification
typically requires vendor involvement and licensing fees, which are likely to increase over
time. In contrast, open-source language models provide greater flexibility, transparency,
and cost-effectiveness.
For example, the JOS Group is developing an “AI Navigator” platform designed to be
flexible and collaborative, supporting a wide range of AI use cases. The platform

3.) TD-13 : Providing API-based access to the full scope of a patient's EHI if the data could
be in a standardized format would create several opportunities:
• Improved Data Portability: Standardized formats make it easier to import and export
data across multiple systems and platforms, reducing data silos and improving care
coordination.
• Enhanced Patient Engagement: When data is easy to read and understand, patients
are more empowered to actively participate in their care decisions.
• Innovation in Digital Health: Developers can build more intelligent applications and
tools that support decision-making, care navigation, and population health
management.
• Better Governance and Security: APIs designed with standardized security
protocols and governance frameworks can provide more consistent protections for
sensitive data, including PII/PHI.
USCDI is limited in scope and may not include all the clinical details necessary for
comprehensive care coordination, population health management, or advanced analytics.
Electronic Health Information (EHI) also has issues. While it offers access to a broader
and richer set of clinical data, it has its own tradeoffs with highly sensitive data requiring
more robust governance and compliance frameworks to protect PII/PHI and meet HIPAA
and federal standards. It needs more complex infrastructure, has higher operational costs,
has proprietary EHR vendor lock-in challenges, and perpetually needs updates to handle
evolving formats and volumes of data. EHI also contains inconsistent or unstructured
data. It frequently incorporates a range of proprietary and overlapping code sets (e.g. CPT,

4.) TD-15 : Increased use of bulk FHIR APIs can significantly enhance data flow and
support a variety of use cases by enabling standardized data refresh cycles across
systems. This leads to more consistent and reliable data sharing. Bulk FHIR also improves
performance by reducing the need for repetitive individual record queries, which in turn
minimizes server load and accelerates data access. Additionally, it helps prevent data
duplication and simplifies data aggregation by supporting more efficient, large-scale
exports. Centralized management of keys and tokens in bulk operations also streamlines
authorization processes and reduces administrative complexity.
However, ensuring the bulk request has the right beneficiaries (meaning specifically the list
of patients for whom there is an actual care management relationship established under
HIPPA) is important. Implementing these APIs can be complex, requiring a deep
understanding of the FHIR standard and careful configuration. Integrating bulk FHIR with
legacy systems will be difficult, as older platforms lack the technical capability or
scalability to support bulk data transfers and often run on batch cycles. There is also
increased overhead in managing access keys and tokens securely, which may necessitate
updated governance policies. Ensuring data consistency across large exports is another
challenge, particularly when systems are updated in real time. Lastly, bulk operations can
strain system performance if not properly optimized, requiring investment in infrastructure
and monitoring.

shared network infrastructure is more efficient and scalable, enabling broader
participation. That said, shared infrastructure requires standardization, coordination, and
shared governance to function effectively. To support either approach, consistent data
