Patient Needs: Insights and Recommendations Background WHOOP offers a fully integrated platform for real-time health insights, which is a valuable tool for managing individual health needs. Our wearable technology provides a centralized app- based experience with integrated biometric trends including heart rate, heart rate variability (HRV), blood oxygen saturation, skin temperature, stress scores, and sleep staging. Additionally, our most advanced WHOOP device supports users with on-demand ECGs, identifies irregular heart rhythms, and provides wellness-focused blood pressure insights. These insights empower users to take proactive steps to support daily health and improve their performance. Looking ahead to the future of health technology, several possible integrations could offer additional benefits and efficiencies for individuals, caregivers, and providers. A digital health assistant could support both users and caregivers with on-demand or proactive insights into data, trends, and individualized needs and recommendations. While many devices and platforms, including those developed by WHOOP, deliver up-to-date technology, broader integration with Medicare data and appointment navigation tools would also make the ecosystem more complete. Full electronic health record (EHR) integration with clinical systems is not supported, which limits visibility into several health measures. Integrating labs, prescription histories, imaging results, and social determinants data into Blue Button 2.0 would allow wearables to offer contextualized insights. Additionally, interoperability with Medicare claims via an expanded Blue Button 2.0 application programming interface (API) would bridge this gap. Medicare Beneficiary and Caregiver Considerations Regarding Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers, wearable technologies are uniquely well-suited to enhance health management in independent living or through remote monitoring use cases. It is key to note that wearable technologies also support transparent disclaimers that distinguish wellness features from regulated diagnostics. From our experience, including a recent home redesign to simplify display, continuous monitoring for physiological changes and delivery through a clear, app-based interface is optimal for users. Tools like passive monitoring, simple presentation of daily scores, clear explanations of trends, accessible interfaces (large fonts, high contrast), and caregiver integration are among the specific features we believe benefit older adults. As desired, users can also share their ECGs or summaries with caregivers or physicians via PDF reports. In future considerations, CMS could validate and endorse qualified digital wellness tools, offer coverage or partial reimbursement under Medicare Advantage for qualified digital wellness tools to aid chronic condition monitoring, and expand education on safe use of wellness wearables. Another practical option would be the development of a voluntary wearable data registry. Participants could share anonymized biometric trends linked to claims events, enabling CMS to study preventive interventions' impact at scale. Finally, CMS could consider streamlining 
developer access with a centralized credentialing and approval process. WHOOP is eager to participate in such initiatives in the future. Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks: Insights and Recommendations Collaboration, Data Access and Digital Identities There is tremendous opportunity for collaboration between CMS and technology developers to build meaningful digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers. In the short term, steps could include funding developer sandboxes and API integrations. Longer term, launching innovation accelerators and establishing interoperability compliance frameworks tied to reimbursement would be beneficial. It would also be interesting to explore challenge grants and testbeds that would accelerate wearable innovation for aging populations. Beyond the innovation framework, CMS can also partner with technology developers through data sharing. Specifically, data related to labs, imaging, medication adherence, and long-term care data are valuable. Today’s obstacles to overcome in future data sharing include limited API scope, inconsistent formats, and delays in claims data availability. WHOOP recommends expanding real-time data access for proactive care planning. Digital identity implementation is a clear component of future partnerships between CMS and technology developers. Digital identities improve data security, consent management, and user control. Challenges include onboarding digitally inexperienced users. CMS could streamline access by integrating digital IDs (Login.gov, ID.me) into Medicare enrollment workflows. System Interoperability,Technical Standards and Data Exchange In future work together the use of application programming interfaces over proprietary interfaces is an issue to consider. APIs enable interoperability without dictating user experience. CMS should fund API reliability benchmarks and encourage full data export capabilities. A national Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) endpoint directory would also allow WHOOP and other developers to identify authorized provider endpoints for real-time data exchange and patient-directed sharing. CMS or ONC would be an optimal host, with sharing offered at no cost and data updated in real-time. Sharing Electronic Health Information Working with patients through shared electronic health information offers the potential for increased efficiency, convenience, and understanding of health metrics. Wearables specifically could personalize care based on lab results, diagnoses, and longitudinal history. This would improve risk prediction and early intervention across use cases. At present, resistance to sharing EHR data despite user consent and arbitrary fees for FHIR access are known issues. CMS should enforce timelines and penalties for noncompliance. 
Value-Based Care Organizations: Insights and Recommendations Delivering high-quality health care at accessible costs and prioritizing positive outcomes and experiences is a shared goal between the federal government and the health technology ecosystem. CMS has the opportunity to incentivize the use of digital tools to assist in this goal. It is key that CMS considers capabilities in biometric trend monitoring, device-to-clinic data sharing, longitudinal healthspan metrics and coaching tools that translate insights into actions when assessing how technology might enhance value-based care. CMS should support devices and technologies that deliver predictive analytics, personalized alerts, and care plan integration. Additionally, CMS could allow wearable engagement to count toward quality measures and offer bonus payments tied to usage thresholds. This bonus payment mechanism could live within Medicare Advantage or value-based care arrangements with incentives tied to defined wearable usage thresholds. For example, CMS might establish a tiered incentive structure in which providers receive performance-based bonuses if a certain percentage of attributed beneficiaries meet sustained engagement criteria with approved wearable devices. CMS could align bonus eligibility with validated device capabilities and documented improvements in care coordination, patient engagement, or outcomes. In order to facilitate a strong, value-based relationship, straightforward data collection and data exchange are critical areas for consideration. For example, in addition to EHR and claims data, daily stress patterns, HRV, ECG outcomes, and sleep quality offer predictive power and should be supported features. Additionally, the lack of mutual APIs between wearables and provider systems presents a challenge. CMS could support bi-directional access and subsidize FHIR integration to improve upon current status. Finally, developing a unified identity and a nationwide FHIR provider directory would help route consented data and support transparency. CMS should implement and maintain these standards with minimal burden to patients. Conclusion WHOOP strongly supports CMS’s vision for a connected, patient-first health system. Our platform bridges the gap between wellness and clinical care, empowering users to take control of their health while supporting providers with real-world data. We appreciate that this RFI represents an important shared step toward this goal. We are eager to partner with CMS in pilot programs and policy initiatives that prioritize proactive care, improved health outcomes, and system-wide interoperability. Thank you for the opportunity to contribute to this critical dialogue. Please do not hesitate to contact us if you have any questions.
