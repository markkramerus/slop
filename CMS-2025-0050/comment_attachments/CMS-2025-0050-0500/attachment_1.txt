 Patients Rising Response to CMS–0442–NC RFI: Health Technology Ecosystem Submitted to: Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) & Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ASTP/ONC) Submitted by: MacKay Jimeson, Executive Director, Patients Rising Date: June 16, 2025 Executive Summary Patients Rising is a national patient advocacy organization representing individuals living with chronic, rare, and complex conditions. We applaud CMS and ASTP/ONC for initiating this critical dialogue on digital health transformation. Our comments respond directly to questions posed in the Request for Information (RFI) and emphasize five urgent priorities: 1. Patient Data Ownership & Decentralized Health Vaults 2. Consent & Privacy-by-Design 3. Marketplace Transparency & Navigation Tools 4. Tokenized Incentives for Data Sharing 5. Safe Harbor Reform for Patient Savings Participation By elevating the patient to an active node in the health ecosystem and aligning financial and digital infrastructure, CMS can catalyze a more responsive, ethical, and innovative healthcare system. 1. Patient Data Ownership & Decentralized Vaults (Responds to Patient & Caregiver [PC]-2, PC-8, PC-10; Provider [PR]-3; Technology Developer [TD]-9) Patients deserve full control over their health data, including the ability to collect, manage, share, and monetize their information securely. CMS should: ● Recognize personal health data vaults as permissible read/write endpoints under the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA). ● Enable Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR)-based interactions between payers/providers and patient-controlled repositories. 1 
● Include diagnostic imaging, raw device telemetry, and social determinant data as part ofthe standard patient export. We recommend CMS launch a "Patient Node" pilot program integrating decentralized vault platforms (e.g., Lydion Vaults) into existing Qualified Health Information Networks (QHINs). 2. Consent & Privacy-by-Design (Responds to PC-5, PC-6, PC-14; TD-3, TD-17) Patient participation in digital health tools must be rooted in opt-in consent, not mandatory biometric capture or digital identity requirements. CMS should: ● Require platforms to offer tiered consent frameworks (e.g., sharing vitals but not genomic data). ● Enforce the right to be forgotten, including cryptographic erasure and deletion application programming interfaces (APIs). ● Prohibit data brokers, insurers, and third parties from accessing health data without explicit patient authorization. ● Clarify that charging patients or their designees excessive fees for access to their electronic health information (EHI) constitutes information blocking. CMS should also accept World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) decentralized identifiers (DIDs) bound to National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) 800-63-3 credentials across all patient-accessible CMS APIs. 3. Marketplace Navigation Tools to Drive Transparency & Savings (Responds to PC-1, PC-12; TD-19; Value-Based Care [VB]-1) Patients need intuitive digital platforms that allow them to search, compare, and book care based on cost, quality, and convenience. We urge CMS to: ● Endorse or certify consumer-facing marketplace platforms that: ○ Display procedure-level cost estimates ○ Offer provider comparisons (training, volume, outcomes) ○ Support scheduling/logistics (e.g., care concierge models) ○ Deliver savings through pre-negotiated or bundled rates ● Integrate such tools into Medicare Advantage and Medicaid managed care as eligible navigation and cost-sharing services. ● Require public hospital and payer transparency data to be machine-readable and usable by these platforms. 4. Tokenized Incentives & Data Economics (Responds to PC-7; TD-12; VB-4) 2 
CMS should pilot the use of data tokens to reward patients for voluntarily contributing high-quality, timely data. These can be funded from existing value-based care (VBC) shared savings pools and should: ● Be de minimis (less than $5 per life per year) to align with Internal Revenue Service (IRS) de minimis benefit rules ● Be tied to actual patient-controlled sharing of claims, outcomes, or device data ● Support verifiable chain-of-custody and lineage tracking for research-quality data Such networks should qualify as "trusted exchange frameworks" when they support FHIR Release 4 (R4), meet Identity Assurance Level 2 (IAL2) / Authentication Assurance Level 2 (AAL2) identity standards, and publish chain-of-custody proofs. 5. Modernize Health Savings Account (HSA) Safe Harbor Rules (Responds to VB-1, PC-5) To unlock the power of digital choice and price transparency, CMS should work with the U.S. Department of the Treasury to: ● Modernize HSA safe harbor rules to allow patients to receive shared savings (cash rewards, refunds, or tokens) without jeopardizing HSA eligibility. ● Encourage patients to use price comparison tools or curated networks with demonstrated value. This would allow truly consumer-directed care and reward patients who take proactive steps to lower system costs. Conclusion We believe that digital transformation in healthcare will succeed only if it puts patients—not platforms, not payers—at the center. CMS can lead the nation by advancing decentralized, privacy-first models that recognize patients as partners in care, not just data sources. We thank you for the opportunity to comment and are available to support implementation efforts. MacKay Jimeson Executive Director, Patients Rising 3
