In response to the Health Technology Ecosystem RFI, Kreative puts forth the following inputs for
Section E. Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks.
TD-1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to
stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare
beneficiaries and caregivers?
The technology challenges in healthcare are complex and multifaceted. No single product
can address all the obstacles, issues, and systemic problems that exist across the sector.
Effective solutions must be built on highly interoperable, user-centered software that
prioritizes real-world usability and inclusivity.
Strategic incentive programs—both short- and long-term—can help shape the market in
two key ways. First, by encouraging the development of high-quality software that
addresses the most pressing problems in healthcare, not solely those that yield the highest
profits. These programs should reward innovation that improves access and care quality

for underserved populations. In the near term, expanding public access to digital health
portals can immediately increase both market size and healthcare accessibility.
To drive interoperability, one approach is to offer short-term incentives for collaborative
data-sharing and integration projects. These efforts could be supported by longer-term
benefits, such as tax incentives, that reward organizations for sustaining and expanding
interoperability over time. Additionally, establishing clear standards for security and
interoperability would not only support system-wide consistency but also enhance the
credibility and appeal of compliant solutions—improving public trust, increasing adoption,
and ultimately driving better health outcomes.
TD-2. Regarding CMS Data, to stimulate developer interest—
a. What additional data would be most valuable if made available through CMS APIs?
Anonymized health datasets for disease patterns would be a massive benefit to technology
companies looking to build improved health pattern datasets. This would empower better
digital therapeutics, diagnostics, and monitoring programs.
TD-3. Regarding digital identity implementation:
a. What are the challenges and benefits?
b. How would requiring digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR,
Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs) impact cybersecurity
and data exchange?
c. What impact would mandatory use of the OpenID Connect identity protocol
have?
On the benefits side, adopting strong, standardized digital identity credentials (such as
those aligned with NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 standards) enhances trust, security, and
interoperability across systems. This enables CMS to better safeguard sensitive health and
beneficiary data, streamline access to services, and improve user experience through single
sign-on (SSO) and reduced identity fraud. Challenges include ensuring equitable access for
all populations, addressing technical integration complexities across legacy systems,
managing vendor lock-in risks, and maintaining compliance with federal privacy and
accessibility requirements.
Requiring digital identity credentials from approved Credential Service Providers
(CSPs)—such as Login.gov, ID.me, or CLEAR—would strengthen CMS’s cybersecurity
posture by reducing unauthorized access and ensuring higher assurance of user identities
during data exchange. It would also enable more secure interoperability with external
partners and systems by providing consistent, verifiable identity assertions.
Mandating the use of the OpenID Connect (OIDC) protocol would further benefit CMS by
standardizing authentication and enabling scalable, secure federation of identities across
healthcare and government entities. OIDC’s support for modern security features and
compatibility with OAuth 2.0 would also improve integration with cloud-based services
and APIs. However, the requirement could impose initial technical and policy alignment

burdens on partners unfamiliar with the protocol, requiring targeted support and transition
planning.
TD-4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available
APIs over proprietary APIs?
CMS can better encourage the use of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs by
aligning incentive programs to reward interoperability and long-term collaboration. This
includes offering financial or regulatory benefits to organizations that adopt and maintain
open APIs, particularly those that support underserved populations and improve access to
care. Additionally, CMS can establish certification standards that elevate the visibility and
credibility of open API solutions, encouraging industry adoption. By coupling short-term
incentives with sustained benefits—such as tax credits or preferred contracting status—
CMS can shift market behavior away from proprietary systems and toward a more open,
accessible health technology ecosystem
TD-5. How could a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints improve access to
health information for patients, providers, and payers? Who should publish such a
directory, and should users bear a cost?
The government could host the directory and charge a minimal licensing fee for companies
seeking to utilize the information, while rigorously enforcing a standard of what it may be
used for (like an academic board’s IRB, which determines if the ethics of a study are valid).
This can be an elected or appointed body, or any arbitrating body or standard set forth by
the government. If the users (patients and caregivers) must bear a significant cost burden,
it will dissuade users who may benefit the most from the applications.
TD-9. Regarding certification of health IT:
a. What are the benefits of redefining certification to prioritize API-enabled capabilities
over software functionality?
b. What would be the drawbacks?
APIs are essential to building the CMS technology ecosystem. They enable
interoperability across companies and infrastructure, reduce manual work, empower
patients by giving them access to functionality and data, and allow innovation through
third-party developers. In many ways APIs will become the foundation for connected
healthcare.
CMS should balance its emphasis on APIs with foundational software. Modern platforms
build for clinical workflow and patient support (medical and administrative) enable the
core functionality behind how healthcare is planned and delivered. If there is inconsistent
API design and delivery, or if core systems are neglected, it could lead to poor user
experiences and inefficient healthcare operations and processes.

TD-12. Should CMS endorse non-CMS data sources and networks, and if so, what
criteria or metrics should CMS consider?
CMS should endorse non-CMS data sources that meet a certain rigorous standard of
quality, which could be determined by industry or government. This would require the
government to evaluate datasets but would increase the collaboration within industry and
empower digital health insights.
Specific non-CMS data sources that should be assessed for integration and collaboration
include commercial Healthcare Information Exchanges, payer databases, social services
platforms, and community-based networks. As the CMS assesses these various
stakeholders and datasets, it needs to consider data quality, data standards, interoperability,
privacy, and accessibility.
TD-18. Information blocking:
b. What additional policies could ASTP/ONC and CMS implement to further discourage
healthcare providers from engaging in information blocking practices?
CMS should consider the following innovations in the area of information blocking:
• Expand FHIR API adoption to enable seamless data exchange between systems and
patient apps.
• Incentivize real-time data sharing, like hospital admission/discharge alerts and
EHR-to-EHR notifications.
• Link compliance to reimbursement by tying information blocking enforcement to
participation in CMS programs.
• Promote patient access tools like Blue Button 2.0 and universal portals for
Medicare/Medicaid beneficiaries.
• Support participation in national data networks such as TEFCA to improve
interoperability.
• Fund AI tools for data normalization to standardize and clean health data for
sharing.
• Standardize SDOH data sharing to integrate social and clinical care for chronic
disease prevention.

In conclusion, Kreative offers the following recommendations for CMS to consider as it continues
to shape policy and implement technological frameworks:
• Mandate Standards-Based Interoperability
Promote adoption of FHIR-based APIs and enforce data sharing protocols that mirror those
required across DHA systems to ensure seamless integration of devices and platforms.
Consider interoperability incentives that encourage private technology vendors to develop
open source/shared interoperable segments/functions. Use these incentives to enforce a

rigorous standard among businesses – improving operational efficiency, security, and
perceived credibility across the entire ecosystem
• Prioritize Security and Zero Trust Architecture
Integrate cybersecurity best practices drawn from DHA’s zero-trust architecture and RMF
(Risk Management Framework) models when approving digital tools for Medicare use.
• Expand Value-Based Reimbursement for Digital Care
Enable CPT/HCPCS code expansion for remote patient monitoring and digital
therapeutics, with reimbursement models that reward proven outcomes and sustained
patient engagement.
• Design care and supporting infrastructure around wellness, and the prevention and
management of chronic disease.
Leverage AI and analytics to provide preventative care to at risk populations, engage
citizens through mobile apps and patient portals, provide telehealth for medical care and
mental wellness, design digital therapeutics and clinical decision support tools around
electronic health records.
• Make the Patient the Data Owner
Emulate patient portal features that allow patients to access and manage their health data
directly—enhancing transparency and engagement. Utilize educational resources
presented in consumable packaging to further empower the users to understand and act on
their health data. In the long term, this empowers the individual to manage and improve
their own health.
• Bridge the Digital Divide with Targeted Investments
Fund broadband expansion, device access, and digital literacy programs for rural and
underserved populations to ensure no beneficiary is left behind.
• Establish a Digital Health Evaluation Framework
Support a CMS-led evaluation model for usability, clinical impact, and data integrity
before new digital tools are added to the Medicare ecosystem.
• Launch CMS-DHA Innovation Pilots
Consider CMS/DHA joint pilot programs that bring together Medicare and TRICARE
learnings to advance remote care for chronic disease, aging in place, and care transitions.
