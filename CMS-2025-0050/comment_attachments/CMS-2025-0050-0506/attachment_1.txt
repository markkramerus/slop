Society for Digital Mental Health Comments PC 5: What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver inter est? To foster trust and drive engagement among Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers, CMS should focus on three core pillars: education, trust-building, and integration into care workflows . We recommend: ● National campaigns for digital health literacy: Partner with community-based organizations, libraries, senior centers, and health plans to roll out education campaigns focused on teaching digital health basics, reducing stigma, and building trust in technology . ● Patient-center ed design evaluations: Support development of digital health tools that meet adults’ usability needs. CMS could incentivize developers to meet co-design standards that include direct input from Medicare users. ● Clinical integration incentives: Encourage providers to incorporate digital health tools into care pathways and patient conversations. While informing patients of the availability of digital mental health treatments (DMHT s) can theoretically reach large numbers of patients, this practice alone results in low DMHT uptake. Provider endorsement has been shown to be critical for promoting patient adoption. 1 ● Support healthcar e systems in cost-effective methods for supporting patients in registering and engaging in DMHT s . Research and experience in DMHT implementation has shown that while many patients are interested in DMHT s, they rarely follow-up on referrals without further assistance. There are several reasons for low patient uptake on DMHT referrals, including forgetfulness, low motivation and or anxiety related to the mental health condition, unfamiliarity with DMHT services, and low digital literacy . Particularly in medical settings such as primary care, where most common mental health treatment is provided, clinicians typically do not have the time to assist patient onboarding or support longer term engagement. We applaud CMS for recognizing the need for patient support services in the current HCPCS Codes (G-0553, G-0554). SDMH, working with seven large healthcare systems, identified the following criteria that are important in evaluating digital mental health products 3 : ● Clinical efficacy data from robust, well-designed randomized controlled trials (RCT s) are the gold standard evidence to demonstrate that a digital mental health tool is effective at improving mental health symptoms. ● Real-world data (RWD) is an important complement to RCT data. While RCT data provides the strongest proof-of-principle support for the efficacy and effectiveness, RCTs often suffer from sample biases, as they require considerable time and effort for the completion of research procedures (e.g., consent and often substantial assessment), thereby selecting highly motivated participants. RWD, collected in the process of care delivery , does not involve any additional patient research-related effort. Thus, RWD offers evidence that the digital mental health service can provide value under real world circumstances. ● Clinical review by the healthcare system or clinics providers is commonly conducted to ensure the digital mental health product employs valid clinical strategies and aligns with providers’ values and clinical approaches. ● Usability for the intended population . Many healthcare systems engage their patients in evaluating the usability of the product. ● Health equity review is conducted to ensure that the digital mental health service is accessible to all patients, and does not exclude specific groups (e.g., rural, low income, patients with disabilities or with low connectivity). ● Data privacy and security compliance (HIPAA, cybersecurity standards) must be reviewed. ● Inter operability with EHR systems and potentially with CMS infrastructure to ensure data integration. What technology or policy recommendations can increase adoption? ● Reimbursement adjustments: While the new HCPCS codes for digital mental health treatment have been developed, it does present some challenges to widespread adoption and are not representative of all high-quality products within this space. To ensure uptake of these services, SDMH advocates for expanded reimbursement for other categories of DMHT that demonstrate safety and effectiveness outside of CFR 882.5801. ● Expanding reimbursement to include a broader range of evidence-based digital mental health products: To support patient access and provider flexibility , we recommend expanding reimbursement to include evidence-based digital mental health products beyond those with FDA clearance. PC-5 b. What changes would enable timely access to high quality CMS and provider generated data on patients? To unlock the full potential of digital mental health tools, developers and providers must be able to access CMS and clinical data in a secure, timely , and structured way. We suggest: ● Fast-track data access for validated use cases : Create pathways for developers with approved IRB protocols or partnerships with CMS providers to access anonymized patient data sets more rapidly . ● Real-time claims data pilots : Fund pilot programs that allow real-time or near-real-time access to claims data for performance feedback and early signal detection. ● Promote FHIR-based interoperability : Ensure providers participating in Medicare are incentivized to support FHIR-based data exchange with third-party digital tools, reducing friction for integration. Providers also may not know rates in advance, how rates were determined or when they might change, making longer term planning difficult. Shifting to national pricing would reduce burden uncertainty for providers, thereby supporting adoption and integration of DMHT s. ● Currently G-0552 for the DMHT device requires billing by the provider . Pharmacists play a supporting role, such as dispensing and counseling on prescription medications that are often prescribed in conjunction with DMHT s. We encourage alternative billing methods, such as using the Pharmacy Benefit Model, which would allow devices to be dispensed and adjudicated through pharmacies, similar to prescription drugs. . b. What information should providers share with patients when using digital products in the provision of their care? Providers should provide the following information when discussing DMHT s as a treatment option: ● Basic information on the effectiveness of the DMHT , including the possible benefits of the DMHT . ● What is expected of the patient (how frequently they should use it, and how much time it takes). ● How the DMHT fits into the care pathway . This includes what happens if the DMHT is not effective, and what other types of treatments might be offered at that point. ● What the provider ’s role in the treatment will be (if the provider will be receiving and reviewing symptom severity measures, or what the provider ’s role will be if the DMHT is not effective). In addition, the provider or another member of the treatment team (e.g., digital navigator , coach, peer specialist) should assess factors that may interfere with the patient’ s ability to use the DMHT , including technological literacy , any disabilities (e.g. vision or hearing), capacity of the patient’ s CMS–0042–NC Society for Digital Mental Health 7
