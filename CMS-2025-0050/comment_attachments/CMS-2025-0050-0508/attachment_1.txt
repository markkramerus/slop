Dear Secretary Kennedy:
On behalf of Citizen Health, I appreciate the opportunity to provide some responses to the questions in the Request for Information on the
Health Technology Ecosystem (RIN 0938–AV68).
Citizen Health empowers patients (and caregivers working on their behalf) to have complete control of their health data: to use it and to share it
with whomever, whenever, and wherever they want. With a particular focus on helping sick patients and their caregivers, the Citizen Health
online platform democratizes health care by putting health information directly in the hands of patients - the stakeholders most motivated to
collect it, use it, and share it liberally to save their own lives and the lives of others just like them. Our mission is to harness the power of data
and shared experiences to provide relief to patients and families navigating rare and complex conditions.
The age of machine learning and artificial intelligence brings with it enormous potential to improve the lives of patients, reducing their burdens
and improving their outcomes. At Citizen Health, we see a world where any patient, at any stage, with any condition, has immediate access to
exceptional guidance on the best personalized actions to take at that point in time. We are hard at work building these tools for our users and
communities of patients – and data is the fuel that powers these tools.

When we first started Citizen Health back in 2017 (at that time, it was known as Ciitizen), it was difficult for patients to easily obtain copies of all
of their health and medical records, even though for more than 20 years the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) Privacy
Rule included a patient’s right to be able to easily obtain complete copies of all of their medical and health plan records. We built a system from
scratch that leverages this HIPAA right of access to obtain records on patients behalf, and it is still the predominant way that we obtain complete
medical records for users of Citizen Health (including data not typically available through FHIR APIs or networks). Over the past 7+ years, the U.S.
has made progress in providing patients with easier, digital access to electronic medical and health plan records, such as through electronic
medical record certification standards mandating patient-facing FHIR APIs, periodic financial incentives from CMS to share data with patients,
CMS requirements for patient access to health plan records (for plans under CMS purview), promulgation of the 21st Century Cures Act
Information Blocking Rules, and enhanced enforcement of the HIPAA Privacy Rule, to name some key initiatives.
But there are still too many unnecessary obstacles to the use of this taxpayer supported, technological infrastructure by patients and caregivers,
including through apps or platforms like Citizen Health working on their behalf – obstacles that HHS, using all of its various authorities, has the
ability (and hopefully the courage) to address. We urge you to review the comments of b.well Connect Health (a company we work with
frequently on patient/caregiver access issues), the CARIN Alliance (a multistakeholder organization supporting patient and caregiver access to
data and for which we are a member of the Board), Selfiie (a company that works with health information exchanges (HIEs) to facilitate the
sharing of data held or exchanged by HIEs with patients) and Dr. Josh Mandel (https://joshuamandel.com/cms-rfi-collab/0) for more
comprehensive assessments of some of the technological and policy obstacles they have seen or directly experienced and concrete
suggestions for addressing them. We will focus on our comments in this RFI on our direct experiences at Citizen Health, including some input
from the patient advocacy organizations who are users of our platform, on some of the key questions in this RFI. For ease of use for CMS and
ONC, we have created a table of all of the questions for which we had additional input, in the order in which they appear in the RFI.
We note that some of our recommendations involve clarification of, or issuance of additional guidance under, the HIPAA Privacy Rule, which is
currently under the authority of the HHS Office for Civil Rights. HIPAA governs data sharing from some of the most important data holders in our
ecosystem - providers and health plans and the vendors who serve them. Uncertainty about HIPAA’s regulations and how they apply is frequently
an obstacle to data exchange. An HHS strategy for improving patient access to their health information should include clarifications, guidance or
even proposed modifications to HIPAA where needed to smooth the path to patient access. Our responses to the RFI include specific suggestions
for updating HIPAA to meet modern data needs and capabilities.

Relevant Excerpts from Ciitizen (now Citizen Health’s) HIPAA Proposed Rule Comments
May 4, 2021
Robinsue Frohboese

Dear Acting Director Frohboese:
On behalf of Ciitizen, I appreciate the opportunity to provide public comments on the proposed rule entitled Proposed Modifications
to the HIPAA Privacy Rule to Support, and Remove Barriers to, Coordinated Care and Individual Engagement (RIN 0945-AA00).
Ciitizen’s mission is to empower the world’s 7 billion citizens to have complete control of their health data: to share it with
whomever, whenever, and wherever they want. With a particular focus on empowering sick patients, the Ciitizen online platform
democratizes health care by putting copies of health information in the hands of patients - the stakeholders most motivated to
collect it, use it, and share it liberally to save their own lives and the lives of others just like them.
Ciitizen helps patients leverage their right of individual access under the HIPAA Privacy Rule to have copies of all protected health
information (PHI) in a designated record set gathered for them in one place. Ciitizen is initially focusing on very sick patients –
beginning with cancer and recently expanding to rare neurological conditions and other diseases – who need their complete health
histories in order to participate more fully in their care, to obtain second opinions and pursue other treatment options, and to
contribute their data to power medical research. Ciitizen users need more information than is currently available to them through
36

health care provider or health plan portals or application programming interfaces (APIs). As a result, Ciitizen obtains records for its
users the “old-fashioned way” – by submitting record requests on behalf of its users to medical records and radiology/imaging
departments or staff at health care providers and plans and having those records sent to Ciitizen for population in the user’s account.
Many of the challenges we have faced in helping our users obtain their medical records are due to some health care providers, or
release-of-information vendors working on their behalf, treating Ciitizen as a “third party” seeking PHI instead of as a service that
individuals engage to collect, organize, store, and manage their health information on their behalf, pursuant to their preferences.
These challenges were exacerbated by the court’s decision in Ciox Health LLC v. Azar (hereinafter, Ciox v. Azar). We greatly appreciate
and support OCR’s efforts in the proposed rule to draw a clear distinction between services like Ciitizen, which stand in the shoes of
the individual, and services where the individual right of access is a mere conduit for data to benefit a third party. We also greatly
appreciate and support, in the wake of the Ciox v. Azar decision, OCR’s efforts to further flesh out the right of access regulatory
provisions versus relying more on informal guidance to apply those regulations to common scenarios. Our comments (summarized
below) are intended to enhance OCR’s efforts to improve the ability for individuals to obtain access to, or copies of, their PHI:
• OCR’s proposal to require business associates to respond to individual access requests only when directed to do so by
their business associate agreement ignores provisions in the 21st Century Cures Act that require certain business
associates to refrain from “blocking” information, including in response to individual requests. We urge OCR to revise the
proposed rule to fully implement the clear intent of Congress in the Cures Act that “actors” (such as health information
exchanges and networks (HIEs) and certified electronic health record (EHR) vendors) not use contractual provisions to
block requests for electronic health information (EHI), particularly from individuals or apps or services acting on their
behalf.
• We urge OCR to further clarify the definition of “personal health application,” or use the term “personal health record,” to
expand the types of tools and services individuals can engage to obtain, manage, and store their health information
beyond just “apps” and to enable covered entities, and business associates working on their behalf, to accurately
distinguish between personal health records engaged by individuals and tools or services primarily intended for the
benefit of third parties.
37

• We urge OCR to maintain an expansive definition of “electronic health record,” (EHR) consistent with the definition in the
Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health Act of 2009 ( HITECH) and align the right of individuals to
have information from an EHR sent directly to the third party of their choice with the existing HIPAA right of access and
ONC’s Information Blocking regulations.
• We support the proposed changes in the right of access regulations giving individuals the right to inspect their PHI,
including to take notes, videos, recordings, and photographs, using personal resources.
• We support proposals shortening the amount of time entities have to respond to an individual’s request for access to “as
soon as practicable, and no later than 15 days” in most cases. We also support conditioning extensions of this deadline on
entities implementing policies to assure faster delivery of copies when needed (such as for urgent treatment-related
purposes or to meet a benefits application deadline).
• We support the express prohibition on unreasonable measures for individuals seeking access to or copies of their records,
and we urge OCR to further clarify what constitutes unreasonable identity proofing requirements. We further urge OCR to
add additional examples of unreasonable measures to the regulation, including refusal to accept electronic signatures,
refusal to accept requests for access by e-mail, and requirements to complete an entity’s particular request form, even if
that form is short.
• We support the proposed provisions regarding permissible fees charged for access to PHI – and the proposed requirement
to publicly post those fees - but urge OCR to: be clearer in the Privacy Rule regarding the application of state law fees to
the individual right of access, clarify what is intended by an “internet-based method,” and publish more guidance on
which fees are permissible in particular circumstances.
• We question the policy rationale behind the proposal to require entities to accept oral requests from individuals when
they are seeking to direct that information in an EHR be sent directly to a third party (including another health care
provider or health plan) given the potential for abuse by someone claiming to be acting on behalf of the individual.
However, we support that entities should be required to accept an oral request from an individual seeking to
38

inspect/videotape/record/photograph their PHI during an office visit or hospital stay, as there is no doubt regarding the
origin and legitimacy of that request.
• We support OCR’s proposal to consider provider queries to HIEs to fulfill an individual right of access request to count as
“customer service” under “health care operations.” We further urge OCR to consider making such HIE queries in response
to individual access requests mandatory.
• We do not believe that covered entities (or business associates operating on their behalf) should be required to warn
individuals about potential privacy risks associated with sharing their health information with a personal health
application/record or third party not covered by HIPAA; however, if such warnings are voluntarily provided, we urge OCR
to prohibit long and overly complicated warnings that could serve as an unreasonable measure to facilitating individual
access to their health information via the tool or service of their choice.
Our detailed comments on these provisions are set forth below.
Recognizing the Role of Certain Business Associates as “Actors” under the Information Blocking Rules (45 CFR 164.502(a)(4)(ii))
Historically OCR has taken the position in guidance that whether business associates (BAs) are required to respond directly to
individuals seeking access to their PHI depends on the terms of their business associate agreements (BAAs). In the proposed rule,
OCR inserts this guidance into the Privacy Rule. However, this proposal runs counter to the Cures Act and ONC’s Information Blocking
rules, at least for entities covered by those rules. Overall, OCR needs to take further action to assure the Privacy Rule is consistent
with these new federal laws.
The Cures Act establishes penalties for “information blocking” by health care providers, certified EHR vendors, and HIEs (hereinafter,
“actors”). Information blocking is defined as a practice that, except as specified by the HHS Secretary in regulations, “is likely to
interfere with, prevent, or materially discourage access, exchange, or use of electronic health information.” (42 USC 300jj-52(a)(1)(A))
The statute provides a list of non-exclusive examples of information blocking, including “practices that restrict authorized access,
39

exchange, or use under applicable State or Federal law of such information for treatment and other permissible purposes under such
applicable law….” (42 USC 300jj-52(a)(2)(A) emphasis added)
In the Information Blocking rules (and guidance accompanying those rules), ONC correctly recognizes that contracts can be vehicles
used by actors to interfere with the sharing of EHI, to assure that actors do not attempt an end-run around Cures through contractual
provisions. Unfortunately, existing provisions of the HIPAA Privacy Rule run counter to the Cures Act and Information Blocking rules,
as applied to actors. Pursuant to the Privacy Rule, BAAs are required to include provisions establishing the
BA’s permitted uses and disclosures of the PHI covered by the agreement. (45 CFR 164.504(e)(2)) Historically these BAAs have been
used to limit how a BA can use and disclose PHI, sometimes legitimately to protect individual privacy but more frequently to protect
[1]
a covered entity’s business interests. And covered entities could count on OCR to enforce these limitations, as BAs are not
permitted to violate the terms of their BAAs. (45 CFR 164.502(a)(3)) However, instead of addressing this conflict, OCR exacerbates
the problem – not only by failing to address the Cures Act and Information Blocking rules through amendments to the BA provisions
of the Privacy Rule but by proposing a regulatory change that cements the BAA as arbiter of whether a BA discloses information to
individuals. This provides actors with legal cover for declining to share data with individuals who request copies of their electronic
health information.
Essentially, the Cures Act and the Information Blocking rules establish a new governance regime for electronic health information
held by actors, and the Privacy Rule must be modified to recognize this reality. The HIPAA Privacy Rule still constitutes the outer
boundary of what uses and disclosures of EHI are permitted, providing important guardrails to protect patient privacy. But the
Privacy Rule should not expressly require or invite entities to place further limitations on the sharing of this information, limitations
which would then be enforceable by OCR. Although the Information Blocking rules have their own enforcement and penalties, it is
highly unlikely that penalties will be imposed by one branch of HHS for activities or behavior that is expressly endorsed by another
branch. This type of conflict is a result HHS was expressly directed by Congress to avoid. (See 42 USC 300jj-52(b)(3) & (d)(4))
Of particular concern to Ciitizen is allowing covered entities and BAs, through voluntarily entered contractual provisions in a BAA, to
limit the mechanisms through which individuals can access EHI from actors. The Cures Act expressly prioritizes actors sharing
electronic health information with patients. For example, Section 4006(a) of the Cures Act envisions that certified EHR vendors and
40

health information networks or exchanges would provide patients with health information held by these networks and requires OCR
to set forth guidance on this topic.[2] OCR has yet to provide this guidance; instead, the proposal to reinforce the BAA as controlling
whether any BA can disclose information to individuals conflicts with what Congress specifically directed with respect to actors and
electronic health information.
Further, disclosures to individuals upon request is a required disclosure under the Privacy Rule, with limited exceptions. The text of
the existing Privacy Rule already establishes that there is no HIPAA violation when a BA discloses PHI where required by law to do so -
and the Cures Act establishes a separate legal obligation for actors to refrain from blocking the sharing of EHI, including with
individuals upon request, absent application of an Information Blocking rules exception. For example, the HIPAA Privacy Rule
provides that BAs may use or disclose PHI (of which EHI is a subset) only as permitted by their BAA “or as required by law.” (45 CFR
164.502(a)(3)). The Privacy Rule also expressly requires BAs to make data available to individuals. For example, the Rule states that
BAAs must include provisions requiring the BA to “make PHI available in accordance with” the HIPAA individual right of access
provisions (45 CFR 164.504(e)(2)), and BAs are required to disclose PHI “to a covered entity, the individual or the individual’s
designee.” (45 CFR 164.502(a)(4)(ii) (emphasis added)) Although in preexisting guidance OCR has urged covered entities and BAs to
establish in the BAA which entity is responsible for responding to an individual’s right of access request,[3] the regulations clearly
obligate BAs to make PHI available to individuals and envision that this information could be provided directly. Consequently, the
proposed regulatory change in Section 164.502(a)(4)(ii) arguably takes a step backward from the Privacy Rule’s existing text, when
read in combination with the new obligations under the Cures Act. Given the emphasis in Cures on patient access, coupled with text
in the Privacy Rule that mandates disclosures to individuals in most cases and provides that BAs can make these disclosures directly,
it makes little sense for OCR to propose an amendment to the Rule that goes in the opposite direction and could block individuals
from accessing their information from actors.
Finally, Section 4006(b) of the Cures Act specifically allows (but does not require) “business associates” to respond directly to
individual access requests for PHI. This is a separate subpart of the Cures Act, which does not expressly override or supersede
Sections 4004 (the Information Blocking provisions) and 4006(a) (expressly referencing patient access to PHI from actors). A logical
reading of all of these Cures Act provisions together yields a conclusion that Congress intended that actors (some of which also are
BAs) be directly accountable to responding to requests for EHI, particularly from individuals, and intended for BAs that are not actors
to have the authority to respond to individuals. (Of note: OCR also should consider whether allowing a BAA to preclude any BA from
41

responding directly to an individual access request conflicts with Section 4006(b), where Congress expressly authorizes BAs to take
such actions.)
To the extent that some BAs face challenges in providing individuals – or their apps – with access to PHI, actors covered by the
Information Blocking rules can look to leverage one of the eight safe harbors or document why declining to provide such access does
not constitute information blocking. OCR should refrain from providing actors with a built-in excuse in the form of a contrary BAA
provision.
Allowing the BAA to dictate whether actors subject to the Information Blocking rules are permitted to disclose EHI will significantly
undermine the impact of the Cures Act and fails to account for what is often a significant power imbalance in the covered entity/BA
relationship. The Privacy Rule presumes that the covered entity dictates the terms under which a BA can use and disclose PHI, with
the BAA serving the CEs’ needs within the outer boundaries of the Privacy Rule. But in many cases, the BA is the one to dictate the
terms of the relationship – either because that BA has more economic power or is otherwise in a position to set the terms of the
relationship (for example, an HIE that seeks to have all of its provider members sign the same BAA, or a certified EHR vendor setting
the same BAA terms for all of its provider customers). If the BAA is the determining factor for whether a BA– even one covered as an
actor by the Information Blocking rules - is required to share EHI with an individual, it creates a significant end run around the Cures
Act. This is because it provides entities covered by the Information Blocking rules with an easy vehicle for significantly limiting the
purposes for which they will share information with individuals or other requestors, notwithstanding the clear intent of the Cures Act
and the Information Blocking rules to facilitate broader data sharing for permitted purposes than what occurs today. We are already
seeing examples of this, with HIEs declining to share with individuals due to BAA terms. Because the BAA can be – and often is - used
to significantly curtail how a BA can share EHI, Congress could not have intended for OCR to implement the Cures Act by allowing a
contractual vehicle to create obstacles to sharing of EHI otherwise permitted by law. We urge OCR to work with ONC to assure that
the HIPAA rules fully implement the Cures Act and are in sync with Information Blocking provisions.
From a timing perspective, waiting for clarity on these provisions until OCR issues its final rule will significantly delay full
implementation of the Cures Act and Information Blocking rules. OCR has issued helpful guidance to facilitate the sharing of PHI
related to fighting or managing COVID-19. Many actors under the Information Blocking rules (for example, HIEs and certified EHR
vendors) are playing key roles in facilitating the sharing of EHI related to COVID-19. For example, individuals would benefit
42

tremendously from being able to access COVID-19 testing and vaccine information directly from HIEs and certified EHR vendors,
particularly as employers and places of public gathering are increasingly requiring (or leaning toward requiring) proof of vaccine
status. Clarifying that:
1) actors cannot use or rely on BAAs to block requests for information from individuals and
2) actors can be assured that they can share such information with individuals notwithstanding their BAAs
will be critical to assuring that individuals can obtain the necessary information to prove COVID-19 negative or vaccination status.
We urge OCR to issue preliminary guidance promptly in advance of finalizing this rulemaking.
Clarification of Individuals’ Right to Have Information Sent to a “Personal Health Application” (45 CFR 164.501 & 164.524(b)(3) &
page 6468 question (k))
Ciitizen strongly supports OCR’s proposal to include, within the right of access provisions, the right of an individual to receive PHI in
the form and format requested by the individual and interpreting “form and format” to include when individuals want to have
information sent to a personal health tool – identified in the proposed rule as a “personal health application.” We applaud the key
elements of a “personal health application” - chiefly that it “can be drawn from multiple sources” and is “managed, shared, and
controlled by or primarily for the individual and not primarily for a covered entity or another party.” However, we have concerns
about the decision to use the term “personal health application” versus relying on the original term in HITECH, “personal health
record”[4]. Not all tools or services selected by individuals to manage and store their PHI will necessarily be apps, at least as that term
is generally understood. For example, Ciitizen is not a mobile app; we are an online platform accessible by individuals via any web
browser either on a phone or via computer. Many online tools and services will meet the essential prongs of the proposed “personal
health application” definition but will not technically be an “app” accessible from an “app store” hosted by a mobile phone service
provider. To maximize individual choice with respect to the service or tool they choose to store their health information, and to avoid
locking in today’s technologies, we suggest that OCR use the more functional definition of “personal health record” from HITECH or
at a minimum define “personal health application” so that it covers more tools than those that today are commonly recognized as
apps.
43

It is also critical that OCR not limit this definition to only those tools or services that obtain PHI for individuals via connection to a
standards-based API in a covered entity EHR. Today (and for several years into the future), individuals are not able to obtain the
entirety of their PHI in a designated record set through standards-based APIs, so it is essential that OCR recognize tools and services
that help individuals obtain their health information through other mechanisms. Although the proposed regulatory definition does
not contain this limitation, the use of the term “application” and some of the language of the preamble open the door to
HIPAA-covered entities adopting a narrower definition. OCR should be clear that covered entities (and BAs) should honor an
individual’s form and format request as long as the tool or service being used by the individual meets the core elements of the
definition and delivering the copy to the tool or service is “readily producible” by the covered entity or BA.
In the preamble OCR states that a “personal health application,” based on the proposed definition of that term, would not be
covered by the HIPAA Rules. However, OCR should consider the possibility that an individual could choose to use an app offered by a
particular covered entity and seek to have other providers or plans send data to populate that app. We suggest OCR may want to be
clear that the definition does not preclude an individual’s right to have PHI sent to an app offered by a covered entity, as long as that
app otherwise meets the essential prongs of the definition. It is doubtful that a covered entity would decline to send PHI held by that
covered entity to that covered entity’s consumer app - but other covered entities may decline to do so if an app that is covered by
HIPAA cannot meet the definition of “personal health application.”[5]
The preamble also states that individuals (or their personal health representatives) use a personal health application “for the
individuals’ own purposes, such as to monitor their own health status and access their own PHI.” (page 6457) We have concerns that
the use the phrase “for the individuals’ own purposes,” coupled with the limited examples, could be interpreted to limit the
definition to tools or services that are for personal use only. Individuals should be able to use information in a personal health
application or personal health record for any purpose that suits them, including sharing it with others to get treatment
recommendations, sharing it with friends and family members to keep them abreast of what’s going on with their health, sharing it
with social networks, sharing it with a health plan as part of a wellness program, or contributing it to research. As long as the
personal health application or record is “managed, shared, and controlled by or primarily for the individual and not primarily for a
covered entity or another party,” that app, tool, platform, or service should meet the definition versus imposing additional
constraints that the information only be for the individuals’ “personal” use. We urge OCR to provide further clarification when
finalizing this definition.
44

It is critical that the definition of “personal health application” or “personal health record” be sufficiently clear as to apply only in
circumstances where the tool or service has been engaged by the individual, to further distinguish this pathway for individual access
from circumstances where third parties offer apps or tools in order to seek to access PHI largely for their own benefit. The failure to
clarify this definition in a way that minimizes abuse by third parties seeking data primarily for their own business purposes is critical
to enabling individuals to use personal health tools seamlessly to obtain, manage, and control their health information. Today,
Ciitizen is often treated like a third party and not as a tool engaged by the individual, notwithstanding that our policies clearly
provide individuals with the ability to manage, share, and control their health information. When we are treated as a third party, high
fees are often levied for information access. To avoid passing that high fee on to the patient, the patient has to play middleman: first
she must request that her records be sent to her and then she has to make the effort to forward the information to us in order for
that information to be populated in her Ciitizen account. Individuals shouldn’t have to work that hard just to get their health records
into tools or services they have chosen.
OCR further asks what types of activities should be encompassed in the terms, “managed,” “shared,” and “controlled” in the
proposed definition of personal health application/record. Activities that might be considered to be part of an individual managing,
sharing, or controlling his/her personal health application/record should include whether the individual (or a personal
representative): has a choice of whether or not to open an account with the personal health application/record; has unfettered
access to their information in the account; determines with whom the information can be shared and under what circumstances; has
the capability to use (or to facilitate use) of the information in the record for health, wellness, and other personal purposes; and has
the capability to shut down the account and take the information with them or have it deleted.
Definition of “Electronic Health Record” (45 CFR 164.501, page 6468 questions (a)-(h))
In light of the federal district court decision in Ciox v. Azar, OCR has proposed new regulations to replace those established in the
Omnibus Rule implementing the HITECH provision giving individuals the right to have “health-related information” on an individual
from an “electronic health record” sent directly to the third party of their choice. OCR proposes to further refine the definition of
“electronic health record” set forth in HITECH by stating that it includes, “but is not limited to” providers that have a “direct
treatment relationship” with individuals, and to clarify that “health-related information” means the same as the HIPAA-defined term
45

“individually identifiable health information.” (45 CFR 164.501) OCR states in the preamble that “the Department does not propose
to include covered health care providers who have indirect treatment relationships with individuals.” (page 6456) OCR further states
in the preamble that the definition “would not include health-related electronic records of covered health care providers that only
supply durable medical equipment to other providers.” (Id.). OCR also questions whether electronic health records held by health
plans should meet the definition. (page 6462)
HITECH provides a workable definition of “electronic health record” – a digital health record that is “created, gathered, managed, and
consulted by authorized health care clinicians and staff.” [6] OCR does not provide a policy rationale for its further narrowing of this
definition. The right of individuals to access their PHI in a designated record set applies to all covered entities, not just those with
direct treatment relationships, and includes both providers and payers. In the context of an individual exercising their right of access
to have information sent directly to a third party versus the individual having to first get the record herself before sending it to the
third party of her choice, what is the policy rationale for narrowing the definition instead of giving it the broadest possible
interpretation?
OCR may be justifying the exclusion of indirect treatment providers on the assumption that these providers furnish all the health
information that they generate to the direct treatment provider, so individuals may readily access all their information through the
direct treatment provider. However, this assumption is not always accurate. For example, individuals may want a copy of an MRI or
an x-ray. In our experience at Ciitizen, the fastest way – and sometimes the only way – to obtain copies of images is directly from the
radiologist (in addition, imaging centers are often separate from the health care facilities of the ordering providers). The situation
may be worse with respect to providers who furnish and monitor durable medical equipment (DME) such as heart monitors. The
DME provider may have full electronic records of the data recorded by the monitors, while the referring provider may be sent only
summary information or alerts when monitors are malfunctioning. As a result, if the individual can only direct their health
information from the direct treatment provider, the individual could only have a
partial record sent to the third party of their choice. Consistent with the breadth of the right of access as augmented by HITECH (as
well as the court’s opinion in Ciox v. Azar), we urge OCR to interpret “electronic health record” as including the electronic records
consulted not only by direct treatment providers but also by providers who have indirect treatment relationships with individuals.
46

In the Omnibus Rule, OCR relied on this HITECH provision (as well as the original HIPAA statute) to initially provide individuals with
the broad right to have their health information sent to third parties of their choice, which suggests the need to broadly interpret the
“electronic health record” definition so that it matches the scope of HIPAA right of access as closely as possible. In this proposed rule,
OCR has established another pathway for individuals seeking to send information (regardless of whether or not it comes from an
“electronic health record”) to personal health records/applications engaged by the individual, but given the Ciox v. Azar opinion,
some may question whether there is sufficient statutory authority for this regulatory change.[7] As a result, we believe it makes sense
for OCR to interpret the definition of “electronic health record” broadly, in order to more closely match the individual right of access.
We also urge OCR to clarify that the information that an individual can direct to a third party is limited to information in a designated
record set, consistent with the overall HIPAA right of access. We also urge adoption of other provisions (for example, requiring that
requests be submitted in writing – see below) designed to reduce the potential for third party abuse.
Support for the Right to Inspect (45 CFR 164.524(a))
We support the proposed changes in the right of access regulations giving individuals (and their personal representatives) the right
to inspect their PHI, including to take notes, videos, recordings, and photographs, using personal resources. This should be permitted
at the point of care whenever such access would not genuinely risk possible exposure of another individual’s PHI (and OCR should
make clear that such point-of-care access should be provided promptly and not just “within 15 days” (see further comment below on
the proposed changes to the timing of providing individuals with access to their PHI). An oral request should be more than sufficient
for a point of care request, and entities should be required to accept such oral requests (versus being merely permitted to do so).
OCR asks in the preamble if there are circumstances when a covered entity (or BA acting on its behalf) would be allowed to provide
copies in lieu of inspection. Ciitizen is hard pressed to think of such a circumstance – but if such circumstances exist, providers
should not be permitted to charge fees for the copies, since the individual requested inspection, for which fees would not be
permitted.
Timeliness of Response to Individual Requests (45 CFR 164.524(b)(2)(i) and page 6468 question (n), page 6469 questions (r) & (s))
Ciitizen is pleased to see the proposal to adjust the deadline for entities to respond to HIPAA right of access requests from 30-60 days
to 15-30 days. On average, Ciitizen users (with our assistance) are able to obtain their records in two weeks on average – but too
often entities take almost the full 30 days or beyond to produce records for users. We also support the proposal to allow entities to
47

take beyond 15 days IF they have a policy in place (and that policy is in effect) whereby individuals needing their health information
more quickly than 15 days (such as for second opinions to treat a serious illness or to try to meet eligibility requirements for a clinical
trial, or to meet a benefits application deadline) can have those requests processed more quickly. We recognize that such a provision
might require individuals to specify the purpose of their requests, but we think it makes sense that individuals seeking information
for an urgent purpose would need to at least generally indicate that purpose in order to justify prioritizing that request over others in
the queue, as long as the default for most requests is that individuals are not required to indicate any particular purpose. We also
support OCR’s policy position that time spent by the entity in clarifying an individual’s request does not stop the clock regarding
meeting the 15-day maximum.
Although we understand OCR’s desire to maintain a medium-neutral Privacy Rule (to the extent possible), a 15 day timeframe is
much more time than should be needed to produce records held electronically (hybrid – combination of paper and digital – requests
may take longer, at least for the paper portion). The adoption of even a 15-day expected turnaround time for all PHI puts the Privacy
Rule well behind expectations set by the Information Blocking and Interoperability Rules (from ONC, and with respect to the latter,
from CMS), at least with respect to EHI. We also believe requests for inspection – particularly when an individual seeks to examine,
or even take photos of or record, their PHI while at an office visit or while hospitalized – should be processed far more quickly than
15 days. We strongly support that OCR has worded the proposed regulation so that it requires entities to respond to individual access
requests “as soon as practicable, but no later than 15 days” (emphasis added) (again, with the possibility of extension – largely
intended for paper records – if the entity has a policy in place that facilitates expediting individual requests). This regulatory
language should be interpreted to create an expectation that an entity will respond much sooner than 15 days when it is
“practicable” for them to do so, similar to OCR’s expectations regarding notification of individuals in the event of a breach of PHI. If
this is what OCR intended, and that OCR could enforce a failure of an entity to respond within 15 days when it clearly was practicable
for them to do so, we urge OCR to make it much clearer (either in the preamble or in the regulations or both) so that the new 15-day
standard does not become the default for all requests.
Prohibiting Unreasonable Measures for Individuals Seeking Records (45 CFR 164.524(b)(1)(ii) & 164.514(b)(2)(v), and page 6470
question (ll))
48

We strongly support OCR’s proposal to expressly prohibit entities from imposing unreasonable measures for individual access. These
measures are typically imposed out of convenience to the entity and, to some degree, to give the entity greater assurance that the
individual requesting the information is authorized to receive it. But regardless of the benign intent of these measures, they often
impose significant obstacles to individuals seeking to access their health information or on personal representatives seeking to access
information of a loved one.
In sourcing records for our users, we have found the following to be among the biggest obstacles:
● Entities requiring individuals to come “in person” to obtain records (guidance currently addresses this).
● Entities requiring individuals to submit requests either by mail or by fax (guidance currently allows; mail delays entity
receipt of requests, and many individuals don’t have access to fax machines).
● Entities not accepting a “digital” signature, making it challenging for individuals to submit remote requests.
● Entities requiring individuals to use the entity’s particular request form, which can be a burden even when that form is
short and is not the lengthier HIPAA-compliant authorization.
● Entities imposing high fees for copies (see separate section below regarding fees).
● Entities requiring that requests be submitted and processed through a portal or API.
● Entities requiring that signatures be notarized, particularly when the requester is a personal representative.
● Entities imposing unreasonable verification requirements (in our experience, this occurs mostly through the refusal to
accept digital signatures).
● Entities refusing to e-mail records to individuals, even after individuals accept the security risks of unsecure email
transmission.
49

The proposed rule expressly includes a “non-exhaustive” list of unreasonable measures. We support this proposal, although we do
wonder whether – notwithstanding the labeling of the list as “nonexhaustive” – it will be difficult to have other measures that are
not expressly listed still be considered to be unreasonable. Nevertheless, we agree with the approach of providing more clarity in the
regulations themselves, since regulations are a stronger source of authority, and entities are frequently less aware of guidance, which
tends to be created episodically and therefore exists in multiple places.
We support that OCR has included several of the obstacles identified above in the list of obstacles that are determined to be
“unreasonable” – including requiring individuals to come in person, prohibiting the use of a lengthy request form, prohibiting
requirements for notarized signatures, and prohibiting requirements that requests be submitted or processed through a portal or
API. We also appreciate that OCR is making clear in the regulations that individuals can obtain their health information by e-mail
(presumably without the need for the individual to acknowledge a light security warning). With respect to prohibiting imposition of a
notary requirement, we urge OCR to be clear that this also applies to requests submitted by personal representatives; we have seen
entities requiring notarization when attesting to personal representative status (such as familial relationship, or legal representative
status for a decedent). Such personal representatives should not be required to meet a higher standard for identity proofing, beyond
also needing to demonstrate their personal representative status with appropriate documentation.
We strongly urge OCR to prohibit entities from insisting that individuals use their own forms, even when those forms are short. When
individuals need to gather PHI from multiple places, and to submit rerequest forms in order to make sure their records are
up-to-date, having to submit a different form for each location is a burden. Entities should be required to accept any writing from an
individual that meets HIPAA (and any applicable state law[8]) requirements – i.e., that includes information needed from the
individual to match to records (name, demographic information like date of birth and address), and records sought) versus requiring
individuals to complete the entity’s standard, even lightweight form.[9] Those forms should be offered as assistance to individuals
seeking their records, not as requirements. Typically, users of Ciitizen have 2-4 providers from whom they are seeking records;
individuals with rare disorders on our platform make requests from an average of 7 providers. In general, we have found that most
providers will accept a well-crafted request letter versus insisting that individuals use the provider’s own form – but some health care
providers do insist on their own forms, and it definitely creates unnecessary hassle in obtaining records. Insisting on a particular form
is “form over substance” and is another way that entities create unreasonable obstacles for individuals seeking their PHI.
50

Too many entities still do not accept requests from individuals by simple e-mail. There is nothing in the HIPAA Privacy Rule that
prohibits entities from collecting information from individuals (patients, subscribers) in ways that are convenient for the individual.
Today, the only digital pathway that a covered entity is required (either by regulation or guidance) to open to individuals requesting
their records is fax. Most requests Ciitizen sends for its users are submitted by fax – but individuals often do not have faxing
capabilities. Entities should be required to accept remote access requests from individuals, including by e-mail.
Today the Privacy Rule, consistent with the federal e-SIGN Act, allows entities to accept requests from individuals using a digital
signature – but entities are not required to accept a digital signature, which imposes an obstacle to individuals submitting requests
remotely. Today, digital signatures are widely acceptable for a broad range of transactions, including establishing bank accounts and
nearly every step of a home purchase up to the point of executing closing documents (for example, I submitted the initial offer on a
home purchase using Docusign (and that offer was accepted using Docusign); every contract we execute binding Ciitizen, including
employment and nondisclosure agreements, is done using Docusign). Refusal to accept a digital signature creates an unreasonable
obstacle to individuals trying to access their records.
Finally, we strongly support the express prohibition on the imposition of unreasonable identity verification requirements, but we
have concerns that the proposed definition of an unreasonable identity verification measure – one that requires “an individual to
expend unnecessary effort or expense when a less burdensome verification measure is practicable for the particular covered entity”
– is unlikely to result in much clarity or change in behavior. Most identity verification measures are imposed as risk reduction
measures for the entity, which means they may define less burdensome measures that potentially increase some risk for them as
“not practicable.” We urge OCR to provide more guidance on steps that would be acceptable. For example, is asking individuals to
provide a copy of their driver’s license (and a birth certificate, in the context of a parent lawfully requesting records of a minor)
sufficient? Is it sufficient if a personal health record/application attests to identity proofing using a vendor used by banks or other
financial services tools for the opening of accounts remotely? Is it sufficient to identity proof using methods calculated to meet or
come close to meeting NIST level of assurance 2 (or conversely, would rejecting this approach constitute an unreasonable measure)?
Would a text message, e-mail, or phone call (using information on record with the covered entity) to confirm that the individual
made the request be sufficient? Are portal credentials initially granted to an individual sufficient for access through the app or
portal? Additional guidance on this topic from OCR will be extraordinarily helpful, both to entities seeking to comply with the rules
but also to the public in terms of what they may need to submit in order to obtain records, particularly in remote request scenarios.

Clarification of Fee Provisions (45 CFR 164.524(b)(4) & (d)(6) and page 6469 questions (aa), (dd), (ee),
(ff), & (hh), and page 6470 question (ii))
We strongly support the clarification that the individual cannot be charged in circumstances where they request to inspect their PHI
in person or when records are sent using an “internet-based method” to the individuals, to a personal health record/application, or,
when coming from an “electronic health record,” to another health care provider or to any third party. We further ask OCR for
additional clarification on what constitutes an “internet-based method.” We recommend alignment with ONC’s Information Blocking
rules, which prohibit fees to individuals (or apps or services acting on their behalf) for “electronic access,” which typically involves
little to no manual effort to send the record (fees for search and retrieval, in even in an electronic context, have long been prohibited
by the Privacy Rule).
Ciitizen also supports the proposed regulatory requirement that entities post fees for individual access, as well as for third party
requests with an individual’s valid authorization. We also support inclusion in the proposed regulation that individuals be provided
with a fee estimate and itemization of charges (currently in guidance but not part of existing regulatory text).
We further urge OCR to be clearer in the regulations regarding the ability of entities to charge up to the maximums established in
state law. At Ciitizen we frequently see individuals still being charged state law rates, notwithstanding extensive guidance from OCR
on this topic. In particular, state law rates are still frequently applied to requests for paper records. There is a conflict in OCR’s
guidance between what OCR issued in 2016 - making clear that fees for paper copies must be established in accordance with HIPAA
regulations, and that state law rates, often calculated on a per page basis, may result in charges that are significantly higher than
what HIPAA allows – and prior guidance from OCR in 2000 indicating that state law per page rates would be considered to meet
HIPAA requirements for paper records.[10] Consequently, it is critical for OCR establish more definitive regulatory provisions on this
topic. It is not unusual for individuals to be charged hundreds of dollars for their records, calculated based on state law maximums.[11]
OCR should also consider clarification in the regulations of fee provisions as applied to the provision of images to individuals. Images
are digital files – frequently very large digital files - yet often cannot be sent to the individual via e-mail due to their size. In our
52

experience, entities either provide these to individuals for free – or the charges can be quite high. Entities need to be reminded that
requests under the individual right of access are subject to fee limitations, including when those images are to be sent to a personal
health record/application or to a third party (assuming the definition of “electronic health record” is modified to be clear that it
includes records kept by imaging providers). It is possible for entities to establish fee schedules based on the amount of time
required to upload images, plus (where applicable) any necessary supplies.
In general, given that the proposed rule carves up the right of access into different “sub-rights” – the right of inspection, the right to
access of PHI regardless of format and source (which includes the right to send to the personal health record/application of an
individual’s choosing), the right to have information from an EHR sent to a third party of the individual’s choosing – it is now more
challenging to follow which fee provisions apply to which types of access. The chart on page 6465 is helpful – but doesn’t account
for all of the different pathways to access. We urge OCR to issue further helpful charts and roadmaps to help individuals and entities
covered by the Privacy Rule to better navigate this landscape. A chart we developed with AHIMA can be found in Appendix 1.
Requirement to Accept Oral Requests from Individuals to Direct Information in an EHR to 3rd Parties (45 CFR 164.524(d)(1) and
page 6469 questions (q), (t), and (v))
HITECH established the right of individuals to have EHI from an EHR sent to the third party of that individual’s choice, as long as the
information was “clear, conspicuous, and specific.” Under the Omnibus Rule, OCR interpreted this HITECH requirement to require a
written request from an individual. Now OCR is proposing to require entities to accept “clear, conspicuous, and specific” oral
requests from individuals.
Although in general we are supportive of any efforts that are designed to make it easier for individuals to access their PHI – including
to send it to the third party of their choice – we have concerns about the potential for abuse when entities are required to process
requests to third party designees that are submitted verbally. Historically, OCR minimized the potential for abuse by requiring the
execution of HIPAA-compliant authorizations any time a third-party was requesting an individual’s PHI. Although one can never be
sure that an individual has read through the components of the authorization and fully understands it, at least the opportunity for
transparency is present (and the entity has a clear record of having received the individual’s authorization).
53

In this case, the potential for the recipient of this data to claim that the individual is requesting the information is arguably greater
when these requests are presented verbally and are required to be honored with simply a verbal request. These concerns are not
alleviated when the requests for information are to be submitted by the recipient health care provider or plan. How can the record
holder be sure that this is actually a request coming from an individual (particularly in a circumstance where we are also seeking to
relax identity verification requirements to make them more accessible to individuals)? Although providers and health plans already
can share PHI with one another through one or more of the “treatment, payment, or health care operations provisions,” the
conditions for one of those provisions must be met or an individual’s written authorization would be required to disclose PHI. In this
case, by simply claiming the verbal request of an individual, PHI could be shared, regardless of the purpose for the disclosure - which
has high potential for abuse. We support that individuals should be allowed to submit only an oral request for inspection of their
records (and in this circumstance, the entity should be required to accept that request) – but for information directed to any third
parties, it is imperative to take measures to assure that the requests are coming from individuals. Requiring that such requests to
have information sent to third parties be in writing does not seem like too great of an obstacle, particularly in light of the potential
risks. Individuals obtaining or directing information themselves (or via an app) from a portal or API should not have to provide
additional documentation of a request, as this is information that is coming to the individual, via a form/format (personal health
record/application) chosen by the individual.
Queries by a Health Care Provider to an HIE for Individual Access Requests (page 6469, question (y))
OCR also specifically asks whether broadcast queries by a health care provider (presumably also by a health plan) to an HIE in
response to a request from an individual would be permissible as a customer service activity under health care operations. We
support this interpretation. However, OCR should instead consider whether, under a combination of HIPAA and the Information
Blocking rules, providers should be required to submit such queries absent application of a legal exemption (beyond contractual
prohibitions). No doubt a covered entity must obtain information from a BA - such as an HIE - in order to comply with an individual’s
request for information where the information required to be provided by the covered entity is held by the BA. But where
information in an HIE is often not separable by provider/health plan source – and is all information subject to the HIPAA right of
access – and where providers are easily able to query all of this information for treatment purposes, OCR and ONC should explore
viable avenues for further leveraging interoperability as one pathway to facilitate easier individual access to health information.
(Given that not all providers are connected to HIEs, it is still critical for OCR to modify the Privacy Rule to assure that HIEs can
54

facilitate providing information directly to patients, consistent with the Cures Act and the Information Blocking rules, versus patients
having to rely on their providers to query an HIE.)
Warning Individuals Regarding Use of a Personal Health Record/Application (page 6469, questions (o) & (p))
OCR specifically asks whether entities should be required to provide individuals with notice about the risks of transmission to third
parties from an electronic health record. While we do not think such notices should be required (particularly if steps are taken to try
to curb potential abuses by third parties, such as requiring a written request from the individual), entities should be permitted to
provide such notice as long as they are doing so in a way that does not create unreasonable obstacles to individual access. Notices
that are overly long and complicated, that are unduly alarming (emphasizing only risks and not potential benefits), or that require an
individual to take an extraordinary number of steps (clicking several boxes) are edging into creating unreasonable obstacles, and this
is particularly the case where the individual is seeking to have their information sent to a personal health record/application.[12]
(Frankly, in the latter case, education of individuals is far more timely when it takes place before the individual chooses the record or
app, not when the individual is trying to get their health information into a record or app they have already chosen.) But some
transparency in the context of third party transmissions could be beneficial, including to make sure that the individual authorized the
transaction (something which could not take place in a circumstance where a health care provider or plan is seeking records from an
EHR and verbally claiming that they are doing so for the individual, at least as currently proposed by OCR).
Miscellaneous questions from OCR regarding the HIPAA Right of Access
OCR has asked a number of questions relevant to its proposed right of access modifications, some of which we have answered in the
comments above. We also offer a few thoughts on some of the other access-related questions below:
• Re: intersection with other interoperability programs/laws (page 6468 question (i)): We support the proposed regulatory
provision requiring entities to honor a form/format request from an individual in circumstances where that form/format is
consistent with technology requirements either imposed on that entity or on the entity’s technology vendor (for example,
requirements on certified health information technology vendors). We agree this means turning on the functionalities
provided with already-purchased technology, so that individuals can take advantage of them. At the same time,
individuals should not be required to use these functionalities to access their health information if that is not their
55

preferred form/format. Requirements by these vendors that personal health records/applications “register” with the
vendor must be done in a way that does not exclude a personal health record/application chosen by an individual unless it
would raise genuine security risks for the vendor or its covered entity (or BA) customer.
• Summary in lieu of copies (45 CFR 164.524(c)(2)(iv)(B)): We support OCR’s proposals to require that when individuals are
offered a summary in lieu of copies of their PHI, entities are required to inform the individuals that they retain their rights
to get copies of their PHI if a summary would not meet their needs.
