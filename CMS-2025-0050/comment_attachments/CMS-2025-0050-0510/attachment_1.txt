Executive Summary
The Problem
Healthcare costs in the U.S. are projected to reach $5 trillion annually by the end of 2026, according to
CMS.1 A significant portion of this cost stems from inefficiencies related to fraud, lack of interoperability,
information blocking, and poor data quality. These systemic challenges hinder the ability to deliver
effective, efficient care and must be addressed to support Secretary Kennedy’s initiative to “Make
America Healthy Again.” The Secretary has stated that he “will provide Americans with transparency
and access to all the data so they can make informed choices for themselves and their families.”2
How federated, Portable Digital Identity can help
A federated digital identity solution offers a powerful approach to unlocking transparency and
access to data — especially if it is grounded in TEFCA, OMB M-19-17, and National Institute of
Standards & Technology Special Publication (NIST SP) 800-63-3, Digital Identity Guidelines
(AAL2/IAL2). Across 20 federal agencies, 46 state agencies, and over 30 EHR’s, ID.me has consistently
shown the benefits of this model. We are the longest-tenured holder of Kantara certification, the only
FedRAMP authorized provider to offer three (3) pathways to verification, and have already credentialed
more than 74 Million Americans to NIST AAL2/IAL2 standards. These “preverified” users can simply
login with ID.me, complete MFA, and consent to release their existing credential to a new agency, with
pass rates of 99%+. Compared to algorithm-only solutions, ID.me has doubled access rates, generated
hundreds of millions in operational efficiencies, and played a critical role in preventing $273 billion in
fraudulent unemployment insurance payments during the pandemic.3
The IRS, the VA, and SSA currently rely on federated identity models that include ID.me and Login.gov
as credential service providers (CSPs). If CMS were to follow suit – and demonstrate the benefits to the
rest of the healthcare industry – the benefits of the federated identity approach would compound for
providers and patients alike. For example, a beneficiary that uses an ID.me credential at Social Security to
begin the application for Medicare could use that same credential to manage benefits at Medicare.gov,
The beneficiary experiences less friction and CMS would have more certainty that the person creating a
Medicare.gov account is in fact the same individual that Social Security determined to be eligible for
Medicare. This approach eliminates friction for millions of existing credential-holders and strengthens
data quality and record matching across agencies. In regard to providers, ID.me protects the National
Provider Data Bank at HRSA, the National Board of Medical Examiners, and 30% of the nation’s
electronic prescriptions.
Benefits for CMS, Medicare, and Medicaid
When leveraging ID.me, CMS will significantly enhance online access and transition from a reactive "pay
and chase" model to a proactive "prevent and detect" framework. ID.me’s user-centric digital wallet
model is already in-use across federal, state, and private sector healthcare entities and has successfully
participated in pilot programs under HHS and the CARIN Alliance.
Incorporating the ID.me login button on the CMS beneficiary and provider-facing websites enables these
74 Million pre-verified users to seamlessly share their credentials without additional verification steps.
This integration delivers a streamlined user experience, enhances security for both users and CMS,
accelerates system deployment, improves data quality, and enables robust identity management while

Recommendations
ID.me’s immediate and specific recommendations to CMS are to:
1. Encourage sector-wide adoption of portable, NIST-conformant, FedRAMP authorized
digital credentials by adopting them at CMS and demonstrating the benefits. SSA (an entry
point for Medicare) and the VA (the largest integrated health system in the U.S.) both offer access
via federated identities: ID.me and Login.gov. More than 74M citizens are already using ID.me to
access these agencies and their records. Re-use of these credentials at Medicare.gov and
Healthcare.gov would increase American’s access to their health records, reduce onboarding
friction, increase security, and improve care quality.
2. Require CSPs to deploy advanced fraud controls capable of stopping emerging threats from
generative AI (e.g. deepfakes). NIST 800-63-3 is an effective standard, but it is also a ten year
old standard. Threats from generative AI are evolving faster than federal guidelines can, and so it
is up to CSPs to stay ahead of emerging threats through biometrics and their own AI.
3. Require capabilities beyond NIST conformance that are critical to managing business user
applications: ability to manage delegated access, and have a unified identity graph with
continuous trust monitoring. CMS, payers, and providers interact with a broad range of
personas and relationships: patients, providers, clinical staff, caretakers, and plan managers. CSPs
that can support delegated and relationship-based access control models will enable more secure
transactions across personas.
4. Update Interoperability & Patient Access Rule. ID.me recommends updating the
Interoperability and Patient Access Rule to mandate use of third-party credentials that meet
federal policies and assurance standards like the Office of Management and Budget (OMB)
Memorandum 19-174, NIST SP 800-63, and NIST 800-53 (i.e. FedRAMP).

Today, a citizen is able to log into the SSA, using their ID.me credential, and apply for eligibility for

and effort expended by the beneficiary. It would also help with deterministic record matching across
agency back-end systems between CMS and SSA.

d. How would encouraging the use of CSPs improve access to health information?
Leading CSPs can improve both access to health information and the quality of that information.
● Access to information. ID.me has been shown to increase access to online services by 2x relative
to algorithm-only solutions and 3x for underserved populations. Once access is granted, adoption
of digital services increases. We expect the same to be true for beneficiaries and patients viewing
health information and managing care, as we see at the VA today.
○ Quality of the information. CSPs can improve interoperability by reducing record
duplication and fragmentation. Portable NIST credentials can do this because they
eliminate reliance on probabilistic matching and replace it with deterministic matching of
records. Deterministic matching occurs when truly unique identifiers deliver a definitive
match to a patient’s identity, validated by their real-time control of the digital
session—through methods like biometrics or live video—and enforced within a
single-tenant identity system to ensure the identity hasn’t already been claimed. This
reduces risk of HIPAA violations in response to records queries because it is a binary
“yes/no” for the record match rather than a sliding score. This improved record matching
and easier transfer of records means patients will have more complete clinical
information aggregated into a single record.
For AAL2/IAL2 identity proofing, ID.me enforces uniqueness per NIST guidelines for identity resolution
by assigning a Unique Identifier (UUID) to each account based on Name, Date of Birth (DOB), and
Social Security Number (or another unique identifier, such as a passport or national ID number). This
“rule of three” ensures a 1:1 relationship between a user and their ID.me UUID, preventing fraud and
unauthorized access. Once a user verifies at IAL2 and adds an AAL2 passkey, no one else can claim
their identity.

● Increased adoption of digital services: With higher access rates and a familiar login experience,
federated digital identities from ID.me have been shown to increase adoption of digital services.
This eases the burden on in-person administrative activities at hospitals and clinics. At the
Virginia Employment Commission, claims submitted digitally increased by 173% vs. paper
submissions once ID.me was offered as a login option on the claims portal.
● Better fraud Prevention: A shared authentication service across providers can see how threat
actors are targeting multiple providers using the same stolen identities and block fraudulent users’

Seven (7) workforce agencies are on the record crediting ID.me with helping to prevent over

● Improved Data Quality. CSPs support interoperability by enforcing uniqueness per NIST
