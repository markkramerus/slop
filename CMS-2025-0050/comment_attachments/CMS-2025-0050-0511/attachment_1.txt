Prior Authorization
The administrative burdens associated with prior authorization contribute to
major delays and denials of necessary, appropriate—and in many cases,
lifesaving—care. Prior authorization is consistently identified as the largest
barrier to care for insured patients. Providers and patients have offered to
collaborate on reforms to prior authorization processes, but these calls have
largely gone unheard.
The Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final rule (CMS-0057) was a critical
step in ensuring that patient care takes priority over paperwork. Far too often
necessary medical services are delayed due to excessive and burdensome prior
authorization processes. Ensuring there is a single, easy-to-access electronic
process in place to file forms and receive approvals within federal health plans

is a welcome change. Timely access to care is critical for people with cancer and ASCO hopes this will
help alleviate delays and lessen providers’ administrative burden.
However, we are disappointed that the Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final rule does not apply
to physician administered drugs—which will continue to be an administrative burden to clinicians and
their patients. CMS stated in the proposed rule that drugs were excluded from the interoperability
requirements “because the processes and standards for prior authorization applicable to drugs differ
from the other ‘items and services’ for which we propose regulation.”1 However, the National Council
for Prescription Drug Programs (NCPDP) Telecommunication Standard Implementation Guide Version
D.0 for retail pharmacy drugs to which CMS was referring applies only to Part D drugs, leaving Part B
drugs without standards.
Urgent relief is needed now, and the additional delay risks further harming patient access to cancer
care. A significant portion of cancer treatment requires patient access to life-saving drugs and the prior
authorization requirements associated with these drugs are extremely burdensome for oncologists and
their patients. The omission of drugs from this rule is deeply concerning and provides no relief for the
provider and their patients, who will continue to experience delays in obtaining necessary therapy.
On page 29 of the display copy of the final rule, CMS states “we will consider options for future
rulemaking to address improvements to the prior authorization processes for drugs.”2 We strongly urge
CMS to establish an electronic prior authorization process for Part B drugs as soon as possible.
Real Time Benefit Tools
ASCO supports real time benefit tools (RTBTs) that would allow enrollees to view accurate, clinically
appropriate, real-time formulary and benefit information. Lack of prescription formulary transparency at
the point of prescribing leads to challenges for patients, physicians, and pharmacists at the pharmacy
counter when a patient’s medication requires prior authorization, is not covered by their plan, or comes
with a high co-pay. More concerning, patients facing these challenges may completely abandon
treatment, leading to medication adherence issues and worsening health outcomes. Requiring certified
vendors to support standard RTBT technology will enable informed conversations between physicians
and patients during medication selection and ameliorate many of the problems listed above.
RTBTs allow providers and their patients to compare the patient-specific cost of a drug to that of a
suitable alternative, compare prescription costs at different pharmacy locations, view information about
out-of-pocket costs, and learn whether a specific drug is subject to utilization management restrictions
such as prior authorization, step therapy, or quantity limits. Increasing physician access to these high-

Bulk FHIR Exports
Using Bulk FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) data exports from Electronic Health
Records (EHRs) to CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services) for clinical quality data submissions
presents a transformative opportunity with significant pros and cons. While the concept of leveraging
Bulk FHIR data exports for CMS quality data submission offers theoretical advantages, ASCO has
significant reservations regarding its immediate feasibility and potential implications.
Arguably the most notable benefit is to reduce provider burden. Automated extraction and submission
of large datasets would drastically reduce the manual effort, chart abstraction, and administrative time
currently spent by providers on quality reporting. Instead of generating custom reports for each quality
measure, EHRs could export data in a standardized FHIR format, making the process more efficient and
less disruptive to clinical workflows. Data submitted via Bulk FHIR could potentially be leveraged for
multiple quality programs and reporting requirements, further minimizing redundant efforts. Bulk FHIR
can facilitate more frequent data updates, allowing providers to monitor performance and identify care
gaps more proactively, rather than waiting for retrospective reporting cycles.
In theory, Bulk FHIR could improve data quality and accuracy. FHIR provides a standardized way to
represent healthcare data, leading to greater consistency and reducing errors that arise from disparate
data formats. With automated extraction, there's a higher likelihood of capturing all relevant data
elements for a given quality measure. Eliminating manual data entry and manipulation significantly
lowers the risk of human error. CMS could gain a more comprehensive and granular understanding of
population health trends, allowing for better policy decisions and targeted interventions. Access to
standardized, bulk data would allow CMS to perform quicker and more sophisticated analyses of quality
performance across the healthcare system. With richer data, CMS could provide more meaningful
benchmarks to providers, helping them identify areas for improvement.
The availability of standardized, granular data could also facilitate the development of more
sophisticated and outcome-focused quality measures. Bulk FHIR could be the foundational component
for the shift to digital quality measures, enabling the creation of fully electronic, computable measures.
Medical societies have the expertise and access to providers to develop evidence-based clinical quality
measures; however, they experience substantial challenges accessing the data CMS has via the ResDAC
(Research Data Assistance Center). It is laborious and very expensive. A more efficient and less costly
system would need to be put in place.
Despite the potential to reduce administrative burden and facilitate digital quality measures, ASCO is
not confident that Bulk FHIR data exports should be provided to CMS for quality reporting for data
security and privacy reasons. Transferring massive amounts of sensitive Protected Health Information
(PHI) in bulk increases the attack surface for cyber threats and data breaches. Robust encryption, access

controls, and compliance with HIPAA are paramount. While FHIR supports de-identification, ensuring
complete de-identification of large, complex datasets while maintaining data utility for quality
measurement can be challenging. While certified EHRs are mandated to support FHIR APIs, the full
capabilities for bulk export and the robustness of these implementations can vary. Some EHR vendors
may require significant upgrades or development. Both CMS and providers need substantial IT
infrastructure, including high-performance servers, robust network bandwidth, and storage capacity to
handle the volume of data. This represents a significant financial investment. Integrating Bulk FHIR
exports with existing reporting systems and data warehouses can be complex, especially for legacy
systems.
Data quality and standardization challenges persist. FHIR standardizes the format of data, but it doesn't
automatically fix underlying data quality issues within the EHR. If the data within the EHR is inconsistent,
incomplete, or poorly documented, the FHIR export will reflect those issues. While FHIR aims for
standardization, variations in how clinical concepts are captured and coded across different EHRs may
still require significant effort in data cleaning, mapping, and transformation to ensure comparability for
quality measure calculation. Ensuring clinicians consistently document data in a structured, FHIR-
compliant way within their daily workflows is crucial, but can be a persistent challenge.
There are implementation complexities that require technical expertise. Organizations need skilled
personnel with expertise in FHIR standards, data management, security, and system integration to
effectively implement and maintain Bulk FHIR pipelines. Establishing clear governance frameworks for
data sharing, consent management, and ensuring the ethical use of large-scale health data is critical.
While designed for bulk data, processing and transferring extremely large datasets can still lead to
latency and performance issues if systems are not optimally designed and scaled.
Should CMS move forward with bulk FHIR data exports, it is imperative to conduct pilot testing with EHR
vendors to assess the current state of data quality and the true feasibility of this centralized model. CMS
must also consider the impact on small and rural practices. Their capacity for technological adoption and
integration often lags behind larger institutions. A clear plan would be needed to support these
practices in transitioning to more modern data submission methodologies.
Interoperability and Quality Reporting
Investing in robust FHIR-based APIs and ensuring data aligns with the United States Core Data for
Interoperability (USCDI) are important steps in improving interoperability and quality reporting. FHIR
provides the "how" for data exchange, and USCDI defines the "what" (the core data elements). When
clinical data is consistently captured and exchanged using FHIR and USCDI, it becomes much more
amenable to automated extraction and calculation for quality measures. This eliminates the need for
manual chart abstraction or bespoke data transformations for each reporting program. If an EHR system
is truly interoperable and can reliably export patient data in a standardized, computable format, then
the effort to generate quality reports shifts from data gathering and formatting to simply running
predefined queries against that standardized dataset.
