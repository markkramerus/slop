2.2 Data Access and Integration
2.2.1 From Fragmented Data to Actionable Knowledge
The health data most readily available and valuable to patients and caregivers include
medications, diagnoses, lab results, and imaging reports, as well as comprehensive,
interrelated visit summaries (PC-8). HIPAA guarantees individuals the right to access their

information immediately accessible.
Despite this technical accessibility, a fundamental challenge persistsâ€”patient health data
remains fragmented across providers, payers, and legacy systems (PC-8). Information is
siloed and poorly integrated, lacking the functional and logical (contextual) interoperability
needed to create meaningful, actionable insights for informed healthcare decisions.
The Current Reality
Current systems fail to deliver what consumers need for informed healthcare decisions, as
patient information remains scattered across multiple touchpoints without proper
integration or context (PC-8). Information that exists in isolationâ€”a lab value here, a
prescription there, a diagnosis code elsewhereâ€”provides limited utility for understanding
overall health status or making informed care decisions.
High-Value Data Categories
Beyond basic clinical records, social determinants of health (SDOH)â€”such as housing
status, food security, transportation access, education, and employmentâ€”represent
some of the highest-value data sources for comprehensive patient understanding (PC-8b).
Additionally, patient-reported outcomes, functional assessments, and behavioral health
indicators provide crucial context that traditional clinical data often misses.
The Path Forward
The transformation requires moving from data availability to data usabilityâ€”creating
integrated, contextualized information that empowers patients and caregivers to make
truly informed healthcare decisions (PC-8). This means not just providing access to data
but delivering actionable knowledge that connects individual health information to broader
health outcomes and care options.

In a free-market healthcare system, patientsâ€”not providers, payers, or EHR vendorsâ€”own

2.2.4 Transforming Data Architecture for Consumer Empowerment
True patient empowerment requires sophisticated data representation architecture that
goes beyond simple connectivity. Many assume that structured data automatically
provides meaning, but the confusion between structural interoperability (how data is
stored), logical interoperability (how data is understood), and knowledge management
(how knowledge is captured, and information is derived and utilized) leads to major
challenges in health and health-related data exchange, integration, and reasoning. There is
important interplay between structure and semantics. It is important to know why
databases are simply containers (current EHRs), knowledge bases (graphs) capture
information, and knowledge management systems support reasoning and decision-
making.
2.3 Interoperability and Clinical Research Integration
Improving accessibility, interoperability, and integration of clinical data requires a
foundational shift in how we structure and interpret health information. The modern
healthcare digital landscape is tightly interconnected, yet interoperability and knowledge
management are often discussed but not always well understood. (PC-8c).
2.3.1 Key Challenges
Information Blocking and Regulatory Barriers: Despite federal regulations like the 21st
Century Cures Act, information blocking remains a significant challenge. Healthcare
organizations sometimes resist data sharing due to competitive concerns, technical
limitations, or compliance uncertainties.
Technical and Structural Limitations: Much of today's health data exists in unstructured
formats or proprietary systems that resist integration. Many assume that structured data
automatically equals interoperable data, but true interoperability requires semantic
understandingâ€”not just technical connectivity. Legacy systems often lack modern APIs,
8

and even FHIR-compliant systems may interpret data standards differently. Current legacy
EHR platforms demonstrate no operational logical data interoperability.
Data Quality and Standardization: Inconsistent data quality, varying terminologies, and
lack of standardized coding across systems create barriers to meaningful integration.
Clinical data often lacks the context and metadata necessary for research applications.
2.3.2 Key Opportunities
Advanced Standards Implementation: Widespread adoption of FHIR R4 and other
modern interoperability standards creates opportunities for real-time data exchange and
integration. When properly implemented, these standards enable seamless data flow
between previously incompatible systems.
AI-Powered Data Integration: Machine learning and natural language processing
technologies can help extract meaning from unstructured clinical notes, radiology reports,
and other text-heavy data sources, making previously inaccessible information available
for enhanced patient-level insights as well as clinically relevant research.
Patient-Mediated Information Exchange: Empowering patients to control and direct their
health data creates new pathways for data integration across care settings. Patient-
directed exchange can overcome some institutional barriers to data sharing.
Real-World Evidence Generation: Integrated clinical data enables more robust real-world
evidence studies, supporting regulatory decision-making and clinical guideline
development. The combination of claims data, clinical records, and patient-reported
outcomes creates a more complete patient-level picture for both clinical and operational
outcomes improvement and advanced real-world research.
2.3.3 Strategic Recommendations
1. Semantic Interoperability Focus: Move beyond technical connectivity to ensure data
maintains meaning and context across systems.
2. Knowledge Management Systems: Implement platforms that do not just store data
but create actionable clinical knowledge.
3. Privacy-Preserving Technologies: Leverage technologies like federated learning and
differential privacy to enable research while protecting patient confidentiality.
4. Stakeholder Alignment: Create incentive structures that encourage data sharing while
addressing legitimate competitive and privacy concerns.
The ultimate opportunity lies in creating a healthcare ecosystem where clinical data flows
seamlessly to support both individual patient care and population health management,
generating evidence that continuously improves care quality and outcomes across the
entire healthcare system.

2.4 Information Blocking and Digital Identity
An increase in reported information blocking incidents can serve as a critical mechanism
for identifying and addressing entities that hinder the exchange of health information. Early
identification of such actors enables timely course correction and promotes broader
compliance with federal interoperability standards.
To support this effort, CMS could strengthen patient awareness and engagement by
incorporating clear, patient-facing prompts that encourage the reporting of information
blocking. These could be included in:
ď‚· Explanation of Benefits (EOB) statements
ď‚· Medicare handbooks or enrollment materials
ď‚· Digital platforms such as embedded links to the ONC complaint portal within Blue
Button 2.0 and the MyMedicare mobile app
Digital identity credentials and associated credentialing service providers (CSPs) are
useful for safe health data exchange. Yet, the healthcare industry faces challenges with
12

utilization and adoption. Most patients/caregivers have never heard of NIST or digital
identification systems. There is little or no awareness of these systems' benefits and
separate logins. Since patients/caregivers are required to recall existing logins for local
portals, patients and caregivers see little payoff. (PC-14-a)
If consumers used single sign-on more frequently, the benefits would be clear. These
logins allow an independent platform to house medical records and cost and quality data,
access data, and permit real-time comparison-shopping. Every credentialed provider can
pull participating patients' complete longitudinal records. It would also enable family
proxies to manage care without the traditional paperwork required, speeding up market
demand for home-based preventative services. The downsides include potential privacy
concerns, credential recovery hassles that could delay urgent care, and potential
exclusion risk for tech-limited patients. However, these risks or challenges are no different
than the contemporary patient portals and EMRs. (PC-14b,PC-14c)
CSPs improve access by giving any consumer app a pathway to universal healthcare data.
CSPs map cleanly to individual patients in any EHR, reducing duplicate and mismatched
records. This credentialing allows patients to grant access to particular providers more
easily. If providers were to model adoption and switch to CSPs for their portal, usage would
increase. CMS could drive adoption by incentivizing early adopters through free marketing
on their portals and interfaces. (14d,14e)

6.5.2 Overcoming Barriers
Common interoperability challenges include fragmented EHR ecosystems that do not

across the marketplace. This core standardization ensures data liquidity without

marketplace architecture should allow for specialized modules that build on universal
interoperability rules while maintaining flexibility for different care models. (VB-12)
