Digital Identity TD-3. Regarding digital identity implementation: 
TD-3. As mentioned previously, it would be most beneficial for CMS to utilize remote identity proofing for authentication and not rely on the patient authenticating directly using credentials with CMS or via the password methods at login.gov . Other federal and nationwide data access systems utilize identity proofing. Patients have “portalitis” and do not recall their USERID/password combinations. Moreover, patients in third party applications can’t rely on their browser remembering their pa ssword or their password manager knowing it when in an alternate app from the website. TEFCA uses IAL2 to enable patients’ self -service requests for data as the necessary authentication. Our experience with IAL2 is that 10 to 12% of patients will fail (due to ID issues or corroboration issues). While we mentioned earlier that a theoretical IAL1.8 standard may be more suitable (and would perhaps enable just a 2% or lower fail rate), some form of patient identity will always be better than site credentials. When a patient is unable to pass IAL1.8 or IAL2, CMS can leverage pharmacies’ existing in -person identi ty verification workflows for prescriptions to address these failures in remote scenarios. Pharmacies already validate government -issued IDs (e.g., driver’s licenses, passports) under DEA and state regulations making them ideal for offline identity proofin g. Allowing patients who fail remote IAL2 checks to visit a CMS -certified pharmacy for in -person verification significantly reduces the burden on the patient. Pharmacies would validate IDs against DEA/state standards (e.g., 8 C.F.R. § 274a.2) and update th e patient’s verification status in CMS systems (e.g., via NCPDP DataQ). Integrate pharmacies into CMS’s identity management framework as trusted “IAL2 exception” validators, similar to HHS’s XMS exception process. Use pharmacies enrolled in the Medicare Transaction Facilitator (MTF) as verification hubs. MTF’s reliance on NCPDP DataQ (Section 2, CMS MTF FAQ) enables real -time validation of pharmacy credentials and patient interactions. Align with DEA’s EPCS guidelin es, which permit institutional practitioners (e.g., pharmacies) to conduct identity proofing (DEA -DC-9 R1). The benefits include: Equity: Addresses IAL2’s current corroboration challenges with some phone plans by providing a physical fallback. Security: Pharmacies’ existing anti -diversion protocols (e.g., DEA Manual §1301.22) ensure rigorous ID checks, reducing fraud risk. Efficiency: Reuses established workflows, minimizing new burdens on pharmacies or patients. An example: A patient failing IAL2 online walks into a local MTF -enrolled pharmacy. The pharmacist verifies their ID per DEA/Florida HB 21 standards, confirms their identity. Apps and vendors can then use this identity verification to provide follow -on ser vices. 
Separately, but along the same lines, CMS could ensure that the post -office in person validations covered by login.gov could be extended to all Kantara -initiative certified identity service providers. In that way, patients having trouble getting identity -proofed for CMS or any other healthcare platform could go to their local post office to resolve it. a. What are the challenges and benefits? TD-3a. Identity Assurance Level 2 (IAL2), as defined by NIST, should be revisited to assure it is not too high a standard for for patients to successfully pass identification. While this level of assurance is intended to protect sensitive information, it c reates a barrier for some patients trying to access their medical records, especially "in the wild" —that is, outside controlled environments or without institutional support. Another benefit is how digital identity will thwart anti -competitive practices that are rampant in the industry. EHRs requiring direct authentication with their portal (again, inducing “portalitis”) is mainly a hidden way for those EHRs to build a moat to their data as well as boost their business models (EHRs requi ring portal authentication are typically generating revenue by increasing user counts of the portal) . We believe there are reinstated executive orders targeting this kind of anti -competitive practice (e.g. EO 13813), and they should be enforced. b. How would requiring digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800 -63-3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs) impact cybersecurity and data exchange? TD-3b. Better adherence than userid/password. Requiring patient authentication in a third party application will have a high failure and abandonment rate. Users do not remember their credentials, their browser password solutions won’t work in third party applications, nor will their password managers automatically fill in in a different application. Demographic Matching Improvements. For IAL2 and most other remote identity standards, patients are required to provide up -to-date, high -quality identity documents. That said, address mismatches are still common —up to 15% of consumers have outdated or inconsistent address information on their ID or in healthcare systems, which can prevent s uccessful demographic matching. Selfiie has successfully addressed this challenge with the HIEs it queries for patient data by working with them to increase the matching value of verified phone number and email address fields, while lowering the matching value of home address fields. We recommend CMS do the same for probabilistic patient matching fields. Demographic matching works very well when tuned correctly, and there are no better identifiers in healthcare than a patient’s mobile number and email address. 
Additionally, once a patient has been identity -proofed to IAL2, they should be allowed to add their other past or current addresses for medical records look up. Even though these addresses are not the “verified addresses” as scraped off their government ID , the patient has been proven to be who they say they are, and their additions of alternate home addresses should be accepted and used. Alternatively, using a USPS API to get known valid alternate addresses for an IAL2 patient should be acceptable. This will ensure the highest level of demographic matching. Prepaid Plans and Underserved Populations . Consumers using prepaid mobile plans or those on family cell phone plans can face additional hurdles with respect to IAL2 corroborations. These individuals may lack the necessary documentation or have difficulty passing stringent checks, making it nearly i mpossible for them to meet IAL2 requirements. The standard's reliance on strong evidence and sometimes biometric verification disproportionately impacts these under privileged and under represented groups, effectively locking them out of digital health access. Consider supporting an IAL1.8 standard that will still ensure a high degree of certainty for any patient while not keeping approximately 10 - 12% unable to use it. See next section below. A task force to take a position on AL1.8 , which is a hypothetical slight lessenging of IAL2 frameworks, and could address IAL2's accessibility barriers by balancing verification rigor with practical flexibility for the underprivileged. While IAL1.8 isn't explicitly defined in NIST standards, extrapolating from FHIR's IDIAL1.5/1.6 tiers , it might incorporate th e following solutions: 1. Reduced Document Burden . IAL1.8 could accept alternative evidence like medical record numbers, insurance cards, or digital identifiers instead of strict government -issued IDs. This would help prepaid plan users or those without traditional documentation, who often fail IAL2's rigid evidence requirements. 2. Flexible Address Verification . By allowing temporary addresses (e.g., shelters) or partial matches (e.g., zip codes only), IAL1.8 would mitigate the 15% failure rate often caused by exact address matching in corroborations. Notifications could shift from postal mail to SMS/email, aligning with mobile -centric populations. 4. Tiered Evidence Combinations . A structure requiring two "Fair" evidence types (e.g., prepaid phone number + clinic -issued ID) instead of IAL2's "Strong + two Fair" rule would lower abandonment rates by accommodating non -traditional users. 5. Progressive Escalation . IAL1.8 could allow initial access with basic verification (e.g., email + phone control), then prompt for additional evidence only when accessing sensitive records. This contrasts with IAL2's all -or-nothing approach. While IAL1.8 wouldn't match IAL2's fraud prevention, its hybrid model —combining IAL1's accessibility with incremental proofing —could resolve the key issues of exclusion, technical 
complexity, and high failure rates inherent to IAL2 implementations. Regardless, we still strongly believe IAL1.8 or IAL2 is significantly better than patients being required to supply userid/password credentials with TFA. c. A key feature is the Common Agreement participa nts sign because it provides a unified, federally recognized set of rules and technical standards for health information exchange, greatly reducing the burden on networks that previously had to negotiate multiple, inconsistent agreements. These two are very aligned, with one being government directed public/private (TEFCA) and the other being industry driven (Carequality, Commonwell, eHealth Exchange).

To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange and what are its limitations? USCDI has improved interoperability by standardizing key data elements across EHRs, and supporting major federal initiatives like TEFCA and CMS rules. But adoption is uneven, with large health systems and EHR vendors leading the way while smaller providers and specialty settings face technical and financial barriers. a. Does it contain the full extent of data elements you need? TD-7a. USCDI is a regulated, standardized health data set mandated for nationwide interoperability, but it does not encompass the full set of information which patients are entitled to under their HIPAA right of access. While USCDI includes core data class es such as medications, laboratory results, clinical notes, and some diagnostic imaging data, it does not require the inclusion of all imaging files, full payment and claims data, or the entirety of doctors’ notes, pathology, and radiology reports. And yet , under HIPAA, all of this missing information is the right of the patient to attain a copy of. For example, only certain types of clinical notes are mandated, and imaging data is represented primarily through reports rather than the actual images or all a ssociated documentation. Payment and claims data are not comprehensively included, and narrative or specialty notes may be omitted depending on the USCDI version. USCDI v4 builds on previous versions and adds more fields, but ultimately ALL healthcare and related data held by a covered entity should be shared with a requesting patient. b. If not, is it because of limitations in the definition of the USCDI format or the way it is utilized? TD-7b. Gaps in USCDI coverage arise from both its foundational design and practical implementation: the standard prioritizes broadly applicable data elements, often excluding niche or emerging types, and its annual update cycle lags behind rapidly evolving real-world needs like those seen during public health emergencies. c. If so, would adding more data elements to USCDI add value or create scoping challenges? How could such challenges be addressed? 
TD-7c. TD-8. What are the most effective certification criteria and standards under the ONC Health IT Certification Program? TD-8. The least effective certification program has been for EHRs. While it moved the ball with meaningful use and standardizations of collected data, it negatively affected the fidelity of the data captured. Most EHRs moved to templates with checkboxes and drop down li sts to ensure their provider users met all the requirements. Drop down lists, no matter how lengthy, do not usually capture exactly what the doctor is considering. Given that the computer can easily remind a physician if they forgot to record something in their encounter (e.g. did they ask about smoking cessation for a current smoker) it doesn’t behoove the provider to have the EHR to structure their input. In healthcare data, the most valuable data is the narrative, and restricting that to templated paragr aphs or drop down lists hurts the fidelity of the medical record. 
The interoperability requirements were very effective. Virtually every commercial EHR supports production and exchange of CCDA documents, and support integration with Direct Secure Messaging. A key idea of the NHIN (and susbsequent i terations, including TEFCA) was to be a ne twork of networks connecting regional and statewide HIEs , not replacing them .
