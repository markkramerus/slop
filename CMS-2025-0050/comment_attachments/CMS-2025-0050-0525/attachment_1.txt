Google welcomes the opportunity to provide comments in response to the Request for
Information (RFI) from the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) and the
Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy/Office of the National Coordinator for Health
Information Technology (ASTP/ONC).
At Google, we believe technology can help everyone live a longer, healthier life. We envision
a health ecosystem where secure, seamless data flow empowers patients, supports
providers, and enables the transition to a value-based healthcare system. This requires a
modern infrastructure built on open standards, intelligent data processing, and a policy
framework that fosters innovation. Below we provide both a high-level vision and concrete,
actionable ideas for CMS/ASTP consideration.
The Foundation: An Intelligent and Interoperable Data Infrastructure
The RFI correctly identifies that a lack of true interoperability is a foundational challenge.
Traditional data models fail to capture the full clinical narrative, much of which resides
outside of structured fields. To build a truly learning health system, we must address this
data in its native form. The solution lies in two parallel efforts: open standards and intelligent
tools to handle legacy and unstructured data formats.
● Recommendation: CMS should continue to adopt and espouse interoperability with
Health Information Technology while expanding the requirement to internal systems
within the agency. With the development of multi-cloud solutions built on open
standards and adopted by industry modern capabilities can be deployed to enhance
and integrate with existing systems. Specifically, working with IT providers that
emphasize the development of APIs, protocols, and other means of communicating
between systems will allow CMS and the health care industry to begin buying based
on standards rather than brands. This will ensure CMS and the American healthcare
system’s approach to technology serves as a model to domestic and international
healthcare organizations alike.

● Impact for Beneficiaries: Interoperability breaks vendor lock-in, sparking
competition that lowers technology costs and improves functionality. This allows all
healthcare providers, particularly under-resourced facilities, to access higher-quality
systems. For the beneficiary, this ensures the quality of their care is not limited by the
technology available at their local clinic or hospital. As agencies move towards using
AI tools that link into disparate data sources, the ability for systems to seamlessly
communicate will be critical for success based on data-driven decision making.
Connect the Disparate Health IT Ecosystem
To realize the vision of procurement based on standards and functionality rather than
brands, CMS must be able to connect systems and data regardless of where they are
hosted. True interoperability is hindered when critical information is distributed across
separate cloud environments, partner systems, and on-premises data centers. Similarly, past
attempts at monolithic data centralization have shown difficulty in scaling. These approaches
create technical and vendor-specific silos that prevent a unified view of patient data and
operations.
● Recommendation: CMS can accelerate its progress by enhancing its current
federated data strategy to be cloud-agnostic. The critical next step is to enable
capabilities that operate seamlessly and natively across CMS’s entire hybrid and
multi-cloud landscape, from its on-premises data centers to all of its public cloud
environments, from a single point of control. This approach does not require replacing
existing systems, but rather augmenting them with key capabilities such as:
○ A unified analytical layer that allows for the analysis of data directly in other
public clouds and on-premises systems, eliminating slow and costly data
movement.
○ A centralized governance and API management layer that can secure and
monitor data sharing between all connected systems, regardless of where they
reside.
○ AI-powered tools that can standardize multi-modal data (like FHIR, HL7v2, and
DICOM) as it is accessed through this common interoperability layer.
○ Deploying specialized AI agents that can execute automated workflows across
these previously disconnected environments. For instance:
1. An agent for claims processing could support a human adjudicator by
automatically gathering all relevant information for complex claims, such
as beneficiary eligibility, provider status, and prior authorizations, from
multiple disparate systems in seconds. This allows the adjudicator to
focus on making faster, more accurate decisions.

2. An agent for 1-800-Medicare could provide beneficiaries with instant,
personalized answers to complex benefit questions by accessing their
plan and deductible information in real-time. This would reduce wait
times and provide 24/7 support for common inquiries.
● Impact for Beneficiaries: This unified approach directly impacts the speed and
quality of service, as well as the accuracy and veracity of individual health information.
A truly interoperable platform can process a beneficiary's claim in near real-time, even
when the required information comes from a provider whose systems are on a
different cloud or EHR. This eliminates delays caused by technical incompatibility
between systems, leading to faster payment determinations and ensuring that the
underlying technology infrastructure is never a barrier to receiving timely care and
benefits.
Address Data Fragmentation with Modern Technology
A significant barrier to interoperability, quality of care, and a unified view of patient and
claims data results from large volumes of health data being trapped in unstructured formats
such as free-text notes, scanned documents, medical images, and faxes. Recent advances in
AI allow this challenge to be addressed directly, moving beyond data access to true data
utility.
● Recommendation: CMS should champion the adoption of AI-powered data
understanding tools that enable standardization across integrated systems. Google
Cloud solutions, for example, use Optical Character Recognition (OCR) and Natural
Language Processing (NLP) to automatically extract, structure, and map information
from a faxed patient record to standardized Fast Healthcare Interoperability
Resources (FHIR). This process can identify key clinical concepts, diagnoses,
medications, and procedures, making previously inert data computable and ready for
use in clinical workflows and analytics.
● Impact for Beneficiaries: This capability is particularly transformative for rural and
underserved hospitals. A rural facility could automatically convert a faxed referral into
a structured FHIR record, ensuring seamless interoperability with a larger urban health
system. This helps guarantee that a patient’s geographic location does not determine
their access to high-quality, coordinated care, and it reduces the significant provider
burnout associated with manual data transcription.
Empowering Individual Health Through a Modern PHR
The RFI notes the fractured, labor-intensive nature of managing multiple provider portals. A
modern Personal Health Record (PHR) can solve this, but it must be more than a simple data

repository; it must be a dynamic, patient-controlled hub for engagement that fosters a
competitive marketplace of innovation.
Foster a Federated, User-Centric Health Record
One option is for CMS to foster an ecosystem built on a multi-cloud platform where
third-party applications, including PHRs, can securely access a beneficiary's data from
multiple sources with their express consent. This approach stimulates innovation by allowing
developers to create tailored solutions for specific populations or conditions.
On-device sharing is also important for beneficiaries. Principles behind solutions such as
Health Connect by Android—which provides a secure, on-device hub for users to manage
granular data sharing permissions between health and wellness apps—offer a model for how
to empower users with privacy and control at the point of interaction. Having standardized
API requirements through the Medical Records that support PHRs, including new clinical
categories like problems and conditions, medications, allergies, immunizations, and lab
results, will be helpful in democratizing information to all. These APIs can enable applications
to read and write medical record information in a standard FHIR format. This type of
integration aims to provide a more complete view of a user's health by connecting health
and fitness data with medical records, leading to continuous, proactive, and personalized
experiences.
● Recommendation: CMS can provide the key infrastructure components for this
ecosystem. Drawing on our experience with Public Sector cloud projects and the
Medical Records Android API, this would involve:
1. A Secure Identity Layer: Fostering an ecosystem of compatible and trusted
digital identity providers. This allows beneficiaries to use a compliant identity of
their choice (NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2) to eliminate the need for multiple
portal logins and passwords.
2. An API Gateway: Building on the success of Blue Button 2.0, create a robust
API gateway that allows authenticated and authorized third-party apps to
access data from participating payers and providers, enforcing security and
consent protocols.
● Impact for Beneficiaries: A beneficiary could use a trusted application of their
choice to see a holistic view of their health. They control their data and how it’s used.
For example, they could choose to grant temporary access to multiple specialists for
different opinions on treatment options and related costs, share specific wellness
data from a personal device with their primary care physician to manage a chronic
condition, or authorize a family caregiver to view appointment schedules and
medication lists. This platform, built with granular consent controls at its core, gives
individuals true ownership and control over their health information.

AI-Powered Assistance for a Proactive Beneficiary Experience
The concept of a Personal Health Assistant (PHA), mentioned in the RFI, is now achievable
through conversational AI, but it must be built on a foundation of user trust and control.
Deploy an AI-Powered Beneficiary Navigator
CMS can enhance its existing touchpoints, like Medicare.gov and 1-800-Medicare, with
AI-powered virtual assistants and call centers that can provide personalized, proactive, and
scalable support and human-like interactions.
● Recommendation: This "Beneficiary Navigator" could perform specific, high-value
tasks once a user grants permission:
○ Answer Complex Benefit Questions: Instead of searching documents, a
beneficiary could ask in plain language, "How much will my copay be for a
cardiology visit with Dr. Smith?" and receive a personalized answer based on
their specific plan, deductible status, and the provider's network participation.
○ Facilitate Care Navigation: After answering a question, the assistant could
say, "Dr. Smith is a high-quality, in-network provider in your accountable care
organization with availability next Tuesday. Would you like me to help you
schedule an appointment?" This integrates provider directories, quality data,
and scheduling into a single, seamless workflow.
○ Provide Proactive Reminders: The assistant could provide reminders for
preventative screenings, medication refills, or annual enrollment deadlines
based on the beneficiary’s data.
● Impact for Beneficiaries: This transforms the experience from reactive and difficult
to proactive and supportive, reducing administrative burden for beneficiaries and
freeing CMS contact center staff to handle the most complex cases.
Overarching Policy Recommendations
The actionable ideas above require a supportive national policy framework. A reliable,
secure, and resilient health data infrastructure is essential for innovation, efficiency, and
improved health outcomes for everyone. To that end, we recommend focusing on:
1. Crafting a Pro-Innovation Federal Framework: Establish a clear, national policy
framework for health data privacy, security, and interoperability to prevent a
patchwork of state laws that stifle innovation. This should include regulatory
sandboxes for responsible AI development and ensure that privacy rules do not
unnecessarily hinder the secondary use of de-identified data for vital public health
research. This framework should also encourage and empower state, local, and tribal
health agencies to adopt AI and cloud solutions that drive true data modernization at

sub-national levels. This could be accomplished through a combination of policies and
guidelines along with provisioning of key technologies through a CMS-approved
cloud marketplace.
2. Accelerating and Modernizing Government Technology Adoption: The federal
government, including CMS and ASTP, should lead by example in adopting modern
identity, cloud, and AI solutions that are based on open standards and support a
vendor-agnostic approach to technology. This requires streamlining outdated
accreditation and procurement practices. Furthermore, government data rights
clauses in contracts must be updated to guarantee interoperability and data
portability in a way that will prevent the harms that come from vendor lock-in and
concentration risk associated with working with only one or a limited number of
solution providers, increasing the resilience of the healthcare supply chain and
ensuring a competitive and dynamic technology marketplace. Additionally, CMS and
ASTP should explore modern methods of communicating policies with its
stakeholders beyond document-based guidance and publications and leverage
interactive, video, and audio modalities to dramatically improve clarity.
3. Investing in Health Technology Innovation: We support sustained public and
private investment in foundational AI research, the development of high-quality,
representative datasets, and the "last mile" of technology adoption. This includes
funding workforce development initiatives to ensure the healthcare workforce is
equipped to use these new tools effectively, ethically, and responsibly. Critically,
standing up and supporting a regulatory sandbox, or an otherwise controlled
environment, would provide CMS, HHS, and the healthcare industry with a powerful
tool focused on demonstrating and testing innovative new solutions. Ensuring such an
environment is interoperable and vendor-agnostic will ensure any new idea can be
analyzed, not just those built on incumbent’s often legacy architecture.
Google is committed to applying our technology and expertise to solve hard problems in
health. We believe that by fostering an interoperable ecosystem built on open standards and
intelligent data processing, we can empower individuals, support providers, and accelerate
the nation's transition to a system that delivers better health outcomes for everyone.
