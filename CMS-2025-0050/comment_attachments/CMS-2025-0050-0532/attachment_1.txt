On behalf of the Massachusetts Telemedicine Coalition (tMED), representing more than 50
healthcare provider organizations, consumer advocates, technology organizations, and
telecommunication associations in Massachusetts, we appreciate the opportunity to submit
comments to the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) on the Request for
Information; Health Technology, published in the Federal Register on May 16, 2025. Our
comments highlight key recommendations as well as areas of concern, focusing on telehealth, in
particular.
The Massachusetts Experience
Based on the March 2024 report from the Massachusetts Center for Health Information and
Analysis,1 in 2022, telehealth spending reported by commercial, Medicare Advantage, and
MassHealth plans totaled $1.6 billion (2.4% of total healthcare spending in the state). Overall,
telehealth spending in 2022 remained significantly higher than the $3 million reported in the pre-
pandemic year 2019 in Massachusetts. However, it has also become clear that telehealth has
expanded access to services to patients, particularly for behavioral health services.
Spending for telehealth services delivered by “other professionals” (non-physician providers),
which includes occupational and physical therapists, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and

In particular, it is notable that 10 diagnosis categories accounted for more than two-thirds of

categories, nine of the top 10 diagnosis categories were behavioral health conditions, consistent

Congressional Action is Needed
The tMED Coalition fully supports immediate action from Congress to make important
telehealth flexibilities permanent prior to the statutory expiration of those flexibilities on
September 30, 2025. Acting as soon as possible will provide much needed certainty and
safeguard this important policy.
Clinicians need telehealth to expand access to care and support strong patient relationships, and
they value the flexibility created by the option for remote care when clinically appropriate.
Safety net providers, such as community health centers and rural health clinics, have depended
on these flexibilities. Current telehealth flexibilities have played a critical role in promoting
access to vital healthcare services, including those provided by advanced specialists (such as
oncologists) and mental health practitioners, without requiring a previous in-person appointment.
This access benefits are particularly critical for patients in rural and underserved areas, those
with mobility issues, and patients with transportation or other limitations that prevent them from
accessing in-person care in a timely manner.
Predictability in telehealth policy now, in advance of the September 30 deadline, would:
• Create certainty for Medicare beneficiaries, who will otherwise face confusion as to
whether they will have continued access to clinicians and services they are using
virtually.
• Strengthen our national healthcare workforce by enabling a greater number of clinicians
to provide telehealth services and allowing for investment in flexible virtual staffing
models that address current workforce shortages while maintaining high-quality
healthcare.
• Ensure continued investment in the technology tools and infrastructure needed to offer
telehealth services, particularly for smaller providers serving the rural and underserved,
who cannot afford to invest in these tools without a reimbursement pathway.
Without clear guidance from Congress on a long-term approach for telehealth flexibilities,
healthcare delivery systems will not be able to invest sufficient resources to allow maximum
access to patients. We believe that policymakers now have more than enough evidence to see the
benefits of telehealth so that they help create a permanent pathway to ensure that telehealth
continues to be available and accessible.

We appreciate CMS’ focus on this critical issue, and we invite you to join us in urging Congress
to make the following specific telehealth flexibilities permanent before they expire on September
30, 2025:
• Removing geographic restrictions and expanding originating sites to include any site at
which the patient is located, including the patient’s home.
• Extending the Acute Hospital at Home waiver so that hospital may continue to take
advantage of this innovative program for patients in their residences.
• Expanding the ability for eligible practitioners to furnish telehealth services to include
occupational therapists, physical therapists, speech-language pathologists, and
audiologists.
• Extending the ability for federally qualified health centers (FQHCs) and rural health
clinics (RHCs) to furnish telehealth services, and allowing critical access hospitals
(CAHs) to have the same ability to offer and bill for telehealth services by including
language to include CAHs as eligible distant sites.
• Removing the six-month in-person requirement for mental health services furnished
through telehealth, including the in-person requirements for FQHCs and RHCs.
• Extending coverage and payment for audio-only telehealth services.
• Extending the ability to use telehealth services to meet the face-to-face recertification
requirement for hospice care.
Additionally, the tMED Coalition encourages CMS to reinstate remote virtual services for Partial
Hospital Programs (PHP). Many of our member hospitals offer PHP services, and during the
Public Health Emergency implemented during the first Trump administration, patients benefitted
enormously through Medicare’s coverage of virtual PHP services. Unfortunately, Medicare
members are not currently afforded the same virtual treatment options as non-Medicare
members, unless they are able to pay out of pocket.
We urge CMS to reinstate virtual PHP services for Medicare beneficiaries since these services:
• achieve similar clinically equivalent outcomes, such as reduction in symptoms, improved
functioning, and participant satisfaction as in-person care;
• improve attendance and adherence to treatment and reduce barriers such as
transportation, caregiving responsibilities, disability barriers for wheelchair bound
patients, and access despite geographic distance or rural isolation;
• sustain treatment for those who suffer from disabilities and chronic medical conditions
that make in-person attendance extremely difficult;

• allow those who may have a contagious illnesses to participate in virtual PHP without
jeopardizing others;
• support workforce retention for staff who are at risk of leaving the field due to health
concerns, caregiving responsibilities, or geographic constraints; and
• maintain continuity of care for discharged patients following a psychiatric
hospitalization, thereby helping to reduce more costly inpatient re-admissions.
Thank you for your consideration of these comments. Collectively, the tMED Coalition strongly
urges the adoption of the policies as outlined above that will protect and continue to provide
opportunities for healthcare via telemedicine for our patients. Should you have any questions or
concerns, please contact Adam Delmolino, senior director, virtual care & clinical affairs, at the
Massachusetts Health & Hospital Association at adelmolino@mhalink.org.
