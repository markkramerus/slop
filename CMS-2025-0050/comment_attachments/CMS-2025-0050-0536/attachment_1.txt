 2. Digital Identity TD-3. Regarding digital identity implementation: a. What are the challenges and benefits? Digital identity credentials bolster security and trust, facilitating consistent identity verification across diverse systems, which can enhance interoperability and patient safety. However, scaling digital identity implementation poses considerable workflow and integration challenges, especially for organizations managing identity across both internal systems and external applications. A primary obstacle is establishing cross-organizational trust, specifically how third-party applications and healthcare providers authenticate and accept credentials without established relationships. The lack of defined governance, implementation standards, and use cases may hinder broad adoption. 3. Technical Standards and Certification TD-4: How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs over proprietary APIs? CMS can foster greater interoperability by mandating the use of standards across all parties involved in data exchange. This means requiring standards not only for healthcare providers and EHR developers but also for payers, public health agencies, and other stakeholders. Rallying the entire community around a single certification, like the Da Vinci standard, and incorporating feedback from EHR vendors will help achieve this. When standards are only required in one direction, it creates an imbalance that limits true interoperability and discourages investment in scalable solutions. TD-5. TD-8. What are the most effective certification criteria and standards under the ONC Health IT Certification Program? The U.S. Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) and the underlying principles that enable effective Clinical Decision Support (CDS) have significantly advanced healthcare data standardization and communication. These initiatives have provided a foundational framework, demonstrating the clear value of adopting shared standards across the healthcare ecosystem. However, several years into their implementation, the benefits of standardization appear to be disproportionately realized. To truly unlock the full potential of interoperability, it is imperative we move beyond a one-sided adoption model. MEDITECH recommends actively encouraging and incentivizing payers and Public Health Agencies (PHAs) to adhere to the same data standards that have been established for other stakeholders. This current disparity creates significant challenges, as evidenced by the difficulties encountered with electronic prior authorization (ePA), where Electronic Health Record (EHR) vendors struggle to find payers willing to test due to a lack of consistent standard implementation. This lack of standardized data also directly hampers the effectiveness of Clinical Decision Support tools, as they rely on comprehensive and consistent information from all sources to provide accurate and timely guidance to clinicians. TD-9. Regarding certification of health IT: a. What are the benefits of redefining certification to prioritize API-enabled capabilities over software functionality? Redefining health IT certification to prioritize API-enabled capabilities over traditional software functionality offers benefits. This shift allows for access to the entire patient record, moving beyond visit-based data (like C-CDA) to create a comprehensive, 4 
 longitudinal view of a patient's health. Furthermore, certification based on APIs ensures systems can precisely meet and support diverse, specific use case requirements. Amidst this evolution, the USCDI remains important as the standardized base foundation, providing the common language for data exchange. This pivotal shift necessitates the use of well-vetted testing pools that can simulate complex, real-world, multi-system interactions, as such rigorous testing is vital for ensuring true interoperability, identifying potential vulnerabilities, building essential trust among stakeholders, and driving continuous improvement in the realm of health IT. b. What would be the drawbacks? A significant drawback is the potential for new certification requirements to inadvertently burden healthcare sites. Consideration should include the practical side for implementers as prioritizing new API capabilities could create major hurdles, including overlooking ease of adoption, necessary infrastructure changes, or impact to existing workflows. Our goal must be to ensure that the benefits of enhanced interoperability—like comprehensive patient records and efficient data exchange—actually lighten the administrative load and improve patient care at the front lines, rather than piling on new layers of complexity and cost. c. How could ASTP/ONC revise health IT certification criteria to require APIs to consistently support exchanging data from all aspects of the patient's chart (for example, faxed records, free text, discrete data)? ASTP/ONC could revise health IT certification criteria by focusing on fewer, more impactful requirements that are available for a larger stakeholder pool. The emphasis should be on practical solutions that enable broader data exchange while acknowledging the inherent complexities of unstructured information. d. What policy changes could CMS make so providers are motivated to respond to API-based data requests with best possible coverage and quality of data? While API data requests and responses offer significant benefits, widespread provider adoption hinges on substantial incentives, as implementation demands considerable staff effort and training. Although Information Blocking rules establish compliance, providers currently receive minimal practical benefits for delivering robust API responses. To address this, CMS could enhance incentive alignment, such as rewarding automated electronic case reporting (eCR) in lieu of manual submissions. Ultimately, policy strategies should be collaboratively developed with providers, who are best equipped to advise on how API usage can be integrated into clinical workflows while considering resource limitations. e. How could EHRs capable of bulk data transfer be used to reduce the burden on providers for reporting quality performance data to CMS? What capabilities are needed to show benefit? What concerns are there with this approach? 5 
 Bulk data transfer functionality lacks thorough testing and validation due to limited utilization. Further, this functionality can be quite taxing on systems. To demonstrate benefit, a robust and scalable infrastructure is essential which must facilitate an efficient data transfer process that places minimal strain on systems and the network. This approach prevents disruptions to clinical workflows and avoids imposing prohibitive costs on healthcare providers. Concerns include timely access to performance data as well as provider attribution. Providers need consistent, usable information to track their ongoing quality performance and bulk transfer handles periodic reporting. EHRs must be capable of generating and presenting real-time or near real-time performance to allow for timely quality improvement efforts to occur. Further, accurately attributing quality performance to individual providers or groups, especially in complex care settings with multiple contributing clinicians, can be challenging even with comprehensive data. Bulk transfer needs to support sophisticated attribution rules to avoid misrepresentation of performance. TD-10. As of January 1, 2024, many health IT developers with products certified through the ONC Health IT Certification Program are required to include the capability to perform an electronic health information export or “EHI export” for a single patient as well as for patient populations ( 45 CFR 170.315(b)(10) ). Such health IT developers are also required to publicly describe the format of the EHI export. Notably, how EHI export was accomplished was left entirely to the health IT developer. Now that this capability has been in production for over a year, CMS and ASTP/ONC seek input on the following: a. Should this capability be revised to specify standardized API requirements for EHI export? 6 
 MEDITECH echoes comment by the EHRA: " Yes, the EHI Export capability should be revised to include standardized API requirements. In its current form, EHI Export has not met its intended goals of simplifying patient access to their complete electronic health information or streamlining provider transitions between health IT systems. There are numerous variables contributing to this, but chief amongst them is the lack of defined, commonly accepted standards for both data format and delivery mechanisms. While developers are required to publicly document their export specifications, healthcare data remains highly complex and difficult to interpret consistently without a shared, well-understood standard, regardless of how detailed and thorough a developer’s format specification documentation is. This is especially true for population-level exports, where the volume and variability of data make it unrealistic to assume a seamless, low-burden export and import process. Even with thorough documentation, significant human intervention is typically required to process such exports meaningfully. The EHR Association recommends re-scoping the EHI Export criterion to focus not on “all EHI” but instead on all USCDI data and reference guidance on how to support EHI Export using FHIR Bulk Data that includes an organized USCDI data set. We suggest that EHI Export be repurposed considering these realities and scope to a HL7 FHIR-based data set that can be shared using HL7 FHIR Bulk Data Access for a single or multiple patients that contains all USCDI scoped data as expressed in HL7 FHIR US Core, and that can grow not only based on HL7 FHIR US Core expansion (which is only a limited data set) but on an identified combination of HL7 FHIR US Core and profiles from implementation guides reflecting specialized data such as full laboratory results, care plans, and other full data sets. Any data not available in FHIR would inherently require more effort by the receiver and should not be expected to flow seamlessly under a standardized export. Additionally, it should be clarified that EHI Export is not appropriate as a standalone solution for HIT-to-HIT migration, which often requires tailored workflows and complex mappings beyond a universal dataset. In effect, EHI Export should be repositioned as a FHIR Bulk Data use case with a defined implementation guide, rather than an open-ended export of all possible EHI. " b. Are there specific workflow aspects that could be improved? Exporting Electronic Health Information (EHI) presents challenges unrelated to workflow execution. Developers should maintain discretion over initiating and conducting exports, aligning with individual product design and usage. Standardizing workflow specifics across vendors would introduce needless inflexibility and complication without providing tangible benefits. Certification should prioritize demonstrating capability and reliable functionality of the export feature, rather than specifying internal processes or user interfaces. c. Should CMS consider policy changes to support this capability's use? 7 
 Requiring EHI Export is unnecessary due to its infrequent usage, primarily triggered by patient data requests or provider EHR transitions. Existing standardized exchange mechanisms, like FHIR and C-CDA, already effectively address most real-world interoperability needs. Thus, mandating EHI Export adds no value. Instead, promoting the widespread adoption of standard exchanges such as HL7 FHIR and CDA would better serve the majority of interoperability requirements. For the few situations demanding complete datasets, custom methods can be used until USCDI or FHIR profiles evolve to handle all EHI use cases. 4. Data Exchange TD-13. What new opportunities and advancements could emerge with APIs providing access to the entirety of a patient's electronic health information (EHI)? APIs providing access to the entirety of a patient's electronic health information (EHI) can open opportunities such as population level research. a. What are the primary obstacles to this? Primary obstacles include data volume, unstructured data, and performance issues when accessing multiple patients. What are the primary tradeoffs between USCDI and full EHI, especially given more flexible data processing capabilities today? The primary tradeoff between USCDI and full EHI stems from a fundamental gap in standards. USCDI mandates specific data elements and references standards for their representation. Full EHI offers the complete picture, but its lack of universal standardization beyond the USCDI scope presents a significant hurdle for automated processing and trusted exchange. The ongoing challenge is to expand USCDI to encompass more EHI while simultaneously developing robust, standardized methods for accessing and interpreting the remaining unstructured and less standardized data within the broader EHI landscape, all while keeping costs and provider burden in mind. 8

What are the potential disadvantages of their use? The conceptualization and specifications for bulk FHIR APIs are functionally sound and capable of facilitating data exchange as outlined. Conversely, within a shared network infrastructure, the identity of access requestors is often not truly known. However, a shared network infrastructure facilitates data exchange between disparate organizations even in the absence of pre-existing relationships. a. Do current rules encourage scalable network participation? Current rules and initiatives, particularly TEFCA and its strong emphasis on standards-based FHIR APIs, are strategically designed to encourage and enable scalable network participation. However, the voluntary nature of TEFCA and the practical challenges related to governance complexity, upfront investment, and technical readiness mean that significant ongoing effort is required to fully achieve broad, scalable network participation across the entire healthcare ecosystem. b. What changes would improve alignment (for example, API unification, reciprocal access)? A significant change that would improve alignment is to separate document requirements from API data exchange requirements as these should not be the same rules. Compliance TD-19. Regarding price transparency implementation: a. What are current shortcomings in content, format, delivery, and timeliness? Current limitations in price transparency implementation manifest across content, format, delivery, and timeliness. A primary shortcoming is the extensive volume of data that hospitals are mandated to compile into machine-readable files, encompassing all items and services. Furthermore, hospitals confront considerable challenges stemming from the substantial data volume originating from disparate internal sources (e.g., chargemasters, billing systems, contract repositories). This fragmented data landscape renders timely compilation difficult. Consequently, comprehensive adherence to the evolving price transparency regulations becomes problematic, frequently resulting in increased auditing activities and associated compliance pressures on healthcare organizations. b. Which workflows would benefit most from functional price transparency? Workflow optimization opportunities for enhanced price transparency encompass streamlined appointment scheduling, mitigation of surprise billing, facilitation of patient service comparison, and more efficient revenue cycle management processes. c. What improvements would be most valuable for patients, providers, or payers, including CMS? Improvements of value would encompass the simplification of price transparency, as the intricacy of current detailed requirements can cause ambiguity. Streamlined requirements would be more conducive to consumers, impose a reduced burden upon healthcare providers, and afford a clearer understanding of aggregate costs. Requirements must be stated clearly and concisely, bundled costs to provide a more transparent framework for cost estimation, and transferring a greater proportion of the onus to payers through the 11 
 provision of advanced explanations of benefits, which would consequently alleviate the burden on providers and mitigate the incidence of inaccurate estimates. d. What would further motivate solution development? In order to incentivize the development of solutions, the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) should streamline reporting requirements, as the current volume of data is excessive. VB-2. How can key themes and technologies such as artificial intelligence, population health analytics, risk stratification, care coordination, usability, quality measurement, and patient engagement be better integrated into APM requirements? To enhance the integration of critical technologies and themes, such as artificial intelligence (AI), population health analytics, risk stratification, care coordination, usability, quality measurement, and patient engagement, within Alternative Payment Model (APM) requirements, federal programs and guidelines should be strategically leveraged. Existing frameworks like the CMS SAFER Guides already encompass foundational elements for effective technology utilization, including usability and data integrity. APM requirements could explicitly reference and incentivize adherence to these guides, potentially by linking compliance to favorable shared savings rates or reduced downside risk, thereby fostering a culture of safety and robust technological deployment. 12 
 Furthermore, the Quality Payment Program (QPP) Improvement Activities (IAs) category presents a valuable existing mechanism. Compliance and Certification VB-5. In your experience, how do current certification criteria and standards incorporated into the ONC Health IT Certification Program support value-based care delivery? The ONC Health IT Certification Program's current certification criteria and standards have been essential in fostering interoperability and standardizing practices for certified health IT developers.
