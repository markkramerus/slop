Improving Medicare Technology RFI
C. Providers
PR-1. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage providers, including those in
rural areas, to leverage approved (see description in PC-5) digital health products for
their patients?
As an FQHC-led Accountable Care and Management Services Organization supporting
Medicare beneficiaries in 8 states and the District of Columbia, we see strong potential
for digital tools—like appointment scheduling, medication tracking, and provider
messaging—to improve care coordination, reduce burden, and empower patients.
Barriers to adoption include:
• Limited digital literacy and lack of access to devices or broadband
• Low interoperability across tools and EHR systems
• A mismatch between tool functionality and patient/provider needs
We recommend CMS:
• Provide financial incentives for FQHC-developer partnerships, ensuring that new
digital tools address the real needs of underserved populations.
• Tie incentives to interoperability with major EHR platforms (e.g., MyChart) to
improve care management and reduce redundancy.
• Fund broadband and device access programs for patients to bridge the digital
divide, especially in rural communities where internet access is not readily
available.
• Establish certification pathways for digital tools optimized for safety-net providers,
including multilingual and low-bandwidth features.
• Support shared infrastructure (e.g., regional digital platforms or consortia) to
make adoption more feasible for small FQHCs.
F. Value-Based Care Organizations
VB-1. What incentives could encourage APMs such as accountable care organizations
(ACOs) or participants in Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) to leverage digital
health management and care navigation products more often and more effectively with
their patients? What are the current obstacles preventing broader digital product
adoption for patients in ACOs?

Improving Medicare Technology RFI
Digital health management platforms—especially those using predictive analytics and
AI—can identify care gaps, risk factors, and opportunities for timely intervention. AI
scribes can streamline documentation, support accurate coding, and reduce
administrative burden. These tools are highly promising, particularly in resource-
constrained settings like FQHCs. However, barriers include:
• High costs of evaluation, implementation, and maintenance
• Limited EHR integration
• Lack of guidance on scalable, high-impact solutions for underserved populations
We recommend CMS:
• Create financial incentives tied to successful tool integration into care
management workflows
• Prioritize tools with demonstrated ROI for underserved communities
• Fund shared digital infrastructure that can be leveraged across FQHCs
• Incentivize adoption of AI-assisted documentation to improve risk adjustment and
reduce provider burnout
VB-9. What technology requirements should be different for APM organizations when
comparing to non-APM organizations (for example, quality reporting, and
interoperability)?
To reduce administrative burden and enhance data utility for APM participants,
especially FQHCs, we recommend:
1. Align CMS and HHS data standards and reporting systems to eliminate
duplicative reporting and streamline quality measurement.
2. Allow UDS data submission in lieu of separate MSSP and ACO REACH
reporting. Since FQHCs already report through HRSA’s Uniform Data System,
using this for CMS reporting would reduce burden and encourage broader
participation in value-based care models.
This alignment would improve data integrity, reduce costs, and help ACOs focus
resources on improving care—not navigating redundant reporting processes.
