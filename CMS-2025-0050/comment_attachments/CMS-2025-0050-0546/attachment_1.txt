Section C. Providers
Providers are the nucleus at the center of what drives our healthcare system. When they – and
the network of connected services that support them — are plagued with chaos our entire
system breaks. Unfortunately, it’s been broken for a long time. Patients, regulators, payers, and
vendors have long grappled with how to fix it. While we’ve made significant progress, solving
the fragmentation in healthcare requires a new way of thinking.

We thank CMS, ASTP/ ONC for the opportunity to contribute our perspective — one grounded
in decades of interoperability. As we look ahead, we’re committed to advancing solutions that
reduce the burden of unstructured data while ensuring that providers with limited technical
resources are not left behind.
PR–3. How important is it for healthcare delivery and interoperability in urban and rural areas
that all data in an EHR system be accessible for exchange, regardless of storage format (for
example, scanned documents, faxed records, lab results, free text notes, structured data
fields)? Please address all of the following:
Despite advances in health care information exchange, the flow of data between large hospitals
and small care facilities is anything but smooth and seamless. Health care providers consistently
face breakdowns in communication and care coordination due to gaps in information sharing.
The promise of healthcare IT was to streamline processes, improve efficiency, and most
importantly, enhance patient outcomes. However, the reality for many, particularly in rural and
underserved communities, is that the benefits of this technological revolution often stall at the
"last mile." The gap between a multi-million-dollar urban EHR and a modest rural clinic's system
represents more than just differing technology; it embodies a systemic failure to prioritize
seamless, patient-centered data flow.
Consensus Cloud Solutions sponsored a survey of CIOs to ask them about this challenge. The
survey, commissioned by Consensus Cloud Solutions and conducted by the College of
Healthcare Information Management Executives (CHIME), explored the state of data exchange
between healthcare’s digital “haves” and “have-nots,” including rural hospitals, post-acute care
facilities, and community clinics that aren’t connected to an electronic health record (EHR).
Barriers to data exchange abound, given the disjointed patchwork of various platforms,
systems, and technologies that healthcare organizations employ today. While EHR adoption has
made it easier to share patients' medical information between connected facilities, many
smaller providers still don’t have the funds or resources to implement these systems. Without
an interoperable EHR, even a query from a QHIN is challenging.
In the survey, healthcare CIOs ranked breakdowns in care coordination as the top challenge
facing their data sharing struggles, followed by difficulties making (and following up with)
referrals, finding patient information, and determining how and where to send information.
Among rural hospitals, receiving referrals from larger partners emerged as a particular pain
point.
These barriers don’t just impede an organization’s operational and financial performance; the
implications can impact patient care and health outcomes down the line. More than half of the
survey respondents (54%) say these challenges have had some impact on care continuity with a
lens on poor health outcomes, while nearly 42% say the impact has been significant. The vast
majority (83%) agree that tech equity is critical to achieving better quality of care, which means
equal access to patient data. So it’s understandable that 62% of these leaders placed health

outcomes in underserved areas among their organization’s top five priorities. These issues
demand solutions.
a. Current challenges in accessing different data formats.
By re-evaluating the tools they’re using to exchange information, healthcare organizations are
exploring new ways of streamlining communication across the continuum of care.
According to the Consensus/CHiME survey, many facilities (46%) still rely on paper fax to send
information to other providers in the absence of an EHR. While 29% have moved toward digital
cloud fax, 12.5% just pick up the phone and 8% send a direct secure message.
Rural and urban providers in many care settings, such as the substance abuse center, smaller
community clinics, and Critical Access Hospitals do not have updated technology that can send
and receive FHIR messages, for instance. While some areas allow for structured data standards,
we can’t ignore the fact that the largest percentage of healthcare data is not structured and
cannot be accessed via FHIR.
Data extraction technologies — including OCR, NLP, and AI — have evolved from these
unstructured forms can be automatically extracted and integrated into the patient’s chart
without manual intervention. Some solutions even transmit the original form along with a
confidence score in the payload to the receiving system.
This means care can begin immediately, without waiting for data to be manually entered — a
process that not only slows things down but introduces the risk of human error.
PR–6. Is TEFCA currently helping to advance provider access to health information?
TEFCA is a framework for facilitating the secure and interoperable exchange of health
information. It is not a technology. So, agreeing to go ‘all in’ on TEFCA doesn’t mean the
preferred data standard of FHIR will start flowing. This may take systems some amount of time
to engineer the data exchange.
This also does not take into account those providers that don’t have the interoperability
exchange technology in place or the funds to invest in FHIR exchange, they will not be able to
participate in TEFCA. Here is an example. An FQHC that uses Epic can’t easily communicate with
the two large hospitals in San Diego that also use Epic. Just because these two providers are
using Epic does not mean the data they are sharing will interoperate even if the hospital
systems agree to be on TEFCA by the end of the year – a requirement from Epic. We still need
to consider lower cost alternatives and technology that exists today to help bridge this divide.
a. Please provide specific examples.
b. What changes would you suggest?

Allow for the use of other data sharing technologies, such as digital cloud fax, combined with
intelligent data extraction and translation be used to help promote Direct Secure Messaging
(DSM) or FHIR. Going from ‘Fax 2 Direct’ is a solution available today. This allows the ‘have not’
systems to ease into their digital maturity or investigate funding to modernize their exchange
technology.
c. What other options are available outside of TEFCA?
The healthcare industry generates vast amounts of unstructured data — and current
movements toward TEFCA and FHIR often overlook this reality. Unstructured documents —
such as faxes, scanned forms, and PDFs — are difficult to process within automated workflows.
Yet they remain so pervasive that excluding them from interoperability strategies would be a
serious oversight.
These documents can contain valuable clinical information needed for efficient workflows. For
example, handwritten or unstructured prior authorization forms can be processed using data
extraction technologies and converted into the FHIR format.
Similarly, the CDC’s Data Modernization Initiative relies on FHIR-based exchange. Providers
unable to support FHIR risk being excluded from population health efforts — introducing
potential bias into the data. By leveraging extraction and translation technologies, we can
bridge this gap and ensure a more complete, equitable exchange of healthcare information.
PR–7. What strategies can CMS implement to support providers in making high-quality,
timely, and comprehensive healthcare data available for interoperability in the digital
product ecosystem? How can the burden of increasing data availability and sharing be
mitigated for providers? Are there ways that workflows or metrics that providers are already
motivated to optimize for that could be reused for, or combined with, efforts needed to
support interoperability?
How can the burden of increasing data availability and sharing be mitigated for providers?
Providers want to make the right decisions about data sharing; they know the risks of
incomplete data and are suffering through the challenges to make that data accessible in
workflow. Yet when you take a closer look at an often overlooked tool that is pervasive in
healthcare, you'll see a cloud-based solution that exchanges data more quickly, efficiently,
securely and cost-effectively that is available today – digital cloud fax. Moreover, pairing digital
cloud fax with AI can achieve intelligent data extraction—the ability to pull data from scanned
images, PDFs and even handwritten text. This solution reduces the burden of cost and
technology implementation and can support exchange in direct secure messaging or FHIR.
To achieve digital maturity, we must look for ways to transform all forms of data—including
unstructured data, or data that isn't captured or stored in a standardized format—into
actionable intelligence. Secure cloud fax can offer a pragmatic approach for achieving this state

for all organizations, including those that can't afford more advanced technologies for sharing
information.
A recent study by HIMSS shows that only 57% of healthcare data is used to make decisions
related to clinical care and operations. Data breakdowns typically stem from challenges in
transforming unstructured data (e.g., PDF attachments and handwritten comments in patient
charts or text in open-note fields of the EHR) into structured intelligence. The risk that patient
care will be compromised rises with every piece of inaccessible data.
When AI is applied to unstructured documents like digital cloud fax, organizations can gain the
ability to pull the data needed from these scanned documents. From there, organizations can
apply the data to structured fields in the electronic medical record, where those involved in a
patient's care can see it most easily. It's an instance when AI is speeding access to clinical
intelligence directly within clinicians' workflows, strengthening care decision making.
This type of innovation doesn't readily come to mind when we think about fax, but it should. It's
the type of digital superpower that is easily accessible even to organizations that fall lower on
the digital maturity scale, not only because it's affordable, but because it uses a technology
most organizations still possess: digital fax. Once a document is digital, then AI can be applied
to make it structured and actionable.
Are there ways that workflows or metrics that providers are already motivated to optimize
for that could be reused for, or combined with, efforts needed to support interoperability?
Outside of the federal government providing the funds every provider not on a certified EHR or
for hospital systems to level the technology playing field with their trading partners by using
their own investment, hospitals can be incentivized with CMS quality metrics that enhance
reimbursements for investment in a data sharing ecosystem that includes the SNFs, the
community clinics, the behavioral health providers, home health and others. This doesn’t have
to be expensive technology; intelligent data extraction and translation can facilitate this
exchange today, starting with just a digital fax.
c. Are there requirements CMS should consider for data registries to support digital quality
measurement in a more efficient manner?
Allow for all providers to populate registries, even using unstructured forms. As you look at
public health workflow, CMS could consider an intake process whereby unstructured forms can
still be accepted but this inbound traffic can have extraction technology employed and then the
structured data can be mapped to the public health registries more seamlessly. This would be
an efficient investment from the CDC so that more data can be included in public health that
would provide greater and more accurate analysis.

Blue Button is based on claims data and doesn’t include critical patient data that is not

3. Technical Standards and Certification
TD–6. What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform?
TEFCA supports QHINs that have the promise of greater query technology that will return more
patient data for immediate action, such as discharge summaries, as well as enhance
longitudinal records for patients. You cannot query, however, what is not structured. In order
for more, and sometimes critical patient data, to be queried, the data needs to be unlocked.
That can be done with intelligent data extraction technology.
Like HIEs of the past, some QHINs will fail, mostly due to bad business practices. It is hard to
understand the economic model that will be essential for QHINs to succeed. This will require an
understanding of the investment and the value stakeholders will garner in order for a revenue
model to work.
a. What existing alternatives should be considered?
Solutions that help move unstructured data to structured data that can be converted to Direct
Secure Messaging or FHIR. Such a solution can convert a digital cloud fax, scanned images and
other unstructured notes to structured data to support future communication protocols. This
provides a greater level of tech equity.

d. Given improvements in language models, would you prefer a nonproprietary but less
structured format that might improve data coverage even if it requires more processing by
the receiver?
Language models and AI are beginning to transform healthcare data sharing, especially in
bridging the gap between large health systems and smaller, less technologically equipped
providers. While not data pipelines themselves, language models and data extraction
technologies serve as powerful intermediaries — making data more accessible, understandable,
and actionable across disparate systems.
These technologies can extract key information — such as diagnoses, medications, and follow-
up instructions — from unstructured clinical text, even when formats vary across systems. Once
structured, this data can be more easily integrated into other providers' workflows, reducing
manual effort and minimizing the risk of missed details.
One of the greatest challenges in healthcare data exchange is the overwhelming volume of
unstructured documents, including prior authorizations, discharge summaries, and intake
forms. Language models and extraction tools can convert these into structured formats suitable
for payer systems, admissions, or population health management, driving true interoperability
and more informed care.
TD–9. Regarding certification of health IT:
c. How could ASTP/ONC revise health IT certification criteria to require APIs to consistently
support exchanging data from all aspects of the patient’s chart (for example, faxed records,
free text, discrete data)?
Faxed records are unstructured, so it will be necessary to extract structured data if the FHIR API
is used. That technology exists today through digital cloud fax and AI. Otherwise, fax APIs can
integrate unstructured faxes to a patient’s chart by extracting patient demographics and
document type to be automatically attached to the patient's chart without the need for human
intervention.
4. Data Exchange
TD–17. Given operational costs, what role should CMS or ASTP/ONC or both have in ensuring
viability of healthcare data sharing networks, including enough supply and demand, that results
in usage and outcomes?
Providing for participation for all providers and stakeholders is key for data sharing networks to
work. HIEs have struggled with economic models to support their networks, as many smaller
EHRs and providers can’t afford to connect.

Many of these models that are supported by payers provide incentives for providers to connect
or pay for the integration and ongoing fees. Some models are supported by larger health
systems and financed to connect the entire community.
Healthcare data sharing networks are mostly community based and not national networks that
should be financed by the federal government, excluding the framework for QHINs. The state
networks, however, need to provide access for Medicaid to participate and that can require an
investment. Supporting Medicaid’s participation and provide state funding for modernizing
Medicaid data so that it can be exchanged within a network will be necessary to so that all
patient data is accessible through data sharing networks.
