Re: Docket CMS-2025-0050
Executive Summary:
The Radiological Society of North America (RSNA) applauds federal efforts to build a truly interoperable Health
Technology Ecosystem. However, medical imaging remains insufficiently integrated into national data-sharing
initiatives today. To close this critical gap, we urge CMS, ASTP/ONC, and HHS to take two targeted actions that
will ensure every patient and provider can securely access imaging studies alongside other health data.
• Mandate Imaging Standards in Certification:
Require all certified Health IT products to support DICOMweb, HL7 FHIR Imaging profiles, and relevant
IHE integration frameworks for patient- and provider-requested image retrieval.
• Include Imaging in TEFCA QHIN Requirements:
Expand the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) Qualified Health
Information Network (QHIN) framework to formally recognize and onboard imaging-capable QHINs,
ensuring diagnostic images flow seamlessly across networks.
Request for Information; Health Technology Ecosystem
The Radiological Society of North America (RSNA) is pleased to provide this response to the Centers for
Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS), Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy (ASTP)/Office of the National
Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ONC), and the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS)
regarding their request for information (RFI) on the Health Technology Ecosystem (Docket CMS-2025-0050).
The Radiological Society of North America (RSNA) is a nonprofit organization representing over 52,000 medical
imaging professionals in more than 150 countries worldwide. The RSNA Scientific Assembly and Annual Meeting
is the world’s largest radiology conference, drawing tens of thousands of radiologists, researchers, and industry
leaders from around the globe. RSNA’s mission is to promote excellence in patient care and healthcare delivery
through education, research, and technological innovation.
RSNA strongly supports the efforts of CMS, ASTP/ONC and other federal agencies to build a more connected
healthcare technology ecosystem through initiatives such as the Health IT Certification Program, the expanding
Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) and the TEFCA nationwide framework for health information sharing.
These programs have laid the groundwork for seamless data access across health information networks.

Medical imaging data, however, remains insufficiently integrated into these efforts. While radiology is among
the most highly valued and widely used diagnostic tools, patients and their care providers still frequently do not
have a convenient way to access medical imaging studies, particularly when (as is commonly the case) the care
process spans institutional boundaries. This gap leads to unnecessary delays, inconvenience, frustration, and
expense, and seriously compromises the safety, efficiency, andquality of patient care. RSNA thus urges
ASTP/ONC to recognize imaging as a high priority in national interoperability planning and implementation.
RSNA stands ready to work with ASTP/ONC better to integrate medical imaging into the national health
information ecosystem. RSNA has a long and successful history of working to advance standards-based
interoperability in health IT.
• RSNA is a founder of the Integrating the Healthcare Enterprise (IHE) initiative, which has published
implementation guides for the use of data standards (HL7, DICOM, W3C, and others) that provide the
foundation for healthcare institutions and health information networks around the world.
• RSNA partnered with the Sequoia Project to establish the Image Share Validation Program to test
imaging systems for interoperability and conformance with data standards.
• The RSNA annual meeting has hosted multi-vendor demonstrations critical to advancing imaging
technology, including the first public demonstrations of the DICOM standards, and continues to promote
standards-based interoperability as an accelerator of innovation, most recently in the Radiology
Reimagined demonstrations that showcase the successful integration of AI tools in medical imaging.
These efforts underscore RSNA’s commitment to fostering a connected, patient-centered health information
ecosystem.
We provide our responses to the RFI’s specific questions as follows:
Questions PC-2c; PC8a; PR-3; and TD-9
Medical images are a critical component of the electronic health record and should be made conveniently and
securely accessible to patients and their providers across care settings and geographic areas. Mature data
standards and implementation guidance are in place to provide this capability today. Doing so will provide
substantial benefits in reducing redundant imaging, enabling better-informed care decisions, and expediting
care delivery.
To achieve these goals, RSNA recommends that ASTP/ONC expand the Health IT Certification Program to
mandate convenient access to medical imaging. Require certified health information technology systems to
support DICOMweb, HL7 FHIR, and IHE standards necessary to enable seamless access to medical imaging for
patients and their care providers.

Questions PC-10; PC-11; and PR-6
RSNA supports the TEFCA process and expansion of QHINs as an important pathway for enabling patient and
provider access to electronic health records. To fully realize the value of this network, it is critical that medical
imaging be included in the body of accessible data.
RSNA thus recommends that ASTP/ONC expand TEFCA to include imaging-capable QHINs. Incorporate standard
architectures for image sharing within TEFCA and formally recognize imaging-capable Qualified Health
Information Networks (QHINs).
RSNA appreciates this opportunity to provide comments around the state of the health technology ecosystem.
These targeted actions will help ensure that imaging data is an accessible component of the electronic health
record, advancing a truly interoperable and patient-centered health information ecosystem.
