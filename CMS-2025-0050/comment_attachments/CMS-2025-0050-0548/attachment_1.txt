Our work with health plans and lab providers is about making actionable, real-time lab data available to reduce unnecessary medical interventions and support evidence-based care decisions. We recognize the limitations of current data exchange practices and the need for policy improvements. Laboratory testing is a foundational component of modern healthcare and influences approximately 70% of all medical decisions. With nearly 14 billion clinical laboratory tests conducted annually in the U.S., it is the most utilized medical benefit. Given the central role of laboratory testing and CMS’s focus on making quality, actionable data available through modern technology, laboratory data must be a priority. CMS should recognize laboratory testing as the gateway to personalized care. Ensuring the interoperability and usability of diagnostic data – particularly genomic and biomarker results – is essential to timely, individualized care informed by the most precise clinical insights. Avalon offers its expertise to make the following recommendations about three RFI questions:  How important is it for all data in EHRs to be accessible for exchange, and what are the current challenges, technical barriers, and privacy or cost implications for making that data available?  Should ASTP/ONC consider removing or revising any of the information blocking exceptions to further the access, exchange, and use of electronic health information (EHI) and to promote market competition?  How can CMS encourage providers and payers to submit information blocking complaints to ASTP/ONC’s Information Blocking Portal? 1. Mandate a universal standard for the transfer of all clinical information to improve the usability and reliability of data. While the 21st Century Cures Act’s API mandates create technical pathways for data exchange through FHIR and USCDI standards, they primarily address the avenues for data transmission rather than transmission of clinically meaningful data, which requires consistency in how laboratory data is recorded, transmitted, and interpreted. Current implementation varies in terminology mapping, value set adoption, and clinical context preservation, creating data that is technically accessible but clinically unreliable. CMS's Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final Rule reinforces this need by requiring standardized FHIR-based APIs, yet semantic interoperability challenges persist. CMS and ASTP/ONC should mandate comprehensive semantic interoperability requirements, including required terminologies, standardized units of measure, and consistent reference ranges to transform fragmented data into actionable clinical intelligence. This comprehensive approach would ensure that laboratory results are interpreted consistently across all payers and providers, reducing medical errors and spending on inappropriate testing while facilitating seamless care coordination throughout a patient’s entire health journey. 2. Strengthen enforcement of information blocking provisions to ensure timely access to complete datasets. Current information blocking enforcement allows penalties up to $1 million per violation, yet varying investigation procedures and response timeframes create uncertainty that undermines deterrent effects. Create a minimum security framework to protect patient data across all healthcare ecosystem entities handling PII/PHI . As an entity that exchanges critical diagnostic information between providers and payers, Avalon recognizes that inconsistent security requirements create vulnerability gaps and undermine patient trust . While HIPAA establishes security requirements for covered entities and business associates, third-party applications accessing patient data through FHIR APIs, health information intermediaries, and digital health product developers operate under varying security standards. A standardized security framework would democratize access to healthcare data for vetted developers, shifting competitive focus from data acquisition challenges to delivering clinical value. Avalon recognizes this requires striking a balance between privacy protections and innovation. While emerging technologies and digital health tools introduce novel privacy risks, future regulatory changes should not prevent the development of advanced technologies or the use of tools that can improve disease prevention and chronic disease management. With laboratory data driving most medical decisions, addressing these foundational challenges will unlock the full potential of digital health technologies to improve patient outcomes and reduce healthcare costs for Medicare beneficiaries.
