On behalf of Alliance of Wound Care Stakeholders (“Alliance”), Chair Dr. Matthew G. Garoufalis, Vice Chair
Kara Couch and I are pleased to provide our recommendations on the Administrations’ request for information
on the Health Technology Ecosystem. The Alliance is a nonprofit multidisciplinary trade association of
physician medical specialty societies, clinical and patient associations, wound care provider groups, wound care
clinics and wound care product manufacturers whose mission is to promote quality care and access to products
and services for people with wounds through effective advocacy and educational outreach in the regulatory,
legislative, and public arenas.i
The 21st Century Cures Act of 2016 included several provisions intended to address barriers to electronic data
sharing, reduce administrative and cognitive burden associated with certified health information technologies,
and hold actors accountable for blocking or inhibiting the flow of patient data throughout the health care
system. However, the demand and dependencies on timely and high-quality electronic health information across
the health care and technology innovation ecosystems have grown, actors are not being held accountable for
blocking or inhibiting the flow of patient data, and the health care delivery system is changing - all pointing to
the necessity for change. The Alliance appreciates the focus the Agency is taking to address gaps and challenges
in data sharing and our health data infrastructure.
This work is not only essential to empowering patients with a full view of their digital health record and
clinicians with the information they need to make informed care decisions, but it also helps to support
biomedical innovation, improve public health responses, and can and will modernize coverage.
Accelerating the adoption and use of data standards, protecting individuals’ privacy, and readying the healthcare
ecosystem for adoption and implementation of artificial intelligence tools are all important topics to be
addressed in order to address remaining gaps and existing challenges with data sharing. Our comments on the
RFI follow.

General Comments
The Alliance has the following general comments:
1. Medicare insurance and drug programs are difficult to navigate when a Medicare beneficiary is trying to
choose a plan that works. Most seniors have a difficult time navigating all of the choices, what they
mean and what is the best plan for them. Most seniors are not technologically savvy and cannot do web
searches. Even so, many plans are not transparent in the benefits being offered and/or the services
covered especially in the managed care space and therefore the searches are often complicated. While
this is not a wound care specific issue, it is an area in which we are all impacted and our clinical
community often has to address when treating their patients who have been led to believe that their
wound care treatments will continue to be covered when, in fact, many plans do not cover or
significantly limit the services being provided. We believe that tools need to be created that are
transparent, easy to use and accessible to all Medicare beneficiaries. In addition, the plan options need
to be more simplistic and easier to understand.
2. Accessing our own health records is often challenging and does not allow for care coordination unless
we as patients are the coordinators of information (conduit). Our health records should be accessible to
all providers on our care team/journey. It should not be necessary to bring paper copies and multiple
discs of our records each and every time we see a new provider in order for them to understand the
condition in which we are seeking their care.
3. It appears that electronic health record developers are often the barrier to effective care coordination as
well as data collection (quality metrics etc.). This is hugely problematic not only for care coordination
but for facility implementation of quality metrics to ensure that patients are receiving the best care
possible.
4. Patients are not always very compliant with plans of care. The medical model is moving more care into
the patient’s home and therefore, monitoring the patient is becoming increasingly important. The
Alliance supports implementing a comprehensive digital health ecosystem that integrates telehealth
services, remote patient monitoring, and community-based health applications as well as the
adoption/coverage of devices that will help to guide a patients’ plan of care including Artificial
Intelligence (AI). The system should incorporate any and all remote monitoring devices such as blood
pressure cuffs, glucose monitors, and pulse oximeters that transmit data directly to healthcare teams for
timely interventions as well as point of care imaging technologies for wound care patients (we are not
referring to portable x-rays but rather point of care imaging for wound care patients to address the size
of the wound as well as bacteria, bioburden, and infection).
5. Managing different workflows, processes, and requirements for different types of plans is becoming
increasingly difficult and burdensome. Pushing alignment across various government plans as well as
commercial plans would be extremely helpful in many areas including:
• Interoperability requirements
• Prior authorization requirements
• Price transparency requirements
• Reporting requirements
There are some requirements that already extend across all or most plan types (i.e. provisions in the No
Surprises Act), but many data, interoperability, and health IT requirements imposed by law and subsequent
2

regulations are only applicable to government plans (one or more of Medicare, Medicaid, CHIP, exchange
plans, etc.) and sometimes there are inconsistencies and differences between the various government plans.
This makes it challenging and burdensome for providers with patients spanning different types of payer
coverage.

Patients and Caregivers
PC-3: Are you aware of health management, care navigation, or personal health record apps that would be
useful to Medicare beneficiaries and their caregivers?
There are several health management, care navigation, and personal health record (PHR) applications that could
be highly beneficial to Medicare beneficiaries and their caregivers, particularly in the context of chronic wound
care. Both remote patient monitoring and point of care imaging devices exemplify digital health products
supporting individuals and providers across care settings by leveraging AI and technological integration to
provide predictive health insights as well as information to help inform plans of care aligning with CMS’s goals
of improved health outcomes and provider burden reduction. These tools can support better self-management,
enhance care coordination, and reduce preventable complications—especially for older adults managing
complex conditions like diabetic foot ulcers, venous leg ulcers, or pressure injuries. These apps/tools include
the following:
1. Health Management Apps with Wound Care Device Integration
Apps that integrate with advanced wound care devices can help patients and caregivers track healing progress,
medication adherence, and signs of complications:
• Point of Care Wound Imaging platforms like Mimosa, Modulim, Spectral, Swift Medical, MolecuLight,
or Intellicure use smartphone or tablet cameras, often with 3D capability or AI algorithms, to monitor
wound size and tissue health over time. Some allow patients or caregivers to capture wound images at
home, which can be remotely assessed by clinicians. These devices with their associated platforms can
serve as an extension of care between visits and reduce unnecessary clinic travel for Medicare patients
with mobility issues. Some devices can also detect infection and bioburden that are not always seen by
the naked eye thus allowing clinicians to establish and/or alter care plans so that the treatment is the
most beneficial to the patient.
• These platforms often provide dashboards and alerts, enabling real-time remote monitoring by
clinicians—making them valuable tools for home health providers and care navigators managing
patients in rural or underserved areas.
2. Care Navigation and Coordination Tools
Apps that support shared care plans and care team communication are particularly important for Medicare
beneficiaries, who often receive care from multiple providers.

device use—key adherence factors in wound healing.
• Some apps enable two-way communication between patients, caregivers, and wound care specialists,
helping to escalate care early if healing stalls or infections emerge.
3. Personal Health Record (PHR) Apps Commented [KR2]: Are there any others that I should put in
here?
PHR platforms such as Apple Health or MyChart enable beneficiaries or their caregivers to store, access, and
share wound care-related data across providers:
• These platforms are useful for organizing care notes, wound imaging, medication lists, and treatment
milestones.
• Medicare patients frequently transition between care settings (e.g., from hospital to skilled nursing to
home health), and PHRs can help ensure continuity—especially when wound care protocols are complex
or customized.
In order to promote adoption of these types of apps/tools, the Alliance recommends that CMS could:
• Encourage integration of wound care device data into PHR and care navigation platforms, using
standards like FHIR to support interoperability.
• Develop a standard for the shared information as the ability to exchange information is not enough.
• Provide technical and financial incentives to wound care clinics and home health agencies that engage
patients using these tools.
• Evaluate the inclusion of digital wound care platforms in Chronic Care Management (CCM) or Remote
Therapeutic Monitoring (RTM) billing structures to ensure they are accessible to Medicare populations.
Apps that connect wound care devices with patient self-management and care navigation tools can significantly
enhance outcomes for Medicare beneficiaries. These tools empower both patients and caregivers to play an
active role in healing, prevent complications, and improve communication across the care continuum.
PC-5: What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in
these digital health products?
CMS can encourage adoption through clear and comprehensive reimbursement incentives, education, and
reimbursement parity for remote patient monitoring as well as point of care imaging devices.
Lack of a clear reimbursement pathways and coverage policies for these types of devices remains a significant
barrier to provider adoption and patient access. We believe that CMS’s leadership in establishing
reimbursement frameworks and their integration into clinical care would greatly accelerate the deployment and
utility of predictive digital health tools.

PC-8: What specific opportunities and challenges exist to improve accessibility, interoperability and
integration of clinical data from different sources to enable more meaningful clinical research and generation
of actionable evidence?
CMS should end the program of EHR certification. The testing protocols are sufficiently divorced from the
functions that they are supposed to test that many “certified” EHRs cannot perform those functions. The US
Wound Registry (USWR), a CMS endorsed QCDR, attempted to do basic quality reporting for 32 different
(mostly podiatry) EHRs and 31 of them could not transmit quality data in the required format. Indeed, none of
them could even produce a Continuity of Care Document (CCD). Thus, the certification process is a farce. The
process of de-certifying an EHR is so burdensome that it is not worth reporting these deficiencies. Technical
certification should not be handled via the regulatory process. The market can and should manage this.

1. Reimburse for Use of Digital Tools That Support High-Quality Care
CMS can expand and clarify reimbursement pathways for digital health tools that demonstrably improve
outcomes. For example:
• Point of Care Wound Imaging platforms that use AI or 3D technology to assess wound size, depth, and
healing trajectory can reduce the need for in-person visits, standardize assessments, and trigger early
interventions. Yet, these tools are often underutilized because their use is not explicitly reimbursed.
CMS should develop specific billing codes (e.g., for image capture, interpretation, and care planning)
for approved wound care imaging tools, especially when used in chronic wound populations like
diabetic foot and venous stasis ulcers as well as in pressure injuries.
• RPM platforms can enable continuous monitoring of wound progression, off-loading adherence, or vital
signs (e.g., glucose control in diabetic patients), which is especially important for rural patients who face
transportation barriers. Expanding the scope of RPM and Chronic Care Management (CCM) billing
codes to include more wound-related use cases would incentivize adoption in both rural and urban
settings.
2. Support Access and Training for Rural Providers
Rural providers often lack the bandwidth—both technical and operational—to integrate new tools. CMS can
help by:
• Providing financial support or grants through CMMI or HRSA to offset upfront costs (e.g., devices,
software licenses, connectivity infrastructure).
• Supporting technical assistance and training programs, possibly in partnership with regional extension
centers, to educate clinicians and staff on how to integrate imaging and RPM into existing workflows.
• Encouraging partnerships with home health agencies or rural health clinics that can serve as hubs for
digital data capture, enabling patients to access imaging tools locally even if their primary provider is
remote.
3. Streamline Integration Through Interoperability and Standardization
Providers are more likely to adopt tools that work seamlessly with their existing EHRs and documentation
systems. CMS should:
• Promote FHIR-based implementation guides specifically for wound care imaging and RPM data
7

streams, allowing these tools to feed structured data into clinical documentation and quality reporting
frameworks.
• Encourage EHR vendors to offer plug-and-play integration with certified digital health products by tying
such capabilities to CEHRT requirements or through public-private partnerships which they are
currently not supporting or are charging providers substantive amounts of money to integrate.
4. Link Digital Tool Use to Quality Measures and Value-Based Payment
Finally, CMS should recognize and reward the use of digital health products as part of value-based care
delivery:
• For example, providers using wound imaging to document healing progression could meet "Appropriate
Use" quality measures, such as documentation of wound stage, off-loading adherence, or infection
surveillance.
• Use of RPM to detect early complications could be tied to cost savings metrics and reduced
hospitalizations—especially relevant in rural areas where timely in-person care is limited.
• Reward patients for better controlling their blood pressure, glucose, foot pressure or oxygenation at
night. Pilot programs that tried this with even modest incentives have been successful.
PR-2. What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective utilization of the most useful and
innovative applications for physician workflows, such as quality measurement reporting, clinical
documentation, and billing tasks? How could these obstacles be mitigated?
Barriers in the Development and Deployment of Digital Tools
Significant barriers hinder the development and deployment of digital health tools. These barriers include
fragmented data standards, lack of standardized APIs that support both structured and unstructured data, and
administrative burdens. CMS can address these challenges by promoting open, standards-based APIs like FHIR,
and supporting bulk data access for efficient quality measurement reporting.
Additionally, work-flow saving tools that improve clinical efficiency—such as automated coding and voice-
enabled documentation—are often not reimbursed. Despite their proven value, the lack of payment mechanisms
discourages adoption. Expanding reimbursement codes for technology-enabled services (e.g., for time saved)
could allow for greater use.
Challenges in Quality Measurement and QCDR Integration
In 2014, the US Wound Registry (USWR), in collaboration with the Alliance, developed wound-care-specific
quality measures for its Qualified Clinical Data Registry (QCDR). Although these were made freely available
and engaged with EHR vendors in order for these measures to be integrated into their EHR systems, the vendors
refused to integrate them unless physicians paid large fees. With little incentive to adopt these measures,
clinicians instead continued reporting on measures entirely irrelevant to their practice. Even perfect
performance often yields bonuses of less than 2%. The result: specialty specific QCDR measures – while well-
designed – fail. This failure is not due to the measures being poorly designed, but rather due to barriers of
incorporation within the EHR, the high integration costs and the lack of a clear link between quality of care and
payment.
8

Systemic Failures in MIPS and Quality Reporting
The current Merit-based Incentive Payment System (MIPS) is fundamentally flawed. The standard MIPS
measures are often too generic or too narrowly focused. Studies have shown MIPS scores are no more effective
at distinguishing good from poor care than chance. Evidence also suggests MIPS may have decreased the
quality of patient care, particularly for vulnerable populations, and it has dramatically increased physician work
and overhead costs.
Furthermore, CMS has historically undervalued process measures—even when these processes (e.g., assessing
nutritional status before advanced wound care) are directly linked to improved outcomes. The refusal to
recognize such links undermines quality of care. Simple processes around which there is no debate such as off-
loading diabetic foot ulcers cannot obtain national MIPS certification because the studies proving the benefit of
this vital, basic care are deemed “too small.” Essential, evidence-based practices such as off-loading diabetic
foot ulcers or compressing venous leg ulcers are excluded for lacking "sufficient" trial size, even when studies
with 100+ patients clearly demonstrate their importance and show clear benefit. In fact, relatively small trials in
wound care (e.g. 100 patients) that clearly demonstrate the importance of adequate compression to the healing
of venous leg ulcers and adequate off-loading of diabetic foot ulcers were deemed by the National Quality
Forum as being “too small to be reliable for the development of quality measures.” However, in wound care,
many of the “process” measures are interventions that lead to healed wounds and are low-cost for which
multimillion dollar trials will never be performed.
FHIR and Interoperability: Gaps in Execution
Even when quality measures are built as SMART Apps using the FHIR standard, major EHR vendors
frequently block implementation by charging prohibitive fees. While FHIR has potential, it currently lacks the
specificity needed for real-world application. A successful system requires focused implementation guides—for
example, starting with standardized definitions of vital signs. Without these, data may transmit, but its
destination and use remain unclear. Ambiguity like the existence of hundreds of ways to label a “progress
note”—renders implementation impractical.
A Flawed Quality Infrastructure
The quality reporting system is deeply perverse. Clinicians are not required to report poor performance, so only
the measures with the highest scores are reported. When measure scores are consistently high (inevitable given
that only high scores are reported) the measure is considered “topped out” and the measure is retired.
Meanwhile, thousands of practitioners cannot or do not perform the needed quality action. Though MVPs
(MIPS Value Pathways) offer a more structured measure set, cost measurement methodologies are so inaccurate
they are often meaningless. The entire cost reporting framework, in its current form, needs a complete overhaul.
PR-7. What strategies can CMS implement to support providers in making high-quality, timely, and
comprehensive healthcare data available for interoperability in the digital product ecosystem?
The Alliance suggests further leveraging the investment in healthcare information technology (HIT) to
implement Medicare payment policy at the point of care, decreasing the cost of expensive auditing programs by
increasing the efficiency of human auditors, while ensuring that patients get the treatment they need.
9

Currently, both prior authorization (used in Medicare Advantage) and post-payment audits (used by Medicare
Administrative Contractors for Fee-for-Service patients) are expensive and inconsistent. Review processes vary
widely, and auditor checklists often include inaccuracies. These issues have significantly contributed to the
backlog of cases awaiting review by Administrative Law Judges.
We believe that HIT presents a better alternative. Clinicians could be offered a choice: participate in prior
authorization or pre-payment reviews, or instead submit “Appropriate Use” quality measures for high-cost
treatments—such as cellular or tissue-based products (CTPs) for wound care. These measures could reflect the
core coverage requirements, like diabetic foot ulcer offloading, venous ulcer compression, arterial and
nutritional assessments (all of which are processes but required for coverage).
Clinicians would report appropriate use quality measure data to a QCDR as part of a “quality improvement”
project under the terms of MIPS. Their performance data could be made public and linked to Medicare payment
adjustments if they fall below established, achievable benchmarks. Clinicians with good quality measure
performance would be exempt from post payment reviews which can delay payment for years, or prior
authorization which often deny needed treatments unfairly.
PR-7: How can the burden of increasing data availability and sharing be mitigated for providers?
Many certified EHRs are unable to meet the requirements of certification. Many are unable to transmit even a
simple Continuity of Care Document (CCD) but there are no consequences for them when they are unable to
transmit the required data in the required format. Many barriers to data sharing are actually due to poorly
designed EHRs that are unable to meet certification specifications.
PR-8. What are ways CMS or partners can help with simplifying clinical quality data responsibilities of
providers?
The Alliance suggests the following:
• Require EHRs to incorporate QCDR measures that are available as SMART apps.
• Reward clinicians who report difficult to pass appropriate use quality measures in lieu of easy to pass,
simplistic measures such as reporting BMI and “counseling” patients.

Value Based Care
VB-1: What incentives could encourage APMs such as accountable care organizations (ACOs) or participants
in Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) to leverage digital health management and care navigation
products more often and more effectively with their patients? What are the current obstacles preventing
broader digital product adoption for patients in ACOs?
Value-based care models can drive the adoption of digital health by integrating predictive tools into clinical
work flows, supported by aligned incentives and technical standards. In the wound care, such tools can help
clinicians assess the likelihood of wound healing and identify the most appropriate products to support optimal
outcomes.
AI-assisted Clinical Documentation Integrity tools can streamline chart reviews, identify undocumented
diagnoses, and improve the accuracy of diagnosis-related group assignment and risk adjustment, key
components of value-based care. However, AI systems must be used with caution, as they can misinterpret
clinical nuance, trigger inappropriate compliance queries, or overwhelm providers with unnecessary alerts,
increasing burnout. Additionally, reliance on AI without human clinical oversight erodes professional judgment
and may introduce unintended bias. CMS should encourage innovation in AI while ensuring transparency,
clinical oversight, and enforcing adherence to CDI and query guidelines - ensuring AI enhances rather than
undermines ethical, patient-centered, and data driven care.
VB-2. How can key themes and technologies such as artificial intelligence, population health analytics, risk
stratification, care coordination, usability, quality measurement, and patient engagement be better integrated
into APM requirements?
The Alliance offers the following strategic integration priorities:
1. Artificial Intelligence (AI)
• Leverage AI for decision support, risk coding, and predictive analytics.
• Require transparency in algorithms and encourage CMS-certified AI use in clinical workflows.
• Pilot AI-driven models within the CMS Innovation Center to validate impact.
2. Population Health Analytics
• Mandate analytics platforms to support proactive care and performance tracking.
• Reward organizations integrating social determinants of health and claims-EHR data for full
population visibility.
3. Risk Stratification
• Standardize tools for segmenting patients by clinical and social complexity.
• Align stratification outputs with performance benchmarks and shared savings thresholds.
4. Care Coordination
11

• Tie reimbursement to shared care planning, closed-loop referrals, and telehealth use.
• Promote partnerships with community-based organizations and non-clinical care partners.
5. Usability
• Embed usability requirements into health IT certification for APM participation.
• Encourage use of modular SMART-on-FHIR applications that reduce cognitive load and
documentation fatigue.
6. Quality Measurement
• Replace burdensome reporting with automated digital quality measures (dQMs) extracted from
structured EHR fields.
• Support AI-assisted quality scoring with provider oversight.
7. Patient Engagement
• Incentivize adoption of patient-facing technologies (e.g., PROs, remote monitoring, secure
messaging).
• Integrate shared decision-making tools and longitudinal patient feedback into performance metrics.
Conclusion
The Alliance appreciates the opportunity to provide our feedback on this request for information. We urge
CMS to adopt policies fostering reimbursement, interoperability, provider education, and infrastructure support
to unlock the full potential of predictive health digital tools. These steps will drive improved patient outcomes,
lower wasted costs, increase access to care at home, and facilitate value-based care delivery.
