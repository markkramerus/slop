Executive Summary
Leidos thanks the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) and the Assistant
Secretary for Technology Policy/Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information
Technology (hereafter ASTP/ONC) for the opportunity to respond to the Request for
Information (RFI) during the current public comment period.
As CMS pursues a bold agenda to enhance transparency, drive value-based outcomes,
empower patients, expand accountable care, and modernize the U.S. healthcare
ecosystem, it’s clear thoughtful application of technology can be an essential enabler.
From digital health tools that support chronic disease prevention to open, standards-based
platforms that improve interoperability and transparency, today’s innovations can address
longstanding challenges in healthcare delivery.
To modernize healthcare, CMS should emphasize:
• Reducing administrative burden,
• Expanding access to actionable health data, and
• Fostering AI-driven insights to support clinical decision-making, fraud detection,
and population health strategies.
These priorities closely align with Leidos’ commitment to harnessing the power of smarter
technologies and integrated teams to help achieve the shared goals of improved
outcomes, better experiences, lower costs, and equitable access to care.
Leidos, a Fortune 500® company, leverages its extensive expertise in health technologies to
deliver impactful, outcome-based solutions that address today's healthcare challenges.
Recognized as a Top 10 Health IT provider, Leidos integrates engineering, science, and
clinical insights to develop scalable solutions for hospitals, health systems, and
biomedical organizations. Supporting every U.S. federal health agency, Leidos deploys
advanced electronic health record (EHR) management, agile and DevOps-based practices,
and innovative IT environments to enhance clinical, operational, and financial outcomes.
Leidos’ response to this public comment RFI addresses the needs of Patients and
Caregivers (PC), Providers (PR), and Technology Developers/Vendors (TD). We appreciate
the chance to provide input and look forward to receiving feedback as part of an ongoing
dialogue focused on health quality measures for providers, large-scale technology trends,
and human-to-human interactions

PART B: Patients and Caregivers
1. Patient Needs
PC-1. What health management or care navigation apps would help you understand
and manage your (or your loved ones) health needs, as well as the actions you should
take?
We see a growing number of mobile apps that are designed to support patients in these
efforts, including Blue Button 2.0 Connected Apps such as iBlueButton. Yet most of these
apps still don’t provide the depth of support and features that patients need as they move
across healthcare settings. Specifically, they lack interoperability between patients’
healthcare providers, health systems, and other stakeholders.
We understand CMS’ long-term vision of interoperability and urge the agency to continue to
prioritize the patient digital health experience by supporting health policies and technology
solutions that foster the growth of interoperability.
What’s needed are applications that put interoperability and date exchange front and
center—and bring about an enhanced digital experience for the beneficiary. Compliance
with FHIR standards forms the foundation to allow seamless data sharing across providers,
payers, and public health system. On top of that, mobile applications should have the
ability to aggregate data from EHRs, claims, pharmacy, and social services.
Patients should be able to share personal health information with any healthcare
stakeholder at any time, without fear that information from one physician on a different
network will go missing—or prove difficult to obtain. Patients using consumer healthcare
apps to track their heart rate or blood sugar levels, or send messages to physicians should
feel confident that they won’t have to repeat themselves as they move across the care
continuum. We also encourage CMS to continue to support the development of digital
health solutions (like consumer-facing apps) and other technologies including telehealth,
messaging, scheduling, and patient portals.
To stimulate the growth of digital health, CMS should consider ways to incentivize its
partners to develop and roll out additional, robust features that can be incorporated into
patient-facing communications, such as AI voice-enabled technologies, to optimize return
on investment.

PC-2. Do you have easy access to your own and all your loved ones' health information
in one location (for example, in a single patient portal or another software system)?
Accessing health information is often challenging for patients and their caregivers,
primarily due to a lack of interoperability.
CMS beneficiaries would benefit from apps that synthesize medical and clinical data
(including historical health information) so it’s accessible at a moment’s notice. The
challenge is that even the most comprehensive apps do not have the ability to share
information across the continuum of care or display healthcare data in a caregiver-friendly
format.
As CMS works to improve patient access, the agency must also focus on improving
caregivers’ experiences (e.g., spouses of patients with a primary diagnosis that requires
round-the-clock care). Caregivers need to have easy access to health information at a
moment’s notice.
We suggest CMS and its partners continue strengthening digital health workflows that
enable the transmission and receipt of critical, time-sensitive information to caregivers as
needed (e.g., when a physician needs to determine whether to prescribe an opiate for pain
for a patient with a history of substance-use disorders).
We also see the benefit of CMS adopting smarter enterprise imaging solutions, which are
built with sophisticated sharing capabilities. Currently, many legacy imaging solutions are
inefficient and lack advanced data-sharing features and require hundreds of thousands of
dollars to maintain. CMS should consider upgrading cloud-based (imaging as a service)
solutions, and/or partnering with a third-party provider that can leverage more
sophisticated image-capture and image-share capabilities.
PC-3. Are you aware of health management, care navigation, or personal health record
apps that would be useful to Medicare beneficiaries and their caregivers?
We are aware that a growing number of patients are using digital health—including 30% of
adults over age 65. Yet while many of these apps are useful to caregivers—such as apps
that help patients manage and adhere to medication schedules—the vast majority could
use improvement.
Due to the lack of interoperability and incentives to engage patients and caregivers,
adoption of digital health apps by patients and providers is still limited.
We recommend CMS work closely with its technology partners to roll out patient-centered
digital health solutions or encourage the development of digital products that would have a
measurable, meaningful impact on clinical outcomes. For example, utilizing the principle

PC-5. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in
these digital health products?
CMS should focus on actively marketing the benefits of digital health products directly to
healthcare providers and consumers by extending its outreach efforts. In turn, CMS should
support expansion of Medicare coverage of digital health solutions. These efforts have
proven successful in the recent past.
Case in point: when CMS initially expanded telehealth coverage in March 2020 beyond rural
territories, thousands of patients received lifesaving care they otherwise wouldn’t have
been able to access—which prevented thousands of adverse events. Today, as a result of
these telehealth policy expansions, virtual care continues to benefit a greater number of
Medicare and Medicaid beneficiaries who can now manage chronic conditions more
effectively and avoid hospitalizations.
CMS beneficiaries shouldn’t have to worry about steep out-of-pocket costs. Given ongoing
physician shortages in most of the U.S., we should continue to grow digital health solutions
that offer a clear, proven return on investment.
Swift action now to promote use of smart, digital health technologies will pay dividends far
into the future.
In addition to supporting the expansion of coverage to digital health, and amplifying
marketing efforts to patients and providers, CMS should continue to champion health
policies and technology innovations that further the growth of interoperability.

PC-10. How is the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common AgreementTM (TEFCATM)
currently helping to advance patient access to health information in the real world?
Since going live in December 2023, TEFCA has gone from a great idea to a promising real-
world framework that facilitates nationwide health information exchange among
designated Qualified Health Information Networks (QHINs). We are encouraged to see use
of the TEFCA exchange accelerating, with the retrieval of more than nine million documents
and counting, according to recent estimates from The Sequoia Project.
We suggest that CMS continue to support TEFCA and incentivize network participation and
work with partners committed to building out interoperability frameworks, such as Leidos.
PC-11. How are health information exchanges (HIEs) currently helping to advance
patient access to health information in the real world?
User adoption of HIEs has not reached its full potential. Even in states like New York and
Vermont, which have higher success rates with HIE adoption, onboarding providers
remains challenging. As studies like this have pointed out, there is a notable gap between
hospitals' adoption and the actual use of different HIE methods to send inside information
to outside organizations, largely due to usage barriers (e.g., lack of training, inadequate
funding, lack of infrastructure, privacy concerns, etc. ).
Additional funding, support, and training, therefore, will be necessary to sustain the
momentum around HIEs. We suggest creating additional incentives to providers, perhaps
through vehicles such as the Merit-Based Incentive Payment System (MIPS) program,
Accountable Care Organizations (ACO), technical assistance grants, or HIT modernization
funding, to encourage participation from smaller and/or rural providers to connect to HIEs.
3. Information Blocking and Digital Identity
PC-13. How can CMS encourage patients and caregivers to submit information
blocking complaints to ASTP/ONC's Information Blocking Portal? What would be the
impact? Would increasing reporting of complaints advance or negatively impact data
exchange?
The best way to improve user engagement is by raising awareness of the issue of
information blocking—and its consequences. The process to submit complaints must use
clear examples and plain language for non-technical users. We also recommend the use of
Sentiment Analysis technologies on submitted complaints to more effectively categorize,
summarize and identify focus areas and trends.

PART C: PROVIDERS
1. Digital Health Apps
PR-1. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage providers, including those in
rural areas, to leverage approved (see description in PC-5) digital health products for
their patients?
While information-sharing technologies have evolved considerably, thus encouraging
provider adoption of digital health apps, there are still a few obstacles CMS needs to
address to boost provider engagement.
Challenges such as patient-record infrastructure and connectivity limitations, financial
constraints, reimbursement, and the pressure to do more with less plague providers in all
healthcare settings. Other challenges, such as information blocking, as well as a lack of
interoperability, continue to stymie adoption of all digital health products.
With projected physician shortages of 40,800 to 120,000 by 2030, according to the
Association of American Medical Colleges (AAMC), now is the time to double down on
growing digital health solutions that work.
CMS should continue its efforts to foster innovation within the health technology
ecosystem, enabling organizations to create digital health products for providers to care for
their patients. The agency must continue to support interoperability, to protect the
continuity of care: Providers should know that patient-encounter data is visible at every
touchpoint of their experience, across the care continuum. Every time a clinician checks a
patient’s vitals, that data should become immediately available from that point forward,
whether the patient’s next visit is in the same facility or a different state.
It’s impossible to convince patients to use digital health products without fully embracing
and promoting interoperability.
Incentives also play a crucial role in generating physician buy in to any initiative, as we’ve
seen since the onset of the EHR Incentive Program. In tandem with rolling out healthcare
policies that incentivize providers for adopting and implementing technologies, CMS could
work directly with third-party vendors to develop systems and solutions that demonstrate
clear clinical and financial benefits.

PR-2. What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective
utilization of the most useful and innovative applications for physician workflows,
such as quality measurement reporting, clinical documentation, and billing tasks?
How could these obstacles be mitigated?
The key barriers to development, deployment, and utilization of digital health applications
include cost, accessibility, and provider adoption. We believe CMS should address provider
challenges and risks through various mechanisms, such as a formal mentoring program,
award recognition programs, annual achievement awards, training programs, structured
lines of communication, and online portals.
Under the National Healthcare Safety Network (NHSN), Leidos has created a variety of
technology solutions that meet the provider where they are to facilitate a variety of data
delivery and sharing mechanisms.
PR-3. How important is it for healthcare delivery and interoperability in urban and rural
areas that all data in an EHR system be accessible for exchange, regardless of storage
format (for example, scanned documents, faxed records, lab results, free text notes,
structured data fields)?
Ensuring that providers have the necessary data to accurately diagnose and improve the
quality of patient care, regardless of format or location, remains paramount.
Integration allows data in an EHR to be accessible. While HIEs can facilitate integration
between providers, getting disparate systems to communicate efficiently can prove
challenging.
We support CMS in promoting standards for communicating within the healthcare
environment, such as using Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine (DICOM)
standards for the movement and storage of medical imaging data, or Clinical Document
Architecture (CDA) for document-based standards for clinical summaries. Even with the
use of standards, providers must plan to enhance their security measures to protect
patients' Personally Identifiable Information (PII), ensuring only those required or
consented to access the data can do so.

PR-4. What changes or improvements to standards or policies might be needed for
patients' third-party digital products to have access to administrative workflows, such
as auto-populating intake forms, viewing provider information and schedules, and
making and modifying an appointment?
CMS needs to push for an entirely digital, seamless patient-provider experience across the
healthcare continuum. This includes everything from patient intake forms to lab tests and
results, to data-rich image files.
Organizations must update changes that focus on API access, data availability, consent,
and governance. Securing patients' PHI/PII information remains the utmost priority when

every application and user, micro-segmenting APIs to control access to information, and
using explicit user consent for data access with full audit and reporting.

PR-8. What are ways CMS or partners can help with simplifying clinical quality data
responsibilities of providers?
We recommend the following strategies to simplify clinical quality data responsibilities:
1. Standardize and Streamline Data Collection: Continue to develop and promote a
core set of standardized quality measures across all reporting programs (e.g., MIPS,
ACOs).
2. Automate Data Extraction and Reporting: Expand the use of APIs (FHIR) to enable
seamless data exchange between EHRs, registries, and CMS systems.
3. Reduce Redundant Reporting: Implement a "collect once, use many times"
approach to data management by allowing providers to submit data once and use it
for multiple reporting programs.
4. Focus on Patient-Centered Outcomes: Shift the focus from process measures to
outcome measures that are more meaningful to patients.
5. Collaborate with Stakeholders: Engage with providers, EHR vendors, and other
stakeholders to identify and address the challenges of quality reporting.

3. Digital Identity
PR-9. How might CMS encourage providers to accept digital identity credentials (for
example, CLEAR, ID.me, Login.gov) from patients and their partners instead of
proprietary logins that need to be tracked for each provider relationship?
We recommend a multi-pronged approach to encourage providers to use publicly
acceptable digital identity credentials. CMS should focus on the creation of a secure,
standards-based digital identity framework that is based on NIST 800-63-3 (IAL2/AAL2) tied
together with OAuth 2.0, and OpenID Connect. This standardized approach to digital
identity across CMS programs could improve interoperability by enabling consistent,
authorized access to patient data across different platforms and services, facilitating
better continuity of care and compliance with privacy regulations such as HIPAA.
This approach includes updating standards and certifications to align with ONC Health IT
Certification Criteria, which would require EHR vendors to support OAuth 2.0/OpenID
Connect. This update will enable patients to use federal digital identity providers, such as
Login.gov, that enable patients to access their own health information.
To encourage providers to accept identify credentials, CMS will need to communicate their
benefits. This would include showing that fewer manual processes related to account
creation, identity verification, and password resets would be needed within the provider
environment. Identity credentials also minimize the need to manage multiple proprietary
login systems, reducing overhead and improving workflow efficiency. When patients can
securely authenticate themselves using a trusted digital credential, providers gain faster
access to accurate intake forms, insurance data, and consent records that then allow
providers to focus on clinical care rather than administrative validation.
PR-11. How could members of trust communities [3] (for example, QHINs, participants
and subparticipants in TEFCA, which requires Identity Assurance Level 2 (IAL2) via
Credential Service Providers (CSPs)) better support the goals of reduced provider and
patient burden while also enhancing identity management and security?
Trust communities should support federated identity models that allow for reusable,
verified credentials across networks. This effectively enhances security and user
convenience, and interoperability. Credential Service Providers (CSPs) meeting NIST IAL2
standards, such as Login.gov or ID.me, enable providers and patients to securely access
multiple services without maintaining separate logins for each system, reducing friction
and eliminating redundant identity verification across organizations.

We support CMS’ commitment to secure equitable digital identity frameworks and
recommend several enhancements to existing efforts. CMS already relies on federally
approved identity providers like Login.gov and supports OAuth 2.0/OpenID Connect
standards through its FHIR-based APIs. However, further steps are needed to broaden
adoption, reduce beneficiary burden, and strengthen the digital identity ecosystem across

Leidos recommends that CMS expand support for NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2-compliant
identity proofing by developing toolkits and libraries for developers, subsidizing identity

with FAVES principles—fairness, appropriateness, validity, effectiveness, and safety.
Equally important, CMS should continue to promote OAuth 2.0 and OpenID Connect as the
baseline standards for authentication and token-based access. These technologies, when
implemented through federated models, reduce credential fatigue for users and minimize
attack surfaces. CMS could advance adoption by establishing a FHIR-based identity token
exchange architecture across its digital properties and encouraging integration with state
Medicaid systems and third-party app developers.

CMS should promote API-first development by tying financial incentives, such as

demonstrate compatibility with public implementation guides and discourage proprietary
extensions that limit interoperability. To support adoption, CMS should also fund API
toolkits, developer sandboxes, and public directories of certified APIs to lower the barrier
for integration and innovation across the ecosystem.

TD-6. What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform? a. What existing

TEFCA uniquely enables a nationwide, policy-based framework for health information

TD-7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange and what are
its limitations?
USCDI has improved interoperability by providing a standardized structure for core clinical
data, fostering consistency across EHR systems and APIs. However, its scope remains
limited in supporting comprehensive, person-centered care. CMS should support the
continued expansion of USCDI to prioritize data classes relevant to home health, long-term
care, social determinants of health, and patient-reported outcomes. To better integrate
unstructured and semi-structured data, CMS should also promote the use of flexible
formats—such as HL7 CDA with embedded narrative text—that support natural language
processing (NLP) and enhance the utility of less structured clinical content.
