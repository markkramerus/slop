CMS -0042 -NC Battelle | June 16 2025 | Restricted 1 Introduction Battelle Memorial Institute (Battelle) welcomes the opportunity to provide input to CMS and ASTP/ONC on strategies to advance interoperability and health data infrastructure in support of value -based care. As the current CMS -contracted Consensus -based Entity (CBE) we write from the perspective of stakeholders implementing clinical quality measures (CQM) both for payment models focused on accountability and for payment models focused on care transformation, structural investment, and evidence generation. In these latter models , provid ers and communities are empowered to generate value through transformation infrastructure , including tools like SCAMPs (Standardized Clinical Assessment and Management Plans)1, FHIR standards for positive deviation reporting2, and registries that support learning from local variation3. Summary Recommendations In response to the RFI, we offer the following summary recommendations . Detailed responses to individual questions are included below. 1. Addressing Provider Obstacles to Quality Measurement and Workflow Optimization Key Recommendation: CMS and ASTP/ONC should consider invest ing in standards -based infrastructure that supports both accountability and transformation , including: • Adoption of Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR ®)-native artifacts (e.g., PlanDefinition, ActivityDefinition, Measure, MeasureReport), • Development of SCAMPs registries and transformation -focused decision support, • Infrastructure for capturing override reasons and positive deviations to support learning health systems. Mitigation Strategy: Prioritize Electronic Health Record ( EHR ) certification criteria, implementation guides (e.g., Making EHR Data More Available for Research and Public Health or MedMorph), and funding that encourage clinical workflows to support both outcome measurement and innovation tracking . 2. Essential Data Types for Value -Based Care To successfully participate in modern VBC models, providers and intermediaries must have access to a core set of data types supporting both accountability and transformation . Conclusion A transformation -focused health IT infrastructure offers a blueprint for aligning federal policy with local agency and innovation. CMS and ASTP/ONC can accelerate meaningful value -based care by: • Consolidating measurement infrastructure using FHIR, • Separating outcome -based accountability from care transformation responsibilities, • Supporting bi -directional data flows, and • Recognizing and funding registries, tools, and applications that enable continuous improvement. Question PR-8 What are ways CMS or partners can help with simplifying clinical quality data responsibilities of providers? For example, a. What would be the benefits and downsides of using Bulk FHIR data exports from EHRs to CMS to simplify clinical quality data submissions? Can CMS reduce the burden on providers by performing quality metrics calculations leveraging Bulk FHIR data exports? I. Key Ways CMS Can Simplify Clinical Quality Data Responsibilities for Providers 1. Separate Accountability and Transformation Workflows: o Use CMS -centralized calculation (via Bulk FHIR) for accountability -focused quality metrics (e.g., outcomes, equality , utilization). o Delegate transformation -focused measurement (e.g., appropriateness, shared decision -making, SCAMPs adherence) to provider networks using FHIR -based registries and local analytics infrastructure. 2. Adopt FHIR -Based Quality Measure APIs (e.g., DEQM, Quality Measure IG ( QMIG )): o Reduce documentation and data element mapping burden by automating computable CQMs from standardized FHIR data using CMS infrastructure. o Encourage vendors to natively support FHIR profiles aligned to CMS quality reporting programs. 3. Standardize FHIR Artifacts for Measurement Contexts: o Promote uniform FHIR representations of measurement metadata (e.g., Measure, MeasureReport) to ensure semantic consistency across sites and vendors. 4. Support Population -Level Data Use Agreements: o Enable delegated intermediaries (e.g., Health Information Exchanges or SCAMPs registries) to handle transformation -related data flows, reducing the number of reporting endpoints for providers. II. Bulk FHIR Export: Benefits and Downsides Benefits: 1. Reduces Provider Reporting Burden: o Eliminates the need for redundant data mapping , customized report generation, or vendor -specific interfaces for CMS submissions. 2. Enables Centralized Measure Calculation: o CMS can run consistent, transparent calculations across all providers using a shared measure library (e.g., Clinical Quality Framework (CQF) -ruler, Clinical Quality Language ( CQL) engine). o Reduces variation and errors due to site -specific interpretation. 3. Leverages Existing Standards Infrastructure: 
CMS -0042 -NC Battelle | June 16 2025 | Restricted 6 o Bulk FHIR is an emerging requirement under Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) , USCDI+, and payer interoperability rules — leveraging it aligns with the broader federal ecosystem. 4. Supports Equality and Population Monitoring: o Allows CMS to calculate subgroup performance and equality metrics at scale without imposing new provider workflows. Downsides / Limitations: 1. Limited Local Context: o Bulk FHIR lacks the ability to capture contextual features critical for transformation (e.g., reasons for deviations, SDH variables not in EHRs). 2. Lag in Data Readiness and Mapping: o Many EHRs still lack full support for US Core profiles, let alone accurate and complete mapping to the FHIR resources needed for high -quality CQM computation. 3. Privacy and Trust Barriers: o Providers may resist data exports to CMS without tight control over how data will be used, interpreted, or audited. 4. One-Way Flow Undermines Learning Loops: o Centralized calculation benefits to CMS but offers little immediate feedback to providers unless results are returned quickly and with actionable insights (e.g., as part of transformation dashboards). Recommendation: Hybrid Model - CMS should consider perform ing centralized quality measurement for accountability purposes using Bulk FHIR, but delegate responsibility for transformation metrics and improvement feedback loops to trusted local or regional partners (e.g., SCAMPs registries, collaboratives, or state transformation entities). This hybrid approach ensures: • Equality in oversight (federal -level accountability) • Agency in improvement (local -level transformation) • Simplification of reporting (automatic data export, fewer labor intensive burdens) • Feedback -driven learning (looping insights back through SCAMPs on FHIR) b.
