I. Clover’s Model of Care & Technology Platform
Clover was founded in 2014 with the mission of improving every life. Today, we offer MA
plans in New Jersey, Georgia, Pennsylvania, South Carolina, and Texas. As of 2025, our plans have

A. Broad Networks and Affordability
We believe that patients are best off when they see a doctor of their own
choosing—someone the patient trusts in good times and in bad to have their best interests in mind.
So unlike many other payors that funnel members through their narrow provider networks (often
filled with provider groups they have purchased over the years), we give our members broad
networks and honor their choices. In most of our plans, we double down on that commitment to
patient choice by minimizing any cost-sharing differential between in- and out-of-network providers.
Nearly all of our members are in PPO plans with large provider networks and no referral needed.
And we offer $0 to low monthly plan premiums and copays with a maximum out-of-pocket limit.

B. Cutting-Edge Technology
Clover empowers clinicians to detect and manage chronic conditions earlier through its
proprietary healthcare technology platform, Counterpart Assistant. Unlike many payors, whose
technology focus is often on the “insurance” aspect of managed care—e.g., using AI to more tightly
manage claims processing and prior authorizations—our focus is on helping clinicians improve care
quality.1
Counterpart Assistant does this by aggregating data from over 100 sources across the
healthcare system. We use AI and our machine-learning models to curate patient-specific insights
from the data based on clinical standards of care. And we deliver those insights to clinicians at the
point of care. For example:
● If pharmacy records ingested by Clover reflect that a member has a prescription that is
overdue for pickup, Counterpart Assistant can let the doctor know, so she can nudge the
patient or find out if there is some socioeconomic or other deterrent she or Clover can help
with.
● If Clover has no record of a member receiving a colorectal cancer screening in the timeline
reflected in clinical guidelines, Counterpart Assistant can let the doctor know about the
potential need and the pertinent guidelines. Within the tool, the doctor can prompt Clover
to send a screening test directly to the patient’s home.
● If lab records ingested by Clover reflect that a member has a GFR (a measure of kidney
function) suggesting possible chronic kidney disease, Counterpart Assistant can let the
doctor know so she can evaluate the patient for the condition. And if the doctor determines
the patient has chronic kidney disease, Counterpart Assistant will surface potential treatment
options for the doctor to consider, and the clinical guidelines supporting those options (e.g.,
prescribing the patient a medication known as an ACE inhibitor).

As highlighted in Questions PC-8 and PC-9 of this RFI, access to complete, high-quality
health data is essential for effective care coordination, population health, and digital innovation. But

systems. We encourage development of a FHIR-based national standard for appointment
scheduling to promote greater interoperability and alignment across platforms.

A. Reducing Data Duplication and Strengthening Provenance
Addressing core themes raised in RFI Questions PC-11 (data accuracy), PR-7 (high quality
and comprehensive data), TD-10 (effortless EHI access), and TD-13 (obstacles to full EHI), we
note that data duplication has long been a challenge in healthcare, leading to bloated patient records
and uncertainty about which version of a record is most reliable. Most recently, new duplication
patterns have emerged as more organizations participate in national exchange frameworks. For
example, after retrieving data via a network query, some organizations may include those previously
obtained records in their own outbound responses, circulating the same information multiple times
and compounding existing duplication risks.
While standards for data provenance exist, adoption and consistency remain limited. We
encourage CMS to work with ONC to incorporate detailed requirements for provenance tagging
and data reuse directly into the ONC Health IT Certification Program to ensure that exchanged
information remains accurate, deduplicated, and clinically meaningful. For example, CMS and ONC
could develop standards under which repeated patterns of data duplication would be considered a
form of information blocking.

B. Strengthening Interoperability through TEFCA and Policy Alignment
We strongly support efforts to advance TEFCA and believe additional steps could help
ensure it fulfills its role as an equitable framework for health information exchange. Our
recommendations for maximizing TEFCA’s impact directly address inquiries raised in RFI Questions
PC-10 (TEFCA’s impact on patient access), PR-6 (TEFCA’s role in provider access), and TD-6
(interoperability functions). To maximize TEFCA’s impact, we recommend:
● Broader Participation: While TEFCA remains voluntary, please explore future pathways such as
certification requirements or participation incentives that encourage adoption among
certified EHR vendors, laboratories, and other critical data holders. For example,
ambient-scribing vendors could contribute raw transcription–with the appropriate patient
consent and other guardrails–to ensure accuracy of ambient-scribing and related AI
products. One way to encourage such participation is to reduce or eliminate various
regulatory or programmatic burdens for those who use TEFCA. We are happy to discuss
various de-regulatory avenues in this regard.
● Expanding Required Exchange Purposes: We encourage expanding TEFCA-required use cases to
more accurately reflect the full scope of permissible data exchange under HIPAA,
particularly with respect to treatment and healthcare operations.8 Under the current
framework, QHINs and their participants are only required to respond to requests for
“Treatment” and “Individual Access Services.”9 This limitation impedes the flow of health
information crucial for modern healthcare delivery and forces reliance on less efficient
non-standardized methods for data exchange. Accordingly, we propose expanding the
mandated scope of “Treatment” to include critical care coordination and pre-encounter
planning activities (e.g., pre-admission planning, proactive care management, medication
adherence, care gap evaluation). Concurrently, we urge making “Healthcare Operations” a
mandated use case to directly enable the robust data flow essential for value-based care,
comprehensive population health, and quality improvement initiatives. Mandating this
exchange is essential to achieve true nationwide interoperability, fostering an effective,
patient-centered healthcare system.

● Reciprocal Data Exchange Obligations: Pertaining to RFI Questions PR-6, TD-6, TD-16, and
VB-11, we propose that to maintain trust, ensure balanced participation, and realize
nationwide interoperability, entities participating in TEFCA must be unequivocally required
to timely respond to inbound data queries. Without clear and enforceable reciprocal
obligations, the framework risks becoming a unilateral data flow, undermining care
coordination and ultimately impeding patient outcomes. Timely data response, subject to
robust privacy, security, and technical feasibility safeguards, is foundational for enabling
critical workflows like clinical decision-making, care transitions, and population health
management.
● Historical Data Access: Please enhance the Payer-to-Payer API (and Provider Access API)
rules to mandate or incentivize impacted payers to proactively transfer a beneficiary’s
historical data (up to the five-year look-back) to their subsequent plan(s) upon disenrollment.
The Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final Rule (CMS-0057-F) currently requires
that “impacted payers” (including MA plans) make five (5) years of a member’s historical
data (e.g., claims, encounters, clinical data, etc.) available to their new plan, upon member
consent, through a Payer-to-Payer API. But this requirement does not cover any plan(s)
other than the one that the patient has just left for the new plan. Given that many patients
switch plans several times within a five-year period, the existing requirement provides
significantly less than five years of patient data to the new plan. We recommend closing this
loophole and making clear that the five-year period extends to all plans that the beneficiary
was a member of within the five years, and that all such plans must automatically transfer
data to the patient-chosen plan upon appropriate notice.
● Expansion of the TEFCA Manner Exception: The current limitation of the TEFCA manner
exception creates a significant disincentive for early adoption, directly addressing concerns
about policy limitations and suggested changes to TEFCA as raised in RFI Questions PA-1
and PR-6. This exception currently requires both the actor and requestor to be connected to
TEFCA for it to apply, meaning actors may be less likely to invest if they cannot use TEFCA
as the default exchange mechanism for the majority of requests.10 To further TEFCA’s
interoperability functions and consolidate redundant channels (as discussed in TD-6), and
consistent with calls to revise exceptions to further EHI access (PR-12), we encourage
expanding this exception. Allowing actors to route EHI requests through TEFCA even if the
requester is not a TEFCA participant would reduce reliance on less standardized exchange
methods, promote a unified technical framework, and substantially advance the overarching
goal of nationwide interoperability.

ensuring secure and compliant interoperability within TEFCA, directly addressing the policy

C. Empowering Patients through Improved Access
Although patients are increasingly empowered to access their health information, many still
face significant barriers related to authentication and digital access. Password-based systems often
pose challenges for seniors or individuals without reliable digital access. To meaningfully address
these challenges, as explored in RFI Questions PC-14, PR-9, PR-10, and TD-3, we strongly advocate
requiring all Patient Access API requests to be satisfied with a NIST 800-63-3 IAL2 authenticated
request. This would standardize digital identity credentials (such as those provided by CLEAR,
Login.gov, or ID.me) for health access. This critical step, while maintaining appropriate privacy and
security protections, would support more accessible and inclusive authentication methods, help
extend the benefits of patient data access to populations often left behind by traditional credentialing
systems, and further enhance patient-centered care.
D. Clarifying the Role of Authorized Vendors Under the Information Blocking Rule
Health-technology vendors like Counterpart play a critical role in enabling providers to
deliver timely, informed treatment at the point of care. Acting under valid HIPAA-compliant
business associate agreements, we facilitate the exchange and use of electronic health information to
directly support provider-directed treatment, care coordination, and clinical quality. Unfortunately,
we have encountered restrictions or fees imposed by data holders. These restrictions limit and delay
access, even when the request is made on behalf of a covered entity for a permitted purpose.

RFI Question TD-18 (examples of information blocking practices), these practices often manifest as
