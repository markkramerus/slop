digital-hie-cms-rfi-20250616-v5.docx 3 / 10 RFI Response PATIENT AND CAREGIVER USE CASE Patient Needs – PC-2 Do you have easy access to your own and all your loved ones’ health information in one location (for example, in a single patient portal or another software system)? This is a “yes” or “no” question. The short answer is no. The longer answer is that the current ecosystem of stand-alone patient portals offers utility to patients and relatively easy online access to their health records at multiple health care enterprise locations, but not in a single patient portal, app, or other software system. Instead, their records must be retrieved from separate data silos that are disconnected from each other. As a whole this record keeping environment can be an inconvenient and cumbersome time sink for patients and caregivers. Patient portals allow a health care enterprise to provide the convenience of secure online customer access to curated patient records as held by each health care organization. By itself this is a good thing. But a single health care enterprise patient portal, whether hosting hundreds, thousands, or millions of patient records, cannot afford the extraordinary expense to build and operate sufficient health data interoperability services to fully align internal patient records with all external records about each patient. The phrase “boiling the ocean” comes to mind. Patient Needs – PC-5 What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in these digital health products? Rather than “encourage patient and caregiver interest” in digital health products, it is better for CMS to ensure and enable the flow of patient data while protecting patient rights. The agency has many opportunities to incentivize adoption or optimization of evidence based clinical data interoperability, and to encourage best practices in clinical data informatics. For example, there is a well known gap between the limited data set mandated under USCDI and the many additional electronic health information (EHI) data types that are are not specified in the current USCDI data set and yet arise with most patient encounters

As a serendipitous example of leadership towards improved clinical data interoperability, an RFP was released this month by SAMHSA that will incentivize and demonstrate community adoption of the expanded USCDI+BH data set.1 This is a small step in the right direction. Please use this approach to encourage the public and private sector to originate and support useful and meaningful expansion of clinical data interoperability. Data Access and Integration – PC-8 In your experience, what health data is readily available and valuable to patients or their caregivers or both? USCDI content helps create limited data set portions of patient encounters that are nominally available with FHIR APIs. But data curated in separate portals can become functionally inaccessible from the time consuming burden of logging to multiple portals and manually reconciling clinical encounters from separate health care enterprises. This question as stated is likely to generate more noise than signal. First, it is critically important for all data in an EHR to be accessible, period. Second, it is critically important for all data about a patient to be in (or accessible from) an EHR, regardless of data format. Simply put, if the EHR is limited to USCDI content, then EHI data gaps will occur.
