PR-1: How Can CMS Encourage Rural Providers to Adopt Digital Health Products? Rural providers face a constellation of systemic barriers that hinder the adoption of digital health tools, including those powered by generative AI (GenAI). These challenges — from misaligned payment models and inadequate capital to workforce shortages and outdated infrastructure — are not merely operational hurdles; they are structural failures that threaten quality and access across the healthcare system. Even clinically effective technologies remain out of reach when fee-for -service reimbursement discourages investment, IT capacity is limited, and tools are poorly integrated into existing workflows. Clinician concerns around workflow burden, autonomy , data privacy , and patient education further suppress adoption. Collectively , these barriers disproportionately restrict rural providers’ ability to modernize care delivery , expand access, and manage population health effectively . Rural healthcare will not benefit from digital transformation through passive diffusion. It will require proactive, sustained intervention. CMS is uniquely positioned to correct these market failures — by realigning incentives, reducing adoption risk, and providing the infrastructure, technical support, and regulatory clarity needed to bring rural care into the digital and AI-driven future. The following recommendations outline a path forward.

Lack of Incentives and Capital in Fee-for -Service Dominated Envir onments Most rural providers operate under traditional Medicare and Medicaid fee-for -service models that reward volume over value, prioritizing in-person visits and procedures over preventive, team-based, or virtual-first care. This misalignment leaves providers with little financial incentive to invest in digital tools, even when those tools can reduce hospitalizations, improve engagement, or enable more efficient care delivery . The stakes are especially high in rural settings, where chronic disease, behavioral health needs, and barriers to in-person care are more acute compared to urban areas. Digital and team-based models — such as remote monitoring, virtual chronic care management, and EHR-integrated care planning — offer a powerful means to close these gaps. Emer ging AI-enabled technologies, like ambient documentation tools, virtual triage, and predictive risk stratification, also hold promise for extending rural care capacity but only if providers can access the infrastructure, training, and support needed to deploy them safely and effectively . Yet small, independent rural clinics often cannot afford the upfront costs of adoption, including infrastructure, software, training, and workflow redesign. Without targeted reimbursement reforms or upfront investment, rural providers are unable to modernize care delivery — even as digital-first approaches are uniquely suited to address the health needs of their communities. CMS has a critical role to play in correcting these structural barriers by realigning payment, reducing adoption risk, and enabling rural practices to build the digital foundation necessary for sustainable, effective care. The recent Rural Patient Monitoring Access Act (S.1535 / H.R.3108) introduced in the U.S. Senate is an example of the kind of reform needed – the measure raises reimbursement of rural remote patient monitoring technologies to parity with urban settings. It offers a compelling model for how targeted policy can unlock digital innovation in the communities that need it most. Recommended CMS Actions: 1. Develop new rural-specific reimbursement policies a. Offer add-on payments within the Medicar e Physician Fee Schedule (MPFS) for key digital health services , including remote physiologic monitoring, asynchronous communication, and tech-enabled patient navigation. This should also include reimbursement for additional underutilized CPT codes critical to digital care, such as (1) Chronic Care Management (CCM), (2) Principal Care Management (PCM), (3) Remote Therapeutic Monitoring (RTM), and (4) Interprofessional Consultations (e-Consults). b. Establish bundled payments for high-frequency digital interventions in chronic care management, making them administratively simpler for rural clinics to implement.

Introduce a Digital First Access Payment Program , similar in structure to Graduate Medical Education (GME) payments, that allocates annual lump-sum payments to rural practices investing in first-time digital infrastructure — something which becomes much more accessible through the advent of GenAI. 2. This includes formally codifying the home as a permanent, eligible site of care under Medicare, which currently allows it only through temporary waivers extended by Congressional action. Low Digital Literacy and Limited Technical Expertise Among Rural Providers Rural providers often operate in environments without dedicated information technology (IT) personnel or digital transformation leadership. Unlike integrated delivery networks or large urban hospitals that can staff CIOs and implementation teams, rural clinics may rely on generalists or external consultants for all technology decisions. This lack of capacity makes it difficult to confidently assess vendors, navigate implementation logistics, or redesign clinical workflows. In many cases, technology adoption efforts are abandoned or underutilized due to these deficits, which compound other systemic challenges such as staff shortages and low financial margins. This barrier is well-documented in federal studies: the Office of the National Coordinator for Health IT ( ONC ) has highlighted that, due to internal resource constraints, small and rural practices face disproportionate barriers to electronic health record (EHR) optimization

Launch a rural AI enablement program that provides small and rural providers with practical tools to assess, procure, and implement AI technologies responsibly . These resources would reduce procurement risk, simplify implementation, and ensure new AI tools align with rural realities like low bandwidth, generalist staffing, and fragmented infrastructure. Distrust of Tools That Add Workflow Burden or Reduce Autonomy Years of exposure to poorly designed digital tools (often deployed for billing or compliance) have left many rural clinicians skeptical of new technology . Tools that slow them down, require duplicative documentation, or dictate clinical workflows are often resisted. This distrust is especially acute in environments where provider burnout (caused by physician and skilled labor shortages) is already high. Unlike larger systems that can absorb workflow disruption through administrative or IT support, rural practices often feel the effects of even small inefficiencies more acutely . This has contributed to a pervasive sense among rural providers that digital tools are burdens rather than supports. To promote adoption, rural providers must see technology as an

Building this trust requires a significant cultural and operational shift that centers the voices of clinicians and aligns design incentives with practical realities. Recommended CMS Actions: Prioritize user-centered design and provider co-creation in certification and funding processes: tools funded or approved through CMS programs should demonstrate clinical utility and provider usability . 1. Establish digital sandbox models through CMMI that allow rural providers to test tools in controlled environments and provide structured usability feedback prior to full implementation. These sandboxes would serve as proving grounds to surface usability issues early , helping vendors adapt before wide-scale rollout. 2. Expand agency support for real-world usability resear ch focused on rural workflows. Funding should prioritize mixed-methods studies that examine how tools impact provider time, clinical decision-making, and overall satisfaction in low-resource environments. 3. Include provider experience and autonomy metrics—such as documentation time reduction, EHR task load, and alignment with scope of practice—within its vendor evaluation frameworks. Vendors should be rewarded for tools that streamline workflows and penalized for those that increase administrative complexity . CMS could also consider pilot testing a voluntary "Rural Usability Seal" for tools that meet threshold performance standards in rural settings, akin to the FDA ’s software precertification framework. Outdated Technical Infrastructur e and Limited IT Support Even when rural providers are motivated to adopt digital tools, many lack the infrastructure to do so. Broadband limitations, outdated hardware, on-premise EHRs, and cybersecurity vulnerabilities create high barriers to entry . Additionally , these providers often operate without dedicated IT support, making it difficult to manage even routine upgrades or troubleshoot implementation issues. Without core infrastructure and support systems, even well-intentioned efforts to adopt digital tools frequently fail or remain underutilized. 
 Homeward Health | 7 5. Fragmented Health IT Systems and Inter operability Challenges While Section 3 above addressed the absence of foundational digital infrastructure — such as broadband access, modern hardware, and IT staffing — this section focuses specifically on the fragmentation and incompatibility of the digital systems that do exist. Even in technically connected settings, rural providers often use disjointed platforms lacking standardization, which makes it difficult to integrate new tools or coordinate care across settings. More specifically , rural providers frequently rely on disparate, unintegrated EHR systems with limited interoperability . Many lack participation in regional Health Information Exchanges (HIEs) or access to FHIR-compatible platforms. These gaps impede data sharing, prevent closed-loop referrals, and make it difficult to layer third-party tools onto existing systems. This fragmentation not only undermines care coordination but also creates implementation and compliance burdens that further discourage digital adoption. 6. Low Awareness of Available Tools Due to Limited Vendor Engagement Digital health vendors tend to prioritize urban and academic medical centers when deploying their solutions, in part because of better reimbursement, faster procurement timelines, and larger contract sizes. This underinvestment in rural outreach results in poor awareness among rural providers of available tools that could meet their specific needs. In many cases, providers are unaware of innovations in areas such as asynchronous triage, low-bandwidth video platforms, or AI-powered documentation tools. The result is a deep digital awareness divide. Without structured channels for rural provider education and market exposure, high-potential tools are often not reaching the communities that could benefit most from them. Conclusion The transformation of rural healthcare through digital innovation is not only possible but imperative. By addressing structural barriers through coordinated policy action, CMS can build a future where digital health is accessible and effective across every zip code.
