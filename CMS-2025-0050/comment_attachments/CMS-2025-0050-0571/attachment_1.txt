E. Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks
1. Ecosystem
TD-1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to
stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare
beneficiaries and caregivers?
First and foremost, we commend CMS for its bold and commendable leadership in advancing
patient empowerment by mandating access to health data for patients and their broader care
ecosystems.
From a conformance and adoption standpoint, the public reporting led by Flexpa through its
State of the Payer Patient Access API report and accompanying webinars has proven to be a
catalyst for action. This transparent and dynamic "leaderboard" approach—ranking payers and
vendors based on performance— captured the attention of many in the industry, including our
clients, partners, and own teams. By publicly surfacing disparities in implementation, it
galvanized a wave of engagement and is stimulating visible improvement across the
ecosystem.
Technically, while the Patient Access API marks a significant step forward, it still leaves a critical
gap: the lack of endpoint aggregation. Each consumer is currently left to resolve this challenge
independently. Without a unified interface, the burden placed on third-party applications to
“ allow patients to easily access their claims and encounter information, including cost, as well
as a defined sub-set of their clinical information through third-party applications of their
choice ” is disproportionately high and impedes any meaningful adoption.

TD-2. Regarding CMS Data, to stimulate developer interest--
There is clear and sustained developer interest in digital health marketplaces—a fact our
Director of Sales Engineering can personally attest to. As a consulting senior strategist and
solutions architect at Sun Life, Canada’s largest private insurer, he played a key role in
designing and implementing the Lumino Health platform. Originally envisioned as a
member-facing digital health app marketplace, Lumino aimed to curate and surface consumer
health applications to support Canadians in managing their health.
As early as 2016—nearly a decade ago—the team had already identified over 300 consumer
digital health applications relevant to this initiative. Remarkably, every developer the team
approached expressed strong enthusiasm to participate. All were willing to invest in aligning
their offerings to meet the marketplace’s standards and integration requirements.
Today, Lumino has evolved into a national, consumer-accessible health navigation hub that
empowers Canadians—regardless of whether they are Sun Life clients—to discover, compare,
and connect with a broad spectrum of healthcare providers and services.
Importantly, Lumino is not an isolated example. Robust developer engagement has been
observed across a range of similar platforms, including Epic’s App Orchard, athenahealth
Marketplace, WELL Health’s apps.health, Huawei AppGallery, Microsoft and AWS Marketplaces,
and mainstream app stores like those operated by Apple and Google.

a. What additional data would be most valuable if made available through CMS
APIs?
One of the most impactful actions CMS could take to advance sustained
interoperability—and, by extension, meaningfully support the goals of the quadruple
aim—would be to eliminate the proprietary data silos entrenched within electronic
medical records (EMRs). A decisive step toward this would be mandating that all patient
data stored in EMRs be made accessible through standardized FHIR APIs.
This approach could begin by requiring the use of prescribed FHIR resources for all
appropriately structured data, supplemented by a generic FHIR resource type for
remaining data elements. Over time, as unstructured or loosely defined data is
progressively mapped to well-formed FHIR resource models, the scope of interoperable
data would expand to encompass the full breadth of clinically meaningful information.
b. What data sources are most valuable alongside the data available through the
Blue Button 2.0 API?
Essentially, all patient-related data are valuable. Please see our response to TD-2-a above.
c. What obstacles prevent accessing these data sources today?
Building on the observations outlined in TD1, accessing patient data through third-party
applications—outside the payer’s designated app marketplace—remains unnecessarily
complex and burdensome. To retrieve data, the application must know the exact API
endpoint, the patient must already have an active portal account within the payer’s digital
environment, and they must also possess and input their member ID and associated
credentials - which is quite often not known/forgotten.
This fragmented process creates significant friction for both patients and developers. A
more elegant and scalable solution would be the establishment of a universal patient
registry, ideally linked to a nationally consistent identifier such as a Social Insurance
Number (SIN). This registry would be complemented by a universal patient consent
framework, enabling streamlined authorization across systems. Such a model would

2. Digital Identity
TD-3. Regarding digital identity implementation:
Implementing digital identity in healthcare presents significant challenges, primarily related to
ensuring the security and privacy of sensitive health information. Establishing trust
frameworks and achieving consistent, reliable patient identification across disparate systems
are complex. There are also hurdles in user adoption, ensuring equitable access, and
integrating with existing and often fragmented healthcare IT infrastructure.
The benefits are substantial, including streamlined and secure access to health data for
patients and authorized third parties. Digital identity can reduce friction in data exchange
processes, improve cybersecurity by providing stronger authentication methods, and enable
innovative applications that rely on verified user identity. A universal patient registry linked to
a nationally consistent identifier could facilitate this.
a. What are the challenges and benefits?
Requiring robust digital identity credentials, such as those meeting NIST 800-63-3
IAL2/AAL2 standards, would significantly enhance cybersecurity by establishing higher
assurance levels for identity verification and authentication compared to current reliance
on portal accounts and member IDs. This can help prevent unauthorized access to
protected health information.

TD-5. How could a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints improve access to
health information for patients, providers, and payers? Who should publish such a
directory, and should users bear a cost?
A nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints would significantly improve access to health
information by directly addressing the current obstacle where third-party applications must
know the exact API endpoint to retrieve data. Such a directory would serve as a fundamental
piece of infrastructure, akin to a phone book for healthcare data endpoints. For patients, it
would facilitate easier connection of their chosen applications to their providers' data sources.
For providers and payers, it would streamline data exchange workflows, such as coordinating
care or fulfilling data requests, by making endpoints discoverable.
Combined with a universal patient registry and consent framework, it would enable a more
elegant and scalable data access model. While the source material doesn't specify who should
publish or whether users should pay, its value in enabling information access and exchange
workflows is clear. Experience in enhancing connectivity and efficiency for health information
exchange across diverse organizations highlights the potential benefits of such a centralized
resource.

4. Data Exchange
TD-12. Should CMS endorse non-CMS data sources and networks, and if so, what criteria
or metrics should CMS consider?
Yes, the value of healthcare data increases substantially when it is comprehensive. The
inclusion of data from commercial payers and non-CMS providers not only enhances the utility
of the dataset individually, but collectively renders it indispensable for achieving meaningful
insights and driving system-wide improvements.
To unlock this value, non-CMS data holders should be actively incentivized to participate in
bi-directional data sharing arrangements. Such reciprocity ensures that all stakeholders
benefit from broader access while contributing to a more complete and interoperable
healthcare data ecosystem.
TD-13. What new opportunities and advancements could emerge with APIs providing
access to the entirety of a patient’s electronic health information (EHI)?
APIs providing access to the entirety of a patient's EHI would unlock significant opportunities
for advancements in care coordination, population health management, clinical decision
support, research, and patient empowerment. With comprehensive data, developers can build
more sophisticated applications that provide personalized health insights, automate complex
workflows like prior authorization, support advanced clinical quality improvements, and enable
seamless information exchange across the entire care continuum. Transforming the full scope
of diverse and potentially unstructured EHI into actionable insights and informed decisions
becomes much more powerful.
a. What are the primary obstacles to this?
The primary obstacles include the continued existence of proprietary data silos
entrenched within electronic medical records that prevent comprehensive access. The
technical challenge of standardizing and structuring data from various sources and
formats, including legacy systems and unstructured data like free text or faxed records, is

b. What are the primary tradeoffs between USCDI and full EHI, especially given
more flexible data processing capabilities today?
The primary tradeoff between USCDI and full EHI lies in scope versus complexity and
standardization. USCDI provides a defined, more manageable dataset, which can facilitate
basic interoperability more easily. However, limiting access to USCDI excludes valuable
data elements necessary for many advanced clinical and operational use cases. Accessing
full EHI, while potentially increasing data coverage, introduces complexity due to varied
formats, structures, and quality issues across different sources. Given advancements in
data processing, the technical challenge of handling less structured data is decreasing.
However, transforming this data into structured, usable formats like FHIR resources
remains crucial for deriving maximum value and enabling advanced analytics and
decision-making. The tradeoff becomes managing the complexity of full EHI to gain richer
insights versus the relative simplicity of USCDI with its inherent data limitations. Solutions
that specialize in high-volume data ingestion and transformation can help mitigate the
complexity tradeoff associated with full EHI.
