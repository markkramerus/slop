RE: CMS-0041-NC, Request for Information; Health Technology Ecosystem
The Kansas Health Information Network, Inc. dba KONZA National Network thanks the Centers
for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) for the opportunity to submit comments on the
“Request for Information; Health Technology Ecosystem (CMS-0041-NC).
KONZA National Network is the only Health Information Exchange (HIE) that is also a designated
Qualified Health Information Network (QHIN) under TEFCA. KONZA was one of the first
organizations to receive the QHIN designation in December 2023. KONZA has successfully
continued to operate as a HIE and a QHIN over the last 18 months, fulfilling our not-for-profit
mission of reducing administrative burdens through secure data exchange for providers, public
health and health plans while providing data for the improvement of care outcomes for patients
through advanced analytics and patient access to near real time data. KONZA has over 15 years
of experience supporting providers, public health, payers and patients and has never experienced
a data breach or cyber security incident. KONZA provides services to over 2000 + organizations
nationwide, over 10,000 providers nationwide and supports all major health plans nationwide.
KONZA has supported patient access to their medical record data since 2013 and was one of the
first members of the Harvard Open Notes model.
As a Qualified Health Information Network (QHIN) stakeholder committed to advancing high-
quality and efficient healthcare delivery, we appreciate CMS’s continued efforts to modernize
health information exchange, reduce administrative burden, and promote interoperability through
initiatives like TEFCA and the broader adoption of FHIR standards. A robust health technology
infrastructure is essential to realizing the goals of value-based care, population health management,
and improved patient outcomes. This comment highlights opportunities and considerations for
tomorrow’s health technology ecosystem that enhance alignment across payers, providers, patients
and public health entities through thoughtful technology policy and investment.

Section E. Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks; 3. Technical Standards and
Certification
TD-5. We believe that a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints is necessary to create
a foundation for future scalable innovation within the health technology ecosystem. Such a
directory would enable and improve access to health information by making it easier, faster, and
more secure for healthcare stakeholders to connect and exchange data. The healthcare system at
large would realize a significant burden reduction and cost savings facilitated by a move away
from antiquated manual chart retrieval, aggregation and abstraction.
With a centralized, validated directory, providers, payers, and other stakeholders would be able to
discover and connect to each other’s FHIR APIs, have fewer failed connections and improved data
matching accuracy more quickly. Downstream value adds will include improved continuity of care
for patients and enablement of expanded flexible and timely access to patient health records.
The existence of a national directory would support a more robust response to public health
emergencies by removing barriers across disparate geographies, networks and care settings to
provide an improved coordination and response effort.
In their designated roles as a trusted intermediary and infrastructure enabler, QHINs are well
positioned to play a pivotal role in supporting a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints.
Below are suggested roles for which established QHINs could enable a federated national directory
model that supports real-time discovery and access.
• Endpoint Discovery Services: QHINs could behave as a lookup service enabling
participants to query for FHIR endpoints associated with specific providers, organizations,
or services enabling seamless data exchange across networks.
• Standards Enforcement and Validation: QHINs could ensure that endpoint data adheres to
established implementation guides, improving consistency and interoperability.
• Security and Trust Frameworks: By leveraging TEFCA’s common agreement, QHINs can
help enforce identity verification, authentication, and endpoint validation, ensuring that
only trusted entities are listed and accessed.
• Support for Public Health and Payer Use Cases: As agnostic services providers, QHINs
could extend directory services to support expanded entity to entity exchange such as
payer-to-payer data exchange, public health reporting, and care coordination, where
accurate endpoint resolution is critical.
TD-9, e. We support the use of centralized data aggregation and automated quality reporting to
reduce the provider reporting burden associated with quality performance data reporting to CMS
and NCQA. While EHRs can incrementally advance this goal, we encourage CMS to consider
leveraging QHINs in this role. QHINs can collect and standardize bulk data across multiple
systems, providers and payers, ensuring consistency, more effective data aggregation and reducing

administrative overhead. As designated national data exchange partners, QHINs offer the
integration of real-time data aggregation, validation and reporting across networks improving the
completeness of data used to calculate quality measures while minimizing provider reporting
burden.
CMS programs that could benefit from QHIN-enabled bulk data transfer include the
• Medicare Promoting Interoperability Program: QHINs can automate the exchange of
clinical quality measures (CQMs), reducing manual reporting efforts for eligible hospitals
and critical access hospitals.
• MIPS (Merit-Based Incentive Payment System): Bulk data transfer through QHINs can
streamline submission of performance data, ensuring providers meet CMS quality
benchmarks with minimal administrative burden.
• ACO REACH and other Value Based Models: Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs)
can leverage QHINs to aggregate claims and clinical data, expediting clinical quality
measure calculations, improving risk stratification and care coordination for value-based
reimbursement.
• Medicaid Managed Care Quality Reporting: QHINs can facilitate standardized data
exchange between Medicaid agencies and providers, ensuring compliance with CMS
interoperability mandates and improving accountable, coordinated care delivery to
Medicaid patients.
• Prior Authorization Automation: CMS regulations encourage FHIR-based APIs for prior
authorization, and QHINs can support bulk data exchange to expedite approvals and reduce
delays in patient care. Medical record retrieval across QHINs will expedite the prior
authorization approval process as necessary data needed by health plans to approve a prior
authorization request can be delivered within the regulatory timeframe parameters.
Section F. Value-Based Care Organizations
VB-2. We suggest that the desired themes and technologies can be better integrated into CMS
APM requirements through the exploration and use of provider incentives as an accelerator to align
the integrated use of advanced analytics with CMS APM requirements. Specifically, QHIN
participation improves data interoperability, risk stratification and the automation of standardized
quality reporting. We encourage CMS to offer incentives that enhance reimbursement and/or
expand reporting flexibilities to providers and payers that demonstrate effective use of data through
QHINs.
The timely availability of accurate data available through QHINs can power AI-driven insights
into population health trends, care gaps, and resource optimization. These capabilities are currently
integrated into selected QHIN services including real-time access and delivery of relevant data
through IHE query and retrieve functions, business intelligence and integrated EHR workflows.

Incentives to scale the adoption and use of QHIN services promote the desired alignment for the
use of advanced analytics in CMS payment models. Automating the flow of information needed
for CMS quality programs, shared savings arrangements, and alternative payment models reduces
administrative burden and reduces costs while promoting value-based care objectives.
VB-3. QHINs are essential to the health technology ecosystem of the future and unlock secondary
data opportunities essential to drive value-based care.
QHINs, as designated under TEFCA, offer a scalable infrastructure to enable the secure,
standardized exchange of clinical and non-clinical data across disparate networks and systems. As
designated, their role in facilitating the secondary use of data is critical to advancing value-based
care models.
QHINs are positioned to facilitate bulk data transfer enabling real-time provider access to claims,
clinical, and social drivers of health data. Access to accurate, real-time data is essential to develop
and deploy risk-adjusted predictive analytics modeling, critical to APM success. As mentioned in
our above response, QHINs are well positioned to play a pivotal role in supporting a nationwide
provider directory of FHIR endpoints.
QHINs serve as trusted intermediaries to enable real-time access to diverse data types including
claims, utilization, clinical and social drivers of health data. The robust stakeholder data exchange
capabilities of QHINs enable population health management and facilitate provider insights for
case management and resource optimization to meet value-based care benchmarks, support timely
interventions and risk stratification.
To maximize these benefits, CMS should consider offering enhanced reimbursement or reporting
flexibilities to providers and payers that demonstrate effective use of data through QHINs. CMS
should also consider expanding QHIN use cases to streamline processes such as prior
authorization, quality reporting, and provider directory management. Expanded stakeholder
participation that includes providers, payers and community-based organizations will strengthen
the future health technology ecosystem.
In conclusion, CMS has a unique opportunity to strengthen the health technology ecosystem by
advancing interoperability, data exchange, and provider-payer collaboration. Encouraging the use
of TEFCA and QHIN participation and aligned incentives will facilitate the evolution of our
national health technology ecosystem to promote efficiencies, improve care and create a
sustainable foundation for future innovation. We appreciate the opportunity to provide input and
look forward to CMS’s continued leadership in shaping a modern, scalable, and equitable health
technology infrastructure.
