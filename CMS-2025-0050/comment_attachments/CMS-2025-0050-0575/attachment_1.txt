Information blocking is a practice by an "actor" that is likely to interfere with the access, exchange, or use of electronic health information (EHI), except as required by law or specified in an information blocking exception. The Cures Act applied the la w to healthcare providers, health IT developers of certified health IT, and health information exchanges (HIEs)/health information networks (HINs). TEFCA , the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement , is designed as a national interoperability backbone that brings together disparate health information systems under a unified set of principles, governance, and technical standards. Here’s how TEFCA stands out and what alternatives you might consider: Unique Interoperability Functions of TEFCA 1. Common Trust Framework TEFCA establishes a baseline of trust among participants through common principles and standardized terms and conditions. This framework mandates the essential elements of openness, privacy, and security across all connecting networks, ensuring that each participant is held to uniform standards. 2. Nationwide Connectivity Rather than encouraging isolated or regional networks, TEFCA’s scope is national. It provides a universal policy layer that works across various health information networks (HINs), effectively bridging the gaps among regional health information exchanges (HIEs ), private networks, and individual provider systems. This nationwide approach facilitates smoother query -based and directed exchange of electronic health information. 3. Interoperability Standards Enforcement TEFCA is technology -neutral but strongly encourages the use of modern data exchange standards such as FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) and the inclusion of data elements defined in USCDI (United States Core Data for Interoperability). This drive for standardization helps overcome the challenges posed by legacy systems and proprietary interoperability models. 4. Data Blocking and Information Governance One of TEFCA’s key roles is to minimize information blocking by establishing penalties and compliance guidelines. It creates mechanisms that encourage, and even require, the sharing of data across different health systems while still protecting patient privacy. This aspect is critical in fostering an environment where health information flows freely yet securely. 5. Support for Both Query -Based and Directed Exchange TEFCA accommodates multiple exchange methods. It supports on -demand query - based exchanges (where users can retrieve data from a network on -demand) alongside push -based directed messaging. This flexibility ensures the system can serve a broad range of use c ases from care coordination to public health reporting and patient access to their own data.
