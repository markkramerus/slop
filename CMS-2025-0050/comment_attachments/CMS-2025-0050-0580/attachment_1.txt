C. Providers
1. Digital Health Apps
PR–1. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage providers, including those in rural
areas, to leverage approved (see description in PC-5) digital health products for their
patients?
The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) and its partners can take several steps to
encourage providers—including those in rural and underserved areas—to adopt approved digital
health products that enhance care delivery and operational efficiency. We recommend seeking
guidance from Medicare Administrative Contractors (MACs) and industry experts currently
managing the provider experience to identify, design, test, and implement more effective
streamlined digital solutions. A multifaceted strategy may also include:
 Implementing Customized Provider/Supplier Communication Plans: Through our
internet portal, we can regularly deliver electronic information to providers that offers a data
visualization of their billing and coding trends compared to historical norms within their peer
group. By alerting providers to emerging patterns and trends, this initiative does not increase
provider burden yet promotes a sentinel effect, enabling proactive responses to potential
provider issues before escalation.
 Engaging Providers Early: Involve providers in the development, testing, and
implementation of digital health products to ensure the tools are relevant, user-friendly, and
aligned with real-world clinical workflows. Provider input increases adoption and helps tailor
solutions to local needs, including rural-specific challenges.
 Streamlining Communication with Suppliers: Develop mechanisms that enable easier,
more direct communication between providers and suppliers, particularly for items like
durable medical equipment. This can remove administrative barriers and expedite access for
patients.
 Aligning Financial Incentives: Offer reimbursement incentives or performance-based
bonuses to providers who successfully adopt and use approved digital tools. These incentives
can help offset implementation costs and encourage widespread adoption.
 Introducing Compliance Expectations: Consider phased-in requirements or incentives for
early adoption of approved products, where appropriate, to ensure consistent adoption across
provider types while still allowing flexibility for resource-constrained practices.
 Reducing Provider Burden: Demonstrate how digital products can simplify day-to-day
operations. For example:
o Patient self-service access to health records can reduce staff time responding to routine
inquiries.
o Automated appointment reminders lower the burden of manual follow-up.
 Improving the Provider Experience: Show how digital tools can streamline workflows and
improve communication. Examples include:

a. What are the current obstacles?
Several obstacles may discourage providers from adopting and promoting approved digital
health products, despite the potential benefits for patient care and operational efficiency. These
challenges fall into both practical and technical categories:
Access and Infrastructure Limitations
 Lack of Reliable Internet: Some rural providers operate in areas where broadband access is
limited or unreliable, making it difficult to consistently access or utilize digital health
applications.
 Limited Availability of Digital Tools: Some rural practices may not have access to the
latest approved digital products, either due to vendor coverage gaps or lack of awareness.
Workforce and Operational Constraints
 Financial Limitations: Providers are already operating on razor-thin margins.
 Staffing Challenges: Rural areas often face workforce shortages, making it difficult for
providers to allocate time or staff resources to learn, implement, or support new digital tools.
 Training and Information Sharing Gaps: Providers may lack access to adequate training
or clear information on how to use tools effectively, especially when supporting patients who
also need help navigating new technologies.
 Implementation Costs: Upfront investments in new systems such as hardware, licenses,
integration, and training can be prohibitive for small or resource-constrained practices.
Technical Barriers
 Multiple Systems of Record: Providers frequently operate across fragmented Information
Technology systems and the need to reconcile data from multiple sources can hinder
adoption of a new digital tool.
 No Dedicated Point of Contact: When digital health vendors don’t provide a clear point of
contact or reliable support (e.g., unresponsive or confusing help desks), providers may
abandon efforts to adopt the tool.

 Resistance to Change: Both providers and patients may be hesitant to transition from

b. What information should providers share with patients when using digital products in
the provision of their care?
Providers can enhance patient understanding, engagement, and outcomes by sharing clear,
accessible information through digital health platforms. Examples of useful information to share
with patients include:
 Simplified Clinical Notes: Providers can use artificial intelligence (AI) generated summaries
to translate medical notes into layman's terms, helping patients better understand their
condition, treatment plans, and next steps without being overwhelmed by complex medical
language.
 Referral Information: Digital tools can be used to deliver timely, organized information
about specialist referrals, including contact details, appointment instructions, and
expectations for follow-up care.
 Medication Instructions and Information: Providers can share detailed medication
guidance like dosing schedules and side effects to watch for, making it easier for patients to
manage their prescriptions accurately.
 Diagnosis Details in Plain Language: AI tools can help break down complex diagnostic
information into simple, patient-friendly language, empowering patients to understand their
health status and participate more confidently in their care decisions.
Sharing this kind of information through digital platforms not only enhances transparency and
trust, but also supports health literacy and strengthens the provider-patient relationship.
c. What responsibilities do providers have when recommending use of a digital product
by a patient?
When recommending use of a digital health product, providers have a responsibility to ensure
patients and their caregivers are informed, supported, and capable of engaging with the
technology in a meaningful way. This responsibility is not limited to physicians alone; given
staffing constraints and limited time, particularly in rural or underserved areas, nurses and health
facility operational staff (such as patient access representatives) can play a key role. These team
members can be trained to educate patients, address concerns, and assist with basic
troubleshooting, helping bridge the gap between clinical care and technology use.

PR–2. What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective utilization of
the most useful and innovative applications for physician workflows, such as quality
measurement reporting, clinical documentation, and billing tasks? How could these
obstacles be mitigated?
Past efforts like Provider Enrollment, Chain, and Ownership System (PECOS) 2.0 highlight key
obstacles to successfully introducing new applications, such as prolonged development, limited
user input, and technical inefficiencies. By learning from these challenges, we can take a more
effective, user-centered approach to developing and deploying future solutions.
Obstacles
 Multiple Programs Used Across Public Health Jurisdictions:
o Obstacle: Public health care programs (e.g., Medicare, Medicaid, Defense Health
Agency, Veterans Affairs) each have unique technologies, thus hindering provider
workflow efficiencies.
o Mitigation: Using the same technology across public health care programs, such as
through standardized application programming interfaces (APIs), offers numerous
advantages by enhancing interoperability. This will improve health care quality, prevent
medical errors, increase efficiency, reduce costs, hinder fraud, expand access to care, and
ultimately, transform health outcomes for individuals and populations.
 Prolonged Development Time and Lack of Usable Output:
o Obstacle: Projects like PECOS 2.0 faced significant delays and failed to produce usable
results after extensive investment and missed deadlines.
o Mitigation: Implement robust Agile project management practices to ensure timelines
are realistic and achievable. Incorporate MACs and proven industry experts in the design,
testing, and implementation to improve the provider experience and ensure satisfaction.
Regularly review progress and adjust plans as needed to maintain momentum and deliver
usable outputs incrementally.
 Contract Cancellation and Financial Impact:
o Obstacle: CMS canceled contracts due to inefficiencies, leading to financial losses and
setbacks.
o Mitigation: Prioritize transparency in contract management. Clearly define expectations,
milestones, and deliverables from the outset. Regularly evaluate contractor performance
against these criteria to detect issues early and consider alternative contracting models if
necessary.

 User Experience and Adoption Challenges:
o Obstacle: Existing systems like PECOS are criticized for being outdated and difficult to
use, hindering adoption.
o Mitigation: Involve administrators (i.e., MACs) and end-users (i.e., providers, suppliers)
extensively throughout the development lifecycle. Use feedback systems to continuously
gather user insights and preferences. Design solutions that address specific pain points
and streamline workflows to enhance usability.
 Technical Issues and Implementation Delays:
o Obstacle: Technical challenges are common, causing delays and impacting
implementation timelines.
o Mitigation: Partner with current administrators (i.e., MACs), leveraging their existing,
proven implementation and provider engagement practices. Conduct thorough technical
feasibility assessments before development begins. Maintain close collaboration between
developers and technical experts to proactively identify and address potential issues.
Implement agile development methodologies to iteratively test and refine solutions, using
the support of MACs.
Lessons Learned
 Effective Project Management: Emphasize Agile project management with clear oversight
and accountability.
 Transparency and Communication: Maintain open communication channels with end-
users, providers, suppliers, and patients to manage expectations and foster trust.
 User-Centric Design: Prioritize user experience and usability throughout the development
process.
 Selecting Reliable Partners: Choose development partners with a proven track record and
capabilities aligned with project requirements.
 Government-Industry Collaboration: Foster closer collaboration between government
agencies and MACs to leverage expertise and resources effectively.
 Flexibility and Adaptability: Build flexibility into project plans to accommodate evolving
needs and technical challenges.
By learning from these challenges, we can take a more effective, user-centered approach to
developing and deploying future solutions.
PR–3. How important is it for healthcare delivery and interoperability in urban and rural
areas that all data in an EHR system be accessible for exchange, regardless of storage
format (for example, scanned documents, faxed records, lab results, free text notes,
structured data fields)? Please address all of the following:
a. Current challenges in accessing different data formats.
Health care data exists in multiple formats: scanned documents, faxed records, lab results, free-
text notes, and structured fields. Many electronic health record (EHR) systems are optimized
only for structured data, making unstructured content like scanned referrals or physician notes
difficult to access, search, or share electronically. This fragmentation can mean delayed or
incomplete access to documentation needed to support coverage determinations, resulting in
delays in claim payments or beneficiaries getting the necessary equipment.

b. Impact on patient care quality.
Lack of full access to clinical data can compromise care:
 Delays and denials of prior authorizations can occur due to insufficient documentation.
 Delays in equipment delivery (e.g., wheelchairs, oxygen tanks) due to missing records
negatively impact patient mobility and recovery. MACs have received supplier feedback
indicating this is a significant pain point.
 Incomplete medical histories can lead to misinformed decisions, redundant testing, or
inappropriate care plans.
 In rural areas, where provider options are limited and referrals often cross systems,
inaccessible data impedes timely interventions and care continuity.
c. Technical barriers to full data accessibility.
 Legacy systems and siloed data architectures make integration across formats difficult.
 Unstructured data (e.g., free text, scanned Portable Document Formats) lacks standard
metadata, making it hard to index, search, or extract meaning.
 Interoperability standards are not uniformly applied to all data types, limiting their
effectiveness in real-world settings.
 Disparate vendor systems lack common formatting or APIs, especially in smaller or rural
facilities.
d. Cost or privacy implications of making all data formats interoperable.
 Costs of converting and standardizing legacy or non-structured data can be high, especially
for small practices or rural hospitals. Incentives could relieve providers of some cost.
 Privacy concerns increase when converting or extracting data from scanned documents or
free text, as sensitive content may be inadvertently exposed.
 Strong safeguards must be implemented to comply with Health Insurance Portability and
Accountability Act while enabling controlled, role-based access to all data formats.
e. Priority level compared to other interoperability needs.
While foundational interoperability issues (e.g., patient matching, standardized coding) remain
important, enabling full access to all data formats is a high-priority for improving operational
efficiency and patient outcomes. For stakeholders like durable medical equipment suppliers—
who are enthusiastic about interoperability—this issue is not just technical but operationally
urgent, as it directly affects their ability to respond to MAC documentation requests and submit
timely claims.
Ensuring all data within an EHR system, regardless of format, is accessible and exchangeable is
critical to advancing health care delivery and interoperability, particularly in urban and rural
areas where care coordination and resource access vary widely. Overcoming technical and cost
barriers must be a shared goal among providers, suppliers, policymakers, and technology
vendors to realize the full promise of interoperability.

Proposed Use Case: Enhancing MSP Coordination via Payer-to-Payer API
From a MAC perspective, the Payer-to-Payer API defined by the Health Level Seven Da Vinci
workgroup presents a valuable opportunity to enhance coordination with the Benefits
Coordination & Recovery Center (BCRC). While the BCRC is not a traditional payer, it plays a
critical role in Medicare Secondary Payer (MSP) operations—identifying other health coverage,

MACs could facilitate structured, consent-aware data exchanges that improve the accuracy and
timeliness of MSP determinations. For example, the API’s support for member match,
provenance tracking, and secure data transfer could reduce misrouted correspondence, accelerate
eligibility verification, and streamline recovery workflows. This would not only reduce
administrative burden but also align with CMS’s broader goals of interoperability, data liquidity,
and patient empowerment.

(FHIR) based APIs, MACs can reduce administrative burden, improve operational efficiency,
and enhance compliance with CMS interoperability mandates. Providers will benefit from faster,
more transparent authorization decisions, enabling more timely care delivery and reducing delays
in treatment. For beneficiaries, this translates into a more seamless health care experience with
fewer disruptions in access to medically necessary services. CMS, in turn, gains improved data
visibility and standardization across payers, supporting better oversight and policy development.
Overall, the Prior Authorization API fosters a more connected, patient-centered health care
ecosystem that aligns with CMS’s broader goals of reducing burden and improving outcomes.

PA–5. What are ways payers can help with simplifying clinical quality data responsibilities
of providers?
Payers and CMS industry partners, such as MACs, play a critical role in simplifying the clinical
quality data responsibilities of providers, especially through collaboration on EHR technology
advancements and the consolidation of interoperability and reporting requirements.
a. How interested are payers and providers in EHR technology advances that enable bulk
extraction of clinical quality data from EHRs to payers to allow them to do the
calculations instead of the provider-side technology?
Payers, providers, and CMS industry partners have all shown strong interest in EHR capabilities
that enable bulk extraction of clinical quality data from the source of truth. This approach allows
payers to assume responsibility for quality calculations, relieving providers of the technical and
administrative burden of managing complex quality reporting requirements themselves. As a
next step, MACs will collaborate with payers, providers, and CMS to implement EHR-based
bulk data extraction capabilities, enabling centralized quality calculations and reducing the
reporting burden on providers.
 For payers, bulk extraction supports more accurate and timely quality measurement,
improves risk adjustment accuracy, and enables stronger benchmarking for value-based
payment models.
 For providers, bulk extraction reduces time spent on documentation, minimizes inconsistent
requests for data submissions, and supports regulatory compliance with less manual effort. It
also opens the door for timely insights into performance metrics that support clinical and
operational improvements.
 For MACs, administrative efficiencies are realized, ultimately benefiting CMS.

b. In what ways can the interoperability and quality reporting responsibilities of providers
to both CMS and other payers be consolidated so investments can be dually purposed?
Are there technologies payers might leverage that would support access to real time
quality data for healthcare providers to inform clinical care in addition to simplifying

Streamlined processes will enhance MAC operations by improving accuracy and efficiency,
bringing direct value to CMS.
Consolidating Interoperability and Reporting Requirements Across Payers and CMS
Currently, providers face fragmented reporting responsibilities across multiple payers and CMS,
leading to duplicated work and inconsistent standards. Payers can help by:
 Aligning data standards and reporting frameworks across public and private sectors, enabling
providers to invest in a single set of tools and workflows that serve both.
 Promoting use of shared, centralized data platforms (e.g., Arkansas SHARE) that allow for
real-time exchange of quality data across systems and stakeholders.
 Supporting federated or centralized models where patient data is accessed once and used for
multiple reporting and analytics purposes—reducing duplicative data entry and decreasing
opportunities for error.
 Encouraging adoption of automated data feeds (e.g., from vital sign devices and labs) into
EHRs to reduce manual entry and ensure consistency in reported metrics.
Technologies Supporting Real-Time Access and Streamlined Reporting
Payers can leverage emerging technologies to enhance both reporting efficiency and clinical care
quality, including:
 FHIR-based APIs and bulk data exchange tools to automate the transfer of quality metrics
from EHRs.
 Data aggregation platforms that consolidate inputs across multiple provider systems into a
single reporting stream.
 Real-time dashboards that provide clinicians with actionable insights at the point of care—
promoting informed decision-making while aligning with value-based care goals.
 Natural language processing and AI tools to extract meaningful data from free-text notes,
scanned documents, and other unstructured formats, ensuring complete quality capture.
Simplifying quality reporting through payer-supported bulk data extraction and interoperability
improvements is a win-win for both providers and payers. It reduces provider burden, enables
more consistent and accurate quality measurement, and enhances patient care through better data
availability. By aligning reporting requirements, leveraging automation, and investing in shared
infrastructure, payers can help transform quality data collection from a regulatory burden into a
meaningful tool for improving care and outcomes.
