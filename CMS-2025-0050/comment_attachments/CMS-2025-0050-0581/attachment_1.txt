Executive Summary
IBM appreciates the opportunity to respond to the CMS Request for Information (RFI) on the
future of the health technology ecosystem. Our recommendations focus on advancing
interoperability, strengthening infrastructure, and promoting secure, patient-centered innovation.
To unlock the full potential of health data, CMS should continue to expand access to electronic
health information (EHI) through standardized, open APIs based on HL7® FHIR® and aligned
with USCDI. APIs reduce administrative burden, improve care coordination, and enable real-time
data access for providers, patients, and developers. Incentives should be tied to measurable
outcomes, with support for smaller organizations to adopt these tools.
We propose evolving USCDI into a modular framework, defining a required core and optional
modules, to support program-specific needs. Expanding USCDI to include richer clinical,
genomic, and real-world data will enhance public health surveillance, precision medicine, and care
for complex patients.
CMS should also endorse trusted non-CMS data networks that meet high standards for security,
quality, and public health utility. Identity assurance protocols, patient consent management, and
app transparency tools will be critical to building trust.
In addition, CMS should invest in scalable bulk FHIR APIs to support population-level analytics.
Standardized API patterns, audit logging, and shared infrastructure will address performance and
reliability concerns.
IBM brings decades of experience supporting CMS, HHS, and other agencies, and has led in the
development of FHIR-based tools, including the LinuxForHealth FHIR Server, clinical trial
matching platforms, post-market safety systems, patient engagement and survey tool, in addition
to developing data standards leveraging FHIR for a variety of data types, including Real-world
Data (RWD), Structured Product Labeling (SPL), and Pharmaceutical Quality and Chemistry &
Manufacturing Controls (PQ/CMC). We propose a centralized infrastructure strategy featuring
consent-based data pipelines, a Federated Digital Identity Hub, pre-certified API toolkits, and
automated “Security as Code” practices. This approach enables CMS to act as both regulator and
enabler, offering foundational identity, consent, and monitoring services to scale innovation
without placing undue burden on providers or app developers.

2 Technical Standards and Certification (Questions TD-4, TD-7 & TD-10)
2.1 TD-4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly
available APIs over proprietary APIs?
Since 2021, U.S. payers have been required to comply with CMS interoperability mandates, which
include the use of open, standards-based APIs to support scalable and secure data exchange. At
first glance, these requirements may appear to be regulatory obligations; however, they are
intended to serve as the foundation for a modern, API-driven health data ecosystem. When
implemented strategically, open APIs, especially those based on HL7 FHIR, can reduce
administrative friction, improve data liquidity, and enhance patient and provider experiences,
leading to measurable return on investment for payers.
Despite these benefits, adoption remains uneven. Some payers treat open APIs as strategic
enablers, while others continue to rely on proprietary systems that limit interoperability, stifle
innovation, and create long-term costs. To accelerate widespread adoption and reduce dependency
on closed platforms, CMS should take the following actions:
1. Reframe API mandates as strategic investments
Communicate that open APIs are not merely compliance checkboxes, but key building blocks for
care transformation, innovation, and operational efficiency. Highlight success stories where open
APIs reduced prior authorization time, improved member access to data, or enabled new value-
based care models.
2. Tie financial incentives to open API usage
Expand CMS bonus programs, rebates, and grants to explicitly reward payers and vendors that
adopt and implement open, standards-based APIs. This includes those certified under the Assistant
Secretary for Technology Policy and Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information
Technology (ASTP/ONC) Health IT Certification Program and aligned with FHIR R4 or higher.
Provide targeted technical assistance to help small and mid-sized payers transition away from
proprietary interfaces.
3. Increase transparency through benchmarking
Develop and publish comparative dashboards showing payer-level adoption and performance of
open APIs. Track metrics and use public benchmarks such as the following to motivate lagging
organizations while showcasing leaders:
 Percentage of member records accessible via open APIs
 Third-party application integration rates

2.2 TD-7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange and
what are its limitations?
The USCDI has improved interoperability and health data exchange across the U.S. healthcare
system by defining a standardized set of health data classes and constituent data elements. To
support evolving public health needs over time, USCDI releases updated versions of their data
standard featuring new data classes and/or elements. Adoption of these additions is embedded in
ASTP/ONC’s certification criteria so that electronic health record (EHR) vendors are required to
adopt the standard. However, several limitations of USCDI still need to be addressed in order to
support more ubiquitous data usage by patients, providers, and federal agencies.
2.2.1 Examples of USCDI Limitations for CMS
Given the USCDI format only accounts for the minimum data necessary for interoperability, the
dataset is incomplete as many clinically relevant data elements are missing from their respective
classes. Some examples of this are detailed below.
1. Lack of Death Information
While patient death date is included within the current version of USCDI (v5), other death details
such as causation, manner, mechanism, and location are not present. Incorporating more detailed
patient death information into USCDI adds significant value to the standardized set of data by
supporting richer, more holistic patient care and coordination. These enhancements would also
improve post-market medical product surveillance efforts, provide additional epidemiological
insights to track trends in disease, and reveal geospatial insights to understand patterns (e.g.,
environmental exposures based on geographic location).
2. Handling of Chronic Disease
Further expansion of the data elements in existing data classes, such as the elements defined within
the Problems data class, would greatly benefit the researchers that rely on USCDI. For example,
certain details on patient problems and conditions are not included, such as data elements specific
to the identification and analysis of chronic diseases, conditions, and/or disorders.

Better tracking of chronic diseases can substantially drive down healthcare utilization and costs.
Given that chronic conditions including diabetes, hypertension, heart disease, cancer, and Chronic
Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) account for the majority of morbidity, mortality, and
healthcare spending in the U.S.1 Currently, USCDI includes general problem lists and vital signs,
but does not include condition-specific markers (e.g., HbA1c for diabetes, ejection fraction for
heart failure, spirometry results for COPD). Addressing these gaps by expanding the data elements
available in USCDI would better support chronic disease management and outcome tracking for
patients. These updates would also reduce costs, given that CMS spending would ultimately
decrease due to early patient intervention which can reduce costly hospitalizations and procedures
needed due to lack of treatment.
3. Need for Additional Data Classes
Furthermore, the USCDI data classes need to be continuously expanded to account for
advancements in health care as technology modernizes the way patients and providers interact with
each other and their data. Certain data domain areas that do not yet exist in USCDI include those
for data on genomics, imaging, and digital health technologies (DHTs) such as wearables. These
technologies and associated data represent a new frontier that support clinical research and enable
more patient-centered healthcare. For example, genomic data enables precision medicine and
personalized, tailored treatments for patients based on their individual genetic variations.
Integrating standardized genomic data into EHRs can make clinicians aware of gene-drug
interactions and certain types of hereditary risks. Similarly, imaging data is central to the diagnosis
and treatment of disease. By standardizing imaging data within USCDI, other approaches such as
AI can be used for imaging (e.g., approaches for tumor detection or fracture classification) to more
quickly diagnose and treat patients. Last, the inclusion of continuous, longitudinal data from DHTs
enables real-time, patient-centered care and early intervention by allowing clinicians to detect
deterioration and progression faster and ultimately treating patients earlier. Longitudinal, real-
world data collection from DHTs also fuels investigation and innovation by researchers, allowing
for long-term continuous monitoring in a patient’s everyday environment which can inform
predictive models and other AI applications.
2.2.2 Use of Language Models to Support Data Standardization
While recent improvements in language models have enhanced our ability to process and interpret
unstructured or semi-structured data, this does not eliminate the need for well-defined data
standards, especially in high-stakes environments such as healthcare. A non-proprietary, less
structured format might offer broader data coverage and flexibility, but it comes at the cost of
increased ambiguity, inconsistency, and processing complexity. Language models can help
interpret unstructured data, but their interpretations are probabilistic, not deterministic, resulting
in higher risks of introducing error, bias, or misalignment, especially when data elements lack
consistent meaning across sources. This creates risks when integrating data, training AI models,
or conducting regulatory reviews.

Standardized data formats, in contrast, enable shared meaning and interoperability across systems

2.2.3 Recommendations to Enhance USCDI for CMS
ASTP/ONC and CMS may consider a tiered approach to expanding USCDI, by opting for a ‘core’
module with ‘optional’ modules, where mandatory core elements are defined and optional modules
are created based on specific use cases. This strategy is similar to how USCDI+ defines data
elements that operate as extensions to the existing USCDI in order to meet specific programmatic
and/or use case requirements. A pilot program could be launched to evaluate this tiered approach
and test new data elements specific to CMS. IBM brings deep expertise and significant experience
in developing, testing, and implementing data standards for structured data from DHTs and real-
world data (RWD) sourced from EHRs, along with the development of corresponding
implementation guides. IBM can support CMS in identifying and refining new data elements for
inclusion in USCDI. Expanding standardized data elements within USCDI will enhance
consistency, diversity, and completeness across vendors and institutions, enabling CMS to gain a
more comprehensive view of patient health, uncover actionable insights, improve outcomes, and
reduce costs.
2.3 TD-10. For EHR and other developers subject to the ONC Health IT
Certification Program, what further steps should ASTP/ONC consider to
implement the 21st Century Cures Act's API condition of certification that
requires a developer's APIs to allow health information to be accessed,
exchanged, and used without special effort, including providing access to all
data elements of a patient's electronic health record to the extent permissible
under applicable privacy laws?
Many digital health applications that patients use to access their health data may fall outside of
HIPAA’s regulatory scope, creating potential for inadvertent misuse or overcollection of sensitive
health information. To protect patient trust and promote responsible data use, CMS should
consider:
 Requiring explicit, role-based patient consent for data access via third-party applications.
This consent should be time-bound, logged, and aligned with HIPAA’s minimum
necessary standard.
 Encouraging transparency by maintaining a CMS-approved registry or voluntary
certification program for third-party digital health apps, enabling patients to better
understand which tools meet federal privacy and security benchmarks.

3 Data Exchange (Questions TD-12, TD-13, TD-15, TD-16 & TD-17)
3.1 TD-12. Should CMS endorse non-CMS data sources and networks, and if so,
what criteria or metrics should CMS consider?
There is a strong case for CMS to endorse non-CMS data sources and networks, provided certain
conditions are met. The following points provide some rationale for endorsement:
1. Accelerating Interoperability: Endorsing non-CMS data sources can accelerate the
realization of nationwide interoperability, enabling patient data to follow the individual
across payers, providers, and care settings.
2. Supporting Patient-Centered Care: CMS policy already encourages patient access to
data through third-party applications and APIs, as mandated by the CMS Interoperability
and Patient Access Final Rule. Endorsing trusted non-CMS networks aligns with the goal
of putting patients at the center of care by broadening access to their health information.
3. Leveraging Innovation: Many non-CMS networks and data sources are at the forefront of
innovation, piloting new interoperability standards (such as FHIR) and use cases.
To ensure that endorsement of non-CMS data sources and networks advances public health goals
and maintains trust, CMS should consider the following criteria and metrics:
1. Standards Compliance: Adherence to established interoperability standards, such as HL7
FHIR and USCDI, and the use of standardized, openly published APIs will facilitate
secure, real-time data exchange. Sample metrics include percentage of API transactions
conforming to FHIR R4 or higher or use of certified Health IT products listed in the
ASTP/ONC Cures Act Certification Program.
2. Data Quality and Completeness: Demonstrating proven ability to provide accurate,
timely, and complete datasets, including claims, clinical, encounter, and social
determinants of health data as well as usage of mechanisms for regular data validation and
quality assurance. Sample metrics include percentage of missing data elements or accuracy
rate from quarterly data audits.
3. Patient Access and Usability: Support for patient-directed access to data, including the
ability for patients to use third-party apps of their choice, and transparent consent
management and clear patient education on data use. Sample metrics include percentage
of members able to access their data through certified third-party apps or percentage of
patients surveyed reporting understanding of data sharing practices.
4. Public Health Utility: Demonstrated value in supporting public health reporting,
surveillance, and emergency response needs plus alignment with public health
accreditation criteria for data sharing and interoperability. Sample metrics include the
number of public health initiatives or registries participated in or average response time to
public health data inquires.

3.2 TD–13. What new opportunities and advancements could emerge with APIs
providing access to the entirety of a patient’s electronic health information
(EHI)?
3.2.1 New Opportunities and Advancements
APIs that enable access to the full scope of a patient’s electronic health information (EHI),
leveraging standards and data sets such as FHIR and USCDI, are transforming healthcare data
interoperability. This shift opens up a wide range of opportunities and advancements across
clinical care, patient engagement, research, and operational efficiency.
1. Enhanced Care Coordination and Real-Time Data Exchange
APIs that provide access to the full scope of a patient’s electronic health information help break
down data silos across disparate healthcare systems, including EHRs, labs, imaging, and billing
platforms. They enable real-time, seamless data exchange among providers, payers, and patients.
This interoperability gives clinicians timely access to comprehensive patient records, reducing
redundant testing, minimizing medical errors, and enhancing clinical decision-making throughout
the care continuum. It also supports smoother care transitions, referrals, and coordinated care
planning, particularly for individuals with complex or chronic conditions.
2. Patient Empowerment and Engagement
Patients can aggregate their data from multiple providers and EHRs into a single, unified record
using consumer-facing apps, empowering them to manage their health more proactively. APIs
enable patients to contribute self-generated health data (e.g., from wearables or remote monitoring
devices) directly into their EHR, fostering more personalized and continuous care.
3. Innovation in Healthcare Applications and AI Integration
APIs providing access to the entirety of EHIs create a fertile environment for third-party
developers to build innovative apps for clinical decision support, medication management, care
coordination, and remote monitoring. AI and machine learning can be layered on top of FHIR
APIs to automate data mapping, quality checks, and deliver real-time, personalized insights for
both clinicians and patients. Frameworks like SMART on FHIR enable secure, standardized
integration of these apps with EHRs, accelerating the adoption of advanced digital health tools.
4. Improved Administrative Efficiency and Reduced Burden
APIs providing access to the entirety of EHIs streamline administrative processes such as claims,
billing, and prior authorizations by enabling automated, real-time data exchange between providers
and payers. This reduces manual data entry, minimizes errors, and speeds up reimbursement
cycles, contributing to a more efficient healthcare system.
5. Advancements in Research and Population Health
Researchers can access richer, more comprehensive datasets (with appropriate consent),
facilitating large-scale studies, real-world evidence generation, and public health surveillance.
Harmonized data standards (USCDI, FHIR) ensure that information is consistent and comparable
across sources, supporting more robust analytics and outcomes measurement.

3.2.2 Primary Obstacles
While APIs for comprehensive EHI access offer transformative potential, significant barriers
hinder their adoption and effectiveness. These challenges span technical, regulatory, financial, and
cultural domains.
1. Technical and Structural Barriers
Many healthcare organizations rely on outdated EHRs and proprietary systems that lack native
FHIR/API support, requiring costly middleware or full replacements. Inconsistent adoption of
FHIR, USCDI, and HL7 across vendors creates fragmented data formats, complicating cross-
system integration. Building compliant APIs that meet HHS/CMS technical specifications
demands specialized expertise, which many organizations lack.
2. Financial and Resource Constraints
API development, cloud migration, and staff training strain budgets, especially for rural/small
practices. Sustaining interoperability infrastructure (e.g., FHIR server updates, security patches)
requires dedicated funding. Organizations often deprioritize interoperability investments in favor
of immediate clinical or financial needs.
3. Cultural and Organizational Resistance
Proprietary EHR vendors discourage open data sharing to retain market dominance, perpetuating
data silos. Clinicians resist changes to documentation practices or new data-sharing workflows.
Limited FHIR/USCDI expertise among IT teams and providers slows adoption.

4. Regulatory and Compliance Challenges
USCDI and FHIR versions are frequently updated, requiring continuous system adaptations to
maintain compliance. Balancing HIPAA/regulatory requirements with data-sharing mandates
increases implementation complexity (e.g., consent management, encryption). HHS API
certification processes are time-consuming, particularly for smaller providers.
5. Security and Trust Concerns
Expanded API surfaces increase vulnerability to breaches, requiring advanced authentication and
monitoring tools. Granular control over data-sharing permissions remains technically challenging
at scale.
As CMS expands API-based access to patient data through initiatives like Blue Button 2.0, the
Provider Access API, and Prior Authorization APIs, the integrity and security of these endpoints
become increasingly critical to adoption and trust. To support secure and scalable integration:
 CMS should adopt SMART on FHIR and OAuth 2.0 as foundational standards for third-
party application access.
 All API implementations should align with HIPAA Technical Safeguards (45 CFR
§164.312), with published technical guidance mapped to NIST SP 800-53 control
families.
These recommendations promote consistent security practices across platforms, reduce
implementation friction for developers, and help mitigate risks related to unauthorized access or
data misuse.
3.2.3 Case Study: Standardize with Pre-Certified API Gateways & Patterns (AWS)
Context: A CMS-aligned health plan is integrating multiple digital health applications that
require access to Medicare beneficiary data via FHIR APIs. Each integration must comply with
SMART on FHIR authentication, enforce API rate limits, and support detailed audit logging.
Challenge: Integrations were inconsistent, and each app required a separate security review —
increasing risk, slowing onboarding, and creating redundant effort across security and operations
teams.
Solution: The organization deploys a pre-certified pattern using AWS API Gateway, tightly
integrated with:
 Cognito for OAuth 2.0 Authorization Code Flow and ID token issuance
 Lambda authorizers to enforce FHIR-specific access scopes and JWT validation
 AWS WAF to provide protection against injection attacks and bots
 CloudWatch Logs for API access logging, traceability, and anomaly detection
 IAM policy boundaries for backend services accessing protected data (e.g., DynamoDB,
RDS, HealthLake)

3.3 TD-15. Bulk FHIR APIs:
3.3.1 How would increased use of bulk FHIR improve use cases and data flow?
Increased use of bulk FHIR will fundamentally improve healthcare data interoperability by
enabling efficient, secure, and scalable data flows for population-level use cases. This will drive
better clinical outcomes, support regulatory compliance, and foster innovation in analytics and
care delivery. Several key improvements emerge as the use of bulk FHIR expands.
Population-Level Data Exchange and Analytics
 Bulk FHIR enables efficient extraction and transfer of large healthcare datasets, which is
essential for use cases involving population health management, quality measurement, and
research. For example, payers can use bulk FHIR to obtain data on all patients in a cohort
to identify care gaps, track chronic conditions, or plan outreach for preventive care.
 This approach replaces the need for thousands of individual API calls with asynchronous,
parallel data processing, drastically reducing latency and boosting performance for large-
scale exports.
Regulatory Alignment and Compliance
 CMS and ASTP/ONC are actively involved in initiatives and coalitions to test and
standardize bulk FHIR data exchange.
 Bulk FHIR supports compliance with the 21st Century Cures Act, which mandates
standardized APIs for patient and population-level access to electronic health records
(EHRs). CMS programs like Data at the Point of Care and the Beneficiary Claims Data
API already leverage bulk FHIR to provide providers and accountable care organizations
with computable, cross-system data.
Improved Data Flow and Integration
 Bulk FHIR streamlines the process of extracting, transforming, and importing large
volumes of healthcare data, making integration across EHRs, payers, and analytics
platforms more straightforward and cost-effective.
 The use of standardized FHIR resources ensures that data is discrete, computable, and
easily accessible, facilitating real-time integration with applications such as clinical
decision support, quality reporting, and patient engagement tools.
 Advanced features such as type filtering and group-based exports allow organizations to
tailor data extraction to specific cohorts or resource types, reducing unnecessary data
transfer and enhancing privacy controls.

Performance and Scalability
 Real-world testing shows that bulk FHIR APIs can handle millions of resources efficiently,

3.3.2 What are the potential disadvantages of their use?
While bulk FHIR is a promising standard for large-scale healthcare data exchange, its current

implementation complexity, and inconsistent vendor support, pose significant challenges.
Addressing these disadvantages will require ongoing collaboration among EHR vendors,
healthcare organizations, and regulatory bodies to ensure bulk FHIR can fulfill its potential for
advancing interoperability and population health.

3.4 TD-16. What are the tradeoffs of maintaining point-to-point models vs. shared
network infrastructure?
3.4.1 Do current rules encourage scalable network participation?
The move toward shared network infrastructure aligns with CMS and ASTP/ONC priorities for
nationwide interoperability, improved patient access, and system efficiency. While point-to-point
models may suffice for limited, bilateral exchanges, they become unsustainable at scale and hinder
the realization of a connected, learning health system. Shared networks, leveraging standards like
FHIR and robust governance, are better suited to meet the demands of modern healthcare data
exchange, though they require careful planning and collaboration to address policy, legal, and
operational complexities.
CMS policies, by prioritizing standardized, API-driven, and patient-centered data exchange,
effectively steer the industry away from point-to-point models and toward shared network
infrastructure. This approach aligns with broader federal goals for nationwide interoperability,
efficiency, and patient empowerment. Organizations that invest in shared network models will be
better positioned to comply with current and future CMS requirements and to deliver on the
promise of interoperable, value-based care.
Key Points from CMS Policy that Encourage Scalable Network Participation
 API-Centric Approach: CMS rules, including the Interoperability and Patient Access
Final Rule and the Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final Rule, require payers and
providers to implement standardized APIs (notably FHIR-based). These APIs are built for
broad, repeatable connectivity, making it easier for many parties to participate in data
exchange without the need for custom, point-to-point integrations.
 Network-Agnostic, But Scalable: While CMS does not currently require participation in
a single "trusted exchange network" (such as TEFCA), the rules are intentionally structured
to support scalable participation in any network infrastructure that adopts these standards.
This allows organizations to connect once and exchange data with multiple entities,
promoting scalability.
 Patient and Provider Access: The rules mandate that data be made available to patients
and providers in near real-time and through any third-party application of the patient’s

3.4.2 What changes would improve alignment (for example, API unification, reciprocal
access)?
Despite the policy direction, implementation remains challenging for many organizations,
especially smaller providers and payers, due to funding, workflow, and technical hurdles. To
accelerate progress and encourage more equitable participation, CMS should consider the
following measures:
1. Provide targeted technical assistance and implementation blueprints and fund public-
private shared utilities (e.g., FHIR endpoint directories, master patient indexes) to lower
entry barriers.
2. Standardize minimum API and FHIR implementation requirements for participation in
shared networks to reduce variability in API behavior across organizations and facilitate
more seamless connectivity.
3. Strengthen incentives for participation in shared frameworks, such as TEFCA or regional
health information exchanges, to promote broader adoption while maintaining a network-
agnostic stance. Many stakeholders view TEFCA and CMS’s API-based requirements as
parallel but disconnected tracks. CMS should issue clear policy alignment statements,
integration pathways, or timelines explaining how participation in TEFCA and
implementation of FHIR APIs complement each other.
4. Require large organizations to regularly report network affiliations, FHIR API capabilities,
and key interoperability metrics, such as data exchange volume and response times, to drive
accountability and encourage lagging entities to invest in scalable, standards-based
infrastructure.
As clinical and claims data move across health information networks, FHIR endpoints, and third-
party systems, maintaining data integrity and consistency becomes foundational to
interoperability.
CMS should consider:
 Promoting adoption of digital signatures or cryptographic hashing to validate the integrity
of FHIR payloads and prevent tampering during transmission.
 Encouraging the implementation of validation protocols across HIEs and exchange
networks to verify schema conformance and data completeness.

3.5 TD-17. Given operational costs, what role should CMS or ASTP/ONC or both
have in ensuring viability of healthcare data sharing networks, including
enough supply and demand, that results in usage and outcomes?
Ensuring the operational and financial viability of healthcare data sharing networks—while
achieving sufficient supply (participation) and demand (usage) that translate into improved
outcomes—requires coordinated action from both CMS and ASTP/ONC. Their roles are distinct
but complementary, leveraging regulatory authority, standard-setting, incentives, and technical
support to create a robust ecosystem for interoperable health data exchange.
How CMS and ASTP/ONC Policies Can Incentivize Healthcare Data Sharing Networks
 Standardization and Certification Requirements: ONC can mature technical standards
(such as FHIR and USCDI) and certification criteria for health IT systems, ensuring that
data exchange is not only possible but also efficient, secure, and outcomes oriented.
 Payment and Participation Incentives Tied to Data Exchange: CMS can continue
leveraging its payment programs (such as the Promoting Interoperability Program and
value-based care models) to reward hospitals and clinicians for the meaningful use of
certified EHR technology and for participating in robust health information exchange
(HIE) networks.
 Reducing Administrative Burden and Streamlining Processes: CMS policies are
increasingly focused on reducing administrative friction—such as through electronic prior
authorization requirements and streamlined payer-to-payer data exchange—making it
easier for providers to participate in data sharing initiatives and focus on delivering better
outcomes. By further simplifying compliance and reporting requirements for those who
participate in robust, standardized exchange networks, CMS can encourage greater
adoption and sustained use of interoperable systems.
 Addressing Information Blocking and Transparency: ASTP/ONC should continue
enforcing information blocking regulations to ensure that providers, payers, and health IT
developers do not unreasonably restrict access to, exchange of, or use of electronic health
information. At the same time, CMS can reinforce transparency by publicly reporting
instances of information blocking and requiring data availability for patients and providers,
thereby incentivizing organizations to prioritize outcomes-driven data exchange.
 Continuous Stakeholder Engagement and Policy Evolution CMS and ASTP/ONC
should regularly seek public input to identify barriers and opportunities for outcomes-
driven data exchange, ensuring that policies remain responsive to evolving needs and
technologies.
By combining these approaches, CMS and ASTP/ONC create a comprehensive framework that
not only incentivizes the adoption of interoperable health IT but also ensures that data exchange
initiatives are tightly linked to improved patient and population health outcomes. Together, their
coordinated actions are essential to the operational and financial viability of healthcare data sharing
networks, ensuring these networks are widely used and deliver improved health outcomes.
