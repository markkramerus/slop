Data Sharing
Data sharing has evolved dramatically over the past decade, shifting from cumbersome file-based
transfers with compromised governance to streamlined, secure, real-time solutions.
A key architectural component of the Snowflake AI Data Cloud Platform is its ability to securely share
data and application assets between Snowflake accounts without the need to move or copy the
underlying data. This feature allows seamless, secure, and near real-time collaboration between different
teams, departments, or external partners, regardless of the choice of cloud provider or region.
At its core, Snowflake data sharing enables:
● Secure Access: Data remains in the provider's account, and access is granted to consumer
accounts via "shares", which are essentially containers pointing to the data. This eliminates the
need for data duplication and transfers, enhancing security and maintaining data integrity.
● Real-time Collaboration: Consumers have live, read-only access to the shared data, allowing
them to query it directly within their own Snowflake account and receive immediate updates as
the data changes.
● Cost Efficiency: Both providers and consumers benefit from cost savings. Providers avoid the
costs of duplicating and transferring data, while consumers only pay for the compute resources
(virtual warehouses) they use to query the shared data.
● Flexibility: Data can be shared internally (within the same account) or externally (across different
Snowflake accounts, even across different cloud providers and regions). Snowflake also offers
reader accounts for sharing data with external users who are not Snowflake customers.
Snowflake enables data sharing through the use of Shares, which are account-level objects created by
data providers and imported by data consumers. When a data consumer imports a share, data is not
moved or copied. The consumer is being granted direct read-only access to the objects that the provider
has authorized for them. All the data remains securely stored in the provider’s account, while different
users or organizations access it in real-time. This method eliminates data duplication, reduces storage
costs, and ensures that all parties work with the most current version of the data.
Snowflake offers several options for sharing data, including Direct Shares, Listings, and Data Exchanges.
Direct Shares enable providers to directly share specific database objects with another account in their
region. A Listing allows providers to enhance the contents of the share with additional metadata, such as
a data dictionary, sample queries, and provider contact information. The listing can be posted to any other
Snowflake account, regardless of cloud or region, as a data product. For sharing with wider audiences,
Snowflake offers both a public Marketplace and private Data Exchanges. Listings in the Public
Marketplace can be seen and imported by any Snowflake customer across the globe, while Private
Exchanges are restricted to invited participants only.
Snowflake Data Sharing reduces the friction between data providers and consumers, increases the
security and governance of the data assets, and can shorten the time to insight for data-driven
organizations.

Current State - Challenges and Opportunities
Historically, HL7/FHIR servers were sufficient to share the volume of data in the healthcare ecosystem
and the fee for service/transactional model. Today, with the implementation of value-based care models
requiring a longitudinal patient record and the explosion of data volumes and data types; from genomics
to device to audio, video, etc., FHIR servers will not be sufficient. Furthermore, the implementation of
FHIR servers today creates a copy of data and therefore data silos. We need to bring data together
without making copies, modern cloud technologies provide such a solution.
A Future State Healthcare Network
We envision a future in which ADT messages still flow to transition patient care from inpatient to
outpatient, medication histories are reconciled, and TEFCA facilitates the flow of information (copies)
throughout the ecosystem to support transactions. We believe that in order for value care-based models
to ultimately succeed, alongside that transactional flow is a “data sharing” flow which will be covered in
detail below.

Introduction
Identity resolution is more than a data quality initiative—it is the foundation for every healthcare
transformation effort that depends on accurate, connected, and complete data. In a health care system
where fragmentation across EHRs, CRMs, analytics platforms, and cloud environments are widespread,
precisely resolving identity is the essential first step toward achieving true interoperability, improving
outcomes, and enhancing operational efficiency.
Critically, identity resolution must go beyond just patients—it must accurately identify and link data for all
individuals involved in care delivery and coordination, including caregivers, family members, and
healthcare professionals. Whether someone is receiving care, providing it, managing benefits, or
supporting a loved one, the ability to reliably match and connect data to the right person is foundational.
This challenge spans the entire healthcare ecosystem: from inpatient and ambulatory settings to long-
term care, health insurance, health information exchanges (HIEs), and health technology vendors.
Without a unified, trusted identity for each individual, efforts in population health, care coordination, and
digital transformation are fundamentally compromised.
1. Interoperability Depends on Identity
FHIR, TEFCA, and cloud-native architectures are designed to facilitate data sharing across healthcare
systems, but interoperability without robust identity resolution only exacerbates the existing chaos.
Identity is the key that enables disparate systems and sources to speak the same language about the
same individual or entity, ensuring that data exchanged is linked to the correct person.
HIMSS (Health Information Management Systems Society) defines four levels of interoperability, each of
which plays a critical role in enabling seamless data exchange across the healthcare ecosystem.
However, none of these levels can function effectively without a solid identity foundation to ensure that
the right person is connected to the right data.
The four levels of HIMSS interoperability1:
1. Foundational: Establishes the inter-connectivity requirements needed for one system or
application to securely communicate data to and receive data from another
2. Structural: Defines the format, syntax and organization of data exchange including at the data
field level for interpretation
3. Semantic: Provides for common underlying models and codification of the data including the use
of data elements with standardized definitions from publicly available value sets and coding
vocabularies, providing shared understanding and meaning to the user
4. Organizational: Includes governance, policy, social, legal and organizational considerations to
facilitate the secure, seamless and timely communication and use of data both within and
1 https://legacy.himss.org/resources/interoperability-healthcare
10

between organizations, entities and individuals. These components enable shared consent, trust
and integrated end-user processes and workflows
While modern systems have achieved much of the above, an essential foundational level is still missing—
an Identity Interoperability level. This level, between Semantic (Level 3) and Organizational (Level 4),
would focus on accurately identifying which individual the data is associated with and ensuring that the
receiving system can match this data to the correct individual. Without identity resolution at this stage,
data exchange is meaningless; the information may flow, but it cannot be utilized effectively, defeating the
purpose of interoperability. Unlike Structural (level 2) and Semantic (Level 3) where most of the
interoperability burden falls on the sender, Identity Interoperability largely falls on the receiver to have
accurate person and provider matching in their technical ecosystem in order to match and land the
incoming data on the correct records within their system(s).
Although some progress has been made in standardizing patient demographic data across systems, the
challenge remains significant. Past efforts have mostly focused on ensuring that demographic data (e.g.,
name, address, phone number) is collected consistently and in a structured way. However, this has seen
limited success due to several key challenges:
● While having a more complete and structured set of demographic data can improve matching
algorithms, this only works as long as that data remains stable. Minor changes, such as a new
address or phone number, can disrupt matching. More significant changes, like a change in last
name due to marriage or divorce, or simultaneous changes to multiple attributes, make accurate
identity matching even harder.
● Patients often hesitate to provide certain demographic details—such as Social Security Numbers
(SSNs)—due to privacy concerns, especially in light of frequent security breaches. This
resistance limits the ability of matching algorithms to reliably link individuals across systems.
● The matching technology most commonly used today relies heavily on accurate data input.
However, data entry errors, such as typos or missing information, can significantly hinder
matching accuracy. A mistake in a key field (e.g., a typo in date of birth) can break the entire
matching process, while issues in other fields (e.g., address or phone number) can still cause
errors—especially when multiple fields are inaccurate simultaneously.
To address these challenges and truly unlock the potential of interoperability, an Identity Interoperability
layer needs to be established. This layer would ensure that the person behind the data is correctly
identified and matched across systems and organizations, even in the face of demographic changes or
incomplete data. The success of all healthcare transformation initiatives and modern health information
data exchange hinges on this foundational layer. Without it, the promise of seamless, secure, and
actionable healthcare data exchange will remain an unfulfilled aspiration, leaving healthcare organizations
unable to fully leverage the data exchanged.
Examples:
● Sending a patient’s comprehensive, well-structured CDA document, fully coded (with the correct
vocabularies and terminologies) to a neighboring provider is meaningless if the receiving EHR
can’t match the identity because of slight discrepancies in demographic information between the
CDA and what is on file at the receiving organization. Without accurate identity resolution, the
data is essentially unusable, no matter how well-structured or standardized it is.
● A TEFCA query to retrieve more information about a patient is only useful if it returns meaningful
data. In reality, inconsistent identity matching across nodes often leads to no results, simply
because the query’s demographic details don’t perfectly match those stored at other nodes.
These small discrepancies can render the query useless, undermining the entire value of data
sharing.
11

● A data lake or warehouse only holds value if it can accurately identify that the patient in the EHR
is the same person in the Customer Relationship Management (CRM), HIE, and call center
systems. Without robust identity resolution, the data lake risks fragmented and disconnected
records, rendering it ineffective.
● A health insurance provider needs to aggregate claims, clinical, and other data from multiple
healthcare facilities and other data trading partners to fully understand the members they support
the care for. However, discrepancies in patient identity across all the sending systems and
organizations result in incorrect matches. This leads to duplicate claims, delays in
reimbursement, and erroneous denials or approvals. Without accurate identity resolution, the
insurance provider risks significant operational inefficiencies, financial loss, and patient
dissatisfaction, as the data being processed may be tied to the wrong individual or treatment
episode.
2. Accurate Identity = Accurate Insights
Without a trusted identity foundation, patient and provider records become fragmented across systems,
leading to data silos, duplication, and critical knowledge gaps. Fragmented data undermines the very
initiatives that are central to improving healthcare outcomes: from enterprise analytics to population
health management, care coordination, and public health reporting. When identity resolution is lacking,
missing links between disparate systems and unresolved duplicate records within individual systems
result in data integrity issues, making it impossible to trust the insights derived from that data.
These data gaps and inaccuracies have far-reaching consequences. Enterprise analytics designed to
improve operational efficiency or drive strategic decisions are compromised when the data doesn’t
represent a unified, accurate picture of each individual. For example, population health initiatives—which
rely on accurate, connected data to monitor and manage patient cohorts—are fundamentally impaired
when identity mismatches prevent systems from recognizing the same individual across care episodes or
healthcare providers. This leads to incomplete patient profiles, which hinder effective care coordination
and make it difficult to identify at-risk populations.
Additionally public health and quality reporting depend on accurate, holistic data to track disease trends,
measure healthcare quality, and guide policy decisions. When patient records are fragmented, this data
can be misleading, leading to poor decision-making at both the clinical and policy levels.
Ultimately, without accurate identity resolution, healthcare systems are unable to fully leverage their data
to improve patient care, optimize operations, or drive innovation. The ability to connect and unify patient
and provider data across systems is foundational to realizing the full potential of digital health
transformation.
Examples
● It’s impossible to accurately track readmission rates, health equity, or cost-of-care trends if the
same patient is counted as three different individuals in your data. For example, if a patient has
multiple records due to an address change or a system mismatch, it will distort performance
metrics, making it difficult to identify opportunities for improvement in care coordination or
resource allocation. This fragmentation can lead to under estimating readmissions or
misunderstanding health disparities, ultimately hindering efforts to reduce costs and improve
patient outcomes.
● Fragmented data can cause false negatives in analytics, leading to missed opportunities in
patient care. For instance, a care gap report might suggest that a patient missed an important lab
test, triggering unnecessary follow-ups or interventions. However, the lab result may already be
documented in a duplicate record from another encounter or location. This results in wasted time
12

and resources while also potentially delaying the timely care the patient needs. Such errors not
only lead to operational inefficiencies but can also impact clinical decision-making.
● In population health management, the goal is to identify trends and at-risk groups. However, if
patient records are fragmented or not linked correctly, it’s difficult to accurately assess chronic
disease prevalence, risk factors, or the effectiveness of health interventions. For example, if a
patient with diabetes is counted as two different individuals due to inconsistent records, it may
skew the health data for a particular community, leading to misdirected public health interventions
or inefficient allocation of resources.
● Accurate public health reporting is vital for tracking disease outbreaks and guiding national health
policies. Fragmented data can create misleading reports that fail to accurately represent the true
state of public health. For example, during a flu outbreak, inaccurate data might suggest that
there are more cases than in reality, leading to misinformed policies or incorrect public health
response efforts. Addressing identity fragmentation ensures that public health data is accurate,
enabling timely responses and more effective interventions.
3. Better Identity Enables Better Outcomes
When clinicians and care teams have a 360-degree view of a patient, powered by trusted identity data,
they are positioned to make faster, safer, and more informed decisions. This comprehensive view allows
healthcare providers to access a patient’s complete medical history, including past treatments, allergies,
medications, and any other critical health information, all from one unified source. This not only enhances
the accuracy of clinical decisions but also enables real-time collaboration across different providers and
specialties.
A complete and 360-degree view of all of a patient’s data is more than just an aggregation of individual
facts; it allows clinicians to personalize care by understanding the full scope of a patient's needs. Whether
it’s addressing chronic conditions, managing mental health, or coordinating multiple specialists, this
holistic approach makes patient care more tailored and effective, leading to better clinical outcomes. By
having all relevant information at their fingertips, clinicians can reduce the risk of medical errors, avoid
redundant tests, and prescribe the most appropriate treatments from the outset.
When clinicians and care teams have a 360-degree view of a patient—powered by trusted identity data—
they can make faster, safer, and more informed decisions. It also empowers more personalized patient
outreach, proactive engagement, and reduce care disparities.
Examples
● When care teams have a complete, unified view of a patient—not only from the local EHR but
also across the entire healthcare ecosystem, including external partners and data shared through
TEFCA queries—they are equipped to deliver whole-person care. This 360-degree view ensures
that clinicians make well-informed decisions, considering every aspect of a patient’s health.
Whether it’s medical history from different care settings, clean non-duplicated records in their
local EHR, or critical social data from community health partners, a connected ecosystem
enables the delivery of personalized care that’s more comprehensive and effective. For instance,
knowing that a patient with diabetes has recently seen a specialist or undergone a procedure
outside the primary care office ensures that all aspects of care are accounted for, reducing
duplication and preventing harmful care gaps.
● Integrating nonclinical data, such as social determinants of health (SDoH) data, into a patient’s
record allows clinicians to better understand the broader factors influencing a patient’s well-
being—beyond just medical history. For example, if a patient has limited access to transportation,
a healthcare system could proactively offer ride-share credits for routine appointments, ensuring
the patient can attend preventive care visits. This reduces the likelihood of missed appointments
13

and costly ER visits due to neglected health concerns. By addressing these non-clinical barriers,
healthcare providers can take more holistic action and improve patient engagement, reducing
overall healthcare costs in the long run.
● A patient recovering from surgery may have a high risk of hospital readmission if they do not have
adequate home support. By matching social data (such as whether the patient lives alone or has
access to caregiving support) with their medical record, care teams can ensure the patient
receives extra home health visits, or additional services like meal delivery or post-surgery
physical therapy. This personalized care helps prevent complications and reduces readmissions,
while also improving the patient’s overall recovery process.
4. Operational Efficiency Requires Trusted Identity Data
From automating workflows to powering AI-driven decision-making and enhancing call center routing,
trusted identity data is the bedrock that enables healthcare systems to operate more efficiently by
removing friction and eliminating waste. When patient and provider data is accurate, unified, and up-to-
date, the entire healthcare ecosystem becomes more agile, reducing unnecessary steps and manual
intervention.
For example, a clean, unified record eliminates the need for duplicate data entry and reduces the time
spent by administrative staff validating patient information. When every healthcare system and application
is connected to a trusted identity, routine tasks can be automated without manual lookups, minimizing the
risk of errors and enhancing the speed and efficiency of patient care.
Examples
● In a hospital or clinic setting, manual entry of patient information is time-consuming and prone to
errors. If a system doesn’t recognize that the patient in the ER is the same patient that has been
treated by the cardiology department, staff will have to manually search multiple systems, re-
enter data, or even call the patient to confirm details. With trusted identity resolution, this process
is automated. The system seamlessly connects patient records across the enterprise, reducing
administrative burden and allowing staff to focus on patient care.
● In healthcare call centers, identity resolution helps to ensure that patients are routed to the right
department and provider based on their needs, significantly reducing the number of transfers and
wait times. For instance, if a patient calls to inquire about their recent blood test results, the
system instantly recognizes their identity, pulling up the correct medical record, so the call can be
routed directly to the correct provider or department, bypassing administrative staff and reducing
patient frustration.
5. Regulatory & Compliance Risks Shrink with Trusted Identity Data
Duplicate or mismatched patient records not only create operational inefficiencies but also expose
healthcare organizations to significant regulatory and compliance risks. Without trusted identity data,
healthcare organizations face the very real danger of violating privacy laws, incurring financial penalties,
and damaging their reputation.
Examples
● Mismatched patient records pose a direct threat to HIPAA compliance. If a healthcare
organization cannot accurately identify and link patient records across different systems, sensitive
information could be disclosed to unauthorized individuals or entities, violating patient privacy
14

protections. Trusted identity resolution ensures that patient data is correctly associated with the
right person and remains confidential, reducing the risk of accidental exposure or unauthorized
access.
● Healthcare providers are required to maintain accurate, transparent audit trails to demonstrate
compliance with HIPAA and other healthcare regulations. Duplicate or fragmented patient records
make it difficult to maintain a consistent and accurate record of data access and usage, which
can result in audit failures and incorrect or incomplete patient rights such as ‘accounting of
disclosures.
● Consent management is a cornerstone of HIPAA compliance and health care operations,
particularly in relation to data sharing across healthcare providers, insurers, and other third
parties. If patient identities are not properly matched, the system may not be able to accurately
track whether a patient has consented to share their medical records with other entities. This data
mismatch can result in violations of patient consent.
● Duplicate or mismatched records often lead to incorrect billing and insurance claims. This may
lead to penalties delay claims processing, affecting revenue cycles.
6. Training & Use of Responsible AI Requires Complete & Trusted Datasets
In healthcare, Artificial Intelligence (AI) is revolutionizing everything from diagnostics and clinical decision
support to predictive analytics and personalized care. However, AI's ability to deliver meaningful,
accurate, and equitable results depends not only on how it's trained and the quality of the training data,
but also on the quality and completeness of the data it uses once trained. Trusted identity resolution is the
foundation for both training responsible AI and ensuring that AI systems in healthcare have access to the
most accurate and comprehensive data possible. Without resolving patient identities consistently across
systems, healthcare AI risks producing results that are biased, incomplete, or inaccurate, which can lead
to poor outcomes.
To harness AI’s full potential in healthcare, organizations must ensure that patient and provider data is
correctly linked, up-to-date, and accurate—across the entire ecosystem of clinical systems, care
coordination tools, and third-party data. Only then can AI systems make safe, fair, and data-driven
decisions that improve patient care, optimize workflows, and reduce healthcare costs while maintaining
regulatory compliance. Whether in training AI models or utilizing them in real-world applications, trusted
identity resolution is essential for the responsible and effective use of AI in healthcare.
Examples:
● An AI system in a hospital uses patient records to help clinicians make diagnostic decisions.
However, due to duplicate records or inaccurate identity resolution, the AI draws on fragmented
or conflicting data about the same patient (e.g., an incomplete medical history, allergy records, or
lab results). The AI might suggest an incorrect diagnosis or fail to identify critical drug
interactions, allergic reactions, or undiagnosed conditions. This can lead to misdiagnosis,
ineffective treatment plans, and potentially dangerous health outcomes.
● A telemedicine platform uses AI for initial diagnosis and care recommendations based on data
pulled from various sources (e.g., EHRs, remote patient monitoring devices). However, if there
are mismatched or duplicate patient records between the telemedicine platform and systems it
pulls from, the AI might process incorrect information or base a recommendation on incomplete
information.

7. Identity Verification/Proofing is an Important & Relate Step
In healthcare, identity verification and proofing are essential steps in ensuring the accuracy, security, and
integrity of patient and provider data across the healthcare ecosystem. Effective identity verification
allows healthcare organizations to confirm that the individual accessing services or data is who they say
they are—whether the individual is a patient, caregiver, family member, or healthcare professional.
The recent federal regulations in the U.S., such as the 21st Century Cures Act and TEFCA (Trusted
Exchange Framework and Common Agreement), emphasize the need for secure and accurate identity
verification as part of the movement toward interoperable health data systems. As healthcare
organizations adopt advanced digital solutions, effective identity proofing ensures that the right data
reaches the right individuals, whether they are clinicians, patients, or third-party partners. Without robust
identity verification, the integrity of healthcare data systems is compromised, which can lead to fraud,
misidentification, and errors in care delivery.
However, the process does not end with identity verification—the verified identity must seamlessly flow
throughout the entire healthcare ecosystem. Too often, identity verification is treated as a standalone
process, and valuable data, such as patient demographics obtained during verification, is discarded after
the verification step. This approach overlooks the critical role that this verified data plays in ensuring
consistent, accurate identity resolution across multiple systems.
Take, for example, a typical use case for patient identity verification: the creation of a portal account.
Identity verification is required at this stage to ensure that the person accessing the portal is who they
claim to be. However, this verification should not be the final step. The verified demographics, such as the
patient’s name, date of birth, and contact information, should not only be retained but also used to match
the portal account to the patient’s correct medical record in the system. This ensures that the individual
interacting with the system is correctly tied to their health data.
The verified identity also needs to flow across the entire healthcare ecosystem. When identity verification
and resolution work together in a holistic way, all systems involved in patient care can leverage the same
trusted identity. Only by ensuring that verified identity data is continuously shared, updated, and
referenced across the entire healthcare ecosystem can organizations fully realize the benefits of
accurate, reliable, and interoperable healthcare data. This alignment solves larger and more complex use
cases than just the initial identity verification needs.
Examples:
● If a patient successfully completes identity verification but is mistakenly linked to a different
patient with similar demographics, the entire process fails. Although the individual’s identity is
verified, the system erroneously connects them to the wrong medical record, which not only
causes significant patient frustration but also risks the exposure of protected health information
(PHI).
● When a healthcare professional completes identity verification but is incorrectly linked to a
different user account, there is a significant risk that inappropriate access rights will be granted.
This not only compromises the security of patient data but could also lead to unauthorized access
to sensitive health records, which may result in legal and compliance violations. Without proper
identity resolution, the professional could be given access to areas they should not, increasing
the risk of fraud, data misuse, and potentially violating various regulations.

8. Longitudinal Views, Not Encounters, Lead to Better Health Outcomes
In the traditional healthcare model, patient data is often fragmented, siloed, and tied to specific episodes
of care or encounters—meaning that information is only collected or updated during a patient’s visit to a
healthcare provider. However, this episodic approach fails to capture the full continuum of care for a
patient and limits the ability to make holistic, data-driven decisions that can improve both health outcomes
and operational efficiency.
Building a longitudinal view of patient health, where data is continuously gathered and integrated across
all encounters, allows healthcare organizations to gain a complete picture of a patient’s history,
behaviors, and ongoing needs. This longitudinal approach ensures that all touchpoints in a patient's
healthcare journey—from primary care visits to hospital admissions to wellness checkups—are captured
and linked together to form an accurate, real-time, and comprehensive health record.
However, achieving this comprehensive view requires more than just aggregating data from different
encounters; it demands consistent identity resolution to ensure that a patient’s health information is
consistently and correctly linked across systems, organizations, and over time. Given the longitudinal
nature, the matching solution must be able to deal with the patient’s demographics changing over time
and with life events (such as marriage and divorce), as well as misaligned attributes from systems that
may collect different demographics and identifiers. Without this foundational step, fragmented,
inconsistent, or inaccurate data can lead to missed opportunities for early intervention, care coordination
breakdowns, and inefficient operations.
Examples:
● A patient has unresolved duplicate records within the same EHR due to slightly different
demographic data (e.g., “John A. Smith” vs. “Jonathan Smith”). Without proper identity resolution,
each record appears as a sperate patient and not included in the comprehensive longitudinal
view.
● A patient is treated at a hospital outside their primary health system. The data is share back with
the primary system, but the data from the external EHR isn’t matched due to variations in
demographic fields.
● An HIE struggles to build out a longitudinal view because its dozens of contributing health system
each send in slightly different demographics and there is no common identifier between systems.
9. Patient Experience Suffers without Trusted & Complete Identity
Identity resolution is a cornerstone of improving patient experience in healthcare transformation. By
establishing a single, accurate, and unified patient identity, healthcare providers can deliver truly
personalized care, streamline administrative processes, and ensure seamless continuity across multiple
care settings. This foundational capability not only supports better health outcomes but also fosters
increased patient satisfaction and stronger, more trusting patient-provider relationships.
Today’s patients expect effortless, secure access to their complete health information—whether through
patient portals, mobile apps, or connected devices. Effective identity resolution ensures that all relevant
data, from lab results to medication histories, is accurately linked to one comprehensive patient profile.
This prevents confusion caused by fragmented or duplicate records, and delivers a consistent, holistic
view of their health journey. Trusted identity also enables healthcare organizations to tailor
communications, reminders, and care workflows to the individual’s unique needs, enhancing engagement
and adherence.
17

Without robust and reliable identity resolution, patients risk encountering incomplete, inconsistent, or
incorrect information due to duplicate or unlinked records across and within systems. Such gaps can lead
to patient confusion, frustration, erosion of trust in the healthcare provider, and even raise privacy and
security concerns when data is mismatched or exposed improperly.
Examples:
● A patient logs into their healthcare provider’s portal to review details of a recent emergency
department visit. However, because the emergency department was unable to locate the patient’s
existing record, a new duplicate record was created for that visit. As a result, the patient’s portal
does not display the emergency visit information, leading to confusion. When the patient calls to
ask about the missing data, they struggle to understand why their records are incomplete or
inconsistent, undermining trust and satisfaction.
● A patient is referred to a specialist within the same health system as their primary care provider
(PCP). Expecting seamless coordination, the patient logs into the organization’s portal but cannot
find any information from the specialist visit. Unbeknownst to the patient, the specialist uses a
different EHR system, and the encounter data was never linked to the patient’s primary medical
record. This fragmentation disrupts care continuity and leaves the patient feeling disconnected
from their own healthcare journey.
Conclusion
Without a trusted identity foundation, data is limiting, even dangerous. With trusted identity foundation,
data becomes powerful, insightful, meaningful, and actionable. Identity resolution is not just a technical
requirement—it is the essential foundation for transforming healthcare delivery, improving patient
outcomes, and driving operational excellence. Accurate and trusted identity enables seamless data
integration across fragmented systems, empowering clinicians, patients, and organizations with a
complete and unified view of health information. Without it, the promise of interoperability, personalized
care, efficient workflows, and advanced analytics remains out of reach. As healthcare continues to
evolve—with increasing reliance on digital tools, data sharing networks, and AI—addressing the lack of
robust identity resolution capabilities is critical to building a healthcare ecosystem that is connected,
trusted, and patient-centered. Ultimately, resolving identity challenges unlocks the full potential of
healthcare data and enables transformation, delivering better care experiences and healthier populations.
It is vital to ensure that all parts of the health care ecosystem have accurate patient matching as part of
their essential infrastructure. This will only continue to increase in importance as health systems, payers,
and health tech companies move toward smarter, more connected care.
Suggestions
● Encourage the adoption and use of better and proven record matching technology. There are
newer matching solutions that are proven to overcome all the challenges mentioned above.
Commonly referred to as “referential matching,” these solutions incorporated a trusted and vetted
set of reference data for the nation-wide population, that is used to overcome changes to a
person demographics overtime, and the missed-aligned attribute problem; where one record
might have name, DOB and address while the other record might have name, SSN and phone
number. These solutions also typically include more advance probabilistic approaches than the
typical EHR and other certified health information technology on the market today.
Incorporate requirements into certified health information technology to have accurate and advanced
matching capabilities. The majority of the existing four levels of the HIMSS defined interoperability levels

have already been addressed with requirements to certified health technology. However, little has been
done to require certified health information technology to address the missing ‘Identity Interoperability’
level. The robustness and accuracy between different vendors vary widely, leading to very inconsistent
results and differing levels of fragmentation depending on what technology vendor does the matching.
That needs to change to require certified health information technology vendors to take up the lack of
identity interoperability in the current systems and data exchanges. Accuracy of the patient record
matching is just as vital as Structural and Semantic interoperability; in fact, it is more important. What is
