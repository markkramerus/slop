PROVIDERS Digital Health Apps PR-1. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage providers, including those in rural areas, to leverage approved digital health products for their patients? Financial and Incentive Alignment : To promote the adoption of approved digital health products, CMS can expand reimbursement models to include virtual visits, chronic care management, remote patient monitoring, asynchronous communication, and AI -assisted diagnostics. Additionally, CMS should subsidize broadband and device accessibility in rural and underserved communities through grants or technology stipends. D igital health integration metrics should be incorporated into value - based care frameworks and the Merit -based Incentive Payment System (MIPS) and Alternative Payment Models (APMs). Establishment of Trust and Standards : CMS should maintain and disseminate an endorsed registry of approved digital tools to reduce ambiguity for healthcare providers. What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective utilization of the most useful and innovative applications for physician workflows, such as quality measurement reporting, clinical documentation, and billing tasks? How could these obstacles be mitigated? The fragmented health IT ecosystem faces significant challenges due to the lack of interoperability and standardization across Electronic Health Records (EHRs), registries, and reporting systems. This fragmentation hinders the integration of innovative tools, leading to redundant data entry, inconsistent data quality, and difficulty in scaling best -in-class applications across different sites. To mitigate these issues CMS should strengthen and enforce interoperability standards, such as expanding the United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) and advancing the implementation maturity of Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR). Additionally, CMS should incentivize EHR vendors to open Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) for third- party tool integration through certification requirements. Ever -changing documentation, quality reporting, and billing requirements create moving targets for application developers. This regulatory complexity results in a focus on compliance rather than usability, often leading to tools that increase rather than r educe the administrative burden on providers. To address this, CMS should align quality measures across its programs and simplify reporting frameworks. Creating “ digital sandbox” environments where vendors can test tools using real -world data and CMS -defin ed synthetic test cases would also foster innovation. The complexity of billing and adjudication codes adds to the challenges. There are over a thousand possible remit codes on electronic remittance advices, creating a labyrinth of combinations that complicate understanding and responding to government payer claim adjudication and denials. This complexity makes automation difficult and costly, as codes are not consistently applied. The sheer volume of possible code combinations increases the provider’s cost to respond and ultimately secure payer payments. Simplifying these codes could reduce costs and streamline the process. Misaligned financial incentives pose another significant barrier to the adoption of innovative workflow tools like AI scribes, ambient documentation, and automation platforms. While these tools can reduce provider time, they often increase IT expenditure with limited reimbursement, making it difficult for health systems to justify their return on investment (ROI). To overcome this, CMS should expand reimbursement for digitally enabled care delivery models, documentation, and reporting solutions through Curr ent Procedural Terminology (CPT) codes and value -based care incentives. Supporting shared savings models where digital efficiency gains are recognized financially would also be beneficial. Cybersecurity is often a secondary concern in application development, especially in systems for documentation, billing, or quality reporting. Many applications lack built -in secure authentication or robust audit trails. Furthermore, integrations between t ools, such as EHRs and billing systems, can introduce vulnerabilities if APIs are misconfigured or unmonitored. To address these concerns, CMS should require security certifications for such applications and promote a “ security by design” approach, ensuring that cybersecurity is prioritized from the outset.

What are ways CMS or partners can help with simplifying clinical quality data responsibilities of providers? a. What would be the benefits and downsides of using Bulk FHIR data exports from EHRs to CMS to simplify clinical quality data submissions? Can CMS reduce the burden on providers by performing quality metrics calculations leveraging Bulk FHIR data exports? We strongly support leveraging modern, scalable data exchange methods such as Bulk FHIR (Flat FHIR) to reduce fragmentation in clinical quality reporting. If executed thoughtfully, this approach can significantly reduce provider burden and increase data consistency , but it also introduces technical, governance, and policy considerations that must be addressed. Bulk FHIR is a promising mechanism to modernize and streamline quality reporting. With the right policy, privacy, and infrastructure safeguards, CMS can reduce provider burden by centralizing quality measurement and harmonizing data pipelines. However, suc cess will depend on investment in infrastructure, transparency in calculations, and continued efforts to close the gap between structured and unstructured clinical data. Utilizing Bulk FHIR for quality data submissions offers several benefits. It significantly reduces redundant manual work by automating the extraction of relevant patient -level data in a standardized format. This automation diminishes the need for local mapping, data transformation, and custom file generation for each quality progr am. Bulk FHIR leverages existing API infrastructure. Many certified EHRs are already mandated to support Bulk FHIR export capabilities under the ONC Cures Act Final Rule. This builds on the same ecosystem used for payer interoperability and patient access, enabling the infrastructure to serve multiple purposes. Additionally, this approach enables CMS to perform centralized quality calculations, shifting the burden of measure computation and versioning to CMS or its designees. This ensures greater consistency in the application of measures, reducing variability ac ross provider submissions. Moreover, Bulk FHIR supports real -time or near -real-time measurement. Scheduled Bulk FHIR exports, occurring periodically (e.g., daily or weekly), facilitate faster feedback loops and dynamic 
Page 9 of 18 performance dashboards. Furthermore, the reuse of Bulk FHIR extracts across programs amplifies the return on investment. The same data can be utilized for population health, value -based care contracts, and public health reporting, promoting multipurpose use. Despite these advantages, there are downsides and challenges associated with the Bulk FHIR approach. Data completeness and fidelity concerns arise since Bulk FHIR currently focuses on structured data elements. Many quality measures depend on clinical nuanc es stored in unstructured data, scanned documents, or embedded workflows that may not be captured in Bulk FHIR resources. Technical, cost, and infrastructure demands also present challenges. The implementation of FHIR is EHR vendor -specific and not fully standardized, with vendors charging for API access and usage, potentially making participation cost prohibitive. Generating large- scale, secure FHIR exports necessitates substantial compute, storage, and network bandwidth, particularly for multi -entity health systems. Small and rural providers may lack the resources to implement and maintain these capabilities without addition al support. Privacy, consent, cybersecurity, and governance issues are critical considerations. Patient -level data exports to CMS raise concerns about data minimization, patient consent, and secondary use, especially under HIPAA and other state privacy laws. Clear gov ernance and use- case definitions must be established. Improperly secured Bulk FHIR exports pose serious risks, including large -scale PHI leakage. CMS should mandate encryption in transit and at rest, require access logging, and enforce identity verificatio n for systems. Performing quality metrics calculations centrally can reduce exposure of sensitive data and ensure stronger, standardized protections. Lastly, versioning and measure logic are essential for maintaining accuracy and fairness. If CMS assumes responsibility for quality calculations, it must be transparent, timely, and responsive to changes in measure specifications and clinical guidelines. P roviders will still need the ability to preview and validate their performance locally to ensure that the calculations are fair and accurate. b. PR-13. For any category of healthcare provider (as defined in 42 U.S.C. 300jj(3)), without a current information blocking disincentive established by CMS, what would be the most effective disincentive for that category of provider? We urge the repeal the overly broad provider disincentives established in the rule entitled “21st Century Cures Act: Establishment of Disincentives for Health Care Providers That Have Committed Information Blocking” (89 Fed. Reg. 54662, RIN 0955–AA05) . We recognize that steps should be taken to avoid harmful practices that obstruct the exchange of important health information and patient access to this information. Under this final rule, hospitals and providers found to engage in information blocking may face reductions in Medi care payment updates, adjustments to reimbursement rates, lower performance scores, and potential ineligibility for certain incentive programs. The disincentives are based on variable aspects of provider payment systems, such as the year-to-year changes in the value of the market basket adjustment and incentives given during different performance periods. This creates an unfair and confusing framework in which providers determined to be information blocking do not know what penalty to expect. Additionally, the final rule appears to create a system in which a hospital potentially could be penalized from multiple directions arising from a dispute over a single information access issue. The rule fails to clarify how HIPAA penalties and other existing accountabi lity measures would interact with the disincentives and future penalties for providers. Rather than imposing blanket penalties as finalized in this rule, a tiered enforcement framework would be a better approach. An appropriate enforcement process should begin with a warning, followed by education and technical assistance so that providers can understand what constitutes a violation, react to investigations and determinations, and take corrective measures. Such a process would set reasonable expectations for providers by following similar approaches in other compliance programs such as breach notifications and conditions of participation. Moreover, this framework would allow for agency discretion depending on whether a provider acted under a good fai th interpretation of the relevant 21st Century Cures Act information blocking provisions or knowingly and intentionally committed information blocking .

What specific interoperability challenges have you encountered in implementing value -based care programs?
