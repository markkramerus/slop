 Section E. Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks 1. Ecosystem TD-1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneﬁciaries and caregivers? Databricks believes CMS can accelerate digital health innovation by expanding API access, including Blue Button 2.0, enhancing developer resources and sandbox environments, and launching pilot programs for new digital tools. For long-term impact, CMS should invest in foundational infrastructure such as a national provider directory, modern identity veriﬁcation systems like Login.gov, digital insurance cards, and secure data exchange networks. Continuously updating API standards and enabling bulk data access will further drive ongoing advancements. Additionally, developers need robust data cataloging to easily discover and securely share not only data, but also business semantics and AI/ML models, ensuring a common understanding of business metrics and accelerating innovation across the healthcare ecosystem. As a uniﬁed analytics and AI platform, Databricks sees ﬁrsthand how adopting data standards like USCDI, HL7 FHIR APIs, and TEFCA streamlines health data exchange and reduces fragmentation. Databricks empowers organizations to unify structured and unstructured health data, leverage real-time analytics, and build interoperable solutions at scale. With Unity Catalog, organizations can centrally govern, discover, and manage data and AI assets across clouds and formats, while Delta Sharing enables secure, real-time, cross-platform data sharing with strong governance, 6 
 making it easier for healthcare innovators to deliver actionable insights that beneﬁt Medicare beneﬁciaries and the broader healthcare community. TD-2. Regarding CMS Data, to stimulate developer interest— a. What additional data would be most valuable if made available through CMS APIs? Developers would beneﬁt from expanded access to clinical EHR data, pharmacy and medication data, imaging, social determinants of health, and real-time eligibility or coverage information, in addition to claims data already available through Blue Button 2.0. Integrating and harmonizing these diverse data types within a uniﬁed platform and making them accessible through secure APIs would support advanced analytics, machine learning, and real-time insights for app developers and researchers. b. What data sources are most valuable alongside the data available through the Blue Button 2.0 API? In addition to the claims and coverage data from Blue Button 2.0, electronic health record (EHR) data and health information exchange (HIE) data are especially valuable, as they provide detailed clinical information such as lab results, diagnoses, and procedures. Integrating these sources creates a more comprehensive and actionable view of a patient’s health for providers and care teams. c. What obstacles prevent accessing these data sources today? Key obstacles include data silos, lack of interoperability standards, privacy and security concerns, regulatory complexity, inconsistent data quality, and challenges with digital identity veriﬁcation and consent management. These are challenges that Databricks has seen across industries and is uniquely positioned to help 7 
 address by enabling organizations through integrated data platforms, adopt health information exchange solutions, leverage standardized APIs, and establish strong data governance frameworks. A well architected Lakehouse architecture can break down data silos by centralizing all data into a uniﬁed Lakehouse platform, enabling teams across departments to access, share, and analyze the same data in real time. This uniﬁed approach eliminates fragmentation, streamlines governance, accelerates insights, and empowers collaborative analytics and AI initiatives across the organization d. What other APIs should CMS and ASTP/ONC consider including in program policies to unleash innovation and support patients and providers? CMS should consider expanding FHIR-based APIs for provider directories, payer-to-payer data exchange, digital identity veriﬁcation, digital insurance cards, and bulk data APIs to support research, care coordination, and TEFCA participation. These API policies, outlined in recent CMS interoperability and prior authorization rules, are intended to improve access to interoperable patient data for patients, providers, and payers while reducing administrative burden. By adopting and continuously updating open API standards (such as HL7 FHIR, CARIN IG for Blue Button, Da Vinci PDex, and Plan-Net IG) CMS can foster a more connected healthcare ecosystem, enable seamless data exchange, and drive innovation in patient-facing applications and digital health workﬂows at scale. A uniﬁed data platform like Databricks can further support these efforts by leveraging its Lakehouse architecture and FHIR interoperability capabilities to ingest, process, and analyze payer claims data in compliance with the CARIN IG for Blue Button. This approach empowers organizations to efﬁciently manage and utilize healthcare data, supporting both regulatory compliance and advanced analytics. 8 
 2. Digital Identity TD-3. Regarding digital identity implementation: a. What are the challenges and beneﬁts? Key challenges of digital identity implementation include fragmentation across networks, lack of interoperability, privacy concerns, regulatory complexity, and user adoption barriers. Databricks has seen healthcare organizations struggle to connect disparate systems and ensure consistent identity veriﬁcation across platforms, often resulting in gaps in data access and increased administrative burden. A uniﬁed data and intelligence platform like Databricks, when integrated with a Single Sign-On (SSO) authentication provider that supports modern protocols such as OpenID Connect (OIDC), helps address these challenges by providing a single, secure environment where identity data from multiple sources can be integrated, managed, and governed. OpenID Connect, as an industry-standard authentication protocol, enables interoperability between identity providers and applications, ensuring secure and seamless user authentication across systems. While Databricks is not an identity manager itself, its integration with leading SSO solutions,leveraging protocols like OpenID Connect. streamlines identity veriﬁcation processes, simpliﬁes compliance, and enables seamless, secure data sharing across the healthcare ecosystem. b. How would requiring digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs) impact cybersecurity and data exchange? Requiring digital identity credentials from trusted providers would greatly enhance cybersecurity and data exchange for CMS’s patient-centered data sharing efforts. These credentials provide strong identity veriﬁcation and multi-factor 9 
 authentication, reducing the risk of unauthorized access and ensuring that only veriﬁed individuals can access sensitive health information. For managing unique patient IDs, this approach offers robust privacy and encryption protections, helping to safeguard personal data throughout the exchange process. By implementing a standardized, high-assurance digital identity system, CMS can enable secure and trusted data sharing directly with patients, empowering beneﬁciaries to access and control their health information conﬁdently. This strengthens trust among patients, providers, and payers while supporting seamless interoperability. To maximize effectiveness, it is important to maintain strict data protection measures and ongoing security monitoring, given the sensitive nature of identity data. Overall, requiring digital identity credentials would signiﬁcantly improve both the security and efﬁciency of patient-centered health data exchange. c. What impact would mandatory use of the OpenID Connect identity protocol have? Mandatory adoption of OpenID Connect would standardize authentication, enable single sign-on across healthcare applications, improve interoperability, and enhance security by centralizing identity management and reducing password-related risks. This would simplify user experience and support regulatory compliance, but may require signiﬁcant integration efforts for legacy systems. Databricks supports open standards based authentication, such as OpenID Connect. 3. Technical Standards and Certiﬁcation TD-4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs over proprietary APIs? 10 
 CMS can promote open, standards-based APIs by mandating HL7 FHIR, requiring certiﬁed health IT developers to publish service URLs and organizational details, and ensuring comprehensive, publicly accessible API documentation. A Lakehouse architecture supports open standards APIs by providing comprehensive REST APIs, open ﬁle formats, and standardized interfaces for data access and integration, enabling seamless interoperability with external tools, BI platforms, and machine learning frameworks. This openness prevents vendor lock-in and ensures ﬂexible, governed access across diverse systems and workloads. Incentives for compliance, robust developer tools, and inclusion in the Health IT Certiﬁcation Program further drive adoption and consistency across the industry. By making open APIs a condition of certiﬁcation and maintenance, CMS lowers integration barriers, reduces vendor lock-in, and empowers patients, providers, and third-party developers with more consistent, portable, and interoperable access to health information. These policies, combined with transparent publication requirements and support for implementation guides like SMART on FHIR, help standardize data exchange, foster innovation, and ensure that healthcare data is accessible and usable without special effort across the ecosystem. Supporting major standards such as FHIR, HL7, X12, and CDA, and partnering with industry leaders like Redox, Komodo Health, and Health Catalyst, Databricks leverages open protocols like Delta Sharing and direct FHIR API support for secure, real-time, cross-platform data exchange. These requirements ensure certiﬁed health IT systems can reliably exchange core clinical data, support patient access, and remain adaptable to evolving standards while maintaining compliance and security. TD-10. For EHR and other developers subject to the ONC Health IT Certiﬁcation Program, what further steps should ASTP/ONC consider to implement the 21st Century Cures Act's API condition of certiﬁcation ( 42 U.S.C. 300jj-11(c)(5)(D)(iv) ) that requires a developer's APIs to allow health information to be accessed, exchanged, and used without special effort, including providing access to all data elements of a patient's electronic health record to the extent permissible under applicable privacy laws? To further implement the 21st Century Cures Act's API condition of certiﬁcation and ensure APIs allow health information to be accessed, exchanged, and used without special effort, ASTP/ONC should encourage the adoption of robust tokenization tools that can mask personally identiﬁable information (PII) and protected health information (PHI) while still enabling data consumers to utilize the data effectively. 17 
 Tokenization replaces sensitive information with encrypted tokens, allowing datasets to be linked and analyzed without exposing underlying identities, thus supporting both privacy and usability. Additionally, Databricks highlights the importance of secure, scalable data sharing through open standards like Delta Sharing. Delta Sharing provides a secure and efﬁcient way to share data and AI models across platforms, clouds, and organizations, with centralized governance and auditing to ensure compliance and privacy. Databricks’ ability to integrate with third-party tokenization vendors, further strengthens the privacy and security of shared data, allowing developers and data consumers to conﬁdently access de-identiﬁed information for research, analytics, and patient-centered applications. By combining tokenization with secure, open-standard data sharing protocols, ONC can help ensure that all data elements of a patient's electronic health record are accessible to the extent permitted by law, while minimizing privacy risks and administrative burden. This approach supports the spirit of the Cures Act by empowering innovation and interoperability without compromising data protection. TD-11. As of January 1, 2024, many health IT developers with products certiﬁed through the ONC Health IT Certiﬁcation Program are required to include the capability to perform an electronic health information export or “EHI export” for a single patient as well as for patient populations ( 45 CFR 170.315(b)(10) ). Such health IT developers are also required to publicly describe the format of the EHI export. Notably, how EHI export was accomplished was left entirely to the health IT developer. Now that this capability has been in production for over a year, CMS and ASTP/ONC seek input on the following: a. Should this capability be revised to specify standardized API requirements for EHI export? 18 
 EHI export capability to require standardized APIs—such as FHIR for both single patient and population-level exports—would greatly enhance interoperability, consistency, and ease of data exchange across health IT systems. Currently, the lack of a mandated standard means formats and export methods vary by developer, leading to challenges in automating data ingestion, migration, and analytics. Standardized APIs would ensure that exported data is computable, structured, and readily usable by different platforms without custom processing. b. Are there speciﬁc workﬂow aspects that could be improved? Workﬂow improvements could include making EHI export more user-friendly and integrated into routine clinical and administrative tasks. For example, enabling automated, scheduled exports, providing clearer export status notiﬁcations, and allowing for granular export options (such as by date range or data type) would streamline processes for clinicians and administrators. Enhanced access controls and audit trails would also improve security and accountability. c. Should CMS consider policy changes to support this capability's use? CMS should consider policies that incentivize or require the use of standardized EHI export functionalities, such as tying compliance to participation in federal programs or quality reporting initiatives. Additional support could include technical assistance, clear guidance on best practices, and aligning reporting requirements with standardized export formats to reduce provider burden and accelerate adoption. These steps would encourage broader use and ensure that EHI export capabilities deliver meaningful value for patients, providers, and the healthcare system. 19 
 4. Data Exchange TD-12. Should CMS endorse non-CMS data sources and networks, and if so, what criteria or metrics should CMS consider? CMS should consider endorsing non-CMS data sources and networks if they meet rigorous criteria related to data quality, interoperability, privacy, security, and governance. Important factors include the scientiﬁc acceptability of the data, its reliability and validity, feasibility of use, and alignment with CMS’s goals for quality measurement and value-based care. Additionally, endorsed sources should demonstrate usability for public reporting, support for health equity, and compliance with regulatory requirements. CMS typically evaluates such data sources through transparent, consensus-based processes like those conducted by the CMS Consensus-Based Entity (CBE), ensuring that endorsed data contributes meaningfully to healthcare quality improvement and patient outcomes.
