As the leading authority on oral health in the United States, the American Dental
Association (ADA), representing over 159,000 dentists across the country, writes to present
our comments on the publication in the May 18, 2025, edition of the Federal Register
entitled “Health Technology Ecosystem Request for Information” released by the Centers for
Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) and the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy,
Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ASTP/ONC),
Department of Health and Human Services (HHS).
We appreciate the HHS, CMS, and the ASTP/ONC's continued emphasis on
interoperability, which facilitates the exchange of information between providers, their
patients, and payers. This enables the effective and responsible adoption of technology,
which empowers patients and aids them in making better decisions about their health and
well-being.
The ADA comments ask that to achieve the objective of optimal health, which includes
oral health, meeting dental providers and practices where they are and supporting them to
ensure meaningful participation in the healthcare system is essential. Electronic dental
records (EDRs) differ from electronic medical records (EMRs) in structure and use. EDRs
are typically procedure-based. Structured dental data is generally less comprehensive and
standardized than medical data. Additionally, most dental practices are small, private offices
with limited information technology (IT) resources compared to larger group practices,
federally qualified health centers (FQHCs), or hospital-affiliated settings. This variation in
practice models presents a significant challenge for the dental health technology ecosystem.

Dental Imaging
Dentists use radiographic imaging to help diagnose and effectively treat dental disease. The
ADA has worked with the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) to publish the ADA / FDA
Guide to Patient Selection for Dental Radiographs.3 These guidelines assist dentists in
using diagnostic imaging based on their judgment. While radiographs help diagnose oral
diseases, dentists must weigh the benefits against the risks of cumulative x-ray exposure.
With their understanding of the patient's health history and disease susceptibility, dentists
are best suited to make these decisions.
In collaboration with industry stakeholders and imaging vendors, the ADA developed
standards for imaging in dentistry that are universally applicable to any digital imaging
system, providing a scientifically validated process from image capture to image display to
achieve an optimal balance between image quality and radiation dose45. The ADA has

ePrescribing and Prescription Drug Monitoring Programs
Prescription drug monitoring is a critical issue in dentistry, given the profession's role in
appropriately prescribing controlled substances, particularly opioids for acute pain
management. A retrospective review of dental, medical, and pharmacy claims found that
older patients with potentially inappropriate medication combinations (PIMCs) involving
opioids prescribed by dentists experienced higher rates of acute care use.6
Dentists are required to register with their state’s Prescription Drug Monitoring Programs
(PDMP) when dispensing or prescribing controlled substances. Some state regulations,
such as New York State Department of Health, require most prescribers to consult the
Prescription Monitoring Program (PMP) Registry when writing prescriptions for Schedule II,
III, and IV controlled substances. The PMP Registry provides practitioners with direct,
secure access to view their patients' dispensed controlled substance prescription histories.
Studies indicate that dental providers find PDMPs useful, and some reported changes in
opioid prescribing behavior after reviewing prescription histories7.
However, dental practices are often challenged by limited integration with state PDMPs.
While the ADA is unaware of studies specific to dental PDMP integrations, we note that prior
research has concluded that EHR integration with PDMP was associated with an increase in
use of the database by physicians and a decline in patterns of opioid polypharmacy.8 The

Dental records' interoperability and data accessibility for patients and caregivers in dentistry
are far more limited than those for medical health information. These gaps become even
clearer when medical treatment is linked to dental treatment.
We know that managing and tracking dental referrals is a challenge for providers: although
97% of dental offices refer patients for additional treatment, only 28% have electronic
referral capabilities. Instead, approximately 75% continue to rely on paper-based referrals,
and more than 60% of dentists are unaware of the status of their outbound referrals, trends
that are consistent for both dental and non-dental referrals. Referral management is
primarily handled by front office staff (92.5%) or dentists (45.4%), requiring an estimated five
hours of administrative time per week.12 Without patent tools that enable electronic access
to health records, treatment plans, provider communication, and orders, patients must rely
on physical paperwork and hours spent calling providers and requesting records. All of this
could be done with a few clicks using standards-based application programming interfaces
(APIs) that are readily available and recognized by CMS.
Despite millions of Americans seeking care in emergency departments for dental conditions
each year, a lack of interoperability between hospital and dental systems results in costly,
fragmented, and ineffective care. Without seamless referral pathways or shared health
records, most patients receive only temporary relief—typically opioids or antibiotics—without
definitive treatment. This disconnect leads to repeat visits, avoidable complications, and
billions in unnecessary spending. Estimates suggest that poor communication and referral
capacity between emergency and dental systems cost the U.S. healthcare system over $3.5
billion annually, driven by avoidable ED utilization, inappropriate prescribing, and missed
opportunities for timely, lower-cost dental interventions. Integrating dental providers into
health information exchanges and enabling real-time referrals through standards like FHIR
is not just a technical fix—it is a critical policy lever to reduce waste and improve outcomes.
High-risk populations, such as the medically fragile, individuals with intellectual and
developmental disabilities, and the elderly, are disproportionately impacted by the inability of
dental records to integrate or exchange information between dental providers and others
involved in their circle of care. Several care domains would benefit from patients having a
complete picture of their dental health, for example, pediatrics, geriatric providers, palliative
care, and post-acute care. App developers and patients can access key dental information
from their records through standards-based APIs, allowing for innovation for remote health
and home health solutions in dental care.

Providers
PR-3. How important is it for healthcare delivery and interoperability in urban and
rural areas that all data in an EHR system be accessible for exchange, regardless of
storage format (for example, scanned documents, faxed records, lab results, free text
notes, structured data fields)?
Ongoing technology infrastructure issues challenge dental providers. Technology used in
practices may determine the possible storage formats and exchange methodologies;
broadband access and high-speed internet are factors. The Federal Communication
Commission has noted “there is a significant correlation between increasing broadband
access and improved health outcomes, and that Internet adoption appeared to have an
even greater correlation to improved health outcomes.”13 The ADA Health Policy Institute
(HPI) reports that in 2023, 74.7% of dentists practiced with one or more dentists in a single
location14. A concerted effort should be taken to ensure that data in a dental software
system is accessible for exchange, regardless of storage format (for example, scanned
documents, faxed records, lab results, free text notes, structured data fields), and that the
ability for a provider to fulfill patient record requests, regardless of format, be easily
accessible and native to the software. These improvements must be sensitive to dental
practice models and rural practices who may face a disproportionate financial burden when
adopting and maintaining new technology.
Dental providers and their patients risk becoming a prominent example of the digital divide
in healthcare. Unequal access to and use of digital tools will affect patient outcomes.
Factors like geographic location and digital literacy, along with limited access to

Dental spending on administrative processes increased in 2023 as dental providers and
staff managed a higher volume of manual and electronic tasks. The 2024 edition of the
CAQH Index is the only health care industry benchmarking tool that tracks and reports
progress on the adoption of electronic administrative transactions and the potential savings
associated with transitioning from manual processes. It is prepared by the Council for
Affordable Quality Healthcare, the health care industry alliance commonly known as CAQH.
The 2024 report consists of data from 2023. Eligibility and benefit verification accounted for
the largest increase in spending, rising 15% to $2.1 billion. Verification checks should ideally
be conducted fully electronically; however, due to a lack of complete and accurate data,
providers often rely on conducting these checks through portals. The portals, maintained by
insurance companies, vary in requirements and formats, which complicates verification.16

Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks
TD. 4 How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly
available APIs over proprietary APIs?
The ADA recommends several actions that would encourage the use of open, standards-
based, and publicly available APIs:
a. Education—Industry-specific education, materials, and communications highlighting
the benefits and return on investment for dental practices, vendors, and data trading
partners. Educate patients regarding their data rights, specific to those CMS
program requirements, and require that plans communicate with patients about
leveraging Patient Access APIs.
b. Transparency and Compliance – Most dental record systems are not certified under
the ONC Health IT Certification Program (Certification Program), nor have vendors
self-selected to attest to meeting technological requirements adopted by the U.S.
Department of Health and Human Services. Encouraging public-facing transparency
of dental vendor functionality and conformance may improve market demand for
compliance and assure software purchasers that these products will meet expected
functionality.
c. Foster Innovation—Create pilot projects and public-private partnerships that test
innovative, standards-based dental data exchange solutions. Tools specifically
tailored to oral health needs, including patient care coordination and integration with
medical disease management, can help move the dental industry forward, paralleling
medical technology. Facilitate collaboration across stakeholders, including payers,
providers, vendors, patients, and standards development organizations, to align
priorities and accelerate innovation.
d. Demand Accountability--Dental data relies on domain-specific clinical data
structures, as identified in ANSI/ADA Standard No.1084 - Reference Core Data set
for communication and adopted in the HL7 FHIR® Implementation Guide: Dental
Data Exchange, Release 1 - US Realm. When applied to dental stakeholders,
federally mandated or recommended FHIR® IGs should harmonize with the above
standards.

The ADA supports efforts to establish a nationwide provider directory for FHIR® endpoints,
as this will aid ongoing initiatives to reduce administrative burdens and facilitate information
sharing among providers, patients, and other stakeholders. The ADA encourages CMS to
consider how provider directories and FHIR® endpoint directories can be harmonized to
ensure accurate and up-to-date information about contracted provider networks while
streamlining access to this information. Patients often struggle to find providers who not only
accept their insurance benefits but also accept new patients. Dental payer-based provider
directory APIs frequently contain outdated or incomplete data, resulting in “ghost” networks.
The ADA has collaborated with CAQH ProView to offer a singular provider information
source of truth for credentialing, surpassing 147,598 dentists using the system nationally,
and over 312 entities that use the repository as consumers. CAQH was formed by health
plans who came together nearly 25 years ago with the desire to make healthcare work
better. The information in this repository supports ADA efforts to promote industry solutions
that reduce administrative burden, allowing practices to spend more time on clinical care
and less on paperwork. Systems that maintain provider data for purposes such as
credentialing can easily be adapted to support use cases such as directories without
the need for duplicative systems. Despite these efforts, there continues to be a
proliferation of directory solutions, often required by mandate for participation in state
Medicaid programs. The need to maintain accurate information in a provider directory is
placed onto the dental practice. Failure to implement a single source of information leads to
significant duplication of effort by providers and practices and creates data inconsistencies.
Furthermore, many dentists opt not to enroll as providers for Medicare due to additional
administrative burdens in enrollment and credentialing through the Provider, Enrollment,
Chain, and Ownership System (PECOS). While it is a centralized system, it does not have
other mechanisms of interoperability with other credentialing systems that collect similar
information. This ultimately duplicates efforts in program enrollment and credentialing
already being done in the private sector in a more efficient, cost-effective manner.
The ADA requests CMS promote the centralization and standardization of provider
data collection, with a program like CAQH, that allow providers to choose a single-
entry point for information, enabling end users of provider information to leverage
FHIR® APIs to receive timely, accurate data nationally.

The ADA, an ANSI-Accredited Standards Developer, sets standards for nearly every tool of
dentistry, ensuring their safety, reliability, and efficacy for dentists and the public. The ADA
Standards Program brings over four hundred volunteer subject matter experts to participate
in the development of national and international dental standards. The ADA Standards
Program is leading efforts to develop ADA standards on data content such that any current
or future exchange mechanisms such as emerging API technology can support exchange of

required data to serve dental patients17. Notably, ANSI/ADA Standard No. 1000 – Standard

structures; ADA Standard No. 1113 for Dentistry – Contents for Dental Procedure-Level
Data Required for Eligibility and Benefit Responses and ANSI/ADA Standard No.1079 for

health care settings.
ADA believes that USCDI should be perceived as the regulatory ground floor for accessible
data and can continue to serve as the benchmark for certification programs. When
identifying certification criteria under the ASTP/ONC Health IT Certification Program, it could
be beneficial to identify domain-specific modules and attestation criteria appropriate for
dental developers, as these may differ from broader interest developers. The ADA would be
available to assist in developing such criteria.
Leveraging API access is a step in the right direction for opening electronic health
information to new opportunities and advancements, especially when thoughtfully utilizing
large language models (LLM) and AI tools that may be able to extract and validate
previously inaccessible information. Precision medicine, clinical decision support, and
innovation will all benefit from opening access to the entirety of a patient’s electronic health
information. Research institutions such as the Regenstrief Institute are using automated
computer algorithms to identify disease early, track disease progression, and identify
opportunities for improved outcomes, all made possible by using information that is not
easily accessible (chart notes)18.
Furthermore, enabling full EHI access through APIs will support efforts to create and
maintain longitudinal dental records, including the ongoing documentation of dental health
over time. A lack of longitudinal dental records may compromise patient care, increase the
risk of medical errors, lead to legal and ethical issues, and negatively impact dental practice
efficiency and patient care. When dental records include complete and comprehensive
longitudinal data, both patients and providers experience significant benefits. This can
empower patients to make informed decisions about their care delivery and improve
outcomes and continuity of care.
The ADA also recognizes the importance of the HIPAA Minimum Necessary Rule, which
requires that covered entities and business associates make reasonable efforts to limit the
use, disclosure, and request of protected health information (PHI) to the minimum
necessary to accomplish the intended purpose. This means only disclosing the minimum
amount of PHI needed to achieve a specific goal, like treatment, payment, or operations.
