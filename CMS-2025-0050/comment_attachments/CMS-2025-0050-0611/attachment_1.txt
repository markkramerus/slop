Response from HealthShare Exchange (HSX) to CMS
Request for Information on Health Technology Ecosystem
Reference: File Code CMS-0042-NC
HealthShare Exchange (HSX) is the Health Information Exchange (HIE) organization for the
Greater Philadelphia area, covering Pennsylvania, New Jersey, and Delaware. We are pleased to
respond to the CMS RFI on Health Technology Ecosystem.
About HSX
Health Share Exchange (HSX) is a health information exchange (HIE), providing secure data
exchange to healthcare providers in the greater Philadelphia region, including New Jersey and
Delaware. HSX connects hospitals, physician practices, nursing homes, post-acute care facilities,
health plans, home care organizations and behavioral health providers. All these organizations
need the most accurate and up-to-date information about their patients to provide the best
and most efficient care possible.
HSX services deliver real-time clinical information to our members, including a patient’s medical
history as well as notifications to enable improved outcomes, care coordination and more cost-
effective care. HSX is a HITRUST-certified organization, a Pennsylvania P3N certified Health
Information Organization (HIO), a State of New Jersey Trusted Data Sharing Organization
(TDSO) and is part of the national eHealth Exchange.
HSX is expanding the connected healthcare circle by adding community-based organizations
(CBOs) in the network of caregivers, moving from an HIE to a Community Information Exchange
(CIE). Food banks, housing agencies, benefits coordinators, transportation agencies, community
centers and other community service organizations play a vital role in the care of the patient.
Through the PA Navigate program, HSX connects health provider organizations with
community-based socio-economic support services to create a closed-loop referral system –
connecting patients with services and keeping the care team in the loop.
For additional information on HSX see www.healthshareexchange.org
Patients and Caregivers - PC
PC-6. What features are most important to make digital health products accessible and easy to
use for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers, particularly those with limited prior experience
using digital tools and services?

One of the major drawbacks of EHR-based patient portals is that patients need to use a
different portal, or app, with a different user ID and password, and different navigation options,
if they see physicians and care providers with different EHRs. A single patient-access
application would give patients access to all their data using a single app. HIEs such as HSX can
function as data aggregators for single patient access applications, avoiding the need for
patients to maintain multiple accounts and multiple portals. HIEs can support proactive care
decisions like navigating a patient towards a lower cost community service (i.e., food pantry) or
connect patients who qualify for healthcare benefits (i.e., prescription assistance) keeping
patients out of high-cost ERs and staying healthy.
HIEs like HSX can also be a key supplier of data to these digital health products further enabling
these products value and capabilities to the patients they serve, resulting in lowering costs,
improving patients experience and overall health outcomes.

c. What specific opportunities and challenges exist to improve accessibility, interoperability and

HIEs such as HSX function as regional health data aggregator for patient health data which

- HSX is a single source for data from multiple providers
- HSX is a single party for contracting, avoiding having to set up individual contracts for multiple
provider organizations. To make this possible, HSX created a research use-case, approved by all
members, with tight provisions for patient consent and data privacy, which governs all clinical
research studies using HSX data.
PC-11. How are health information exchanges (HIEs) currently helping to advance patient access
to health information in the real world?
HSX receives health information from over 700 healthcare provider organizations in this greater
Philadelphia tri-state region. Today, the main use case for this data is to provide access to this
data to other authorized healthcare providers and health plans who have a treatment /
payment /operations relationship with the patient whose data they access. (Patients have the

right to opt out of sharing). Most patients access their data via the EHR portals from their
multiple healthcare providers.
HIEs such as HSX can provide direct patient access to their data via approved apps or
applications, with appropriate consent, identification, authorization and security, controlled by
patients or their care givers. Our platform supports the FHIR APIs to provide this access. We
expect that direct patient access to regional HIE data sources eliminates the challenge that
patients face when they receive care at multiple organizations, with multiple EHRs, requiring
multiple portals.
HSX can filter the data queries by provider organization and by EHR. This means, that if a
patient-facing healthcare app has a direct connection to a national EHR (for example Epic), they
can query HSX for data from non-Epic EHRs, thus avoiding data duplication.
a. How valuable, available, and accurate do you find the data they share to be?
HSX data comes directly from participating organizations EHRs, in the form of C-CDA documents
and ADT messages. The data is accurate, and in real-time. This now is starting to include social
determinants of health (SDoH) data for a broader view of the entire patient’s needs. There is
variability in the data-items that we receive from healthcare providers. We expect that broader
implementation of USCDI will fill in the data gaps that exist. The current USCDI version is
comprehensive, in our view, but not yet fully implemented by EHR vendors and healthcare
providers.
Through the use of HSX’s data sharing platform, overall healthcare costs are lowered and
healthcare outcomes improved.
b. What changes would you suggest?
Broader implementation of data sharing following the USCDI model – not only with support
from EHR vendors, but also with expectations of full implementation and activation by
healthcare providers that they enable and share the full USCDI data set for all patients, all
encounters.
c. Are there particular examples of high-performing HIE models that you believe should be
propagated across markets?
HSX considers its model an example of an high-performing, independent and neutral HIE,
connecting competing entities including health systems and health plans all in agreement in use
of and sharing data via HSX. HSX receives clinical data from a broad range of provider
organizations: acute care facilities, ambulatory practices (primary and specialist practices), post-
acute care facilities, home healthcare organizations, behavioral health facilities, and several
major community-based organizations providing social services. The clinical data set spans a

much broader range of data and healthcare participants than any single organization or EHR
would be able to provide.
A high-performing regional HIE like HSX provides a strong base of active organizations sharing
and leveraging data that is the basis for all health data access initiatives – for patients, providers
and health plans alike.
d. What is the ongoing role of HIEs amidst other entities facilitating data exchange and broader
frameworks for data exchange (for example, vendor health information networks, TEFCA,
private exchange networks, etc.)?
HIEs such as HSX support several essential data-exchange use cases, connecting providers and
health plans at all levels of care. Clinical data exchange is one of several critical use cases. In
addition, HSX supports data exchange for patient panels, including panel-based encounter
notifications and results notifications, which are essential for providers to provide direct and
timely interventions in value based care settings (ACO’s, MA-practices, health plan care
coordination teams). HSX also has direct connections with healthcare providers and their
preferred community-based organizations / social services providers to support health related
social needs, lowering healthcare costs and improving health outcomes.
Many HSX clinical and care-coordination users leverage this proactive encounter data in real-
time to reduce hospital re-admissions EDs, the highest encounter cost.
HSX is ready to support and align TEFCA/QHIN for nationwide data queries. In addition, we
recognize that healthcare is local and our current active use cases go well beyond the proposed
national vendor health networks and EFCA / QHIN exchanges.
Payers - PA
PA-5. What are ways payers can help with simplifying clinical quality data responsibilities of
providers?
a. How interested are payers and providers in EHR technology advances that enable bulk extraction of
clinical quality data from EHRs to payers to allow them to do the calculations instead of the provider-
side technology?
Both payers and providers are interested in simplifying data sharing, and the HIEs are well
positioned to provide this capability. Many HIEs have become certified under NCQA’s Data
Aggregator Validation (DAV) Program, and payers are receiving bulk clinical data today through
this mechanism. Both CCD and FHIR based transfer mechanisms are supported. This works well
at getting data to payers that fall outside the scope of typical claims formats, e.g., vital signs, lab
results, and screening results. This also saves significant time with both payers and providers
gathering this data from desperate patient records. HIEs such as HSX can be the payer’s data

aggregator for this quality data which reduces workload and overall costs for both payers and
providers in gathering quality data needs.
b. In what ways can the interoperability and quality reporting responsibilities of providers to both CMS
and other payers be consolidated so investments can be dually purposed? Are there technologies
payers might leverage that would support access to real time quality data for healthcare providers to
inform clinical care in addition to simplifying reporting processes?
HSX achieved NCQA Data Aggregator Validation (DAV) certification for real time access to
quality data to inform clinical care and simplify quality reporting processes.
Providers - PR

Some but not all HSX member organizations using the EPIC EHR are in the process of switching
to the EPIC Nexus QHIN, which would run side by side with their Care Everywhere data
exchange network. Outside of systems activating the Epic Nexus QHIN, HSX is not aware of
health systems or practices in the Philadelphia area that are prioritizing the need to join the

Unable to answer at this time, because no participating HSX members are currently live on any of the
QHINs. In the near future, we will have more information on how member provider organizations
combine clinical data exchange from a QHIN together with other data exchange services from HSX or
their regional HIE. We believe both exchanges are essessential and needed and should function in a
collaborative model.
b. What changes would you suggest?
c. What other options are available outside of TEFCA?
HIEs such as HSX support the use cases that are envisioned by TEFCA, plus offer additional use
cases which are currently not supported by TEFCA/QHINs.
Use cases supported by both HSX and TEFCA:
 clinical data exchange, via CCD queries, from regional and national provider organizations.
 access to national providers via eHealth Exchange.
Use cases supported by HSX but not TEFCA:
 Encounter Notification, based on patient panel or population (most HSX members rank
Encounter Notification as the top HSX use case for their organization, in particular ACOs and

practices in at-risk value-based care contracts. This use case is not supported by TEFCA/QHINs. It
provides significant value to the members.
 panel-based results real-time notification delivery
 Data exchange with community-based social services organizations via the PA-Navigate
program.
 Clinical research programs spanning across member organizations, using the aggregated data
set as a one-stop shop for clinical data and tracking patients’ clinical trajectories. (research is
based on patient consent, or de-identified data, with strict provisions for patient data privacy
and consent).
In other words, even if TEFCA would provide clinical data exchange nationally, HSX would still
be essential to many of its members through use cases beyond CCD-based clinical queries.
d. Are there redundant standards, protocols or channels or both that could be consolidated?
There is redundancy in functionality between regional HIE’s such as HSX, and TEFCA/QHIN data
exchange. Both networks support clinical data queries yet serve different needs. HSX is
currently connected to eHealth Exchange (eHX), which would be the likely avenue for HSX to
connect into the TEFCA/QHIN network. There are additional use cases currently provided by
HIEs such as HSX which are not yet part of the TEFCA/QHIN capabilities: encounter notification,
patient-panel-based real-time push notifications and results notifications, connections to
community-based organizations, and more.
HIEs need to be sub participants of QHINs, to provide connectivity to ambulatory practices,
post-acute facilities, home care organizations, and other care givers in a variety of
organizations. A careful collaboration between both HIEs and the TEFCA/QHINS model is
essential and the right formula for improved overall data exchange.
Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks - TD
TD-6. What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform?
TEFCA through a series of connected QHINs provides one consistent national clinical data query
capability , via C-CDA document queries. TEFCA also provides a record locator service,
indicating to the requestor at which organizations there may be data for a patient.
What TEFCA does not provide are proactive data pushes in the form of encounter notifications,
a real time push of encounter health data that both providers and payers find very valuable in
lowering costs, improving the patient experience and improving health outcomes. This is what
HIEs such as HSX provide to member organizations. A carefully structured collaboration
between TEFCA and HIEs would meet all needs for providers and payers.

a. What existing alternatives should be considered?
HIEs should be considered as the local and regional data sharing entity that delivers not only
clinical data exchange between clinical providers and payers, but also:
- Additional essential data exchange use cases: encounter notification, panel-based information
sharing for Value-Based Care organizations and health plan care coordination teams.
- Additional participating partners in data sharing, which would not usually be included in the
TEFCA/QHIN network: ACOs, home care providers, community-based social services
organizations.
b. Are there redundant standards, protocols or channels or both that should be consolidated?
See our response to PR6-d.
TD-7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange and what are its
limitations?
a. Does it contain the full extent of data elements you need?
Yes – USCDI gives a very comprehensive set of health data. In our experience, most healthcare
provider organizations are not (yet) sharing the full USCDI data set. We see two challenges:
some EHRs do not (yet) support the inclusion of all USCDI data in their C-CDA documents. And
second, not all healthcare provider organizations have enabled or activated the inclusion of the
full USCDI data in the data they share.
b. If not, is it because of limitations in the definition of the USCDI format or the way it is utilized?
USCDI in its current and upcoming versions does not include images and data that’s not
numeric or text based. We see a future need to also provide access to these data types that are
currently not included in C-CDA documents, using other data transport formats.
HSX has a data quality workgroup where we bring together member organizations, identify
their data needs, and look to improve data capture and sharing to meet the community needs.
c. If so, would adding more data elements to USCDI add value or create scoping challenges?
How could such challenges be addressed?
HSX believes that the current USCDI data set covers the majority of health data exchange
needs. Image- and other modality exchange can be layered on top of the USCDI-based
exchange once it is more firmly established and broadly implemented.
d. Given improvements in language models, would you prefer a non-proprietary but less
structured format that might improve data coverage even if it requires more processing by the
receiver?

Structured, fielded, and properly coded data is still preferred for most of the standardized
clinical entities in the USCDI such as diagnoses, medications, and lab results. However, data
transport mechanisms currently in use can also handle free text of clinical notes, narrative
discharge summaries, and radiology reports, etc. We would advocate for both/and, not
either/or.
TD-17. Given operational costs, what role should CMS or ASTP/ONC or both have in ensuring
viability of healthcare data sharing networks, including enough supply and demand, that results
in usage and outcomes?
The initial HSX implementation, enrollment and connection of participating practices was
partially funded by grants. Current HIE operations come primarily from annual participating
member fees. For long-term viability, CMS and/or ASTP/ONC should consider financial support
for clinical data exchange, through Medicare, Medicaid and/or separate funding. CMS funding
would support on-going operational expenses as a complement to participant payments, and
would help HIEs extend their local on-ground connectivity, and keep software and services
reliable and up-to-date.
Value-Based Care Organizations.
VB-1. What incentives could encourage APMs such as accountable care organizations (ACOs) or
participants in Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) to leverage digital health
management and care navigation products more often and more effectively with their patients?
What are the current obstacles preventing broader digital product adoption for patients in
ACOs?
HSX membership includes several ACOs, ACO-Reach practices, and at-risk VBC primary care
practices. ACOs and their Care Coordination teams are some of the most frequent and
consistent users of HSX data and services – in particular clinical data and encounter notification
for at-risk patient panels. For high-functioning ACOs, HIEs are critical infrastructure for
managing a panel of patients, coordinating care, and reducing acute care utilization. HIEs also
assist in connecting patients to community-based organizations, assisting patients with health-
related social services and closing the referral loop – for example in Pennsylvania through the
PA-Navigate program, where HSX is leading this implementation. Once this connection is
made, HSX closes the referral loop to ensure the provider and payer know this patient has been
enrolled in a community-based service (for example, food delivery and condition-specific
dietary and nutrition support).
Current obstacles preventing broader use by patients in ACOs would be fragmentation of the
healthcare system causing patients to have to use multiple tools to manage clinical care and
health related social services. HIEs such as HSX function as a data aggregator and single point of

contact for all a patient’s data, not only for healthcare providers, but also as a gateway into
social services, such as benefits coordination, food and nutrition, and other services not
typically provided by the healthcare organization.
VB-3. What are essential health IT capabilities for value-based care arrangements?
a. Examples (not comprehensive) may include: care planning, patient event notification, data
extraction/normalization, quality performance measurement, access to claims data, attribution
and patient ID matching, remote device interoperability, or other patient empowerment tools.
b. What other health IT capabilities have proven valuable to succeeding in value-based care
arrangements?
HSX and other regional HIEs provide essential health IT capabilities and tools for managing a
panel of patients enrolled in an ACO or other at-risk plan. This includes panel-based encounter
notification, results notification, clinical data access, and connecting patients with community
organizations. HIEs provide the ability to update panels frequently. HIEs maintain a Master
Patient Index for combining data from multiple providers. HIEs assist in data-exchange at
transitions of care – from acute care to post-acute care to home care – which can be the critical
points of failure, resulting in lowering healthcare costs and improving patient experience and
health outcomes.
VB-9. What technology requirements should be different for APM organizations when
comparing to non-APM organizations (for example, quality reporting, and interoperability)?
Patient care for non-APM organizations (FFS organizations) is typically focused on single-patient
data requirements. APMs require technology for managing patient panels, and for tracking
clinical and related data for these patient panels: encounters, device data, results, social
services. APMs brings an additional set of cross-patient data exchange requirements. APM-
based management and tracking of patient panels includes the ability to define and update
patient panels, and to track patients across multiple providers.
VB-12. What technology standardization would preserve program-specific flexibility while
promoting innovation in APM technology implementation?
HSX and other HIEs would benefit from further standardization of data exchange between
community-based organizations providing non-clinical but healthcare related services to
patients, to allow for better coordination of care across healthcare providers and community
organizations. Healthcare specific APIs, primarily used by EHRs, don’t work for CBOs who often
use simpler technology and Customer Relationship Management (CRM) tools that are not
healthcare specific. We would like to see focus on API standards that are not solely aimed at
EHR vendors, since community organizations use other management software.
