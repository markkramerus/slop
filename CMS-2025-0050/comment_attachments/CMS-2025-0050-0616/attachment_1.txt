This section is intended for all stakeholders to provide input on questions as they relate to use cases and

1. Patient Needs
PC-1. What health management or care navigation apps would help you understand and manage your (or your
loved ones) health needs, as well as the actions you should take? An application that lets the user connect their
own health data repositories to public and private datasets and helps create understandable analytic insights
between the personal data and the connected data.
a. What are the top things you would like to be able to do for your or your loved ones' health that can be enabled
by digital health products? Intermediate on their behalf with healthcare service providers and payers, assist with
research, selection, and scheduling of health services/providers, and help to ensure their data is being used
appropriately and with consent by the right stakeholders.
b. If you had a personal assistant to support your health needs, what are the top things you would ask them to
help with? In your response, please consider tasks that could be supported or facilitated by software solutions in
the future. See response in question a. above
PC-2. Do you have easy access to your own and all your loved ones' health information in one location (for
example, in a single patient portal or another software system)? No. Clinical Squared is developing a platform
that unifies health information for a patient that is scattered across multiple EMR's, patient portals, and
caregiver practice datasets.
a. If so, what are some examples of benefits it has provided? N/A
b. If not, in what contexts or for what workflows would it be most valuable to use one portal or system to access
all such health information? Our workflows feature custom API connections that are compatible with major
EMR vendors (Epic, Cerner Health, and Meditech), support bulk uploads of data, and provide capability for ad
hoc/individual document uploads.
c. Were there particular data types, such as x-rays or specific test results, that were unavailable? What are the
obstacles to accessing your own or your loved ones' complete health information electronically and using it for
managing health conditions or finding the best care (for example, limitations in functionality, user friendliness,
or access to basic technology infrastructure)? Images from DICOM/PACS systems tend to be very large and are

typically not available to the patient without making a special request of the health information management
(HIM) department of the practice facility. In some cases, full lab reports, doctor's notes, or unedited diagnosis
data is not available for fear it might be misinterpreted by a lay person.
PC-3. Are you aware of health management, care navigation, or personal health record apps that would be
useful to Medicare beneficiaries and their caregivers? We are architecting and developing a health data
management app we feel would be useful and intuitive to Medicare beneficiaries and are happy to discuss in
detail with CMS leadership at their convenience.
PC-4. What features are missing from apps you use or that you are aware of today? Integration with multiple,
different healthcare practice EMR systems, scheduling, billing, consent, and data review features on the same
platform, and apps that have an intuitive user interface that is easily accessible from a mobile device.
a. What apps should exist but do not yet? Why do you believe they do not exist yet? An app that has true
interoperability between disparate EMR vendors, payers, and health care practices that can capture data created
about a patient and include contextual data from the patient themselves. This kind of app can be very costly to
build because of the variable data types and sources, and the lack of cooperation between different data
custodians/software vendors in supporting a truly interoperable application that does not originate from them
(see above answers as well).
b. What set of workflows do you believe CMS is uniquely positioned to offer? Workflows around claims data,
physician and healthcare service provider data, and public health trend data based on reported outcomes and
services utilized.
PC-5. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in these digital health
products? Measure the use of innovative technology tools and support high performing products publicly so that
patients and caregivers build trust and the security and accuracy of digital health products that improve the
patient and the physicians experience in healthcare service provision.
a. What role, if any, should CMS have in reviewing or approving digital health products on the basis of their
efficacy, quality or impact or both on health outcomes (not approving in the sense of a coverage determination)?
What criteria should be used if there is a review process? What technology solutions, policy changes, or
program design changes can increase patient and caregiver adoption of digital health products (for example,
enhancements to data access, reimbursement adjustments, or new beneficiary communications)? If CMS were
to suggest high performing, accurate, secure digital health products to the public, we think it would be
meaningful for CMS to have a review process that includes seeking inputs from the health IT industry
(including vendors and software developers as well as patients and providers who use these tools). We also
think that tying greater physician reimbursement to the proven, effective use of digital health products will drive
practices to demonstrate the efficacy of different apps and platforms.
b. What changes would enable timely access to high quality CMS and provider generated data on patients?
Increase the frequency with which high quality data is reviewed and published by CMS. Additionally,
partnering with private sector solution developers and software vendors that are producing high quality data that
is generated by providers and CMS will relieve the burden on agency staff in reviewing and publishing quality
data with its workforce.

Ease of use is critical. Digital health products that have an intuitive user interface and provide data that is
Accurate and easy for the user to understand are always more successful.

2. Data Access and Integration
PC-8. In your experience, what health data is readily available and valuable to patients or their caregivers or
both? We have seen the utility in publicly available data from trusted resources such as CMS, NIH, CDC, and
the FDA. We are strong proponents of creating solutions for consumers that foster access to this public data in a
way that connects to an individual's personal context and informs their health journey.
a. What data is valuable, but hard for patients and caregivers, or app developers and other technical vendors, to
access for appropriate and valuable use (for example, claims data, clinical data, encounter notes, operative
reports, appointment schedules, prices)? Data housed in proprietary systems such as electronic medical record
(EMR) platforms, clinical research repositories, payor databases, and other commonly commoditized sources
are particularly difficult for patients and caregivers to gain access. While an individual may have options of
access to their own data from these sources, access to empirical or general data about similar experiences,
episodes and plans of care, and the like are often unavailable.
b. What are specific sources, other than claims and clinical data, that would be of highest value, and why? We
know patients and caregivers benefit from anecdotal information from other patients and caregivers undergoing
similar treatments, providing similar healthcare services under similar circumstances and so on. The popularity
of patient support groups, professional caregiver cohorts, and other such entities demonstrate that people like to
ask their (relevant) peers about their experiences navigating similar situations in healthcare service delivery.
c. What specific opportunities and challenges exist to improve accessibility, interoperability and integration of
clinical data from different sources to enable more meaningful clinical research and generation of actionable
evidence? Opportunities include the availability of technology that can ensure secure, auditable exchanges of
sensitive data between patients, their caregivers, insurance companies and other stakeholders in a way which
allows them to maintain control of their own data and consent. We are deeply involved in building systems that
utilize blockchain technology for digital ID creation, securing digital documents (assets), and the facilitation of
these kinds of transactions.
The availability and accessibility of AI tools that can parse enormous data sets quickly and with a high degree
of accuracy provide additional opportunities to create tools that make data more consumable and
understandable, especially to the general population. We have developed several open-source tools that help
condense, simplify, and explain healthcare-centric data using AI algorithms of this sort.
Both examples of technology above can be leveraged to interoperate and integrate in legacy systems so there is
no need to incur extreme expense to “rip and replace” in-place systems with new ones.

Some challenges we see around improving accessibility, interoperability, and integration of clinical data from
different sources lie in the siloed state of data. We believe that governmental support of, and sometimes
mandate for, an increase in accessibility and transparency of data for the benefit of consumers and in the
furtherance of public health and safety could increase the availability of valuable data, and in some cases,
diminish costs.
PC-9. Given that the Blue Button 2.0 API only includes basic patient demographic, Medicare coverage, and
claims data (Part A, B, D), what additional CMS data sources do developers view as most valuable for inclusion
in the API to enable more useful digital products for patients and caretakers? We think the inclusion of
physician demographic and practice information (like that in NPPES) can be particularly useful for patients
choosing an appropriate caretaker for their health care scenario.
a. What difficulties are there in accessing or utilizing these data sources today? In our experience, accessing
data sources that are available through the Integrated Data Repository (IDR) requires CMS to approve and
enable that access for individuals needing or requesting it. While this avenue of access allows for more
seamless integration of data from multiple CMS curated data sets, the complexity of this provisioning
process can be inefficient. Conversely, CMS data sources that are publicly available are siloed and
disconnected in such a way that it is difficult for the public to use them, either by cross-referencing related
data or applying their own data context, in a meaningful way.
b. What suggestions do you have to improve the Blue Button 2.0 API experience? We think that expanding the
data sources available in the API (as mentioned above) would be a great improvement. We also suggest
enhancing developer documentation about the API architecture. Publishing clear, detailed guidance would
be useful, especially for developer groups that are newly coding integration for Blue Button 2.0 API
c. Is there non-CMS data that should be included in the API? We believe there is tremendous value in data
from FDA (drug and medical device information), NIH (clinical trials data) and CDC (public health and
safety information). Additionally, tying the API into private sector data such as Healthgrades and Yelp
could lend new options of utility for consumers.

PC-11. How are health information exchanges (HIEs) currently helping to advance patient access to health
information in the real world? The interchange of both routine and critical patient data across various medical
systems has the potential to dramatically impact patient outcomes, however patient access tends to lag behind
clinician access. We believe the primary reason behind this is the security infrastructure necessary to ensure that
the access is protected and the actual patient can be sufficiently verified
a. How valuable, available, and accurate do you find the data they share to be? Historically the accuracy of the
demographic and personal information was a main issue, the clinical info was not. This ultimately led to
clinicians not relying on the HIE for accurate info.
b. What changes would you suggest? Data normalization is critical. Unfortunately, that needs to start at each
facility’s EMR solution. Accurate and consistent name (first, last, middle initial and last 4 of SSN or other
universal unique identifier) would be a good start.
c. Are there particular examples of high-performing HIE models that you believe should be propagated across
markets? The model propagated by the HIE in Maryland called CRISP (Chesapeake Regional Information
System for our Patients) has been effective and successful enough to have expanded their services into 5
other states through shared services partnerships. Their example should certainly be considered.
d. What is the ongoing role of HIEs amidst other entities facilitating data exchange and broader frameworks for
data exchange (for example, vendor health information networks, TEFCA, private exchange networks, etc.)?
PC-12. What are the most valuable operational health data use cases for patients and caregivers that, if
addressed, would create more efficient care navigation or eliminate barriers to competition among providers or
both? Immediacy of information is critical, weekly or monthly batch uploads just don’t help the patients. And
again, data normalization would be key to this issue.
a. Examples may include the following:
(1) Binding cost estimates for pre-defined periods. Agreed. Maryland’s rate commission helps to address this
issue.
(2) Viewing provider schedule availability. Agreed, but again, this relies on normalization of each facility’s
EMR solution.
(3) Using third-party apps for appointment management. Far too often third-party apps are too focused on one
or two solutions to a problem: calendar scheduling and cancellations. Third party apps that consider other
necessary elements like EMR integration, real time alerts to changes, and the other features can be useful,
especially if they can integrate across multiple systems.
(4) Accessing patient-facing quality metrics. We agree that this is a valuable data set, however, facilities are all
over the place tracking this information. Perhaps an operational agreement can help narrow the gap and provide
a benefit for the actual patient.

(5) Finding the right provider for specific healthcare needs. Far too often the facilities don’t maintain this
information on a timely basis, as physician rosters are notoriously out of date for most facilities, payer
networks, and the like. Patient-facing search tools claim providers are accepting new patients, but when the
patient reaches out to the provider directly, they are not accepting new patients.
b. What use cases are possible today? Patient and provider demographics, and clinical outcomes
c. What should be possible in the near future? If stringent requirements are imposed, the data can be much more
valuable to both clinician and patient.
d. What would be very valuable but may be very hard to achieve? The aforementioned data normalization. If all
participating systems can provide consistent accurate data, HIEs can play a much larger role in positive and
informative patient experiences.
3. Information Blocking and Digital Identity
PC-13. How can CMS encourage patients and caregivers to submit information blocking complaints to
ASTP/ONC's Information Blocking Portal? What would be the impact? Would increasing reporting of
complaints advance or negatively impact data exchange? Determining which patient and caregiver submissions
have merit will be challenging and may require resource-intensive manual review. It may be more effective to
enforce at the healthcare provider (HCP) level – make claims subject to denial if it is found that the HCP did not
use reasonable efforts to obtain information, or penalties (even civil action) if an HCP withheld information.
PC-14. Regarding digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800-63-3
IAL2/AAL2 credentialing service providers (CSP)):
a. What are the challenges today in getting patients/caregivers to sign up and use digital identity credentials? A
huge challenge lies in lack of trust in government or corporate use of sensitive personal data. This can be
mitigated by using solutions like Burst IQ’s LifeGraph in which the patient/caregiver owns and controls their
data/identity. Low technology literacy in some populations also proves to be extremely challenging; tech
illiteracy and clinical complexity are directly correlated, further exacerbating the issue since these are the
populations that use the most services.
b. What could be the benefits to patients/caregivers if digital identity credentials were more widely used? Better
care, less duplication of care, increased care coordination, lower costs if digital IDs are used to enable data
sharing between HCPs, reduction of the likelihood of identity or PHI theft.
c. What are the potential downsides? Potentially inequitable care for those who can't or don't adopt digital
identity solutions. Also, if digital ID credentials are implemented in a centralized manner, the risk of a large-
scale breach is significant. This can be mitigated by using a distributed data ecosystem like LifeGraph, which
allows patients, providers, and healthcare organizations to own and control their data. This decentralizes data
access and control, so breaches can't access as much data.
d. How would encouraging the use of CSPs improve access to health information? By improving the security
and authentication that is a critical foundation for all data exchange systems.

e. What role should CMS/payers, providers, and app developers have in driving adoption?
 CMS/payers: see 'f' below.
 Providers: prioritize EMRs, scheduling systems, workforce solutions, and other systems that leverage
digital identities
 App developers: make digital identity solutions user-friendly and adopt standards-based solutions.
f. How can CMS encourage patients to get digital identity credentials? Automatically issue a digital identity for
all Medicare recipients and require Medicaid agencies to provide a timeline for digital identity adoption.
Commercial payers will follow suit.

a. What are the current obstacles? Expense of these novel digital health products, their ability to integrate with

b. What information should providers share with patients when using digital products in the provision of their

unintended consequences, or causes actual damage in the patient’s experience, they should be prepared to assist
in mitigating the damage, at least indirectly.

3. Digital Identity
PR-9. How might CMS encourage providers to accept digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, ID.me,
Login.gov) from patients and their partners instead of proprietary logins that need to be tracked for each
provider relationship? Mandate it. This will force EMRs to incorporate it into their solutions. Providers will
only adopt if the EMRs provide it as part of their solution.
a. What would providers need help with to accelerate the transition to a single set of trusted digital identity
credentials for the patient to keep track of, instead of one for each provider? See above. Also, this requires
patient/consumer adoption, which will be driven by states (i.e., Real ID)
b. How might CMS balance patient privacy with convenience and access to digital health products and services
that may lead to significant improvements in health? Allow patients to opt-in to personalization of
recommended services, potentially in exchange for copay credits or other financial incentives. This is a familiar
paradigm for patients since they are used to personalization in their online retail experiences. LifeGraph can be

used to power patient-centric service marketplaces where patients can access services that are recommended for
them and personalized to them. Data is only shared with the service providers if the patient opts in.
Recommendations are based on each person but also based on what has worked/not worked for millions of
other similar people. This improves engagement, adherence, and clinical outcomes.
PR-10. Regarding digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800-63-3
IAL2/AAL2 CSPs):
a. What are the challenges and benefits for providers? Challenges: lack of understanding of how digital identity
credentials work impacts adoption. Many providers don't understand if/how digital identity credentials meet
their compliance requirements, so they deem it "safer" to stick with legacy manual processes, even though
digital identities are more secure, auditable, and trustworthy than manual credentials. Benefits: reducing fraud,
waste, and abuse, streamlines the credentialing process to get providers working faster.
b. How would requiring their use improve access to health information? Better care, less duplication of care,
increased care coordination, lower costs if digital IDs are used to enable data sharing between HCPs. LifeGraph
can serve as the secure data ecosystem that enables patients, providers, and payers to access and share data
while maintaining ownership and control of their data. Serving as a national HIE and provider credential
network. Data is only accessible to providers who are properly credentialed. Governance and regulatory
compliance, including patient consent, are automatically enforced. All transactions are auditable.
c. What are the potential downsides? Short term: potentially lengthy adoption and learning curve. Long term:
potentially inequitable care for patients who can't or don't adopt digital identity solutions. This could mean poor
HEDIS and eCQM ratings for providers that serve low-tech-literacy populations.
d. What impact would mandatory credentials have on a nationwide provider directory? Mandatory credentials
would be a driving force to make a comprehensive provider directory more useful. Digital credentials would
transform a simple directory into a valuable credential repository that drives next-gen credentialing services,
healthcare workforce solutions, and other operations software. LifeGraph can serve as the secure data
ecosystem that allows providers to own, maintain, update, and share their verified identity and credentials.
Credentialing services, EMRs, workforce management platforms, and other products and services would be able
to access the CMS data ecosystem via API and securely access up-to-date provider data on a real-time basis.
e. How could digital identity implementation improve provider data flow? By providing better
authentication/transparency/accountability for provider-to-provider data exchange. Additionally, having a
provider digital identity linked to provider activity (encounters, claims, etc.) allows for better understanding of
how providers are interacting with each other and with patients. This provides better analytics opportunities for
FWA analysis. Because LifeGraph can manage individual data (e.g., provider digital identities) and operations
data, it can be used to automate many processes that require identity verification and "need to know" standing,
all based on unified knowledge graphs of providers, patients, encounters, claims, operational data, and more.
f. Would combining FHIR addresses and identity improve data flow? Yes. Coupling these together would allow
for end-to-end automation, per 'e.' above.
PR-11. How could members of trust communities [3] (for example, QHINs, participants and subparticipants in
TEFCA, which requires Identity Assurance Level 2 (IAL2) via Credential Service Providers (CSPs)) better

b. How would requiring digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800-

c. What impact would mandatory use of the OpenID Connect identity protocol have? Mandating the use of this

will also ensure that the authentication method used by apps for health data exchanges achieve a benchmark of
quality of security in order to be implemented.

This section is intended for all stakeholders to provide input on questions as they relate to use cases and
workflows that involve value-based care organizations. While we certainly want value-based care organizations

1. Digital Health Adoption
VB-1. What incentives could encourage APMs such as accountable care organizations (ACOs) or participants
in Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) to leverage digital health management and care navigation
products more often and more effectively with their patients? What are the current obstacles preventing broader
digital product adoption for patients in ACOs? Remove the restrictions that insurance plans use to limit patient
choices and movement. Several incentives can be implemented:
 Tie reimbursement and shared savings more directly to the use of digital tools,
 Offer additional bonuses or shared savings for ACOs that achieve high rates of patient engagement
with digital tools or measurable improvements in quality metrics attributable to digital interventions,
 Provide ACOs with enhanced access to real-time data, analytics, and interoperability tools, making
it easier to integrate digital health solutions into care delivery and coordination,
 Reward ACOs that successfully engage patients through digital means (e.g., mobile apps, remote
monitoring, care navigation platforms), as consumer engagement is increasingly recognized as a key
driver of value-based care success.
Obstacles preventing broader digital product adoption include
 The inability for solutions to fit into existing clinical and administrative workflows,
 Limited interoperability between digital tools, EHRs, and other health IT systems,
 If digital tools are not user-friendly or do not demonstrate clear value, utilization rates remain low.
VB-2. How can key themes and technologies such as artificial intelligence, population health analytics, risk
stratification, care coordination, usability, quality measurement, and patient engagement be better integrated
into APM requirements? Mandating and incentivizing the use of platforms and software solutions that support
advanced analytics, the use of responsible and explainable AI, and population health tools will certainly foster
their integration into APM requirements. Making population health analytics and risk stratification core APM
requirements will lead to using these technologies in identifying high risk patients, targeting interventions, and
efficiently allocating resources. Tying provider reimbursement to the successful use of patient engagement tools
like patient portals & digital communication and including metrics that demonstrate these successes as part of
APM performance measures can foster the adoption.
VB-3. What are essential health IT capabilities for value-based care arrangements? We believe all examples
cited below are essential health IT capabilities for a value-based care practice to positively impact patient
outcomes, increase efficiency of care, and lower costs including care planning, patient event notification, data
extraction/normalization, quality/performance measurement, access to claims data, attribution and patient ID

matching, and remote device interoperability. We would like to add the critical need for health IT to include
capturing patient experiences in structured, semi-structured, and unstructured data.
a. Examples (not comprehensive) may include: care planning, patient event notification, data
extraction/normalization, quality performance measurement, access to claims data, attribution and patient ID
matching, remote device interoperability, or other patient empowerment tools.
b. What other health IT capabilities have proven valuable to succeeding in value-based care arrangements?
Telemedicine and virtual care, patient safety and incident reporting systems, and clinical decision support
(CDS) tools have all been very useful in successful value-based care arrangements.
VB-4. What are the essential data types needed for successful participation in value-based care arrangements?
Structured data (e.g., numerical, coded, and tabular formats), semi-structured data (as in clinical notes, images,
and reports), and unstructured data (like free-text narratives, social media, and patient-generated content) cover
the broad formats from the original sources that data comes from, and so, all are essential. That said, essential
data types include:
 Electronic Health Records (EHRs)
 Claims Data
 Social Determinants of Health (SDoH) Data
 Patient Demographics
 Clinical Risk Scores and Health Risk Assessments
 Admission, Discharge, and Transfer (ADT) Data
 Quality Performance Metrics
 Patient Engagement and Experience Data
 Attribution and Patient ID Matching Data
