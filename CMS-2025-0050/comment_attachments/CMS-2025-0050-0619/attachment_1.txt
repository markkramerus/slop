B. Patients and Caregivers PC-8. In your experience, what health data is readily available and valuable to patients or their caregivers or both? a. What data is valuable, but hard for patients and caregivers, or app developers and other technical vendors, to access for appropriate and valuable use (for example, claims data, clinical data, encounter notes, operative reports, appointment schedules, prices)? The Department of Health & Human Services (HHS) has made significant investments in modernizing secure, virtual access to some of the largest health services research data repositories in the country, notably the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (C MS) Chronic Conditions Warehouse and Virtual Research Data Center (CCW VRDC). This comprehensive environment houses over 400 billion rows of Medicare and Medicaid claims data dating back to 1999, with more than one billion new records added monthly. The wa rehouse integrates more than 40 data sources, including beneficiary enrollment, Medicare and Medicaid administrative claims, prescription drug events, managed care encounter data, and assessment data from nursing home and home health care settings. It also includes supplemental sources such as the National Death Index and social risk indicators. The environment supports advanced analytic capabilities , including Snowflake, SAS, R, and Python through a container -based Jupyter platform , as well as Virtual Desktop Infrastructure and data visualization tools like Databricks and Tableau. These tools allow researchers and developers to conduct meaningful real -world research with actionable evidence. Additionally, the CMS Blue Button on FHIR ( BBonFHIR) initiative, supported by a Data -as-a-Service (DaaS) platform, enables beneficiaries to connect their individu al Medicare Part A, B, and D claims data with trusted applications. This DaaS infrastructure is designed to allow data providers to publish analytics -ready data products without requiring subscribers to build data processing pipelines. b. What are specific sources, other than claims and clinical data, that would be of highest value, and why? c. What specific opportunities and challenges exist to improve accessibility, interoperability and integration of clinical data from different sources to enable more meaningful clinical research and generation of actionable evidence? Many types of data that are highly valuable to patients, caregivers, and application developers remain difficult to access. Clinical data, such as encounter notes, operative reports, and real -time

A particularly high -value but underutilized category of data is Health -Related Social Needs (HRSN) data. This includes metrics such as housing instability, food insecurity, transportation challenges, and utility access —factors that directly impact health outcomes but are rarely captured in traditional claims or clinical systems. By systematically colle cting and integrating HRSN data, organizations can: • Inform whole -person care by addressing both clinical and social determinants of health • Predict health outcomes and costs through early identification of risk factors • Support value -based care models • Enable equitable resource allocation • Foster collaboration between healthcare and social service providers Standardized tools such as the AHC HRSN Screening Tool (developed by CMS), PRAPARE (Protocol for Responding to and Assessing Patients’ Assets, Risks, and Experiences), and the Joint Commission HRSN Screening Question Bank provide frameworks for consistent data collection across organizations and geographies. To meaningfully advance data accessibility, interoperability, and integration, HHS has the opportunity to build upon its existing investments in the CCW VRDC and DaaS platforms by: • Expanding integration with electronic health information, environmental datasets, and social determinants of health data; • Streamlining and automating data access across both clinical and community -based data systems; • Supporting API -based data sharing models to accelerate innovation in care delivery and patient empowerment. These steps would enable a more holistic and actionable evidence base, bridging the gap between clinical insight and real -world conditions that affect patient health. PC-9. Given that the Blue Button 2.0 API only includes basic patient demographic, Medicare coverage, and claims data (Part A, B, D), what additional CMS data sources do developers view as most valuable for inclusion in the API to enable more useful digital products for patients and caretakers? There are several CMS data sources that would significantly enhance the Blue Button 2.0 API’s value for building digital products for patients and caregivers: • Medicare Part C (Medicare Advantage) Claims: Currently, Blue Button 2.0 does not provide Part C claims data, which covers a substantial and growing portion of Medicare beneficiaries. Including this would offer a more complete view of patient care and utilization. • Clinical Data including Electronic Health Record (EHR) Data: Access to clinical data, such as lab results, vital signs, immunizations, and care plans , beyond claims , would enable richer, more actionable insights for both patients and care teams.

What are ways CMS or partners can help with simplifying clinical quality data responsibilities of providers? • What would be the benefits and downsides of using Bulk FHIR data exports from EHRs to CMS to simplify clinical quality data submissions? Can CMS reduce the burden on providers by performing quality metrics calculations leveraging Bulk FHIR data exports? Bulk FHIR data exports from electronic health records (EHRs) offer a compelling opportunity to reduce the burden on clinicians participating in CMS quality reporting programs. By enabling continuous and standardized data exchange, this approach could strea mline the reporting process, eliminating the need for clinicians to manually generate and submit custom reports. This automation aligns with CMS’s goals of reducing administrative overhead and allowing clinicians to focus more on patient care. However, th e implementation of Bulk FHIR data exports is not without its challenges. Ensuring the accuracy and completeness of quality measure calculations from exported data is paramount. EHRs —even those from the same vendor —can vary significantly across sites due t o local customizations, and clinician documentation practices differ widely. While structured data supports automated reporting, it often fails to capture the full richness of the patient narrative. If 
CMS ASTP /ONC Solicitation of Public Comments Health Technology Ecosys tem 06/16/2025 5 clinicians must double -document to preserve this narrative while also meeting structured data requirements, the intended burden reduction may be undermined. Another potential downside lies in the cost of enabling and maintaining these data exports. Depending on EHR vendor pricing models, clinicians or healthcare organizations may face new or increased costs, even if other more labor -intensive reporting process es are phased out. Importantly, the broader quality measurement ecosystem could also be affected. Qualified Clinical Data Registries (QCDRs) currently play a key role in developing and maintaining specialty -specific quality measures, often at a faster pace than CMS’s Measure s Under Consideration (MUC) process. QCDRs rely heavily on revenue from clinician participation, which could decline if simplified FHIR -based reporting leads clinicians to disengage. This could severely limit the pipeline of new quality measures, particul arly for underrepresented specialties. The CMS Meri t-based Incentive Payment System ( MIPS ) measure inventory is dynamic , and measures frequently become “topped out,” meaning there’s little room for additional improvement. As such, an ongoing mechanism for new measure development is essential. While CMS has expressed an intent to reduce the overall number of measures, the current MUC process alone may not be agile enough to sustain a responsive and relevant measure set. In summary, Bulk FHIR data exports hold promise for easing clinician reporting burdens, but careful attention must be paid to data integrity, documentation pract ices, cost implications, and the preservation of the quality measure development ecosystem —particularly the role of QCDRs —in order to ensure the long -term success and equity of CMS quality programs. TD-5. How could a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints improve access to health information for patients, providers, and payers? Who should publish such a directory, and should users bear a cost ? A nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints would allow payers to more efficiently maintain current provider directories for the benefit of consumers who need important information when selecting plans or providers within a plan’s network. Data , such as the provider’s name, specialty, address, phone number, availability , and accepted insurance , are important for consumers to make informed choices. CMS could make such a resource available based on provider data already available in National Plan and Provider Enumeration System (NPPES). The use of FHIR and clearly defined proces ses can ensure continual accuracy. A nationwide FHIR endpoints directory would serve as critical infrastructure for healthcare interoperability by: • Improving Access & Understanding: A centralized directory would eliminate the current fragmented discovery process where providers must individually negotiate data sharing agreements. This creates a standardized catalog enabling automated discovery of data sources, reducing integration timelines from months to weeks. For claims data , specifically, it would provide clear mapping between clinical care delivery points and billing entities, resolving the common disconnect between where care occurs and how it's reported financially. The directory would enable real -time patient data aggregation across multiple providers during care episodes, particularly valuable for emergency scenarios and care transitions. It also supports population health analytics by providing a complete view of d ata source coverage and gaps. • Streamlining Payer Operations : A nationwide FHIR endpoints directory would significantly streamline payer operations by enabling automated provider directory updates. Currently, payers manually maintain separate directories through surveys and

With standardized FHIR endpoints, payers could automatically pull updated provider information , including specialty changes, new locations, and network participation status. This ensures consumers have accurate, real -time data when selecting plans or providers within networks, directly impacting healthcare access decisions. Leveraging existing National Plan and Provider Enumeration System ( NPPES ) data as a foundation, CMS could establish authoritative provider demographic information while FHIR endpoints provide the technical connectivity layer. This dual approach ensures both data accuracy and technical interoperability.
