SPECIFIC RFI COMMENTS
B. Patients and Caregivers
1. Patient Needs
PC-1. What health management or care navigation apps would help you understand and manage your (or
your loved ones) health needs, as well as the actions you should take?
Healthcare clearinghouses are well positioned to support digital tools that help patients and caregivers
understand and manage their health needs through access to accurate, payer-verified data. Digital health
products powered by clearinghouse transactions (such as 270/271 for eligibility and benefits, 837 for claims
history, and 278 for prior authorization) can enable several high-impact capabilities.
a) What are the top things you would like to be able to do for your or your loved ones' health that can
be enabled by digital health products?
The top functionality patients want from digital health tools include: verifying coverage for services in advance
of care; identifying in-network providers; reviewing prior claims and remittance details; and tracking eligibility
for preventive services. For example, a patient should be able to open an app and immediately see whether a
flu shot, colonoscopy, or physical therapy visit is covered and when it’s next available—information already
contained in 271 responses.
b) If you had a personal assistant to support your health needs, what are the top things you would
ask them to help with? In your response, please consider tasks that could be supported or
facilitated by software solutions in the future.
If these tools functioned like a personal assistant, patients could receive alerts when a new prior authorization
is required, get reminders when a service is eligible again, or be guided to the most cost-effective provider
based on previous 835 data. Clearinghouses can make this vision real by standardizing and delivering multi-
payer administrative data in real-time to patient-facing apps, helping users take informed actions with less
friction and more confidence.
PC-2. Do you have easy access to your own and all your loved ones' health information in one location
(for example, in a single patient portal or another software system)?
Clearinghouses can aggregate health information across multiple payers, offering a unique path toward a
unified patient view. However, most patients and caregivers today do not have easy access to all relevant health
data in a single system. Information is often spread across disparate provider portals, creating confusion and
administrative burden.
a) If so, what are some examples of benefits it has provided?
In cases where partial integration exists—such as access to Medicare claims via Blue Button 2.0 or through a
third-party app—patients benefit from visibility into their service history, medication fills, and cost-sharing,
which can aid in managing chronic conditions or reconciling bills. This promotes trust and helps prevent
unnecessary repeat services.
b) If not, in what contexts or for what workflows would it be most valuable to use one portal or
system to access all such health information?
Unified access would be especially valuable when coordinating care for aging parents, managing multiple
chronic illnesses, or making time-sensitive decisions, such as evaluating a new provider or comparing costs.
A single portal that includes eligibility (270/271), claims (837), remittance (835), and prior authorization (278)
data would dramatically reduce navigation complexity and enhance decision-making.
2

c) Were there particular data types, such as x-rays or specific test results, that were unavailable?
What are the obstacles to accessing your own or your loved ones' complete health information
electronically and using it for managing health conditions or finding the best care (for example,
limitations in functionality, user friendliness, or access to basic technology infrastructure)?
Key data types such as x-rays, lab results, and care team attribution are often missing or siloed. Common
obstacles include limited portal functionality, payer-to-provider data variability, inconsistent formatting of 835
or 278 data, and lack of patient-friendly interfaces. Clearinghouses are positioned to address these challenges
by standardizing and delivering complete, structured administrative data that reflects what services were
covered, authorized, and paid for—critical insights for managing both health and costs.
PC-4. What features are missing from apps you use or that you are aware of today?
Many current health apps lack the ability to present a complete, payer-verified view of a patient’s healthcare
experience. Missing features include real-time eligibility status for specific services, integrated prior
authorization tracking, explanation of benefits with standardized denial reasons, and consolidated cost-
sharing information across payers. These gaps largely stem from limited access to administrative data such as
270/271, 835, and 278 transactions. Clearinghouses already facilitate these transactions at scale and could
enable apps to show when a service is next eligible, whether an authorization is pending, and how much a
patient is expected to pay—based on actual payer adjudication data. Additionally, few apps support multi-
payer data aggregation, which is essential for beneficiaries with both Medicare and supplemental or Medicaid
coverage. A clearinghouse-enabled platform could fill these critical gaps, making digital tools significantly
more useful for Medicare beneficiaries.
a) What apps should exist but do not yet? Why do you believe they do not exist yet?
There is a clear need for an app that provides Medicare beneficiaries with a real-time, multi-payer view of their
eligibility, prior authorizations, claims history, and out-of-pocket costs—all in one place. This app should also
offer proactive alerts, such as when a preventive service is next eligible or when a claim has been denied. Such
an app does not yet exist largely because of the fragmented nature of healthcare data, limited access to real-
time payer adjudication information, and the absence of common APIs for core administrative transactions.
Clearinghouses already aggregate this data across nearly all payers and could provide the infrastructure for
such an application if policy and technical access barriers were addressed.
b) What set of workflows do you believe CMS is uniquely positioned to offer?
CMS is uniquely positioned to offer workflows that center around coverage, eligibility, and quality improvement.
These include determining next eligible dates for preventive services based on 271 frequency limitations,
surfacing pending prior authorizations from 278 data, tracking total cost of care using claims, and offering
timely alerts when remittance advice (835) indicates unexpected patient financial responsibility. These
workflows rely on standard administrative data that CMS receives today and could be surfaced through patient-
facing APIs powered by clearinghouses. This would advance transparency, reduce patient confusion, and
support more informed healthcare choices.
PC-5. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in these digital health
products?
To increase patient and caregiver interest in digital health products, CMS can leverage its trusted position and
vast data assets by enabling more robust, real-time access to administrative health information through
consumer-facing applications. Clearinghouses already support bi-directional data exchange with all payers
and providers under HIPAA, making them ideal partners in delivering accurate eligibility, claim, and prior
authorization data directly to patient apps. CMS can further encourage adoption by developing a “trusted app”
3

designation tied to data completeness and usability standards. These apps could be featured in Medicare.gov
resources or incentivized through Medicare Advantage supplemental benefits. Additionally, CMS should
expand Blue Button 2.0 to include 835, 276/277, and 278 transaction data, and allow secure integration of
clearinghouse APIs for third-party developers. Clear, plain-language communication campaigns—especially
those embedded in provider portals or remittance notices—can raise awareness among caregivers and
patients about the tools available to support their care decisions.
PC-6. What features are most important to make digital health products accessible and easy to use for
Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers, particularly those with limited prior experience using digital tools
and services?
To ensure digital health products are accessible to Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers—especially those
with limited digital experience—the most important features are simplicity, clarity, and automation. Interfaces
should avoid technical jargon and instead present information in plain language, such as “Your next eligible
mammogram is covered starting June 1” instead of referencing 271 frequency limits or CPT codes. Visual aids,
large fonts, and voice-assisted navigation can further increase usability. Clearinghouse-enabled apps can also
reduce user burden by auto-populating forms with verified eligibility and claims data, showing clear
explanations of coverage and costs based on 835 data, and surfacing alerts for required actions (e.g., a denied
service or a needed authorization). Caregiver proxy access and printable summaries for offline use are also
essential. By integrating these features into workflows powered by real-time clearinghouse transactions, CMS
can help ensure digital health tools are useful and accessible to the full Medicare population.
PC-7. If CMS were to collect real-world data on digital health products' impact on health outcomes and
related costs once they are released into the market, what would be the best means of doing so?
The most effective way for CMS to collect real-world data on the impact of digital health products is by
leveraging existing clearinghouse infrastructure to track changes in utilization, cost, and care quality through
standard administrative transactions. Claims (837), remittance advice (835), and eligibility (270/271) data can
be analyzed pre- and post-adoption to measure outcomes such as preventive service uptake, reduction in
denied claims, or fewer unnecessary emergency visits. CMS could also implement event-based triggers—such
as a prior authorization submission (278) or service claim—that prompt short patient-reported outcome
surveys. These can be distributed through patient portals or partner apps. Additionally, CMS may consider
requiring clearinghouses and payers to append a unique digital health product identifier to claims associated
with app-enabled interactions, creating a consistent feedback loop. This approach minimizes provider burden
and captures impact at scale while respecting existing HIPAA-compliant data flows.
2. Data Access and Integration
PC-8. In your experience, what health data is readily available and valuable to patients or their caregivers
or both?
Patients and caregivers most commonly access eligibility (270/271) and claims data (837) through provider or
payer portals. These data types are highly valuable for understanding what services are covered, what care has
been received, and what financial responsibilities exist. Clearinghouses play a central role in ensuring this data
is accurate, current, and available across all payers.
a) What data is valuable, but hard for patients and caregivers, or app developers and other technical
vendors, to access for appropriate and valuable use (for example, claims data, clinical data,
encounter notes, operative reports, appointment schedules, prices)?
Despite its value, data such as prior authorization status (278), remittance advice (835), appointment
schedules, and denial reasons (CARCs/RARCs) remains difficult to access consistently. Technical and policy
barriers restrict app developers from retrieving this data, even though it is routinely exchanged through
4

clearinghouses. Clinical documentation such as operative reports or encounter notes is often siloed within
EHR systems and not linked to administrative events, making it hard to surface meaningful context in digital
tools.
b) What are specific sources, other than claims and clinical data, that would be of highest value,
and why?
In addition to clinical and claims data, member attribution rosters (834), preventive service frequency data
(within 271), and Z-codes or SDOH indicators are of growing importance. These data points help patients
understand who is accountable for their care, what services they’re due for, and how social risk factors are
influencing their care plans. This is particularly relevant for ACOs and Medicare Advantage plans managing
value-based populations.
c) What specific opportunities and challenges exist to improve accessibility, interoperability and
integration of clinical data from different sources to enable more meaningful clinical research
and generation of actionable evidence?
A major opportunity lies in integrating clearinghouse data with clinical data via FHIR APIs and standardized
document formats such as CCDs and C-CDAs. Challenges include aligning data models (X12 vs. FHIR),
inconsistent payer implementations, and lack of identity resolution across systems. Clearinghouses can help
bridge this gap by matching administrative transactions to clinical events, improving both research data quality
and the delivery of actionable insights to patients and caregivers in real time.
PC-9. Given that the Blue Button 2.0 API only includes basic patient demographic, Medicare coverage,
and claims data (Part A, B, D), what additional CMS data sources do developers view as most valuable for
inclusion in the API to enable more useful digital products for patients and caretakers?
a) What difficulties are there in accessing or utilizing these data sources today?
Today, developers face challenges in accessing this data due to limited API endpoints, lack of real-time
updates, and variability in how payers format and release these transaction types. Most 835 and 278 data
remain locked in proprietary systems or payer portals, with no standardized patient-facing interface.
Clearinghouses already facilitate this exchange at national scale, but there is no current mechanism to expose
it via the Blue Button infrastructure.
b) What suggestions do you have to improve the Blue Button 2.0 API experience?
To improve Blue Button 2.0, CMS should expand data availability to include remittance, authorization, and
claim status data, with support for event-based updates. Incorporating FHIR-based endpoints that reflect the
underlying X12 transactions—supported by clearinghouses—would enable real-time, multi-payer
transparency for patients and their apps.
c) Is there non-CMS data that should be included in the API?
Non-CMS data sources that would further enhance the API include Medicare Advantage encounter data, ACO
attribution rosters, social risk indicators (e.g., Z-codes or SDOH screenings), and Medicaid dual-eligibility
status. These data types are often managed or exchanged through clearinghouses and could be incorporated
through trusted intermediary APIs to broaden patient access beyond Fee-for-Service claims alone.
PC-10. How is the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) currently helping to
advance patient access to health information in the real world?
a) Please provide specific examples.
5

TEFCA has laid a foundational structure to help patients gain access to their health data, specifically through
Individual Access Services (IAS). This is an important step in upholding the patient’s right to access and control
their health information—one of the cornerstones of the administration’s commitment to healthcare
transparency and freedom of choice. However, in practice, most patient access still occurs outside of TEFCA—
through HIPAA-authorized requests, patient portals, and third-party apps leveraging Cures Act APIs. TEFCA’s
real-world impact remains limited, especially given slow adoption and lack of incentive alignment.
b) What changes would you suggest?
To advance the administration’s priority of reducing regulatory burden and promoting private-sector-led
solutions, TEFCA should be limited to core use cases that directly benefit the individual—specifically,
treatment and patient-initiated data access. Expanding TEFCA into operational, payment, or public health
domains risks duplicating private-sector infrastructure, displacing innovation, and creating costly mandates.
Existing systems—particularly clearinghouses—already deliver secure, high-volume data exchange at scale
across payers, providers, and other stakeholders. The government should support, not supplant, these trusted
and market-proven solutions.
c) What use cases could have a significant impact if implemented through TEFCA?
The most impactful role for TEFCA is to empower individuals and providers:
• Patients accessing their complete medical records across networks through simple, secure
applications.
• Doctors retrieving clinical history from multiple unaffiliated systems in emergency or cross-provider
settings.
These use cases fulfill TEFCA’s original promise without expanding federal authority over non-clinical data
flows.
d) What standards are you aware of that are currently working well to advance access and existing
exchange purposes?
Private-sector solutions already rely on widely adopted standards that are functioning effectively:
• X12 standards through clearinghouses for administrative data exchange remain foundational and
efficient.
• FHIR APIs, enabled by the Cures Act, are modernizing patient and payer data access without
excessive government control.
• Direct Secure Messaging and IHE-based protocols continue to support care coordination and
referrals.
These standards are interoperable, scalable, and not dependent on federal overreach.
e) Are there redundant standards, protocols, or channels that should be consolidated?
There is increasing risk of duplication and inefficiency. For example, TEFCA’s push to create public health
reporting pathways overlaps with modernization efforts and existing HL7 channels. Similarly, inserting TEFCA
into administrative exchange introduces redundant regulatory layers atop already effective clearinghouse-
driven networks. Consolidation and restraint—not expansion—should be the guiding principle.
f) Are there adequate alternatives outside of TEFCA for achieving widespread patient access to
their health information?
6

Yes, and they are working. Under policies championed by President Trump and furthered under current HHS
leadership, private innovators have deployed patient-facing apps, interoperable APIs, and low-friction portals.
Clearinghouses, health IT vendors, and payer platforms already deliver access, speed, and value—without
requiring a new layer of centralized government control. CMS and HHS should continue encouraging these
market-led approaches.
PC-11. How are health information exchanges (HIEs) currently helping to advance patient access to
health information in the real world?
a) How valuable, available, and accurate do you find the data they share to be?
While HIEs were designed to improve access to longitudinal patient data, the reality has fallen short.
Inconsistent participation, limited interoperability, and fragmented governance have restricted their data
availability and accuracy across many regions. Much of the data shared via HIEs is still incomplete, out of date,
or siloed within limited provider networks. In contrast, clearinghouses already aggregate a more
comprehensive and real-time view of patient data by virtue of processing millions of claims, attachments, and
medical records—including structured CCDs and FHIR payloads used in prior authorization and clinical
documentation. This claim-linked data often reflects the most recent and billable clinical activities across
multiple settings and payers, making it more accurate, accessible, and actionable than what is often found in
HIE systems.
d) What is the ongoing role of HIEs amidst other entities facilitating data exchange and broader
frameworks for data exchange (for example, vendor health information networks, TEFCA, private
exchange networks, etc.)?
The role of traditional HIEs should be limited and transitional. In today’s data-rich and highly connected
environment, multiple private-sector frameworks already outperform legacy HIEs in scope and utility. Vendor-
driven networks (such as CommonWell or Carequality), TEFCA’s QHINs, and especially clearinghouses, now
serve as the real backbone of clinical and administrative interoperability.
Clearinghouses, in particular, occupy a unique position: they are already embedded into nearly every provider
and payer workflow, operate under HIPAA-regulated trust frameworks, and process vast quantities of
structured, standardized data. Rather than expanding federal or state programs to replicate what the private
sector is already doing, CMS and HHS should recognize clearinghouses as natural successors to the HIE
model, capable of supporting patient access, care coordination, population health, and even public health
reporting—without additional regulatory overhead.
PC-12. What are the most valuable operational health data use cases for patients and caregivers that, if
addressed, would create more efficient care navigation or eliminate barriers to competition among
providers or both?
Operational health data can meaningfully improve care navigation and promote competition if surfaced to
patients in a timely, standardized, and accessible way. Clearinghouses already support many of the
foundational data elements needed for this—including eligibility, remittance, and claims-based quality
indicators—making them key enablers of these use cases.
a) Examples may include the following:
Examples such as binding cost estimates and provider-specific quality data are increasingly in demand.
Eligibility (270/271) and remittance (835) data can support real-time, patient-specific cost estimates, while
claims (837) can inform comparative quality metrics. However, provider schedule availability and appointment
management require integration with clinical systems, which today operate separately from the clearinghouse
infrastructure.
7

b) What use cases are possible today?
Today, it is feasible to offer pre-service benefit verification, estimates of patient responsibility based on 835
patterns, and retrospective quality indicators based on claims (e.g., preventive service completion). Third-
party apps can display in-network providers and cost-sharing details using clearinghouse data.
c) What should be possible in the near future?
In the near future, it should be possible to integrate real-time prior authorization data (278) and payer coverage
policy into appointment scheduling tools. Patients could be alerted if a service requires approval, and digital
assistants could route them to covered providers with high-quality outcomes.
d) What would be very valuable but may be very hard to achieve?
Achieving true, real-time visibility into provider availability and dynamic scheduling across systems would be
highly valuable, but remains difficult due to siloed EHR platforms and a lack of standardized scheduling APIs.
Similarly, translating raw clinical outcomes into meaningful, risk-adjusted quality metrics that patients can act
on—at the point of decision-making—requires ongoing investment in data standardization, attribution, and
usability.

2. Data Exchange
PR-5. Which of the following FHIR APIs and capabilities do you already support or utilize in your provider
organization's systems, directly or through an intermediary? For each, describe the transaction model,
use case, whether you use individual queries or bulk transactions, and any constraints:
TEFCA is beginning to support provider access to clinical records, but its full potential remains under
development. Most current implementations focus on document exchange between provider networks, with
limited integration into administrative workflows that directly impact care delivery and operations.
a) Patient Access API: While primarily implemented by payers, clearinghouses can enable this API by
linking claims and remittance data (837, 835) to patient-authorized apps. The use case is individual-
level data retrieval; constraint includes data access authorization across multiple payers.
b) Standardized API for Patient and Population Services: Not typically supported directly by
clearinghouses, but they can enhance this with bulk eligibility checks or claims-based quality metrics
at the population level.
c) Provider Directory API: Clearinghouses often maintain extensive provider network directories
through trading partner relationships and can supply validated data for this API. This supports both
individual queries and batch directory updates.
d) Provider Access API: Clearinghouses can enable provider access APIs by linking active patient claims
to attribution data and supporting verification of treatment relationships—essential for ACOs and MA
plans.
e) Payer-to-Payer API: Clearinghouses often act as intermediaries in payer-to-payer transitions,
particularly for eligibility (834) and historical claims data. Use case is individual or batch transmission
of historical care data when members switch plans.
f) Prior Authorization API: This is an emerging function. Clearinghouses currently support X12 278
transactions and can serve as a foundation for FHIR-based Prior Auth APIs (PAS). Constraints include
varying levels of EHR integration with authorization workflows.
g) Bulk FHIR: Some clearinghouses support group-level batch queries through their existing EDI
infrastructure, particularly for eligibility and claims history. While not fully FHIR-based, this model
could be extended to meet the use case.
8

h) SMART on FHIR: Deployment is typically handled by provider organizations. However, clearinghouses
could support backend data feeds or integration for claims and eligibility information that would be
accessed via SMART apps.
i) CDS Hooks: Not directly supported, but clearinghouses can supply data inputs—such as
authorization requirements or recent denial patterns—that could trigger clinical decision support logic
when integrated through EHR vendors.
PR-7. What strategies can CMS implement to support providers in making high-quality, timely, and
comprehensive healthcare data available for interoperability in the digital product ecosystem? How can
the burden of increasing data availability and sharing be mitigated for providers? Are there ways that
workflows or metrics that providers are already motivated to optimize for that could be reused for, or
combined with, efforts needed to support interoperability?
CMS can support providers in improving data quality and interoperability by aligning existing reporting and
administrative workflows with digital health data-sharing requirements. Many providers already submit
standardized data to clearinghouses for eligibility (270/271), claims (837), and remittance (835). Leveraging
this infrastructure to pre-populate or validate data for digital products reduces duplication and ensures
accuracy. To reduce provider burden, CMS could allow clearinghouse-submitted data to serve dual purposes—
for example, using 837 claims to meet both payment and quality measurement requirements, or allowing
270/271 transactions to support care coordination and eligibility verification in digital tools. Additionally, CMS
should invest in automation and bulk FHIR APIs that reduce manual chart abstraction, encourage adoption of
CDS Hooks and SMART on FHIR apps that deliver interoperability within the clinical workflow, and promote
data standards alignment across EHRs and clearinghouses. Real-time authorization data, patient attribution
feeds, and service utilization summaries could all be surfaced from existing clearinghouse data flows with
minimal provider effort, advancing interoperability without imposing new documentation burdens.
PR-8. What are ways CMS or partners can help with simplifying clinical quality data responsibilities of
providers?
CMS can simplify clinical quality data responsibilities by aligning reporting requirements with the data
providers already submit through clearinghouses and EHRs. Much of the information needed for quality
measurement—such as diagnoses, procedures, and preventive services—is already present in standard
administrative transactions such as the 837 (claims), 835 (remittance), and 270/271 (eligibility). By allowing
this data to be reused for digital quality measurement, CMS can reduce redundant documentation and manual
chart review.
a) What would be the benefits and downsides of using Bulk FHIR data exports from EHRs to CMS to
simplify clinical quality data submissions? Can CMS reduce the burden on providers by
performing quality metrics calculations leveraging Bulk FHIR data exports?
Using Bulk FHIR exports from EHRs to CMS can streamline quality reporting by allowing CMS to perform
measure calculations centrally, reducing the need for each provider to manage its own quality engine. This
would lower technical overhead, ensure consistency in interpretation, and help smaller or rural providers
participate in programs they might otherwise avoid. However, risks include incomplete EHR data capture,
potential latency in batch updates, and reduced transparency if providers lack visibility into how quality scores
are derived. CMS can mitigate these risks by providing real-time dashboards and feedback loops, and by
supplementing FHIR data with clearinghouse-derived claims and remittance information.
b) In what ways can the interoperability and quality reporting responsibilities of providers be
consolidated so investments can be dually purposed?
Interoperability and quality reporting should be designed around shared data pipelines. Claims (837), eligibility
(270/271), and remittance (835) data already submitted through clearinghouses can serve dual purposes: both
9

for payment operations and for quality tracking. Aligning data submission requirements for MIPS, HEDIS, and
ACO programs around common formats (FHIR, X12, QRDA) and data definitions would maximize the return on
providers’ existing reporting infrastructure.
c) Are there requirements CMS should consider for data registries to support digital quality
measurement in a more efficient manner? Are there requirements CMS should consider for data
registries that would support access to real-time quality data for healthcare providers to inform
clinical care in addition to simplifying reporting processes?
CMS should ensure that registries certified for digital quality measurement can ingest both clinical (EHR-
derived) and administrative (clearinghouse-derived) data. Requirements should include support for HL7 FHIR,
standardized APIs, and timely bi-directional exchange. Registries should also surface actionable insights—
such as overdue screenings or gaps in risk coding—within the provider’s workflow. Real-time registry feedback,
built on claims and EHR integration, would both improve patient care and reduce retrospective audit burdens.
Where feasible, CMS should incentivize alignment between registry functions and existing clearinghouse
services to avoid redundant systems and streamline provider participation.
D. Payers
PA-1. What policy or technical limitations do you see in TEFCA? What changes would you suggest to
address those limitations? To what degree do you expect these limitations to hinder participation in
TEFCA?
TEFCA’s current focus on clinical document exchange limits its utility for payers, who rely heavily on
administrative data such as eligibility (270/271), claims (837), remittance (835), and prior authorization (278)
to manage care and risk. These transactions are not yet integrated into TEFCA, making it less relevant to core
payer workflows and reducing the incentive for participation. Additionally, TEFCA lacks clear alignment with
existing clearinghouse infrastructure, which already connects all major payers and providers through
standardized X12 transactions. Without support for these workflows, TEFCA risks duplicating efforts rather
than enhancing interoperability. Additionally, there is a current lack of alignment on how to securely exchange
sensitive data via TEFCA. In particular, how does one know the entity asking for data has the proper permission
and valid use case under HIPAA to have said data? Clearinghouses have already addressed this issue over
years of use and have processes and procedures in place to validate both parties.
PA-2. How can CMS encourage payers to accelerate the implementation and utilization of APIs for
patients, providers, and other payers, similar to the Blue Button 2.0 and Data at the Point of Care APIs
released by CMS?
CMS can encourage payers to accelerate API adoption by aligning incentives with operational priorities and
leveraging existing clearinghouse infrastructure to minimize implementation burden. Specifically, CMS could
tie Medicare Advantage Star Ratings, risk adjustment accuracy, or participation in value-based programs to
payer API readiness and usage metrics. To drive adoption, CMS should allow clearinghouses to serve as
intermediaries for payers, enabling compliant FHIR APIs that expose existing X12 transactions—such as
eligibility (270/271), claims (837), and prior authorization (278)—through modern interfaces. This would give
patients, providers, and other payers real-time access to actionable information without requiring each payer
to build and maintain standalone systems.
PA-5. What are ways payers can help with simplifying clinical quality data responsibilities of providers?
Payers can simplify clinical quality data responsibilities by leveraging data already exchanged through
healthcare clearinghouses. Clearinghouses process standardized administrative transactions—such as
claims (837), remittance advice (835), and eligibility (270/271)—that contain much of the information needed
for quality measurement. Rather than requiring separate chart extractions or manual submissions, payers can
10

use this existing data to calculate metrics related to preventive screenings, chronic disease management, and
medication adherence. For example, a mammogram billed on an 837 claim or a denial reason coded on an 835
can indicate whether a quality measure was met or missed. Payers can use these insights to pre-populate
reporting dashboards or notify providers of open gaps without requiring additional provider input. This reduces
documentation burden while maintaining accuracy and timeliness. By integrating clearinghouse data into
quality reporting workflows and aligning those measures with CMS programs, payers can create a streamlined,
low-friction process that supports providers without adding new requirements. This approach not only lowers
cost and complexity but also promotes consistent, real-time quality improvement across the healthcare
system.
a) How interested are payers and providers in EHR technology advances that enable bulk extraction
of clinical quality data from EHRs to payers to allow them to do the calculations instead of the
provider-side technology?
Payers recognize that combining bulk clinical data with claims, remittance, and eligibility data already
processed through clearinghouses creates a more complete and reliable dataset for quality measurement.
This hybrid approach allows payers to calculate metrics without placing additional technology or compliance
burdens on providers.
b) In what ways can the interoperability and quality reporting responsibilities of providers to both
CMS and other payers be consolidated so investments can be dually purposed? Are there

Interoperability and quality reporting responsibilities can be consolidated by reusing data already exchanged
through clearinghouses to satisfy requirements across both CMS and commercial payers. Providers already
submit eligibility (270/271), claims (837), and remittance (835) data that contain key quality indicators—such

FHIR-based clinical data from EHRs, this creates a unified view of quality performance that supports both care
delivery and reporting.

3. Technical Standards and Certification
TD-4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs over
proprietary APIs?
CMS can accelerate adoption of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs by expanding the role of
healthcare clearinghouses as API infrastructure providers. Clearinghouses already operate at national scale,
exchanging X12 transactions—such as eligibility (270/271), claims (837), remittance (835), and prior
authorization (278)—across nearly all payers and providers. This uniquely positions them to serve as the
operational backbone for exposing these data sets via FHIR-based APIs. To encourage this shift, CMS should
formally recognize clearinghouses as trusted API intermediaries that can transform existing HIPAA-standard
transactions into patient- and provider-facing APIs. This approach avoids duplicating infrastructure, reduces
compliance complexity for smaller entities, and ensures that API adoption is grounded in workflows already in
widespread use.

USCDI has improved interoperability by standardizing a core set of clinical data elements for exchange, helping
align EHR systems around a common structure. It has enabled more consistent clinical data sharing through
FHIR APIs and TEFCA participation. However, USCDI remains limited in scope for administrative and

TD-9. Regarding certification of health IT:
Reimagining and expanding the role of clearinghouses offers CMS a rapid, scalable path to advancing health IT
certification goals without requiring a complete reinvention of infrastructure. Clearinghouses already serve as
trusted intermediaries under HIPAA, processing and standardizing transactions across all payers and
providers. By building upon this existing foundation, CMS and ONC can accelerate interoperability and data

a) What are the benefits of redefining certification to prioritize API-enabled capabilities over
software functionality?
Redefining certification to prioritize API-enabled capabilities over static software functionality allows CMS to
drive real-time, standards-based data exchange. Clearinghouses can serve as backend API hubs, exposing
eligibility (270/271), claims (837), remittance (835), and prior authorization (278) data through secure, FHIR-

already operating at national scale.
b) What would be the drawbacks?
The primary drawback is that many EHR vendors and small providers may not have direct API capabilities for
administrative data or unstructured content. Without leveraging clearinghouses as intermediaries, this shift
could disproportionately burden providers with limited IT resources.
c) How could ASTP/ONC revise health IT certification criteria to require APIs to consistently support

ASTP/ONC could revise certification criteria to require APIs that support exchange of all patient-relevant data—
including scanned documents, PDFs, free-text notes, and structured fields—by encouraging hybrid models.
Clearinghouses could act as data routers or converters, translating these inputs into standard payloads for
downstream use, minimizing provider burden.

CMS can motivate providers to respond to API-based data requests by aligning reimbursement and quality

responding to payer or patient data requests via certified APIs. CMS should also permit clearinghouses to
mediate these exchanges, allowing providers to fulfill data requests through existing workflows and

EHRs capable of bulk data transfer—especially when paired with clearinghouse administrative feeds—can

measures centrally, and return actionable performance insights. To demonstrate value, capabilities should

their data is being interpreted—challenges that clearinghouses are well-equipped to manage securely and at

4. Data Exchange
TD-12. Should CMS endorse non-CMS data sources and networks, and if so, what criteria or metrics
should CMS consider?
Yes, CMS should endorse non-CMS data sources and networks—especially healthcare clearinghouses—
because they already facilitate trusted, bi-directional data exchange between all major payers and providers
across the U.S. healthcare system. Endorsing clearinghouses would allow CMS to rapidly scale access to high-
value administrative data and complement its clinical data initiatives under TEFCA. To ensure quality and
alignment with CMS objectives, the following criteria and metrics should guide endorsement: Standards
compliance: The network must support HIPAA-mandated X12 transactions (270/271, 837, 835, 278, etc.)
and/or FHIR-based APIs aligned with CMS interoperability rules. National payer and provider coverage:
Endorsed networks should demonstrate broad connectivity across Medicare, Medicaid, commercial plans,
and provider organizations to ensure completeness and utility of exchanged data. Data accuracy and
timeliness: The network must provide validated, real-time or near-real-time access to administrative data, such
as eligibility verification, claim status, and prior authorization outcomes. Security and privacy compliance:
Networks must meet HIPAA security standards and offer mechanisms for identity verification, patient consent,
and auditability.
TD-13. What new opportunities and advancements could emerge with APIs providing access to the
entirety of a patient's electronic health information (EHI)?
Clearinghouses, which already manage comprehensive administrative datasets across all payers, are ideally
positioned to help operationalize full-EHI access by combining their existing transaction streams with
expanding API capabilities. New opportunities include: Real-time cost and coverage visibility: By integrating
claims (837), remittance (835), and eligibility (270/271) data into EHI-accessible APIs, patients and providers
could receive accurate, up-to-date information on coverage, patient responsibility, and benefit limits—
enabling smarter care decisions and reducing surprise billing. Seamless prior authorization workflows: Access
to 278 prior authorization data through standardized APIs would allow digital health tools and EHRs to
automate authorization tracking, reducing delays and provider burden. Integrated quality measurement and
care gap alerts: When combined with clinical data from EHRs, clearinghouse data can support automated
quality reporting and real-time notifications for open care gaps or missed diagnoses, especially in value-based
care settings. Cross-payer, longitudinal health records: Clearinghouse-enabled APIs can unify data across
Medicare, Medicaid, and commercial plans, giving patients and care teams a holistic view of the care journey
14

regardless of plan transitions. Better data for analytics and equity: Full-EHI APIs that include administrative
detail can improve population health analytics, social risk adjustment, and benchmarking efforts by enabling
richer, multi-dimensional datasets.
a) What are the primary obstacles to this?
Data fragmentation: Clinical data is often siloed across EHR systems, while administrative data resides with
payers and clearinghouses. Without unified APIs or shared governance, accessing a complete EHI requires
stitching together disparate sources.
Lack of infrastructure integration: EHR vendors typically do not expose administrative data through their APIs,
and many API certification efforts exclude claims, eligibility, and prior auth—despite their availability through
clearinghouses.
Inconsistent standards: Clinical data flows through FHIR and C-CDA, while administrative data follows X12
formats. Without reliable mapping between these standards, full EHI APIs face implementation friction.
Privacy and authorization complexity: Managing patient consent across multiple data sources, especially for
sensitive or longitudinal data, presents logistical and regulatory challenges that clearinghouses are well-
equipped to help manage but are not yet fully empowered to address under current API frameworks.
b) What are the primary tradeoffs between USCDI and full EHI, especially given more flexible data
processing capabilities today?
USCDI strengths: It provides a consistent, structured clinical dataset with known semantics and broad EHR
vendor support, making it well-suited for interoperability and initial data access use cases.
USCDI limitations: It excludes critical administrative data—such as financial responsibility, prior auth status,
and payer attribution—that are essential to patient decision-making and care management. It also omits
unstructured content such as scanned documents or attachments used in adjudication workflows.
Full EHI value: Full EHI access, particularly when enriched with clearinghouse-supported administrative data,
enables richer analytics, end-to-end care visibility, and more meaningful support for value-based care. It also
allows the inclusion of real-world data inputs (e.g., prior denials, claims timing, or eligibility events) that USCDI
alone does not capture.
Tradeoff: While full EHI may require more sophisticated data processing (e.g., parsing PDFs, mapping X12 to
FHIR), the gain in completeness and actionable insight outweighs the complexity—especially given recent
advances in language models and natural language processing. To address both challenges and tradeoffs, CMS
should support hybrid models that use USCDI as a foundation and expand full EHI APIs with clearinghouse-
enabled administrative datasets for a truly comprehensive health information view.

TD-16. What are the tradeoffs of maintaining point-to-point models vs. shared network infrastructure?
Maintaining point-to-point data exchange models creates significant inefficiencies, fragmentation, and
duplication across the healthcare system. Each payer, provider, and vendor must manage separate
connections, formats, testing, and maintenance—resulting in high costs, slow onboarding, and inconsistent
data exchange. In contrast, the existing clearinghouse infrastructure offers a proven, scalable shared network
model that connects virtually all payers and providers under a unified framework based on HIPAA-standard
transactions. Clearinghouses have already established trusted, bi-directional connections with thousands of
trading partners. They manage enrollment, routing, validation, and compliance across eligibility (270/271),
claims (837), remittance (835), prior authorization (278), and more—enabling seamless, secure
interoperability without the need for duplicative point-to-point integrations.
a) Do current rules encourage scalable network participation?
Current rules, while supportive of open APIs, still allow and often necessitate siloed, proprietary connections—
particularly for prior authorization and payer-to-provider communication. This discourages shared network
participation and creates fragmentation. There is no mandate to leverage existing clearinghouse networks for
API enablement, which overlooks their infrastructure readiness and broad connectivity.

TD-19. Regarding price transparency implementation:
Price transparency remains limited by inconsistent data formats, delayed delivery, and lack of integration into
real-time workflows. Most provider-facing tools rely on payer portals or static machine-readable files that are
difficult to interpret and not actionable at the point of care. As a result, price estimates are often unavailable

Current shortcomings include lack of patient-specific estimates, fragmented delivery through non-standard

for delivering expected costs tied to benefits, service codes, or network status.
