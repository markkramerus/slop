This is especially true for older adults, individuals with chronic conditions, or those managing care for a loved one. Patients and caregivers would benefit from tools that provide proactive, contextual guidance. Many existing health management applications fall short because they assume users already understand their healthcare, their benefits, and what steps to take. These tools often miss the real-world needs of older adults, caregivers, and people with complex or changing health situations. They also tend to ignore the barriers that many CMS beneficiaries face such as low digital literacy, chronic illness, multiple providers, or limited support. a. What applications should exist but do not yet? Why do you believe they do not exist yet? 1. Integrated Care + Coverage View Most tools separate medical care from health benefits. For example, someone might know they have diabetes, but not whether their Medicare Advantage plan covers a new glucose monitor or what paperwork is needed to get it. What’s missing is a tool that shows users: • What condition(s) they have • What services are covered • What’s recommended based on current guidelines • What action(s) to take today This doesn’t exist because of data silos. Providers control clinical data. Plans manage coverage data. CMS holds claims. No tool today can bridge all three without full policy and data support from CMS. 2. Shared Navigation for Families Families rarely have a single person managing care. It’s often a combination where one person handles appointments, another handles insurance, and a third provides in-home help. Tools that allow this kind of shared care management, with secure delegation and different views for different roles, are still missing. We believe they don’t exist at scale because HIPAA compliance, state-by-state laws, and Medicare system complexity create real integration challenges. CMS is in a position to standardize this.

PC-5 What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in these digital health products? CMS and its partners could increase patient and caregiver interest in digital health products by focusing less on features and more on trust, simplicity, and relevance. Right now, many Medicare beneficiaries either don’t know these tools exist, don’t understand how to use them, or don’t believe they’re worth the effort. Caregivers often rely on paper, phone calls, and trial-and- error because they don’t have time to explore digital alternatives that feel complex or risky. This isn’t a technology problem. It’s a trust and usability problem. CMS is in a unique position to solve it. How CMS and Its Partners Could Encourage Adoption: 1. Endorse, Don’t Just List CMS should publicly endorse a small set of tools that meet defined standards for usability, privacy, and accessibility. These tools should be promoted through mailers, enrollment packets, provider portals, and call centers. People trust CMS. That trust doesn’t carry over to a random app store.

PC-6 What features are most important to make digital health products accessible and easy to use for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers, particularly those with limited digital experience? For Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers, especially those with limited digital experience, the most important features in any digital health product are clarity, simplicity, and support. If a tool is hard to understand, confusing to navigate, or filled with jargon, people won’t use it, no matter how powerful it is. We’ve seen this firsthand across systems serving older adults, rural populations, and caregivers juggling multiple responsibilities. When tools fail, it's rarely because of bad technology. It's because users don’t trust the tool, can’t figure out what it’s for, or feel overwhelmed by it. Critical Features for Accessibility and Ease of Use: 1. Plain Language Everywhere Health tools often assume users understand clinical or insurance terms. Most don’t. Interfaces must use everyday language, avoid abbreviations, and explain terms with tooltips, examples, or short videos. We’ve applied this in our DHA and IHS work, where users include military families, elders, and non-native English speakers. A health record isn’t useful if people don’t understand what it says. 2. Guided Flows with One Question at a Time Instead of large forms or busy dashboards, show users one simple question or task per screen. Let them move at their own pace, with clear back buttons, save options, and help prompts. This approach has worked well in our user interface designs for caregiver tools and benefit applications. It reduces cognitive overload and helps people stay focused. 3. Audio and Visual Support Allow users to click a button and hear text read aloud. Include closed captioning and visual cues (like icons or symbols) to support users with hearing, vision, or cognitive differences. These features are standard in tools we’ve delivered to meet Section 508 and WCAG guidelines for federal health clients. We could help CMS bake this into baseline requirements for any approved tool. 4. Multilingual Access Many Medicare users prefer to engage in languages other than English. CMS should require that digital tools support key languages based on regional demographics. We’ve supported multilingual interfaces in tribal and public health systems, using open-source language frameworks and culturally adapted terminology.
