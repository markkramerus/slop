RE: CMS-0042-NC – Request for Information: Health Technology Ecosystem
Dear Administrator Oz and Assistant Secretary Keane:
On behalf of the Healthcare Leadership Council (HLC), we appreciate the opportunity to respond to
the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) and the Assistant Secretary for Technology
Policy (ASTP)/Office of the National Coordinator for Health IT (ONC) Request for Information (RFI)
on the Health Technology Ecosystem. We stand ready to work with CMS and ASTP/ONC in efforts
to modernize digital infrastructure, strengthen interoperability, and empower beneficiaries with
timely, actionable health information.
HLC is an association of CEOs and C-suite executives from all sectors of healthcare working to
shape the future of the U.S. healthcare system. HLC is the exclusive forum for the nation’s
healthcare industry leaders to lead on major, sector-wide issues, generate innovative solutions to
unleash private sector ingenuity, and advocate for policies to improve our nation’s healthcare
delivery system. Members of HLC – hospitals, academic health centers, health plans,
pharmaceutical companies, medical device manufacturers, laboratories, biotech firms, health
product distributors/ wholesalers, post-acute care providers, homecare providers, group purchasing
organizations, and information technology companies – advocate for measures to increase the
quality and efficiency of healthcare through a patient-centered approach.
Given our diverse membership which traverses the country’s health ecosystem, HLC offer
recommendations to empower patients and caregivers; improve provider workflows; bolster
nationwide data exchange; enhance enrollment and eligibility systems; advance value-based care;
and ensure innovation.
1. Empowering Patients and Caregivers Through Interoperability,
Access, and Usability
Patients and caregivers should be able to access their health records in a seamless and reasonably
timely manner— including clinical, claims, coverage, imaging, and prescription information. Too
often, this data is fragmented across systems and difficult to obtain, limiting their ability to be truly
empowered in addressing their unique healthcare needs. We encourage CMS to:

• Support development of standards to advance options for timely movement of cost
data via patient-facing application programing interfaces (APIs), including deductible status,
formulary alternatives, and prior authorization requirements.
• Expand the scope of Patient Access APIs to align with USCDI progression and eventually
include imaging, genomic data, and cancer care information.
• Encourage the use of digital identity credentials (e.g., ID.me, Login.gov) for efficient,
secure access and enrollment validation across federal and state systems.
CMS should support private-sector app usability and accessibility for Medicare beneficiaries and
caregivers, incorporating feedback loops to inform continuous improvement.
2. Improving Provider Workflows and Electronic Prior Authorization
Clinicians face significant friction from administrative burdens, especially surrounding prior
authorization and data gaps. Opportunities exist to meaningfully improve clinical and administrative
processes that will help empower providers to deliver high-quality care in a timely and
comprehensive fashion. To address this, the Administration should consider levers to:
• Accelerate electronic prior authorization (ePA) adoption by ensuring certified health IT
supports the Prior Authorization API and incentivizing provider participation.
• Embed third-party tools into electronic health records using standardized and secure
technologies to deliver real-time benefit and pricing information, medication alternatives, and
stock availability.
• Support appropriate data access across care teams, including pharmacists and care
coordinators, to avoid duplicative services, unnecessary gaps in health histories, and
delayed treatment.
3. Bolstering Nationwide Data Exchange
The success of nationwide data exchange (whether through the Trusted Exchange Framework and
Common Agreement (TEFCA) or another similar model) hinges on broad, trusted participation
across providers, payers, and networks. To support its evolution, CMS and ASTP/ONC should:
• Expand permitted uses to include research and real-world evidence generation,
including patient-directed sharing.
• Ensure TEFCA captures a broad set of clinically relevant inputs, including medication
history and clinical indications, with pharmacist access enabled via Qualified Health
Information Networks (QHINs) and applicable standards.
• Clarify governance roles and data responsibilities for QHINs and participants, especially
related to security breaches and third-party vetting.
4. Enhancing Enrollment and Eligibility Systems for Coverage Access
Millions remain eligible but unenrolled in coverage due to data silos and inefficient exchange
systems. CMS should:

• Support use of the National Directory of New Hires (NDNH) and other independent data
sources to streamline eligibility and reduce consumer burden.
• Modernize account transfer protocols between Medicaid, CHIP, and Marketplace
systems, replacing outdated XML-based formats with more reliable, standardized options.
• Use prior-year income for ACA premium tax credit eligibility to reflect actual
circumstances and reduce error rates in projections.
5. Advancing Value-Based Care with Digital Tools
To drive adoption of digital health technologies in accountable care models:
• Provide bonus payments and shared savings incentives tied to digital engagement
metrics and tool adoption.
• Allow digital investments to count toward risk-bearing thresholds and incorporate them
into quality measurement.
• Enable Accountable Care Organizations to perform real-time eligibility checks and use
APIs for beneficiary alignment, including home-based care relationships.
6. Ensuring Secure, Standards-Based Innovation
Finally, as interoperability and access expand, it is critical to:
• Strengthen patient protections for third-party apps not covered under HIPAA, particularly
regarding data privacy and consent.
• Phase in new USCDI versions and certification requirements in a stepwise, resource-
sensitive approach.
• Advance a dynamic and interoperable national provider directory using FHIR and
UDAP standards to simplify digital identity validation and data exchange.
We commend CMS and ASTP/ONC for advancing a vision of a connected, equitable digital health
system and urge you to continue prioritizing policies that reduce burden, improve care coordination,
and deliver timely value to patients and providers alike. If you have any questions, please do not
