PR-1. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage providers, including those in rural areas, to
leverage approved (see description in PC-5) digital health products for their patients?

To promote provider uptake—particularly in rural regions—CMS can:

* Increase Funding and Policies to enhance Rural Broadband and Cloud Infrastructure:

Dependable internet connectivity is essential for digital health. The presence and consistency of internet
access continues to be a significant obstacle in rural healthcare. CMS can serve a catalytic function by
enhancing support and funding for broadband infrastructure, especially via collaborations with telecom
companies and federal broadband projects. Cloud-based systems are vital in rural regions with limited
access to IT experts required for maintaining and supporting traditional on-premises systems. This
connectivity enables more dependable, updated cloud-based EHRs that are safeguarded by the latest
cybersecurity measures. Moreover, this secure framework will enable EHRs to interact, facilitating health
record portability that enhances care and reduces the chance of overlooked information. Moreover, the
wireless infrastructure will enable remote clinics and home care through wireless monitoring devices that
enhance wellness and signal early indicators of illness, fostering proactive intervention to prevent
expensive health crises. These tools will create the foundation for patients to retrieve their data and
enhance transparency in their care. By collaborating, guaranteeing affordable and strong internet and
cloud access "levels" the digital divide and enables modern digital health products to be viable in rural
practice environments.

¢ Link the use of authorized tools to financial incentives and value-driven care. CMS needs to
incorporate the use of digital health into the payment and quality incentive framework. For instance, CMS
can directly incentivize providers for utilizing certified digital health solutions (virtual care, remote
monitoring, Al-assisted decision tools, patient engagement applications, etc.) that enhance care and
overall efficiency. Additionally, new billing codes and reimbursement structures can be established for
the time invested in utilizing digital therapies or analyzing remote monitoring information. When enhanced
patient outcomes from digital tools generate savings, providers ought to partake in those savings, thus
making adoption financially appealing. Enhanced adoption speed might be realized through initial
transformation funds or incentive payments for introducing remote monitoring in rural primary healthcare.

¢ Create Collaborative Public-Private Partnerships for Expandable Solutions. Collaboration
between the public and private sectors can speed up innovation and lessen the pressure on individual
providers. CMS can collaborate with tech firms and healthcare suppliers to offer secure, interoperable
systems that rural providers may struggle to create or implement independently. An excellent instance is
the Microsoft Cloud for Healthcare platform, which combines EHR data, remote monitoring, tools for care
coordination, and analytics on a fully compliant cloud infrastructure. Collaborating with reliable industry
partners allows CMS to implement pre-made solutions—developed on standards such as HL7® FHIR@—
that integrate into current rural hospital processes. These partnerships would fulfill multiple needs
simultaneously: improved care, reduced provider burnout, strengthened data interoperability, and
enhanced cybersecurity. Such collaboration might establish a national framework for data exchange and
remote patient monitoring with reduced barriers. CMS ought to function as a facilitator and stimulus,
uniting top industry talent with rural providers to ensure that advanced digital solutions are implemented
and backed by specialists, rather than allowing each clinic to independently develop solutions that cannot
operate on the same interoperable framework.

a. What are the current obstacles?

¢ Insufficient Technical Infrastructure in Rural Areas: Rural areas frequently do not have the IT
personnel and basic infrastructure—especially broadband internet and cloud services—that are crucial
for implementing digital health solutions. Even with telehealth solutions in place, their effectiveness is
limited by weak connectivity, old equipment, and insufficient IT support. This digital gap not only restricts
patient access to online care but also hinders providers’ capacity to utilize insights, incorporate the tools,
and sustain the technologies.

¢e Disconnected EHR Systems and Poor Interoperability: A significant challenge continues to be the
absence of interoperability among electronic health record (EHR) systems. Information saved in various
formats—like scanned files, unstructured notes, and organized data fields—tends to be unreachable
between systems. This fragmentation hinders the smooth sharing of patient information, diminishing the
capability of digital health tools to facilitate coordinated, value-driven care.

¢ Concerns from providers regarding liability, data privacy, and reimbursement: Providers are
cautious about the legal and financial implications tied to the adoption of digital health. Issues encompass
ambiguous responsibility when suggesting digital tools, possible violations of patient confidentiality, and
varying reimbursement policies. CMS could enhance its role in defining provider responsibilities and
creating standardized reimbursement models to lessen ambiguity. These issues are particularly
prominent in rural areas, where providers might not have legal or administrative assistance to manage
these complexities.

¢ Training Opportunities: The absence of organized training initiatives for digital health tools and data
literacy hinders the confidence of healthcare system providers and patients, consequently affecting
adoption. Numerous rural providers function in environments with limited resources where time and
financial support for professional growth are in short supply. The RFI indicates that CMS and its
collaborators might consider investing in scalable, role-oriented training modules to enhance digital
fluency and data literacy among healthcare providers and support personnel. Peer-to-peer learning
communities and online training centers might also aid in closing this gap.

¢ Overburdened Personnel: Staffing deficiencies and fatigue are widespread in rural healthcare. Rural
providers frequently find themselves overextended, managing various responsibilities with minimal
assistance. Implementing new digital tools—even if they are advantageous—can seem more like an extra
weight instead of a remedy. In the absence of focused implementation assistance or adjustments to
workflows, digital health projects may be sidelined or scrapped.

¢ Incorporating into workflows: Even with digital tools accessible, incorporating them into current
clinical workflows poses a significant challenge. Numerous digital health products lack a focus on usability
for frontline workers. Providers require tools that match their current needs, workflows, documentation,

their care?

¢ Objectives and advantages of the digital product: Suppliers must clearly articulate the purpose of
any digital health tool, detailing how it aids the patient's care pathway. A care navigation application could
assist patients in finding specialists or booking appointments, whereas a health management application

conveniences—they are intended to enhance results, decrease hospital admissions, and enable patients
to engage more actively in their health. In countryside regions, where in-person healthcare may be
scarce, digital solutions can fill essential voids by facilitating remote consultations, ongoing monitoring,

¢ Safeguards for data privacy and security: Patients should be made aware of how their information
is safeguarded. This encompasses detailing encryption standards, protocols for identity verification, and
adherence to HIPAA, FISMA, and various federal regulations. For example, CMS promotes utilizing
FHIR-based APIs and identity verification partners to guarantee secure data sharing. Providers must also
specify if the data is anonymized, pseudonymized, or fully identifiable, and the conditions under which it
may be shared with external parties. Openness fosters trust—particularly in marginalized communities
where doubts regarding digital monitoring might be greater.

isolated tools but as integral parts of a patient's comprehensive care strategy. Providers must clarify how

straight to the EHR, enabling care teams to proactively modify medications or arrange follow-up
appointments. This integration is particularly beneficial for overseeing chronic conditions such as
diabetes or hypertension, where ongoing monitoring can avert complications.

e Usage guidelines and support materials: Simple, easy-to-understand instructions are crucial—
particularly for patients with minimal digital skills. Providers ought to provide detailed guides, instructional
videos, and support in multiple languages when feasible. Patients should be informed about how to

where broadband availability might be restricted, providers must guarantee that products operate offline
or with minimal bandwidth, and that alternative support options (such as phone-based coaching) are
accessible.

¢ Advantages: Service providers must clearly communicate the concrete advantages of utilizing the
digital product. This could involve reduced in-person appointments, quicker access to treatment,
enhanced medication compliance, and better health results. For instance, CMS has prolonged telehealth
options until September 2025, enabling patients to obtain care from home without location limitations.
Emphasizing these advantages can enhance patient involvement and acceptance, especially when
presented in the context of convenience, safety, and empowerment.

PR-3. How important is it for healthcare delivery and interoperability in urban and rural areas that all data
in an EHR system be accessible for exchange, regardless of storage format (for example, scanned
documents, faxed records, lab results, free text notes, structured data fields)? Please address all of the
following:

In urban and rural healthcare environments, the capability to securely share all types of data in an
Electronic Health Record (EHR) system—regardless of being structured or unstructured—is essential not
merely as a technical goal but as a fundamental necessity for compliant, equitable, efficient, and high-
quality care provision. The availability of all data formats in EHR systems is not a privilege—it is essential

Scheduling of Patient Attribution Models

As previously mentioned, patient attribution refers to the allocation of patients to a particular healthcare
provider, who then assumes responsibility for their care. The assignment of patients to particular provider
entities arises from the demand for responsibility regarding the quality and cost of care in value-based
healthcare payment models. Over 170 attribution models exist, with at least 30 actively in use. The large
variety of attribution models being created stems from the difficulty of establishing a singular, effective
system for calculating payments, monitoring care coordination, and reporting patient results. Every
category of model yields a distinct output depending on the sources of data utilized. Nevertheless, the
primary goal across all models is to ensure precise attribution to improve population health management
and mitigate risk for providers. Without an appropriate attribution model, healthcare providers face
difficulties in recognizing their patients or assigning adequate resources to address their conditions,
resulting in inefficiencies within the healthcare system.

Patient Assignment Methodology

Different factors are employed to assign patients to a particular provider, each having its own benefits
and drawbacks. The three typical attribution methods include geographical, plurality of care, and patient
self-reported attribution. With geographical attribution, patients are allocated to particular facilities
depending on how close they live. Information like zip code is utilized to match patients with a nearby
provider. A significant drawback of this method is that it does not consider the number of times each
patient visits a particular healthcare facility. The visit-based attribution faces no such difficulty as it
employs algorithms to allocate patients to providers using claims data. The provider that filed the most
claims for a specific patient during a designated timeframe is designated as that patient's primary care
provider, which is a standard practice for many government-funded healthcare programs. Although visit-
based attribution relies on data and is simple, its success in correctly linking patients to providers greatly
relies on the quality of data present at the provider level. A frequent assumption in the model is that the
provider with the highest number of claims is the primary provider, which might not be correct. For
example, a specialist treating a chronic illness might file more claims than the patient's primary care
doctor, resulting in misattribution. Additionally, the retrospective approach is utilized in the majority of
attribution methods, resulting in a delay in acknowledging provider-patient connections.

The third attribution method is self-reporting by patients, which is optimal. In this method, the patient
directly chooses or verifies their main healthcare provider. Although this approach effectively allocates
patients to providers, it depends on the patient's willingness to self-report and their comprehension of the
different providers. A patient might not recognize the best provider for their primary health needs, resulting
in a disconnect with the objectives of value-based care. Furthermore, personal choices can fluctuate
frequently because of individual tastes or relocation, requiring ongoing updates. Consequently, each of
the three patient attribution methods has constraints that could impede their effectiveness in creating an
appropriate model for linking patients to providers.

Attribution of Data Models in Alternative Payment Frameworks

Attribution is used in two main alternative payment models to link or assign patients to providers: the
episode-based and patient-based payment models. In the episode-based payment approach, one
payment is utilized to cover multiple predefined services delivered over a specific timeframe (Society of
Actuaries, 2018). This method is typical in maternal care, which is categorized as a set of specified
services. The provider is accountable for delivering all services in every episode within a designated
timeframe. In contrast, the patient-centered payment model focuses on the services provided to an
individual patient. Compensation is given to a provider for all services rendered within a specified
timeframe. ACOs generally adopt this method, receiving one payment to manage care delivery for every
patient. The data-model attribution is important in both payment models as it establishes the foundation
for allocating profits or charges to the provider organizations. Standardized metrics serve to allocate
responsibility to the care providers and then create the formula for determining gains and fines to be
distributed or imposed, respectively. Providers typically concentrate on the data model employed in these
processes to guide their choices regarding enrollment in an attribution program.
