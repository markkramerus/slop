To build a people-centered , technology-driven healthcare system, individual beneﬁciaries must also have access to interoperability. While CMS continues to build the critical infrastructure for interoperability - such as provider directories, veriﬁcation processes, Blue Button 2.0 expansion, Point of Care enhancements, and improved data exchange – additional eﬀorts are necessary to promote Medicare beneﬁciaries’ access to clear, personalized pathways to engage with their health and these tools. This is where Wellth plays a vital role: bridging the gap between the infrastructure CMS is facilitating and the individuals most in need of engagement. By meeting people where they are, with thoughtful behavioral design, aligned incentives, and trusted daily connections - Wellth ensures that even the most disengaged populations are supported, connected, and empowered to improve their health. Working in partnership with Managed Care Organizations—including national payers, integrated delivery networks, and local health plans, Wellth uses claims and encounter data to identify and engage individuals most likely to beneﬁt from personalized digital support. Over the past decade, our users have completed more than 40 million daily check-ins —real actions that build healthier habits and improve quality of life. Many of our most engaged users are in their 80s and 90s, with some even over 100 years old—demonstrating that digital tools, when thoughtfully designed, can successfully engage people across age groups and comfort levels with technology. This consistent engagement translates into meaningful results. Wellth users demonstrate an average of 90% daily adherence to care plans —even among those previously considered non-adherent. Ultimately, our payer partners report a 2–4x return on investment within the ﬁrst year, driven by cost-of-care reductions, improved quality scores, and more engaged, healthier members. By lowering barriers to adherence and empowering individuals with daily, personalized support, Wellth advances the goals of value-based care—delivering better outcomes, greater eﬃciency, and improved quality of life for those who need it most. However, the Wellth app has the potential to serve a much broader segment of the Medicare population, particularly individuals who struggle to consistently manage their health and navigate the healthcare system. With a modest shift in implementation approach, Wellth’s personalized, behaviorally-driven app could support a wider range of CMS priorities. For example, if CMS aimed to drive speciﬁc member actions—such as completing wellness visits, closing preventive care gaps, or using supplemental beneﬁts—Wellth’s adaptive app experience could be conﬁgured and eﬀectively leveraged to meet those goals. 3 
 The app fosters a daily, high-touch relationship with users, creating trust and sustained engagement that CMS could leverage to deepen its connection with beneﬁciaries and plug them into the infrastructure CMS is building. Ultimately, Wellth’s technology could support CMS in advancing person-centered care by increasing beneﬁciary engagement, and improving quality measures across Medicare programs—not just for the highest-risk groups, but at scale. 4

Most engagement eﬀorts and healthcare apps today lack the ability to drive consistent, long-term behavior change—especially among individuals with chronic conditions and complex needs. While many solutions aim to support health management, they often fall short in bridging the gap between intent and action on a daily basis and sustained over time. In addition, many apps aiming to support chronic disease management tend to focus on singular tasks or conditions, rather than addressing the root cause—individual behaviors. Unlike consumer platforms—which successfully create daily habits through gamiﬁcation, personalization, and intrinsic motivation—many health apps rely on transactional engagement: reminders to schedule appointments, reﬁll medications, or complete forms. These touchpoints are often payer-driven and fail to resonate with individuals on a personal level to drive meaningful engagement. And most importantly, they do not create intrinsic motivation for beneﬁciaries to form better habits that improve their health management. Additionally, if the individual is required to pay for the solution, uptake is limited, especially for beneﬁciaries who are most at-risk. Critically, most apps: ● Lack daily habit formation features that are proven to drive sustained care plan adherence and self management. ● Do not personalize content or experiences based on the user’s actual conditions, behavior or motivation. ● Use inconsistent deﬁnitions of "engagement" and "retention," which makes it diﬃcult to compare impact across solutions. ● Are not well-suited to individuals facing social, behavioral, or cognitive barriers—those who arguably need support the most. To drive meaningful adoption and sustained use of digital health products among Medicare beneﬁciaries and their caregivers, CMS has the opportunity to remove barriers to accessing proven digital solutions by focusing on incentive alignment, trust-building, and relevance to individuals’ daily lives. While access to technology is important, proven engagement is the key diﬀerentiator—especially for individuals managing chronic conditions, facing social barriers, or historically disengaged from care. What works: Wellth’s experience demonstrates that when beneﬁciaries are directly incentivized to complete small daily health actions—such as taking medications, checking blood pressure, or completing preventive screenings—adoption is signiﬁcantly higher and long-term engagement is sustained. Over 80% of Wellth’s Medicare users remain active daily and adherent one year after enrollment, a benchmark rarely achieved in digital health. To encourage patient and caregiver engagement at scale, CMS could: ● Incentivize beneﬁciary participation in digital health programs that demonstrate behavior change and measurable outcomes—not just provider or health plan compliance. ● Encourage co-design of improvements to existing solutions with the populations they serve, ensuring cultural and usability relevance and expanded use cases. ● Promote caregiver-inclusive models that recognize and support the critical role caregivers play in day-to-day care management. ● Fund digital literacy and access programs that reduce technical barriers for older adults and underserved communities. Rather than a formal approval process—which may inadvertently slow innovation—CMS could establish a "CMS Validated" or "CMS Endorsed" designation for digital solutions that meet clearly deﬁned criteria aligned with Medicare’s goals. This endorsement could guide payers, providers, and beneﬁciaries toward products that have demonstrated eﬃcacy and interoperability, without adding regulatory burden or having to create unnecessary additional solutions. 7 
 Suggested CMS validation criteria might include: ● Proven impact on health outcomes, adherence, and/or cost reduction ● Demonstrated patient and caregiver engagement and satisfaction ● Support for prevention and chronic disease management ● Access considerations, including multilingual content, caregiver support, and non-smartphone accessibility ● Interoperability with EHRs and claims systems ● Compliance with privacy, HIPAA, and data governance standards CMS can increase adoption through: ● Reimbursement adjustments (e.g., direct support for patient-facing tools via existing or new CPT codes). Payers usually limit access to proven tools like Wellth to 12 months. Many individuals would beneﬁt from continued self-management with validated digital solutions. In fact, some payers have performed analyses of the cost impacts to removing their members from the Wellth program after 12 months. The result is that costs increase and members fall back into old habits. ● Inclusion of validated tools in chronic care management (CCM) or transitional care management (TCM) workﬂows. CMS could consider re-writing the justiﬁcation for these codes to allow providers to “prescribe” a proven self-management solution like Wellth to patients who struggle with managing their conditions or overall health. ● To track eﬃcacy, CMS might add a ﬂag on provider claims, enabling physicians to indicate when a patient is using a digital health management solution. Embedding this process into physician workﬂows and integrating it with EHR systems could remove barriers, improve access, and reduce friction in getting eﬀective self-management tools into the hands of beneﬁciaries who need them. ● Targeted communications to beneﬁciaries and caregivers, reinforcing how digital tools can support their personal health goals. ● Data sharing enhancements that allow digital tools to deliver more timely, personalized nudges and feedback to users. To unlock the full potential of digital health engagement, solutions must be able to access timely and accurate data to personalize outreach, identify care gaps, and trigger proactive interventions. CMS can support this by: ● Accelerating real-time access to claims, encounter, and care coordination data for trusted third-party partners serving beneﬁciaries ● Standardizing APIs and FHIR adoption to facilitate secure, patient-consented data ﬂow across systems ● Supporting data-sharing frameworks that allow care navigators and digital tools to receive timely updates on hospitalizations, medication changes, or new diagnoses ● Exploring caregiver consent models to allow authorized caregivers to access and act on behalf of patients within digital tools 8 
 To improve health outcomes for Medicare beneﬁciaries, CMS has a unique opportunity to support the adoption of digital health solutions that prioritize sustained engagement of patients and caregivers. By setting clear standards for quality, usability, and integration—without limiting innovation—and by improving access to timely data and appropriate reimbursement models, CMS can help scale eﬀective, evidence-based solutions like Wellth. These tools show that when individuals are motivated and supported in building daily healthy habits, the result is better health, stronger patient-provider relationships, and more eﬃcient use of healthcare resources. 9

 PC-7. If CMS were to collect real-world data on digital health products' impact on health outcomes and related costs once they are released into the market, what would be the best means of doing so? To eﬀectively collect real-world data on digital health products, CMS could establish a standardized, yet ﬂexible, post-market surveillance framework that integrates with existing healthcare data infrastructure. Key components would include: ● Partnerships with Health Plans and Providers: CMS could require or incentivize health plans and providers participating in federal programs (e.g., Medicare Advantage, Medicaid MCOs) to report on the use and outcomes of digital health tools. This would enable collection of data across diverse populations, care settings, and geographic regions. ● Integration with Claims and EHR Data: Leveraging CMS’s access to Medicare and Medicaid claims data and aligning it with EHRs and digital product data feeds (e.g., app engagement, task completion, biometric readings) would allow for a more complete picture of a product’s impact on clinical outcomes, utilization, and costs. ● Digital Health Product Registries: CMS could fund or endorse condition-speciﬁc registries for digital health products, where developers and payers submit structured, de-identiﬁed data on utilization, clinical metrics (e.g., blood pressure, A1c), engagement, and retention. This would facilitate comparative analyses and ongoing evaluation. ● Patient-Reported Outcomes and Surveys: Incorporating regular patient-reported outcomes (PROs) and experience surveys into data collection can provide critical qualitative context—especially important for measuring behavioral, mental health, or social outcomes often inﬂuenced by digital interventions. ● API and Interoperability Standards: Mandating adherence to common interoperability standards (e.g., FHIR APIs) for digital health products will make real-world data easier to aggregate and analyze across diﬀerent care systems and vendor platforms. ● Pilot Programs and Public-Private Collaborations: CMS could launch demonstration projects with digital health innovators to evaluate longitudinal outcomes under real-world conditions. These pilots would help reﬁne data collection protocols and validate outcomes-based reimbursement models. By taking a multi-pronged approach that combines quantitative claims and EHR data with qualitative patient-reported outcomes, CMS can build a robust evidence base for evaluating digital health innovations and supporting broader adoption of tools that demonstrably improve care and reduce costs. 11

Wellth's business model as it exists today is to partner with Payers (MCO's, at-risk provider groups etc.) to reach high-risk individuals within their membership to improve their health outcomes, one individual at a time. The method for reaching these beneﬁciaries is through a lengthy sales process with the payer that usually hinges upon cost and quality improvements Wellth can achieve through better engagement and habit transformation with these risky individuals. Upon contracting with the client (Payer), Wellth gains access to some or all of the following data sets, with the goal of identifying the most complex, hard to reach individuals which Wellth's solution will provide the largest impact to cost and quality for the payer. Wellth then performs all the outreach necessary to inform beneﬁciaries of the program, assists them in downloading and using the app, and keeps the support live throughout the care journey. This creates high levels of engagement that is sustained over time. We've found that the following data sets have provided the best chance at identifying the most impactful individuals to target for the Wellth program: ● Medical Claims ● Pharmacy Claims ● Member Base data (eligibility) ● Encounter Data ● PBM Data to further identify low adherence ● In some instances, Provider integrations can be achieved via EHR integration This business model, while challenging, costly and time consuming, provides the ability for beneﬁciaries to have the Wellth solution available to them at no cost, while also beneﬁting from the monetary reward structure of the app which creates intrinsic motivation over time. Often, clients (Payers) are either unwilling or unable to provide all data sources. Better data sharing capabilities and willingness would increase both the amount of individuals who could beneﬁt from this proven solution and the eﬀective monitoring of the impacts of the solution on total cost of care and quality improvements.  with our clients. However, national coordination of this data is sparse. Better integration, which CMS is clearly pushing for, would be helpful for more timely and accurate user experiences.  Providers Domain: PR-1. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage providers, including those in rural areas, to leverage approved (see description in PC-5) digital health products for their patients? A. What are the current obstacles? B. What information should providers share with patients when using digital products in the provision of their care? C. What responsibilities do providers have when recommending use of a digital product by a patient? In an ideal ecosystem, providers—including those in rural or under-resourced areas—would have a simple, supported way to refer patients managing chronic conditions to proven digital tools like Wellth. These tools can extend the provider’s reach and reinforce daily self-management behaviors through accessible, incentivized support. Current Obstacles: ● Unclear payment pathways: Providers often lack clarity on who pays for the tool—whether CMS, a payer, or the patient—and whether the tool is covered under existing beneﬁts. Since the Payer is the usual decision maker for allowing access to no-cost digital tools for patients, access is limited. A solution to this problem could be that CMS modify its deﬁnition for what justiﬁes billing for a chronic care management (CCM) code under the physician fee schedule to include the use of a proven digital health product like Wellth. ● No central listing or vetting process: Without a CMS-endorsed or validated list of approved digital tools aligned with speciﬁc conditions or goals, providers hesitate to refer patients due to uncertainty around quality and ﬁt. Again, the same challenge of the payer-provider-patient relationship makes access a challenge. ● Eligibility complexity: Providers need clear, simple criteria to know which patients qualify for proven solutions, ideally integrated into existing workﬂows (e.g., EHRs). What Providers Should Share with Patients: ● Cost transparency: Will the patient face any out-of-pocket cost? ● Purpose and expected beneﬁts: What does the solution do, and how can it help improve the patient's condition and life? ● User expectations: What tasks will the patient be expected to complete (e.g., daily check-ins)? ● Support access: Who can the patient contact for technical or clinical help? ● Cultural relevance: Is the solution in-language and capable of supporting diverse populations? 14 
 Provider Responsibilities: ● Right-ﬁt referrals: Providers should understand the solution’s general beneﬁts and ensure alignment with a patient’s speciﬁc needs and conditions. ● Expectation-setting: Providers should help patients understand the role of the digital tool in their care journey—including expected outcomes and what participation involves. ● Ongoing support: Providers don’t need to manage the digital product day-to-day but should remain aware of its role in the broader care plan. Recommendations for CMS: CMS could signiﬁcantly accelerate provider adoption by: ● Creating a vetted directory of a small number of validated digital tools aligned to speciﬁc use cases and goals. The directory should be limited, as too much “choice” can lead to confusion. ● Deﬁning standard eligibility and referral workﬂows, potentially integrated into EHRs. ● Establishing ﬂexible reimbursement models that support provider referrals without ﬁnancial burden on patients or administrative burden on providers. These steps would make it easier—and safer—for providers to engage patients in digital care pathways that extend beyond the clinic walls. 15 
 Value Based Care Domain: VB-1. What incentives could encourage APMs such as accountable care organizations (ACOs) or participants in Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) to leverage digital health management and care navigation products more often and more eﬀectively with their patients? What are the current obstacles preventing broader digital product adoption for patients in ACOs? For APMs and MSSP participants to adopt digital health management and care navigation tools more eﬀectively, two foundational questions must be addressed: 1. Which tools are truly eﬀective? 2. How are those tools sustainably paid for? Today, most incentive models focus on the payer–provider relationship, while the patient—the individual who must engage daily for outcomes to improve—is often left out. Digital tools like Wellth demonstrate that when individuals are directly supported and incentivized, sustained behavior change is achievable, even among hard-to-reach Medicare populations. However, the current digital health market is noisy and fragmented. Without clear standards, providers and APM participants are hesitant to adopt solutions due to uncertain ROI, operational complexity, and limited data-sharing infrastructure. Key Recommendations for CMS: ● Create a CMS-Endorsed Directory: Establish a certiﬁcation or endorsement process for digital tools that meet deﬁned standards (e.g., measurable impact on outcomes, integration capability, patient-centered design, behavioral engagement). ● Incentivize use in APMs: Oﬀer incentives for APM participants that deploy CMS-endorsed tools tied to speciﬁc use cases (e.g., chronic disease management, Prevention). These could be in the form of shared savings bonuses, upfront investment payments, or reduced reporting burdens. ● Standardize Eligibility and Data-Sharing Requirements: Deﬁne common eligibility criteria for digital tools and require payers in APM arrangements to share core datasets (claims, encounter, pharmacy) with solution providers under standard agreements. ● Center the Individual: Ensure that digital tool reimbursement models include incentives directly tied to beneﬁciary engagement and outcomes—not just provider behavior. 16 
 Conclusion: To unlock the full value of proven digital health tools for beneﬁciaries in APMs, CMS can play a catalytic role by clarifying what “works,” enabling data access, and aligning incentives around individual behavior change—ensuring the right solution reaches the right person, at the right time. With the right approach, CMS can improve access to proven digital tools that can both prevent chronic illness and the progression of chronic diseases.
