2.0 TECHNOLOGY VENDORS, DATA PROVIDERS, AND NETWORKS
ECOSYSTEM
TD 1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to stimulate
developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers?
To stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries and
caregivers, CMS should pursue a two-pronged approach that combines short-term and long-term actions
to build an ecosystem.
In the short term, CMS can significantly lower the barrier to entry for developers by expanding the scope
and accessibility of existing infrastructure. Enhancements to the Blue Button 2.0 API—such as
incorporating additional claims data from Medicare Advantage (Part C), durable medical equipment
(DME), lab results, and social determinants of health—would immediately improve the relevance and
utility of CMS data for app development. CMS should also invest in improving the developer experience
through a centralized developer portal that offers sandboxes, code samples, and testing tools, along with
clear technical documentation and a certification or validation pathway for third-party apps.
To further incentivize participation, CMS could launch time-limited accelerator programs targeted at
Medicare-specific problems, such as chronic disease management, medication adherence, or caregiver
support. These challenges should be designed to support early-stage and small-to-midsize developers,
offering funding and technical guidance through proof-of-concept, pilot testing, and implementation.
Additionally, CMS could recognize providers and payers that deploy these solutions through bonus points
in ACO or value-based purchasing programs, thereby creating downstream demand for new tools.
Long-term, CMS should establish the foundation for a sustainable digital health ecosystem focused on
Medicare beneficiaries. This includes building a trusted, CMS-endorsed digital health marketplace where
beneficiaries and caregivers can easily find, compare, and authorize applications that integrate with CMS
APIs. Such a platform should emphasize transparency, offering user ratings, privacy and security
indicators, and—where possible—evidence of clinical or financial impact.
CMS can encourage alignment with national standards and identity frameworks by promoting integration
with TEFCA and supporting federated digital identity solutions like Login.gov. This would reduce friction
for both patients and developers, standardizing access across providers and digital services. CMS should
also explore partnerships with developers through a formal CMS Developer Partner Program, which could
offer early access to test data, regulatory guidance, and participation in advisory councils to ensure that
future policy aligns with real-world developer needs.
By reducing technical barriers, creating financial incentives, and fostering a culture of open innovation,
CMS can continue to transform the Medicare landscape into a vibrant environment where developers are
motivated and empowered to create accessible, impactful tools for beneficiaries and caregivers alike.

TD 2 d. What obstacles prevent accessing these data sources today?
While Blue Button 2.0 has improved access to Medicare Fee-for-Service (FFS) claims, key datasets—such
as Medicare Advantage (Part C) encounters, prior authorizations, and prescription drug events—remain
largely inaccessible due to fragmented data ownership and lack of policy authority. These gaps limit
developer innovation and hinder a complete view of patient health.
Many valuable data sources are siloed within private plans, PBMs, or providers, beyond CMS’s direct
control. Even when technically available, these datasets often lack standardized, API-first infrastructure,
especially for lab results, imaging, and DME claims still housed in legacy systems.
The current framework also excludes Medicare Advantage and Part D enrollees, creating inconsistent
access across Medicare programs. Additional barriers include the absence of real-time, standardized
consent management for sensitive data like SDOH and behavioral health, and general industry reluctance
to participate due to risk concerns and limited incentives. Policy fragmentation, outdated systems, and
misaligned incentives continue to restrict meaningful data access. Addressing these issues will require
coordinated leadership, infrastructure modernization, and clearer participation requirements across the
health ecosystem.
DIGITAL IDENTITY
TD 3 a. Regarding digital identity implementation: What are the challenges and benefits?
The implementation of a robust digital identity framework at CMS presents both critical challenges and
transformative benefits. As CMS modernizes its infrastructure to support secure, interoperable, and user-
friendly digital services, managing digital identity becomes foundational to its success.
Challenges. One of the most pressing challenges lies in achieving interoperability across diverse systems—
including legacy platforms, cloud environments, and third-party partners. CMS must align identity
solutions across these disparate systems while maintaining compliance with federal standards such as
FISMA, HIPAA, and NIST 800-63. Ensuring secure identity proofing and authentication, especially for
populations with limited access to digital tools or government-issued credentials, further complicates
implementation. Additionally, as CMS serves millions of users—from beneficiaries and providers to
contractors and internal staff—scalable role-based access and identity lifecycle management are essential
but difficult to implement without creating friction or risk. The transition to a Zero Trust Architecture,
which depends heavily on continuous identity verification, also requires substantial investment in
automation, governance, and change management.
Benefits. The benefits of digital identity modernization are substantial. A centralized, standards-based
identity approach enhances CMS’ security posture by reducing the risk of credential misuse and
unauthorized access. By enabling capabilities like single sign-on (SSO), multi-factor authentication (MFA),
and federated identity, CMS can significantly improve the user experience across Medicare, Medicaid, and

TD 3 b. Regarding digital identity implementation: How would requiring digital identity credentials
(for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs) impact cybersecurity and
data exchange?
Digital identity credentials through NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2-compliant providers such as Login.gov or
ID.me would help to advance both CMS cybersecurity and data exchange. From a security standpoint, it
raises the bar for identity assurance, enabling CMS to better protect sensitive data and align with Zero
Trust principles. From a data exchange perspective, these verified identities enable more seamless,
secure, and privacy-preserving interactions across the healthcare ecosystem. This approach not only
bolsters CMS’ ability to safeguard public trust but also empowers beneficiaries, providers, and partners
with secure, streamlined access to the services and data they need.
Strengthened User Authentication. Implementing identity verification at IAL2 and requiring AAL2
authentication (e.g., multi-factor authentication) greatly reduces the risk of unauthorized access, account
takeovers, and phishing attacks. These CSPs verify identity through government-issued ID and biometric
or knowledge-based verification, making identity spoofing more difficult.
Support for Zero Trust Security. Digital identity credentials form a cornerstone of Zero Trust by enabling
continuous, risk-adaptive access validation and helping enforce least-privilege access policies across
systems and users.
Improved Incident Response and Forensics. Centralized identity providers generate high-fidelity logs that
improve traceability, auditing, and incident triage. This enables CMS to detect anomalies and respond to
threats more rapidly.
Reduced Credential Management Risk. By federating authentication through trusted CSPs, CMS reduces
the burden and security risks associated with managing passwords and local credential storage across
multiple systems.
Enhanced Trust Across Ecosystems. Credentialing users through IAL2/AAL2-compliant providers creates
a verifiable trust anchor for secure data exchange across CMS systems, providers, states, and third parties.
This fosters interoperability between systems (e.g., Blue Button 2.0, Health Information Exchanges) by
ensuring all actors meet the same digital identity assurance requirements.
Improved Data Privacy and Consent Management. With strong identity proofing, users can be more
confidently matched to their health records and can securely grant or revoke consent for data sharing, a
key enabler for patient-directed exchange.
Streamlined Onboarding and Cross-Agency Access. Federated digital identities reduce onboarding
friction for users (e.g., healthcare providers, researchers, contractors) by allowing them to access multiple
services using a single, trusted credential.
Improved Compliance Data Protection Compliance. Verified identities simplify compliance with privacy
and security mandates under HIPAA, FISMA, and the 21st Century Cures Act by ensuring that only
authorized users access sensitive data.

TECHNICAL STANDARDS AND CERTIFICATION
TD 4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs over
proprietary APIs?
Promoting open, standards-based APIs enables CMS to advance the goals of the 21st Century Cures Act
and ONC interoperability rules, while also supporting innovation, equity, and competition in digital health.
Proprietary APIs often limit data portability, increase vendor lock-in, and obstruct seamless integration
between systems. By prioritizing open standards like HL7 FHIR, SMART on FHIR, OAuth 2.0, and OpenID
Connect, CMS can create an ecosystem where healthcare data flows securely and consistently across
platforms and stakeholders.
To drive this transition, CMS can leverage its roles as both a regulator and a payer. Mandating open APIs
in federal contracts, tying program participation to standards compliance, and setting clear deadlines to
retire proprietary interfaces are critical policy levers. Providing developers with robust tools—such as
sandboxes, test data, and reference implementations—can ease adoption and foster real-world
innovation.
Incentives. CMS can encourage provider and plan participation by linking open API usage to quality ratings
or reimbursement models. Offering certification or validation tools for API conformance can further build
trust and reduce ambiguity across the ecosystem.
Finally, establishing a strong governance framework—one that coordinates versioning, security policies,
and stakeholder input—will ensure consistency and sustainability. Partnering with national standards
bodies and aligning with Medicaid and state-level initiatives will help minimize fragmentation.
By reinforcing open API adoption through aligned policy, procurement, technical support, and
governance, CMS can shape a more interoperable, future-ready healthcare infrastructure—one that
supports better data access, lowers systemic costs, and enables more equitable, patient-centered care.
TD 5. How could a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints improve access to health
information for patients, providers, and payers? Who should publish such a directory, and should
users bear a cost?
A nationwide FHIR endpoint directory would be a critical step toward improving interoperability and
simplifying health data exchange across the U.S. healthcare system. One of the greatest current barriers
is simply knowing where and how to connect—especially as patients engage with multiple providers and
systems. To ensure trust and integrity, the directory should be:
• Federally maintained—ideally by ONC, potentially in partnership with a QHIN or other
implementation body
• Publicly accessible, with optional premium services (e.g., real-time monitoring) offered by private
partners
• Designed to promote data transparency, equity, and usability across all stakeholders
A national FHIR endpoint directory would eliminate a fundamental barrier to digital health innovation and
empower patients, providers, and payers to connect and exchange health information efficiently,
equitably, and securely.

TD 6. What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform?
The TEFCA performs several unique interoperability functions that position it as a foundational element
of nationwide health information exchange. While many existing frameworks and networks support data
exchange regionally or within specific networks (e.g., HIEs, EHR vendor networks), TEFCA introduces
standardized governance, legal, and technical mechanisms to unify these efforts under a single,
nationwide framework. TEFCA plays a uniquely integrative role in the U.S. health IT ecosystem by
establishing a federated, interoperable environment for health data exchange across disparate networks,
technologies, and organizations. Developed by the Office of the National Coordinator for Health IT (ONC),
TEFCA formalizes the "rules of the road" for nationwide trusted data exchange, enabling participants to
access, query, and share electronic health information (EHI) at scale—regardless of vendor, geography, or
network affiliation. Unlike traditional HIEs or bilateral exchange agreements, TEFCA provides legal,
technical, and governance alignment across a broad range of stakeholders, including payers, providers,
public health agencies, and digital health developers.
TD 6 a. What existing alternatives should be considered?
Before fully adopting TEFCA, healthcare organizations, payers, and digital health platforms may consider
existing interoperability solutions such as Carequality, CommonWell, and FHIR-based APIs. These
alternatives, while often limited to specific networks or use cases, offer mature and widely adopted means
of health data exchange that may fulfill short-term objectives.
However, TEFCA is not intended to replace these systems, it is designed to federate them under a common
legal, technical, and governance framework. TEFCA introduces a scalable, policy-aligned approach to
nationwide health information exchange through designated QHINs, many of which may include or
interoperate with existing networks. As such, organizations should view Carequality, CommonWell, and
FHIR APIs not as alternatives to TEFCA, but as potential building blocks within the TEFCA ecosystem. A
phased approach that aligns immediate interoperability needs with TEFCA readiness can maximize value
while ensuring long-term compliance and scalability.

TD 7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange and what are its limitations?
The USCDI standard defines a minimum set of structured health data classes and elements to be shared
for interoperability. For CMS, which oversees some of the nation’s largest healthcare programs and data
exchanges, USCDI has been instrumental in establishing a common foundation for information exchange
across EHRs, health plans, providers, and digital health tools. It has enabled greater consistency in data
formats, supported compliance with CMS interoperability rules (e.g., patient access APIs), and advanced
the goals of the 21st Century Cures Act.
However, while USCDI sets a critical baseline, it does not yet meet all the nuanced or advanced needs of
CMS programs, particularly in areas like value-based care, public health reporting, and whole-person care.
The evolving nature of the healthcare ecosystem—especially with growing needs for behavioral health,
social determinants of health (SDOH), and longitudinal care data—means that USCDI’s scope remains too
narrow for some CMS use cases, see Exhibits 4 and 5 for additional USCDI details.

TD 7 a. Does it contain the full extent of data elements you need?
No, the USCDI (United States Core Data for Interoperability) does not currently contain the full extent of
data elements needed from a CMS context. While it provides a standardized foundation for clinical data
exchange, CMS programs require a broader and deeper set of data elements to support complex use cases
such as claims processing, quality measurement, prior authorization, risk adjustment, social determinants
of health (SDOH), and value-based care administration.
USCDI is a critical step toward consistent interoperability, but its focus is largely clinical and encounter-
based, emphasizing EHR-sourced information like medications, allergies, immunizations, and lab results.
CMS, as both a payer and regulator, needs access to a much richer and more varied dataset that extends
beyond direct clinical care documentation to include administrative, financial, behavioral, and social data.
Many CMS programs—such as Medicare Advantage, Medicaid Managed Care, ACO REACH, and other
CMMI models—depend on granular, longitudinal, and cross-sector data to manage risk, evaluate

TD 13. b. What are the primary tradeoffs between USCDI and full EHI, especially given more
flexible data processing capabilities today?
Accessing full Electronic Health Information (EHI) introduces heightened security and privacy risks,
especially when aggregated into a centralized repository or data lake. Full EHI often contains highly
sensitive and unstructured information, including free-text clinical notes, behavioral health data, and
imaging records—elements that may inadvertently expose personally identifiable information (PII) or
protected health information (PHI) not intended for broad use. The larger and more complex the dataset,
the greater the attack surface, and the more difficult it becomes to apply consistent data minimization,
masking, and access control policies. To mitigate these risks, organizations should implement fine-grained
access controls that restrict EHI exposure based on user roles, purpose of use, and patient consent.
Additionally, deploying automated redaction and de-identification tools—especially those augmented by
natural language processing (NLP)—can help prevent sensitive data from being shared inappropriately
while still enabling analytic use. Robust logging, audit trails, and adherence to zero-trust architecture
principles further strengthen the security posture when handling full EHI.

TD 15. a. Regarding bulk FHIR APIs: How would increased use of bulk FHIR improve use cases
and data flow?
Increased use of bulk FHIR APIs would significantly enhance data flow and unlock high-impact use cases
by enabling efficient, scalable access to large volumes of patient data across systems. Bulk FHIR supports
population-level queries, allowing organizations to retrieve data for cohorts—rather than one patient at
a time—which is essential for quality measurement, risk stratification, public health surveillance, and
value-based care analytics. It reduces reliance on manual data pulls or custom integrations, streamlines
reporting, and enables real-time insights across payers, providers, and researchers. Broader adoption
would promote faster, more consistent, and cost-effective data exchange across the healthcare
ecosystem.
TD 15. b. Regarding bulk FHIR APIs: What are the potential disadvantages of their use?
Bulk FHIR APIs enable powerful population-level data exchange for public health, research, and quality
reporting. By allowing extraction of full cohorts, they support deeper insights and more efficient
workflows. However, they come with key challenges: high infrastructure demands, heightened security
risks, inconsistent vendor support, and complex governance around consent and data use. Bulk data is
often difficult to manage and use without advanced filtering and processing. To realize their full value,
stakeholders must invest in scalable infrastructure, standardize implementation, and strengthen policies
to ensure secure, practical, and equitable use.
TD 16. What are the tradeoffs of maintaining point-to-point models vs. shared network

As health data exchange evolves, organizations must choose between point-to-point connectivity and
shared network infrastructure. Point-to-point offers tailored integration and control, making it effective
for bilateral, high-volume exchanges. However, it is resource-intensive, difficult to scale, and prone to
fragmentation as each connection requires separate setup and maintenance.

TD16 a. Do current rules encourage scalable network participation?
Current federal policies—shaped by the 21st Century Cures Act, ONC Health IT Certification Program, and
CMS interoperability rules—have laid essential groundwork for improving health data exchange. These
initiatives promote technical readiness, standardized APIs like FHIR, and discourage information blocking.
However, they fall short of enabling widespread, scalable participation in nationwide health information
networks.
While TEFCA introduces a promising national trust framework, its voluntary nature and lack of financial
incentives have limited adoption among providers, payers, and vendors. Many smaller and under-
resourced organizations still face high costs, limited technical capacity, and fragmented systems, making
meaningful participation difficult.
Enforcement of current rules remains limited, with requirements focused on making data “available”
rather than ensuring its effective use. Without stronger incentives or mandates, the operational and
financial burden of participation continues to outweigh the perceived benefits for many stakeholders.
To move from intent to impact, CMS and ONC may need to strengthen their approach by linking TEFCA
participation to federal program eligibility, integrating incentives with value-based care, and supporting
under-resourced providers. Only then can interoperability evolve into a truly scalable, equitable, and
unified national system.
TD 16 b. What changes would improve alignment (for example, API unification, reciprocal access)?
To realize scalable and equitable health data exchange, federal rules and infrastructure must evolve to
promote stronger alignment across APIs, networks, and stakeholders. Despite regulatory advances,
persistent fragmentation—technical, legal, and operational—continues to hinder seamless data flow and
erodes trust across the ecosystem. Key improvements include unifying API standards around SMART on
FHIR and Bulk FHIR for consistent implementation across vendors and use cases. This would reduce
variability and make integration easier for developers, particularly those serving under-resourced
providers. Ensuring reciprocal data sharing is also critical. Networks should prohibit one-way data flows
and require that all participants contribute data under fair and transparent rules, as outlined in TEFCA and
other national frameworks.
A centralized FHIR endpoint directory and standard OAuth2-based trust frameworks would simplify
onboarding and interoperability across systems, while national standards for consent and sensitive data
handling would strengthen privacy and build confidence in data sharing.
CMS should incentivize participation in TEFCA-aligned networks by linking it to value-based payment
programs. Providers who exchange data through certified APIs could receive performance credits or
reduced reporting burdens.
Achieving true interoperability requires more than technology—it demands unified policies, shared
responsibility, and aligned incentives to ensure health information flows securely and efficiently across
the care continuum.
