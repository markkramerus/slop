We therefore strongly encourage the Secretary to include sleep health as a central part of the MAHA initiative. Sleep is a vital part of human physiology and is a basic biologic necessity. Sleep allows you to stay healthy because it gives your body a chance to heal damaged cells , boost the immune system , recover from activities , and r echarge the cardiovascular system . Yet, a ccording to the National Heart, Blood, and Lung Institute, a bout 50 to 70 million Americans have a sleep disorder. Of those, about 30 million Americans suffer from sleep apnea, but only 6 million are diagnosed with the condition. About 40% of normal adults snore regularly . Often, s noring and fatigue are the most common symptoms of obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) . Approximately 1 in 3 people have OSA , which is a disorder in which the muscles in the throat relax to the point of collapse, restricting airflow. This causes breathing to become shallow and even stop for seconds or minutes at a time, depriving the body and brain of necessary oxygen. This causes a person to wake or become aroused from deep sleep. L ack of deep sleep can cause significant fatigue and elevate the risk for serious health problems. Unfortunately, m ost people with sleep apnea are unaware they even have it. When a person’s airway closes off , it is called an apnea . W hen it’s narrowed but not closed off it is called hypopnea . Sleep apnea severity is typically measured by the apnea -hypopnea index (AHI), which tracks two different types of events: the number of apneas (which are brief pauses in breathing) and the number of hypopneas (which are incidences of shallow breathing) per hour of sleep. The “n ormal ” AHI while a person is sleeping is five or less. But a s a person ages, sleep apnea can worsen due to factors like decreased muscle tone in the upper airway, weight gain, and changes in sleep patterns. As people age, there is also an increased likelihood of other health conditions, like cardiovascular disease, which can worsen or exacerbate sleep apnea and increase episodes of sleep apnea per hour . Mild sleep apnea is considered 5 –14 events per hour , m oderate sleep apnea 15–29 events per hour , and s evere sleep apnea 30 or more events per hour . OSA may affect children as well as adults. If the hourly rate of apnea -hypopnea increases beyond five per night, it is likely the person may be suffering from OSA. P eople may not even realize they have it or changes are happening but as one gets older , sleep becomes more fragmented causing what is often mistaken for age -related fatigue, tiredness , or stress, rather than a sleep disorder . Regrettably, like OSA, many Americans do n’t even know they have it. COPD prevents airflow to the lungs – causing breathing problems . COPD is an umbrella term for two primary conditions: chronic bronchitis and emphysema. Chronic bronchitis is a progressive lung disease that involves inflammation, increased mucus and thickening of the airways. Emphysema causes the alveoli – the small air sacs in the lungs that provide oxygen to the bloodstream – to thin and become and less elastic. There is no cure for COPD but there are treatment options and lifestyle changes that can help you remain active, breathe easier and slow the progress of the disease. Resmed offers devices to help address the problems caused by COPD. Ventilation , for example , can help because s ometimes these condition s do not allow enough carbon dioxide to be exhaled from the body . Non -invasive ventilation, or “NIV, ” can help reduce carbon dioxide levels. This therapy involves delivering pressurized room air through a machine to your lungs via a mask or mouthpiece. NIV changes pressure levels to help a person exhale excess carbon dioxide. COPD patients may also suffer from insomnia and sleep disturbances, including a secondary sleep disorder like OSA . ResMed shares CMS’ commitment to advance digital health solutions that enhance patient access to healthcare data and empower individuals to make informed decisions about their care. ResMed also appreciates CMS’s goal to leverage digital health technologies that reduce fraud , cut adminis trative burden and costs, and deliver high quality care . In alignment with this goal, ResMed supports enhancing healthcare data exchange by promoting the adoption of interoperable health information technology (IT) through Health Level Seven® (HL7®) Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources® (FHIR®) , application program interface (API) standards , and the continuation of frameworks such as TEFCA. ResMed also advocates for a national technology strategy tailored to the diverse needs of Medicare care settings and providers, moving beyond a one -size -fits -all approach . For many years, CMS has been looking for ways to incentivize post -acute care (PAC) and other providers to adopt the ONC EHR certification requirements (CEHRT) that were developed under the HITECH Act. The HITECH Act provided funding to hospitals and "eligible profe ssionals" (like physicians) who adopted and demonstrated the "meaningful use" of certified electronic health record (EHR) systems. However, PAC and other Medicare provider segments were not offered incentives to adopt EHR systems under the HITECH Act . As a result, we believe the ONC EHR certification yielded its benefits early in conception for those specific Medicare provider segments who were incentivized but has excluded the digital health needs of other provider programs and has since lost relevance, as the technology landscape has shifted significantly since the HITECH Act. The most applicable CEHRT criteria to all provider segments are the backbone pre -requisite requirements specifically around security, stability, and observability. The CEHRT modules themselves seem to bring less value, and arguably curtailed meaningful innovation in the areas they govern by being too heavy handed , onerous and costly. Additionally, the modules are very geared toward specific provider segments of healthcare; most post -acute providers/EHR vendors could never achieve full certification because the core modules don’t apply. It is very important for ASTP/ONC to refocus certification criteria onto API enabled capabilities over EHR functionality. This would encourage more organizations to consider certification, broadening the network of providers to whom innovative solutions could be deployed. Ideally , requirements to support APIs would be paired with rapid and secure access to these APIs via frameworks like TEFCA. TEFCA allows for the easiest and fastest path for making electronic connections between providers. All other prescribed connectivity standards require extensive setup times whereas TEFCA can be switched on. While the depth of capability is limited and still in a foundational stage , the breadth of connections that can be made across care segments and geographies is very valuable. Without TEFCA, the alternative is going back to freestanding Health Information Networks and Exchanges serving regional groupings of providers, making it more difficult to achieve a full view of patient health history.

This could and perhaps ought to be a government run service, as means of stimulating innovation in health tech through efficient data access . That said, without a streamlined way to access APIs, a directory alone will not remove barriers to innovative new apps and startups. Rolling a FHIR directory into a program like TEFCA would allow qualified health information networks to act as the trust provider for participants and sub -participants who want to access specific data types ava ilable via FHIR endpoint, without having to go through the onerous onboarding process with an EHR or Health System. Moving forward, CMS should have discussions with all stakeholders in the various Medicare provider program s, inclusive of health IT developers, to understand the workflows, business needs and varying levels of technology readiness to identify the appropriate digital health solutions and implementation timelines within each segment. We recommend these discussions occur early, prior to the pre -rulemaking process, perhaps using the specific Medicare provider quality reporting program Technical Expert Panels (TEPs) to discuss the technical requirements and digital health solutions that best support the implementation of new and updated Medicare policies and requirements. In the existing CMS pre - rulemaking process, there have been occasional TEPs and pilot project opportunities. However, participation from health IT developers has been lacking from both C MS technical staff and the EHR vendors that implement such requirements on behalf of Medicare provide rs and beneficiaries. In addition, CMS should follow through on successful pilot projects and incorporate into rulemaking with incentive structures. Frequently PAC technology vendors find themselves committing resources to projects that never get past a CMS ideation phase, with no incentive or policy/rule to compel the use and therefore no purpose in widening adoption. An example is the Post Acute Interoperability workgroup (PACIO - https://pacioproject.org/ ), a CMS sponsored program that has produced a wide variety of technical specifications and implementation guides geared toward post -acute providers, but with no endorsement or rulemaking to incentivize their adoption. Last, CMS should refrain from requiring CEHRT as a participation requirement by all providers in new alternative payment models being tested in the CMS Innovation Center. Such a requirement deters participation from certain providers (such as PAC) that are vital to the success of the model. For example, CMS requires CEHRT for primary participating providers in the Guiding an Improved Dementia Experience (GUIDE) Model. Although CMS promoted this model as a good fit for h ome health agencies, most HHAs do not have certified EHRs. Alternatively, CMS should focus on the model goals and identify digital solutions that support the model requirements and workflows with consideration o f the varying levels of digital solution adoption by eligible participating providers.
