NextGen Healthcare – RFI Response
Patients & Caregivers
PC-5b. Consistent and timely access to accurate patient health information requires advances in patient
matching. Additionally, standards for health data exchange should extend to ancillary providers (e.g.,
labs, imaging centers), payers, and community-based organizations to ensure data completeness and
interoperability across the ecosystem.
PC-8. Certified EHR technology (CEHRT) makes a wide range of clinical information readily available to
patients, including diagnoses, medications, and test results. To offer patients and caregivers access to
their own information, providers need to implement and use the functionality found in CEHRT. CMS
should build public awareness concerning the availability and capabilities of patient-facing digital
technologies and incentivize provider organizations to implement and train staff on their use, including
FHIR API endpoints that allow patients to access their own health information using an app of their
choice.
PC-8a. Certain social determinants such as food security, transportation access, and family support
strongly influence patient health but are not typically captured in structured, exchangeable formats, nor
are they fully addressed by USCDI.
PC-14a. A lack of patient awareness concerning digital health and identity credentialing, along with
limited health and technology literacy, make it difficult to navigate the health IT ecosystem. Additionally,
inconsistent ID verification practices across healthcare organizations create confusion and hinder
adoption.

PR-4. The significant obstacles to providing access to administrative workflows are not necessarily
technical limitations. The standards are in place to support these types of administrative processes and
workflows. For example, FHIR R4 includes standard resources for Appointment and Financial Transaction

products is the “trust” factor. For this to work, EHRs must allow independent applications with which
they have no direct business relationship to write data into their systems. While this doesn’t present an
insurmountable problem, the use cases are different from allowing patient access to view or retrieve
their health information via read-only transactions.
PR-6.
PR-6c. Options outside of TEFCA. Alternatives include local HIEs, patient portals, DirectTrust networks,
and consumer-facing apps. However, these solutions usually require point-to-point connections, making
them less scalable and comprehensive than whatthan TEFCA envisions. Carequality provides a widely
adopted national exchange framework, however, TEFCA is expected to enable the next level of
nationwide interoperability and become the successor to Carequality.
PR-8.
PR-8a. Benefits and downsides of Bulk FHIR data. Success remains limited with deploying Bulk FHIR
exports to decrease the burden of reporting on providers. EHRs are certified to support Bulk FHIR, but
the lack of industry-wide use remains low. CMS should focus on creating real-world use cases and policy
drivers that compel adoption before expecting it to be a primary mechanism for quality reporting.
PR-8b. Consolidation of provider quality reporting responsibilities CMS can reduce provider burden by
aligning quality measures across its programs and focusing on a narrower set of core measures.
PR-12. NextGen Healthcare urges HHS to maintain existing information blocking exceptions, particularly
the Infeasibility and Manner exceptions, as these exceptions allow actors to balance broad access to EHI
with secure implementation. These exceptions also allow actors to exchange data efficiently without
resorting to expensive, proprietary methods. Narrowing these exceptions could create barriers to
interoperability and hinder innovation for smaller developers and providers. Instead of removing or
modifying exceptions, we recommend continued ASTP/ONC guidance to ensure stakeholders
understand their application to advancing EHI access and maintaining trust and feasibility across the
healthcare delivery ecosystem.
We would support updating the Infeasibility Exception’s timeline condition. A rigid ten-business-day
requirement does not allow sufficient time to account for complex workflows and resolution of
legitimate infeasibilities.

Payers
PA-5. Payers can simplify quality reporting for providers by adopting a consistent set of standards for
quality measure specifications, value sets, data requirements, and calculation logic. Today, variability
across payers, including within CMS programs, adds unnecessary complexity for both providers and
health IT developers. Equally important is the adoption of standardized methods for provider/patient
attribution to support alignment between CMS with private payers to improve accuracy of quality
reporting and reduce administrative burden.
PA-5b. Aligning quality measure specifications, implementation guides, value sets, and calculation
methods with CMS and other payers would enable providers and health IT developers to leverage the
same infrastructure and workflows across all programs.
Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks
TD-1. CMS can stimulate developer interest in the short term by promoting adoption of standardized
APIs to enable seamless integration with Medicare data. Streamlined and transparent regulatory
requirements would also lower barriers to entry for new digital health products targeting beneficiaries
and caregivers. Longer term, CMS should implement reimbursement models that incorporate digital
health products into care delivery and quality programs. This includes end-to-end alignment,
establishing certification requirements and exchange standards for all involved parties (not just EHR
developers and providers), along with downstream incentives for consistent use by providers, payers,
public health agencies, and others.
TD-2a. CMS can further increase developer engagement by making provider directory data available via
API and enabling real-time access to cost data. This type of information needs to be accessible on a real-
time basis for it to be useful and actionable in the present.
TD-2d. CMS should prioritize the development of APIs that enable timely, actionable access to both cost
and quality data. Providers currently lack real-time visibility into CMS-calculated cost scores.
TD-4. To encourage the use of open, standards-based APIs, CMS should ensure that standards apply on
all sides of exchange, including payers, public health agencies, and other stakeholders, not just
healthcare providers and EHR developers. Requiring standards only in one direction creates asymmetry,
limits interoperability, and discourages investment in scalable solutions. Additionally, CMS and
ASTP/ONC should avoid incorporating standards into programs before they are sufficiently mature and
ensure they clearly tie to well-defined, real-world use cases. Developers are willing to adopt standards

to meet market demands, but premature adoption without validation and operational readiness

TD-6. TEFCA offers a centralized alternative to costly and siloed HIE participation, which may improve
cost efficiency for providers. The framework’s unique value lies in its potential to standardize and scale
national health information exchange by establishing a clear governance framework, defining use cases,
and maintaining consistent technical requirements across these use cases. TEFCA’s focus on existing
standards, including IHE Document Exchange APIs and HL7 FHIR-based APIs, is a significant advantage,
as it employs record locator services that enable identification of all a patient’s data holders, thereby
helping organizations and consumers communicate using uniform data structures.
TD-7. USCDI adoption has been a significant step forward for interoperability and standardization across
health IT systems. By defining broad, consensus-driven data classes and elements, USCDI establishes a
common foundation for the exchange of clinically meaningful data via recognized standards such as HL7
FHIR and HL7 CDA (C-CDA). This has improved the consistency and usability of health information at the
point of care and across care transitions.
However, several limitations remain:
• Limited scope: USCDI does not yet cover all EHI, though this can be addressed by incrementally
expanding its content.
• Inconsistency with HL7 standards: These create implementation challenges. Aligning USCDI
more directly with relevant HL7 Implementation Guides would improve technical consistency.
• Update frequency: The cadence of USCDI updates can be difficult for developers and
implementers to track and support, particularly when incorporated into certification and
program requirements without adequate lead time.
• Lack of modularity: The “all-or-nothing” approach to adopting each version of USCDI presents
challenges for specialty developers. This issue could be addressed by introducing stratified or
modular implementation requirements within certification criteria. This would facilitate the
certification of health IT other than EHR for the specific data they manage, which does not
necessarily encompass all of USCDI.
TD-7a. USCDI does not yet encompass the full range of data elements required to support all
interoperability use cases. However, ONDEC offers a valuable pathway for gradually broadening its
scope to provide a more complete and standardized basis for the exchange of EHI.
TD-7b. The limitations of USCDI relate more to scope than format, although ASTP/ONC should require
the use of HL7 FHIR to express the data classes and data elements. This would reduce ambiguity in
translation to supporting standards (e.g., HL7 FHIR, HL7 C-CDA and others). If the goal is to standardize
all EHI under a common framework in the long term, that intent should be clearly stated and supported
with a deliberate, phased strategy.

TD-7c. Continued expansion is essential to standardize healthcare data in a way that promotes
consistent exchange and meaningful use. Adding new data elements inevitably introduces scoping and
implementation challenges, but these can be mitigated by incorporating a modular approach within the
Certification Program, allowing developers and providers to support only those data elements that align
with their workflows or specialties. The timing of USCDI versions also creates challenges. Versions are
published annually in July, but the corresponding HL7 implementation specifications (C-CDA and FHIR)
often aren’t finalized until the following May.
TD-8. Privacy and Security (P&S) criteria have been created as a consistent baseline for safeguarding
health information and offers direction in the absence of HIPAA Security Rule certification. C-CDA-based
criteria and Direct messaging played a critical role in the early phases of interoperability. However, as
the industry shifts away from document-based exchange, the program should focus on modernizing
these criteria, leveraging RESTful and HL7 FHIR. Public health criteria, especially for immunization and
electronic laboratory reporting, have supported scalable reporting infrastructure and would be even
more effective if not for the lack of reciprocal obligations on public health agencies (PHAs) to follow the
same standards.
TD-9a. Emphasizing API-enabled capabilities, particularly HL7 FHIR-based APIs, is key to modernizing
interoperability. Prioritizing these APIs over IHE Document Exchange APIs allows for more flexible and
efficient data sharing, enabling developers to tailor systems to their customers' needs. However,
functionality-based privacy and security criteria remain essential and should be maintained as
certification evolves.
TD-9b. Focusing solely on HL7 FHIR-based APIs may be restrictive. HL7 v2 APIs are still effective for many
public health workflows, and migrating away from proven infrastructure without clear ROI is
unnecessary. Replacing multiple low-burden criteria with a single complex API requirement, especially if
tied to immature standards, can increase the overall burden and costs. Certification must consider cost,
maturity, and feasibility.
TD-9c. HL7 FHIR-based APIs already support unstructured data like faxed or scanned documents and
free-text content through the Document Reference resource. Expanding USCDI to include these data
types and setting clearer expectations for unstructured content would advance interoperability.
However, effective interoperability requires meaningful and relevant data. While unstructured data can
be valuable, applying standards and structure enhances its usability and value. We should focus on
improving data quality and standardization rather than quantity to increase value and reduce cost and
burden.
TD-9d. API-based data requests are valuable, but provider adoption requires meaningful incentives.
Information Blocking regulations provide a compliance framework, but tangible rewards for high-quality
API responses are lacking. CMS could enhance this by aligning incentives, such as rewarding automated
case reporting via eCR. Policy strategies should be co-developed with providers to align API engagement
with clinical workflows and resource constraints.

TD-9e. EHRs with bulk data export capabilities can reduce provider burden under a digital quality
measurement (dQM) model, but only if paired with bi-directional feedback mechanisms. Providers are
accustomed to reviewing performance data through EHR-based dashboards, so if CMS or another entity
performs centralized measure calculation, providers need real-time visibility into those results within
their EHR environment. CMS should adopt a standardized API mechanism for pushing calculated results
back into EHR dashboards to ensure continuity. Additionally, CMS should clarify the timing, frequency,
and format of such feedback.
TD-11a. EHI Export should be revised to include standardized API requirements. In its current form,
codified at 45 CFR § 170.315(b)(10), EHI Export has not met the goals of simplifying patient access or
streamlining provider transitions between health IT systems. Lack of defined, commonly accepted
standards for both data format and delivery are the greatest factors. Developers are required to
document and publicize export specifications, but the complexity of health requires shared, well-
understood standards. We recommend re-scoping the 170.315(b)(10) EHI Export criterion to focus on
USCDI data. It should be clarified that EHI Export by itself is not a robust solution for health IT-to-health
IT migration, which often requires complex mappings based on established workflows.
TD11b. The challenges with EHI Export are not in workflow execution. Developers should have the
discretion to design workflows that align with their products. Standardizing workflow details would add
unnecessary complexity without meaningful value. Certification should focus on ensuring the capability
exists and functions reliably, not on dictating internal steps or user interfaces.
TD-11c. Should CMS consider policy changes to support this capability's use?
EHI Export capabilities are triggered by specific, infrequent use cases, such as a patient requesting a
computable copy of their data or a provider transitioning to a new EHR. Therefore, CMS should not
mandate their use. If existing standardized exchange mechanisms (such as FHIR or C-CDA) fulfill most
real-world needs, there is no benefit in mandating the use of EHI Export for its own sake. Instead, CMS
should support broader adoption of standards-based exchanges (e.g., HL7 FHIR and CDA) that already
satisfy most interoperability needs. For exceptional cases requiring more comprehensive datasets,
custom approaches remain viable until USCDI or FHIR profiles are mature enough to fully support “all
EHI” use cases.
TD13. APIs that provide access to a patient's complete EHI can enhance patient engagement, improve
care coordination, and benefit researchers and public health agencies by offering richer datasets for
analytics and disease surveillance. These capabilities, combined with strong privacy protections and
standardized data structures, can transform health data into actionable intelligence. CMS should offer
more incentives to encourage providers to implement and maximize the functionality of these APIs,
addressing their current uncertainty through payment models or conditions of participation.
Balancing value and complexity is the primary trade-off between USCDI and full EHI. USCDI offers
standardized, interoperable health data, while full EHI includes vast, unstructured data that varies across

TD-17. EHR developers can build and certify the technology, but adoption and meaningful use relies on
perceived value. Providers tend not to implement new capabilities if the primary beneficiaries are other

incentives, and quality program alignment.
Value-Based Care Organizations
VB-3. Essential health IT capabilities for value-based care must support care coordination, performance
measurement, patient engagement, and data-driven decision-making. Standardization and consistency
across CMS programs are crucial, especially in quality measurement and reporting. Value-based
organizations often use multiple EHR platforms, complicating data normalization and aggregation.
Inconsistent reporting requirements create unnecessary burdens and can undermine data quality.
Accelerating dQM with standardized data elements and formats (e.g., FHIR-based exchange of USCDI) is
vital for accurate, near-real-time insights into quality performance.
VB-3b. Bidirectional feedback between CMS and provider systems is an overlooked opportunity for
improving quality measurement. EHRs are evolving to serve as the data source rather than the
calculation engine in dQM models. Nevertheless, providers still expect real-time visibility into quality
measure performance. Establishing standardized APIs to display performance feedback from CMS or
third-party aggregators within the EHR dashboard would be advantageous to both efficiency and quality
of care.
VB-4. Successful participation in value-based care arrangements depends on access to a comprehensive
set of clinical, demographic, financial, and behavioral data, including diagnoses, medications, lab results,
care plans, and claims data. Including access to community-based sources as part of a comprehensive
solution would be advantageous. However, even the most robust datasets are ineffective without
accurate patient identity matching and clear attribution. These foundational elements are essential to
ensuring proper linking of data to the appropriate individuals and that providers understand which
patients they are responsible for managing.
VB-8. To optimize the use of digital health products in APMs, HHS policies should extend beyond vendor
certification to address the broader ecosystem, including payers, third-party application developers, and
public health agencies. CEHRT requirements have driven standardization among EHR vendors, but the
lack of corresponding requirements for other stakeholders hinders widespread adoption.
