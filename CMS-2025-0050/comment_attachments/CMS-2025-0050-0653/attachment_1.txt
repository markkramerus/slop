 Subject : Public Comment from Aloe Care Health in Response to CMS–0042–NC: Request for Information on the Health Technology Ecosystem To : Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) Submitted via : Regulations.gov Date : June 16, 2025 Introduction Aloe Care Health appreciates the opportunity to comment on CMS’s Request for Information regarding the Health Technology Ecosystem. Our team brings extensive experience designing and implementing home-based technologies that support older adults, individuals managing complex health needs, and their care networks. We agree with CMS’s assertion that “effective and responsible adoption of technology can empower patients to make better decisions for their health and well-being.” As innovation continues to expand in healthcare, we encourage a focus on solutions that are not only advanced but also practical, cost-effective, and designed for real-world behavior. In our experience with diverse aging populations, we’ve seen the outsized impact of voice-activated, predictive, and passive monitoring tools in promoting safety, independence, and reduced health system utilization. A. The Fall Risk Crisis and the Role of Passive Prevention (RFI Areas: PC–1, VB–3, TD–13) Falls are among the most preventable and costly health risks affecting Medicare beneficiaries. In 2020, 14 million older adults (27.6%) reported a fall, with estimated direct costs exceeding $80 billion (CDC, 2022). Falls are also an early warning signal of deteriorating health. Studies show that older adults who fall have a 21% increased risk of being diagnosed with dementia within a year (Fritz et al., 2023). Unfortunately, many fall-prevention solutions depend on user compliance or digital literacy—barriers for many high-risk individuals. Aloe Care has found that passive systems—those using ambient sensors or voice-based monitoring—can detect changes in behavior that signal increased risk, such as altered mobility or hydration patterns. Early intervention reduces hospitalization risk and helps people stay safely in their homes. 1 
 “Long-lie” incidents (where someone remains on the floor after a fall for over an hour) are particularly dangerous and predictive of long-term decline. Reliable, voice-activated emergency access—especially with built-in cellular connectivity for homes without Wi-Fi—can be lifesaving. Policy Recommendation : CMS should expand reimbursement pathways for non-wearable, passive safety systems that support early detection, emergency response, and fall prevention. B. Supporting Independence Through Connected Care (RFI Areas: PC–5, VB–1, TD–1) Isolation in older adults is associated with increased cardiovascular risk and reduced lifespan (CDC, 2023). Tools that support communication between individuals and their families or care teams—particularly when they do not require app literacy or broadband—can significantly improve safety and reduce unnecessary emergency utilization. We’ve observed that when caregivers have real-time visibility into health events or daily routines, unnecessary dispatches and hospitalizations are reduced by more than 50%. These tools are particularly helpful for working family members, who make up 61% of all caregivers (AARP & NAC, 2020). Rather than treating caregiver support as a peripheral concern, we believe digital tools should position caregivers as integral partners in home-based care—helping prevent crises, support independence, and reduce cost. Policy Recommendation : CMS should encourage adoption of accessible technologies that enable two-way communication and real-time coordination between patients and their care networks. C. Enhancing Access with Low-Barrier Technology (RFI Areas: PC–6, VB–8, PR–1, PR–3) For digital tools to succeed at scale, they must work for those without advanced devices, broadband access, or app literacy. Overly complex, app-based solutions often exclude individuals in rural areas or those aging independently. Voice-based, multilingual systems that operate offline can dramatically extend reach—no smartphone, internet, or technical experience required. These technologies lower access barriers while supporting better outcomes and reduced utilization. Policy Recommendation : CMS should prioritize reimbursement and incentives for solutions that are broadband-independent, voice-accessible, and usable across age, geographic, and education differences. 2 
 D. Responsible Use of AI in the Home (RFI Areas: PC–5a, TD–13, TD–10, VB–2) Artificial intelligence, when used responsibly, can improve quality of life, promote safety, and reduce costs. Our pilots have shown that AI-powered systems can: ● Detect signs of infection or fall risk through behavior changes ● Offer wellness check-ins and medication prompts ● Help individuals stay on routines that prevent escalation We emphasize that AI should not replace human caregivers but should serve as a support tool. Systems must be secure, easy to understand, and add value to care coordination and decision-making. Policy Recommendation : CMS should support a policy framework for AI that emphasizes transparency, performance, and demonstrated impact on engagement and outcomes. E. Unlocking the Value of Home-Based Data (RFI Areas: VB–3, TD–5, PR–5) The home is rapidly becoming one of the richest data environments in healthcare. Passive sensors, voice systems, and connected devices now generate information that can indicate safety risks, activity levels, and behavioral health—well before such signals appear in traditional medical records. Aligned with FHIR and USCDI standards, this home-generated data can inform predictive models and improve the effectiveness of care teams. Policy Recommendation : CMS should invest in pilots and interoperability standards that enable meaningful, secure integration of home-based data into clinical and value-based workflows. F. Levers for Broader Impact To accelerate progress in home-based care and reduce unnecessary utilization, CMS should consider the following: 3 
 ● Pilot Programs : Fund real-world testing of passive fall detection, AI-powered care alerts, and caregiver-connected technologies. ● Expanded Reimbursement : Broaden HCPCS and benefit categories to cover voice-first and passive safety systems. ● Low-Tech Hurdle Investment : Prioritize technologies that do not depend on broadband or smartphones. ● AI Validation : Establish a clear pathway to validate and approve home-based AI tools with strong safety and performance records. Conclusion The future of care is moving into the home. Aloe Care Health believes that smart, accessible, and caregiver-connected technology can promote independence, reduce system strain, and improve outcomes. We appreciate the opportunity to share our perspective and look forward to supporting CMS in advancing a sustainable, innovative, and patient-centered health technology ecosystem. 4
