E. Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks
This section is intended for all stakeholders to provide input on questions as they relate to use
cases and workflows that involve technology vendors, data providers, and networks. While we
certainly want technology vendors, data providers, and networks to answer questions in this
section (and in other sections) from their point of view, we also invite all stakeholders to provide
their viewpoints on the technology vendor, data provider, and network use cases as appropriate.
1. Ecosystem
TD-1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to
stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries
and caregivers?
● Streamlined approvals, financial incentives for efficiency, reimbursement for AI that
lowers costs/improves population health, broader data access, and near real-time data
access.
TD-2. Regarding CMS Data, to stimulate developer interest—
a. What additional data would be most valuable if made available through CMS APIs?
● For medical AI advancement, CMS API access should expand to include de-identified
imaging, genomic/proteomic, detailed SDOH, comprehensive longitudinal claims and
clinical, caregiver-reported outcomes, and device-specific (RPM) data. This would
improve diagnostics, personalized medicine, health equity, chronic care, caregiver
support, and predictive analytics.
●

b. What data sources are most valuable alongside the data available through the Blue
Button 2.0 API?
While Blue Button 2.0 is an excellent start, the most valuable data sources to integrate
alongside it for a medical AI company would include:
● To effectively train AI for patient health understanding, access to various data sources is
crucial beyond claims data. Electronic Health Records (EHR) provide essential clinical
context like diagnoses and physician notes. Pharmacy data offers a comprehensive view
of medication use. Wearable and sensor data deliver continuous real-world physiological
insights. Provider-submitted clinical registries and quality data provide disease-specific,
high-quality information. Integrating individual patient data with public health data offers
insights into individual and population health trends.
●
c. What obstacles prevent accessing these data sources today?
● Fragmented healthcare data across systems hinders a complete patient view.
Inconsistent data formats and terminology complicate AI training and scalability. Privacy
regulations like HIPAA and IRB processes restrict access to necessary data.
Commercial interests limit access to EHRs and proprietary data, posing financial
challenges. API limitations restrict the volume, frequency, and specificity of data access.
A lack of standardized data governance and trust frameworks impedes data sharing. The
absence of universal de-identification standards and tools creates hesitation in data
sharing.
●
d. What other APIs should CMS and ASTP/ONC consider including in program policies to
unleash innovation and support patients and providers?
● To integrate AI insights into healthcare workflows, CMS should implement standardized
APIs for: enabling data "write-back" to EHRs; accessing predictive analytics/risk scoring
models; providing tailored patient education and shared decision-making resources;
facilitating care coordination and communication; streamlining prior authorization and
claims processing; offering tiered data access for research; utilizing CDS Hooks for
AI-powered clinical decision support; and explaining AI model predictions to build trust.

2. Digital Identity
TD-3. Regarding digital identity implementation:

a. What are the challenges and benefits?
● Challenges: Lack of standardized digital identities hinders data exchange. Privacy and
security of PHI require robust solutions. User adoption faces resistance due to complexity
or lack of understanding. Cost and infrastructure for secure systems can be high. Remote
identity proofing at different levels poses accuracy challenges. Integrating new solutions
with legacy systems is difficult.
●
● Benefits: Enhanced cybersecurity reduces unauthorized access. Improved data exchange
and interoperability streamline care. Automated identity verification reduces administrative
burden. Secure digital identities improve patient experience. Increased trust and
accountability result from verified access. Compliance with regulations like HIPAA is aided
by digital identity solutions.
●
b. How would requiring digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other
NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs) impact cybersecurity and data exchange?
● Stronger cybersecurity through improved authentication (MFA, IAL2/AAL2) reduces
unauthorized access, phishing, and enhances audit trails. For data exchange, reliable
digital identities increase trust, streamline authorization, improve data integrity, and
facilitate interoperability frameworks like TEFCA.
●
c. What impact would mandatory use of the OpenID Connect identity protocol have?
Mandatory use of the OpenID Connect (OIDC) identity protocol would have a largely positive
impact:
Adopting the mature OpenID Connect (OIDC) standard, built on OAuth 2.0, would significantly
improve healthcare application interoperability through simplified secure login and identity
sharing. OIDC's straightforward implementation fosters quicker development and a unified
single sign-on (SSO) system, enhancing user experience and security (authentication layer). As
an open standard, OIDC reduces vendor lock-in and offers scalability. Organizations not yet
using OIDC may experience minor disadvantages during the transition due to development and
training needs.
3. Technical Standards and Certification
TD-4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available
APIs over proprietary APIs?

CMS can encourage open API use by prioritizing them in certification, incentivizing adoption in
payment models, developing reference implementations, offering technical assistance,
showcasing successes, requiring public documentation, collaborating with SDOs, leveraging
procurement, and disincentivizing proprietary APIs.
TD-5. How could a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints improve access to
health information for patients, providers, and payers? Who should publish such a
directory, and should users bear a cost?
A nationwide FHIR endpoint directory would significantly improve health information access
by:
● Patients: Simplifying connection of personal health apps/portals to providers, enhancing
personal health record management and data control.
● Providers: Enabling easy discovery of FHIR endpoints for streamlined data exchange in
referrals, transitions, and coordination, reducing manual processes.
● Payers: Improving claims, care management, quality reporting, and population health
through efficient data access, leading to better payments and outcomes.
● Overall: Accelerating interoperability by lowering adoption barriers for new healthcare
applications and services.
●
Who Should Publish?
● A neutral, trusted, and authoritative entity should publish a directory. Potential options
include ONC/HHS (possibly collaborating with a non-profit), a public-private partnership
involving government and industry, or a TEFCA-designated entity leveraging the TEFCA
trust framework.
●
Should Users Bear a Cost?
● The directory's basic search features should be free or low-cost to ensure broad access
for providers and innovators, enhancing public health. While charging could limit
participation, improved data access offers a greater public benefit. A tiered pricing model
for advanced features and developer fees could supplement federal funding for
maintenance, keeping core lookup functionality free.
TD-6. What unique interoperability functions does TEFCA perform?

● TEFCA establishes a nationwide Common Agreement and governance for all QHINs, creating a
"network of networks" to replace direct connections. It mandates universal purpose of use for
HIPAA-permitted EHI exchange and enforces common technical standards like FHIR and IHE XCA.
TEFCA includes a dispute resolution process and focuses on foundational interoperability to enable
extensive health data exchange.
●
a. What existing alternatives should be considered?
● Existing health information exchange (HIE) alternatives include bilateral agreements
lacking scalability, regional/state HIEs with limited reach, private HINs (like CommonWell
and Carequality) that TEFCA aims to interconnect, proprietary vendor networks restricted
to their users, and patient-mediated exchange which doesn't solve provider-to-provider
exchange at scale.
b. Are there redundant standards, protocols or channels or both that should be
consolidated?
● Opportunities exist to consolidate and streamline data exchange despite its historical
development and varied uses. Key areas include: consolidating document exchange
protocols (considering FHIR's emergence alongside existing methods), standardizing
query mechanisms by promoting FHIR RESTful APIs for new implementations, reducing
variation in FHIR implementations through stricter adherence to standards like US Core,
streamlining consent management by standardizing technical models and policies, and
leveraging TEFCA as the primary data exchange channel to reduce complexity.
TD-7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange and what are its
limitations?
● The USCDI has improved interoperability by establishing a baseline data set for
exchange, driving vendor compliance, facilitating FHIR adoption, reducing data blocking
claims, and enabling patient access APIs. However, it has limitations due to its limited
scope of data elements needed for comprehensive care and analysis, representing a
"least common denominator" of information, and lacking granularity and context. While
relying on FHIR for exchange, variations in implementation can still cause minor
interoperability issues.

a. Does it contain the full extent of data elements you need?
While the USCDI offers a strong starting point and includes essential foundational data, it lacks
the comprehensive data elements necessary for many complex use cases and thorough patient
care. Thus, numerous stakeholders need more detailed, specialized, or context-rich information.
b. If not, is it because of limitations in the definition of the USCDI format or the way it is utilized?
The primary reason for USCDI's limitations in meeting comprehensive data needs lies in the
inherent scope of its definition. USCDI is purposefully designed as a core dataset and is not
intended to be exhaustive. Although variations in its application exist (such as incomplete
vendor implementation), the fundamental constraint stems from its deliberately limited scope.
c. If so, would adding more data elements to USCDI add value or create scoping challenges?
How could such challenges be addressed?
● Adding data elements to USCDI offers value but poses challenges like implementation burden,
complexity, data availability, consensus-building, and version control. These can be addressed
through a phased approach, modular expansion for specific use cases, leveraging industry best
practices, strong governance and prioritization, providing guidance and tooling, and focusing on
robust FHIR profiles with extension mechanisms.
d. Given improvements in language models, would you prefer a non-proprietary but less
structured format that might improve data coverage even if it requires more processing by the
receiver?
● Structured data is strongly preferred for core clinical and administrative information due to its
necessity for accurate processing, automated support, and reliable analytics. While language
models have potential, they pose risks in misinterpreting unstructured clinical text.
●
● For inherently unstructured data like clinical narratives and social determinants of health, a less
structured but universally accessible format, combined with language models, could improve data
coverage and insights.
●
● A hybrid approach is likely best: highly structured formats (like FHIR with USCDI) for critical, discrete
data, and non-proprietary, less structured formats (accessible via API) for narrative data, leveraging
language models for extraction and summarization. Key considerations include non-proprietary
formats, robust language models, data validation, and this hybrid model.
TD-8. What are the most effective certification criteria and standards under the ONC Health IT
Certification Program?
Effective certification criteria and standards promote interoperability (API, FHIR, USCDI, and address

information blocking), enhance patient access (VDT, secure messaging), improve data quality and safety
(decision support, interaction checks), and ensure security and privacy. Specificity, clarity, and rigorous

TD-9. Regarding certification of health IT:
a. What are the benefits of redefining certification to prioritize API-enabled capabilities over
software functionality?
● Prioritizing APIs over software functionality enhances interoperability and fosters data
exchange. An API-first approach drives innovation through third-party apps, reduces
vendor lock-in, and accelerates development via modularity. API-enabled access
empowers patients with their health data and improves data flow for reporting and
analytics, offering organizations greater flexibility in choosing user interfaces while utilizing
certified backend systems.
b. What would be the drawbacks?
● API-focused certification may deprioritize UI usability, risking poor provider experience. Managing
more APIs increases security risks. Maintaining data integrity and clinical context via APIs is
challenging and requires robust modeling. Older EHRs face a heavy implementation burden for API
exposure. "Information blocking" could occur through poorly designed or documented APIs.
Healthcare organizations will rely more on third-party developers, necessitating careful vendor
management.
c. How could ASTP/ONC revise health IT certification criteria to require APIs to consistently
support exchanging data from all aspects of the patient's chart (for example, faxed records, free
text, discrete data)?
● To improve data access, USCDI could include metadata for unstructured data, and standardized
FHIR API endpoints should enable retrieval of documents like faxes and notes. Define and certify
"full EHI" APIs for comprehensive data access. Explore incentivizing NLP for extracting structured
data from free text. Mandate semantic interoperability using standard terminologies for structured
data exchange. API certification should include performance and scalability metrics. Require
thorough API documentation and test tools.
d. What policy changes could CMS make so providers are motivated to respond to API-based
data requests with best possible coverage and quality of data?
● Encourage API adoption and interoperability by linking it to value-based care and quality programs,
strengthening information blocking disincentives, offering performance-based incentives, reducing
reporting burden, and publicizing performance. Provide technical assistance and emphasize the
clinical benefits of data sharing.

e. How could EHRs capable of bulk data transfer be used to reduce the burden on providers for
reporting quality performance data to CMS? What capabilities are needed to show benefit?
What concerns are there with this approach?
● Automated bulk FHIR data exports from EHRs to CMS could reduce reporting burden by eliminating
manual abstraction, improving accuracy/timeliness through standardization, and lessening manual
effort. Benefits require standardized data models, robust bulk FHIR APIs in EHRs, CMS data
ingestion capabilities, data validation/error handling, attestation/audit trails, and consent
management. Concerns include poor underlying data, over/under-reporting, security/privacy risks,
system performance impacts, vendor compliance variability, attribution/accountability challenges,
and implementation costs.
TD-10. For EHR and other developers subject to the ONC Health IT Certification Program, what
further steps should ASTP/ONC consider to implement the 21st Century Cures Act's API
condition of certification (42 U.S.C. 300jj-11(c)(5)(D)(iv)) that requires a developer's APIs to
allow health information to be accessed, exchanged, and used without special effort, including
providing access to all data elements of a patient's electronic health record to the extent
permissible under applicable privacy laws?
To fully implement the Cures Act's API condition of certification for "without special effort" and "all data
elements," CMS should:
● Define "Without Special Effort" with measurable metrics like performance requirements (response
times, throughput), ease of use (comprehensive documentation, sandboxes), predictable
behavior (consistent error handling, versioning, uptime), and minimal customization.
● Clarify "All Data Elements of a Patient's EHR" by defining comprehensive FHIR profiles beyond
USCDI to include all commonly stored EHR data (narratives, images, device data, etc.),
categorizing EHI (discrete vs. unstructured) with expected API capabilities, and providing
pathways for data not yet in FHIR.
● Mandate standardized authentication (OpenID Connect) and authorization (SMART on FHIR),
require API-based consent management (FHIR Consent), prohibit unreasonable fees/restrictive
terms, and implement continuous monitoring and enforcement.
● Incentivize semantic interoperability, and ensure APIs provide data provenance and handle
segregated data appropriately.
TD-11. As of January 1, 2024, many health IT developers with products certified through the
ONC Health IT Certification Program are required to include the capability to perform an
electronic health information export or “EHI export” for a single patient as well as for patient
populations (45 CFR 170.315(b)(10)). Such health IT developers are also required to publicly
describe the format of the EHI export. Notably, how EHI export was accomplished was left
entirely to the health IT developer. Now that this capability has been in production for over a
year, CMS and ASTP/ONC seek input on the following:

a. Should this capability be revised to specify standardized API requirements for EHI export?
The current lack of standardized EHI export methods, leaving implementation to developers, hinders
Cures Act goals and burdens data recipients with diverse proprietary formats. Standardized API
requirements, like FHIR Bulk Data Access (Flat FHIR), would enable true interoperability, reduce burden,
enhance data utility, foster developer innovation, and improve compliance monitoring. FHIR Bulk Data
Access is the recommended, widely adopted, and scalable standard for this purpose.
b. Are there specific workflow aspects that could be improved?
● Standardized APIs could automate EHI export requests and delivery, offering granular data
selection, status monitoring with notifications, and detailed error handling. For population exports,
robust patient identity matching is key. The export process should integrate with consent
management and consider secure archiving and data lifecycle management.
c. Should CMS consider policy changes to support this capability's use?
● CMS should consider policy changes including mandating FHIR Bulk Data Access for EHI export as
a certification criterion, incentivizing its use for reporting to reduce manual burden, reinforcing that
hindering standardized EHI export is information blocking, requiring public reporting of adherence to
EHI export APIs, providing guidance for receiving entities on utilizing standardized exports, and
funding pilot programs to demonstrate their value.

TD-13. What new opportunities and advancements could emerge with APIs providing access to
the entirety of a patient's electronic health information (EHI)?
Access to the entirety of a patient's EHI via APIs would unlock immense opportunities:
● New advancements include comprehensive patient portals, advanced AI-powered clinical decision

support, richer datasets for population health management and precision medicine, automated prior
authorization and claims processing, seamless care transitions, AI/ML-driven research and drug
discovery, reduced administrative burden, and improved emergency care through rapid access to
complete medical histories.
a. What are the primary obstacles to this?
● Ensuring privacy and security for comprehensive data is the paramount obstacle. Defining and
implementing full Electronic Health Information (EHI) APIs is technically complex due to the vast
amount and variety of data. Semantic interoperability remains a challenge despite data exchange
due to inconsistent data interpretation. Data quality and completeness issues in EHRs hinder
reliable full data extraction. Legacy systems and data migration pose difficulties for exposing all data
via modern APIs. Ambiguity in data ownership, use rights, and consent policies needs resolution.
New forms of information blocking may arise despite the Cures Act. Scalability of transferring and
processing massive patient data requires strong infrastructure. Economic disincentives for health IT
vendors may hinder full data access.
b. What are the primary tradeoffs between USCDI and full EHI, especially
given more flexible data processing capabilities today?
The USCDI focuses on core, structured data, offering easier implementation and reliability for
basic interoperability but lacking depth for advanced uses. Full EHI, encompassing both
structured and unstructured data, unlocks greater potential for AI/ML and comprehensive care
by providing complete clinical context. While complex to implement and posing higher security
risks, advancements in language models and AI now mitigate the challenges of processing
unstructured data within full EHI, enhancing its value. These technologies enable information
extraction, improved understanding, and on-demand data structuring. Consequently, while
USCDI remains relevant, the focus should shift towards securely enabling access to full EHI
with clear governance to leverage its increased value for advanced applications.

TD-16. What are the tradeoffs of maintaining point-to-point models vs. shared network
infrastructure?
Point-to-Point Models:
● Pros: Direct control, simple for few connections, customizable.
● Cons: Unscalable ("N squared" problem), high maintenance, inconsistent data,
information silos, limited discovery.
Shared Network Infrastructure (e.g., TEFCA, Carequality):
● Pros: Scalable ("connect once, connect to many"), standardized, reduced maintenance,
enhanced discovery, improved trust/governance, network effects.
● Cons: Initial investment/complexity, less customization, governance overhead, potential
(though less likely) for single point of failure.

a. Do current rules encourage scalable network participation?
● Current regulations like the 21st Century Cures Act (Information Blocking, API Condition of
Certification, USCDI) increasingly promote scalable network participation. The API Condition of
Certification facilitates connections through open APIs. Information Blocking rules discourage
hindering data exchange, favoring network-based approaches. FHIR and USCDI provide the
technical foundation for scalable exchange, and TEFCA directly aims to create a national network of
networks.
b. What changes would improve alignment (for example, API unification, reciprocal access)?
To further improve alignment and encourage scalable network participation:
Recommendations for Enhanced Interoperability:
● API Unification/Consistency: Ensure consistent API design.
● Stronger FHIR Enforcement: Mandate consistent FHIR US Core and profile
implementation for plug-and-play interoperability.
● Standardized FHIR for Full EHI: Develop and require FHIR for all EHI data elements
for comprehensive exchange.
● API Gateway/Discovery Standards: Standardize API discovery and connection across
networks.
● Reciprocal Access Requirements:
○ Mutual Data Contribution: Require entities requesting data to also provide data
under similar terms to prevent data asymmetry.
○ Fair Pricing and Business Terms: Regulate against unfair pricing or restrictive
API access terms.
● Certification Incentives for Network Participation: Incentivize seamless integration
and active participation in national exchange networks.
● Streamlined Onboarding for Networks: Simplify and accelerate network onboarding
into frameworks like TEFCA.
● Public Reporting of Network Interoperability: Increase transparency on effective data
exchange and standards adherence.
● Address Edge Cases and Exceptions: Provide guidance for organizations facing
unique adoption challenges.

TD-19. Regarding price transparency implementation:
a. What are current shortcomings in content, format, delivery, and timeliness?
● Price transparency data from healthcare providers suffers from several shortcomings across
content, format, delivery, and timeliness. Content is often incomplete, lacks personalization and
context, uses varied definitions, and focuses on chargemaster prices instead of negotiated rates.
Formats are often machine-unreadable (PDFs, large CSVs), lack standardization, and have poor
user interfaces. Delivery lacks API access, centralized access, and real-time data. Updates are
infrequent and lag in reflecting negotiated rate changes.
b. Which workflows would benefit most from functional price transparency?
● Transparent pricing could enable: pre-service cost estimates and financial counseling,
cost-conscious referrals, cost discussions in care planning, faster prior authorizations, quicker
benefits verification, easier bill reconciliation, and quality/cost comparison tools.
c. What improvements would be most valuable for patients, providers, or payers, including
CMS?
● Price Transparency Solutions
●
● For Patients: Personalized, real-time out-of-pocket cost estimates; user-friendly tools to
compare costs and quality across providers; proactive communication of estimated costs
before service.
●
● For Providers: Standardized API access to payer-specific rates; integration of cost
estimates into EHR workflows; reduced administrative burden from cost inquiries.
●
● For Payers (including CMS): Machine-readable, standardized data feeds of rates and
patient liabilities; bulk data access via APIs; improved fraud, waste, and abuse detection;
more accurate payment models through real-time pricing data.
d. What would further motivate solution development?

● To improve price transparency, mandate standardized APIs (FHIR-based), strengthen enforcement
with penalties, incentivize early API adoption, consider centralized data repositories, clearly define
and prioritize "patient out-of-pocket cost," and require transparent pricing for all related service
components.
