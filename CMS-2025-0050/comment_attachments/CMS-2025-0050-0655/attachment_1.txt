Introduction to Vitality Group
Vitality Group is a global leader in behavior-based health improvement, operating in 41
markets worldwide and serving over 40 million members. At Vitality, our mission is
simple: To Make People Healthier. This aligns deeply with the administration’s new
initiative to Make America Healthier Again, which rightly emphasizes the root causes of
chronic illness—our behaviors. As CMS and other federal agencies reorient toward
prevention-first policies, Vitality’s global experience shows what’s possible when
incentives, digital tools, and care models support sustainable behavior change at scale.
Over 20 years ago, Vitality pioneered the Shared-Value Health Insurance (SVHI) model,
which aligns incentives across insurers, providers, patients, and society to address the
root causes of illness by rewarding healthy behaviors and promoting prevention.
Implemented at leading insurers globally, the SVHI model integrates behavioral
science, data-driven analytics, and financial rewards to create a virtuous cycle of better
health and lower costs.
Vitality programs leverage gamification (e.g., points, status levels), wearable tracking,
and personalized health goals to engage members in sustained health behavior
change. This behavioral-science approach—anchored in frequent feedback, attainable
targets, and real-world rewards like cash-back or premium discounts—has proven
effective across diverse populations. Our global experience has demonstrated our ability
to drive significant increases in physical activity (by up to 200% - as proven by an
independent RAND study on over 400,000 lives), preventive screening (by 34% on

I. Empowering Patients and Caregivers (PC-1 through PC-7)
CMS’s recognition that upstream behaviors drive downstream costs is timely and
correct. The current medical system is extraordinarily effective at treating disease but
less effective at preventing it. If Medicare is to achieve real sustainability, policy must
shift toward engaging members in behaviors that reduce the need for treatment in the
first place. Vitality has demonstrated, globally and in the U.S., that sustained
improvements in physical activity, medication adherence, and preventive screening are
achievable with the right behavioral architecture.
This is why we applaud CMS’s focus on patient and caregiver empowerment through
digital tools. Our experience shows that a “frictionless” digital front door is essential.
Beneficiaries and their caregivers need a unified portal or app where all relevant data –
claims, clinical records, benefits, and personal wellness data – converge. For example, a
modern Medicare portal should integrate Blue Button claims data with electronic
medical records and patient-generated data (such as activity or nutrition logs from
consumer apps). Vitality recommends that CMS require this kind of open, secure API-
based entry point (analogous to a “Plaid for health” aggregator), which would let
patients import data from wearables and independent health apps into a single
dashboard.
• Integrated Data and Analytics: Medicare beneficiaries benefit from seeing
clinical, claims, and personal health data in one place. Vitality’s global programs
show the value of combining traditional medical data with behavioral and
biometric inputs. For instance, tracking step counts, nutrition, sleep, and blood
pressure alongside lab results enables proactive care planning. We suggest that

CMS expand APIs (beyond Blue Button 2.0) to include real‐time biometric
streams, behavioral data, and patient-reported outcomes. This could also cover
scheduling tools (appointments, screenings) and cost transparency, helping
patients “know before they go.”
• Personalized Guidance and Motivation: Digital tools should do more than
display data – they should translate it into action. Vitality’s approach uses
personalized goals and nudges to move members toward preventive care (e.g.
screenings, vaccinations) and healthy behaviors. CMS could foster similar
functionality by enabling apps that provide tailored care plans and decision
support. For example, an app could alert a diabetic beneficiary to an upcoming
A1c test and reward completion. By linking such workflows to Medicare
notifications (e.g. annual wellness visits reminders), CMS can ensure these digital
assistants are integrated into standard care processes.
• User-Centric Design and Accessibility: Digital health products must be easy to
use for older adults and those with limited tech experience. Vitality has
demonstrated that well-designed wellness programs can engage people of all
ages and incomes. Key features include simple, large-font interfaces, clear user
guidance, multilingual support, and accessible tutorial help. Apps should allow
alternate interaction modes (e.g. telephone support or mailed-in forms) for those
without smartphones. CMS can drive adoption by encouraging development of
senior-friendly apps (perhaps via design challenges or certification of “silver-
friendly” features) and by incorporating feedback from beneficiaries with
disabilities.
• Streamlined Consent and Data-Sharing: We encourage CMS to simplify
permission processes for patients and caregivers. Complex HIPAA forms or
confusing privacy notices deter usage. Instead, CMS could offer standardized,
tiered consent options (e.g. “share with my family doctor,” “share with my digital
health app”) that are easy to grant and revoke. Allowing beneficiaries to share
records and messages effortlessly – for example, through a proxy portal – would
reduce administrative barriers and increase trust. In short, regulatory policy
should mandate beneficiary-controlled data sharing, so that apps and services
can safely connect to Medicare data without onerous paperwork.
By emphasizing these areas – a unified digital access point, comprehensive data
(including wellness metrics), intelligent care guidance, and accessible design – CMS can
greatly enhance patient and caregiver empowerment. Vitality’s own platforms have
validated these concepts globally, and we stand ready to help CMS pilot such tools.
II. Enabling Value-Based Care (VB-1 through VB-4)
Value-based models and digital patient tools are naturally synergistic. Vitality’s shared-
value framework is a form of value-based care: by aligning incentives, it systematically
prevents costly illness, benefiting both patients and payers. In Medicare APMs (ACOs,
MSSP, etc.), similar alignment can be encouraged:

• Incentivize Digital Engagement: CMS can encourage APMs to adopt patient-
facing digital tools by incorporating engagement into APM incentives (VB-1). At
present, providers are only awarded based on specific actions, unless fully
capitated. Many digital tools, like Vitality, create a holistic intersection of
wellness, benefit navigation, point of care options, and longitudinal health
improvement traced across biometrics, health, and behavioral data. This
engagement is highly valuable and holistically represents foundational health
behavior change in the spirit of MAHA. Incorporating APM incentives for digital
engagement in vetted health apps is therefore a means by which CMS can take
a prevention-first, holistic health stance. For example, shared-savings bonuses
could account for metrics like Medicare wellness-app use or completing a
structured diabetes prevention coaching course. Capitalizing on CMS’s existing
focus on social determinants and population health, plans could receive extra
credit for demonstrating robust digital member engagement (echoing how
Vitality rewards member participation). This would signal to ACOs that investing
in wellness platforms and targeted digital health solutions is valued and would
encourage investment in such tools.
• Essential IT Capabilities: Modern APMs need advanced analytics and
integration. Vitality’s programs use data-driven risk stratification: continuously
updated health and biometric data refine an individual’s risk profile. CMS should
ensure APMs have tools for care planning, event notification (e.g. hospital
admission alerts), and quality measurement that incorporate patient-generated
data. We recommend requiring CEHRT (Certified EHR) flexibility that explicitly
includes these functions. For instance, extensions to CMS’s “Advance APM”
technical requirements could recognize care coordination apps and wellness
platforms as valid APM tools. In practice, this could take the form of technical
guidelines for FHIR-based interfaces to third-party health apps, or special
attestations for digital wellness integrations.
• Broad Data Needs: Participation in VBC demands more than claims data. CMS
should encourage APMs to integrate biometric and behavioral data into their
analytics, data which is valuable but difficult to access (VB-4). Access to
continuous measures (activity levels, blood glucose from home monitors,
medication adherence trackers, etc.) would sharpen risk adjustment and care
management. For example, in Vitality’s Active Rewards programs, linking
wearable data (steps, heart rate) to incentives has driven sustained activity
increases that drive down risks and healthcare costs. CMS could promote pilot
programs that allow ACOs or Medicare Advantage plans to ingest such data
(with consent) and incorporate it into performance scoring. In short, the
“essential data types” for VBC should explicitly include wellness and self-tracking
data alongside traditional claims and lab values.
• Open APIs and Interoperability: Consistent with our “digital front door”
recommendation, a Plaid-style API layer for health data would benefit VBC
models. A nationwide directory of FHIR endpoints (VB-15) combined with open

III. Behavior Change as a National Health Strategy
The health challenges facing the United States today—from chronic disease to spiraling
costs—stem not only from gaps in access or care quality, but from an unaddressed crisis
of behavior. As Administrator Oz and Secretary Kennedy have emphasized, the nation’s
health cannot be restored through treatment alone. It must be rebuilt by changing
what people do every day. That shift—toward activity, prevention, nutrition, and self-
care—demands a new class of benefit design, measurement, and incentives.
Vitality was founded on that principle. Our global experience offers proof that when
behavior is treated as the core unit of health, systems improve. CMS has an opportunity
to lead that transition—by removing the frictions that prevent behavior-driven models
from scaling in Medicare. Doing so will align federal priorities with decades of behavioral
science and open the door to healthier, longer lives for millions of Americans.
IV. Vitality’s Role in Shaping a Frictionless Medicare Future
Vitality submits this feedback to offer expertise and partnership to the CMS for the
rulemaking process – a demonstration of our commitment to constructive policy
development. In highlighting global successes (e.g. in South Africa, the UK, and
employer-sponsored U.S. plans), we aim to show what is already possible. At the same
time, we recognize and emphasize the structural barriers that have so far slowed similar
innovation in the U.S. system (for example, outdated MLR accounting rules, siloed data
ecosystems, and restrictive consent workflows).
Vitality’s Shared-Value model illustrates that aligning incentives for prevention can both
bend the cost curve and improve outcomes. We urge CMS to consider how regulatory
frameworks can remove friction – such as by permitting broad data access (including
behavioral metrics) and requiring open, user-friendly interfaces. Policymakers could also
pursue demonstration projects (in traditional Medicare, MA, or MSSP) that embed
wellness incentives into care, as was recommended in our recent written congressional
testimony to the House Ways and Means Subcommittee Hearing on Modernizing
