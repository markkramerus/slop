Dear Administrator Oz and Assistant Secretary Keane:
On behalf of the Healthcare Information and Management Systems Society (HIMSS), we
are pleased to provide public comments on the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid
Services (CMS) and Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy/Office of the National
Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ASTP) Health Technology Ecosystem
Request for Information (RFI).
HIMSS is a global advisor, thought leader and member-based society committed to
reforming the global health ecosystem through the power of information and
technology. As a mission-driven non-profit, HIMSS offers an unique depth and breadth
of expertise in health innovation, public policy, workforce development, research, and
analytics to advise global leaders, developers, end users, patients, and influencers on
best practices in health information and technology driven by health equity. Through
our innovation engine, HIMSS delivers key insights, education and engaging events to
healthcare providers, governments, and market suppliers, ensuring they have the right
information at the point of decision. HIMSS serves the global health information and
technology communities with focused operations across North America, Europe, the
United Kingdom, the Middle East, and Asia Pacific. Our members include more than
127,000 individuals, 480 provider organizations, 470 non-profit partners, and 650 health
services organizations. Our global headquarters is in Rotterdam, The Netherlands and
our Americas headquarters is in Chicago, Illinois.
To accelerate digital health transformation, we must foster seamless, secure,
ubiquitous, and systemwide data access and interoperable health information
exchange. Interoperable information exchange enables the right people to have the
right access to the right health information in an usable format at the right time to
deliver optimal care. Health information and technology serve as the catalyst for
transforming the health ecosystem, modernizing care delivery, driving health

innovation, improving efficiency, and enabling health research. Actionable information
that helps identify the best pathway for each patient is critical to help Make America
Healthy Again.
The following are HIMSS’s responses to the questions posed in this RFI:
What are the most promising areas for digital health innovation that could benefit
Medicare beneficiaries and how can CMS and other agencies foster the development
and adoption of effective and equitable digital health solutions?
In a recent HIMSS study, hospitals with advanced digital maturity demonstrated stronger
quality and safety outcomes. These hospitals can all mobilize data from multiple
external sources, automate the tracking of patient progress and health outcomes, and
engage patients directly with their health information. Hospitals with advanced digital
maturity were also more likely to have more advanced safety structures and systems,
and stronger quality and safety outcomes in support of workforce capacity challenges.
This wide array of digital health interventions, alongside accessible and accurate
information can significantly impact patient care outcomes and quality of life. Many
care settings (small provider offices, clinics, etc.) are still trying to achieve a higher
degree of digital sophistication.
Technologies that have shown promise in helping all care settings include:
Artificial Intelligence
Artificial intelligence (AI) drives numerous applications that offer the potential to
improve patient outcomes, accelerate early detection of disease, and enhance point
of care, administrative and operational efficiency. AI tools are positioned to drive
innovation and digital transformation.
For effective deployment and use of AI to improve the health and human services
sector, the broader digital health infrastructure must be supported in sustainable ways.
AI and related technologies rely heavily on a robust and mature digital infrastructure
that can produce quality information and inform health and care decisions.
Artificial intelligence is utilized in various ways across healthcare organizations.
According to the 2024 HIMSS Healthcare Cybersecurity Survey, 37% of respondents
(representing healthcare cybersecurity professionals) reported using AI for technical
tasks like support and data analytics, 35% for clinical services such as diagnostics, and
cybersecurity and administrative tasks (each 34%). AI is also used in healthcare
operations (31%), research (24%), patient engagement (21%), and clinical training
(16%). Additional use cases are anticipated in the future as AI rapidly advances. HIMSS
2024 AI in Healthcare survey also shows that AI progress is greatest in larger, better-
resourced organizations, with half of larger healthcare organizations reporting AI
2

projects in the early implementation stages versus only one-quarter of smaller
organizations. These data correlate with digital maturity, with larger organizations
demonstrating higher values.
Policies and regulatory frameworks should find a balance that promotes and
accelerates the responsible deployment and use of safe and trusted AI demonstrated
to benefit patients, providers, and other participants in the health ecosystem, while
ensuring that AI is continually monitored and revalidated following deployment in the
field. Specifically, the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) should consider
the HIMSS Global Artificial Intelligence Policy Principles and HIMSS’s response to the
White House Office of Science and Technology Policy Artificial Intelligence Action Plan
RFI when developing a regulatory framework for the deployment and use of AI. At
minimum, HHS should adopt policy frameworks that:
• Leverage policy guardrails based on specific use case and risk level;
• Focus both on the development cycle and the revalidation of AI models once
they have been deployed and have evolved after being exposed to local data;
and
• Ensure AI and other automated tools should, at a minimum, not amplify harmful
biases, and at best, help address any biases already inherent in health data
In addition, HIMSS encourages HHS to monitor the growing divide between large, well-
resourced health systems and small organizations, particularly those organizations with
small margins that care for at-risk and underserved communities and take steps to
ensure that the benefits of AI are available to healthcare organizations across the entire
spectrum of care.
Telehealth
Telehealth is a safe, effective and essential method of expanding access to care
delivery — particularly to specialists — for countless at-risk patients, including those in
rural and underserved communities. Telehealth can drive operational efficiency,
reduce the burden of the care team, shape patient engagement, and improve quality
and safety. Telehealth, especially when used for mental health services, plays a key role
in managing public health, maintaining continuity of care, and ensures the
sustainability of health care.
The alignment of reimbursement and incentives to expand the deployment and use of
telehealth as a tool to improve care access is critical to digitally transforming
healthcare. The temporary regulatory changes that enabled telehealth services to be
properly reimbursed by Medicare should be extended until Congress can permanently
ensure clinicians can provide millions of Americans access to high-quality virtual care.
3

Finally, HIMSS encourages HHS to work with Congress to clarify reimbursement for
telehealth services. The lack of certainty that telehealth services will be reimbursed at
the same rate as in-person services is putting a strain on providers.
Data Visualization
Providers now have more data and information to drive healthcare decision-making
than at any point in history. The exponential growth of available information has
created a challenging environment for providers as they struggle to identify the key
performance indicators that should have the most impact on patient care decisions.
Currently, much of the information that is generated by health systems is not truly
mobilized to inform decisions. Analytics in the healthcare sector are relatively
underdeveloped compared to other business sectors. Actionable insights from
information lead to impactful results. HIMSS’s 2024 AI Survey shows analytics as one of
the three primary use cases organizations are considering or have implemented AI
solutions. Therefore, public policy should incentivize the use of visualization tools to drive
better care outcomes and performance.
HIMSS developed the Analytics Maturity Assessment Model (AMAM) to support and
guide organizations to realize the full potential of analytics to strengthen their data-
driven decision-making at the patient and enterprise levels. AMAM begins with
foundational requirements of data, digital infrastructure and governance to support
these health organizations advance their digital health transformation and enable
successful adoption of advanced analytics and AI to help achieve organizational
priorities and outcomes.
By encouraging organizations to benchmark their analytics and other measures with
such tools, we all can gain a stronger understanding of the roadmap each organization
will need to follow to achieve transformation.
Digital Quality Measurement
Since 2012, HIMSS has actively requested that CMS support the proliferation and use of
electronic clinical quality measures (eCQMs). The core of HIMSS’s mission is promoting
the use of information and technology to improve the quality of healthcare delivery
through effective performance measurement and clinical decision support. HIMSS’s
recommendations have reinforced that eCQMs provide a more meaningful and
actionable measure of care quality than traditional claims-derived quality measures.
Corresponding recommendations have focused on ensuring eCQMs are appropriately
tested to ensure accuracy in all care settings, drive improvements in patient outcomes,
and are not overly burdensome to collect within a normal clinical workflow.
With CMS focused on transitioning all reporting programs to digital quality measures
(dQMs), HIMSS looks forward to continuing to engage with CMS to ensure measures are
4

feasible, accurate, actionable, and not overly burdensome to collect. HIMSS supports
CMS’ goals of transitioning all CMS quality reporting programs to leverage digital health
technology for less burdensome and more accurate results. These goals should occur
with less latency in reporting through secondary use of clinical data captured and/or
mapped to common national FHIR data standards via a common standards-based
dQM framework) As HIMSS represents the largest community of digital health and
quality measures implementers, HIMSS is launching a diverse Digital Measure Adoption
Work Group with representation from developers, providers, measure developers, and
payers to create guidance to support the widespread adoption of dQMs and remove
adoption barriers.
CMS must address significant barriers before a complete transition to FHIR API-enabled
dQM-reporting can take place. These barriers include, but are not limited to:
• A lack of sufficient eCQM’s/dQM’s availability for many clinical specialties and a
corresponding high cost of between $1 million and $2 million per measure for
development, testing, and endorsement, and a slow development timeline
ranging from two to seven years
• The lack of a compelling business case for diverse end user participation in real
world testing of the measures before including them in a CMS program
• CMS currently requires ASTP health IT certification for all data sources used in
eCQM calculations. While this is valuable for clinical data quality, it should not
apply to the subset of financial/billing data used to supplement clinical data in
accurate dQM calculations. Those data should be acceptable if they comply
with the UB-04 standards for hospital billing and CMS-1500 for provider billing
standards.
• The developer and implementer communities need clarification regarding a
singular national standard for dQM’s. An unambiguous resolution on whether HL7
FHIR Qi-Core or USCDI+ Quality will be used as the national dQM implementation
standard. A clear rationale and process to harmonize the two are necessary in
case both standards are required to support certification and technical
implementation guide use cases.
• The fundamental issue of assuring data quality that feeds accurate dQM
calculation engines and its reporting still has not been fully addressed.
HIMSS recommends that CMS take the following actions:
1) Collaborate with ASTP to launch a pathway to merge the clinically nuanced
data elements available in USCDI+ quality domain with future versioning of
USCDI. Accordingly, CMS should announce the timeline for sunsetting legacy
reporting formats, including QRDA, and align these timelines with adoption
milestones for FHIR-based dQM across all programs. This will ensure smoother
transitions and reduce the burden associated with reporting using multiple
methodologies. This will require some level of government funding or

sustainability training for providers and health systems. If the government is not
prepared to provide funding, HHS should partner with industry to educate
provider and health system constituencies on non-government sustainability
resources.
2) While the development and feasibility testing of future versions of USCDI is taking
place, a small number of dQMs are currently available that use underlying data
elements that are found in the latest versions of USCDI. HIMSS recommends that
those measures be made available as voluntary reporting measures, with scoring
bonuses to eligible hospitals and critical access hospitals who choose to report
those measures. Examples include (add language when available.)
3) Provide funding support for pilot projects to ensure that the testing cohort for
new and/or substantively updated eCQMs and dQMs include a significant
sample size, including large and small hospitals across a wide geographic and
patient demographic spectrum.
4) Provide a significant scoring bonus for hospitals participating in testing in the
Inpatient Quality Reporting program and newly developed value-based care
models. This heightens critical access hospitals, rural hospitals, and community
hospitals opportunities to receive incentive program dollars.
5) Implement a requirement for CMS-funded future measure development and
testing contracts to allocate sufficient funding to facilitate testing, mapping, and
implementation work for field testing at testing sites. As mentioned in the first
recommendation, if the government is not prepared to fund the improvements,
then HHS should work with providers and health systems to train them on how to
find sustainable resources.
What safeguards are needed to ensure the responsible and ethical use of digital health
technologies in Medicare?
Advancing cybersecurity, information security, physical security, and information
privacy measures in healthcare organizations serving Medicare beneficiaries should be
supported to: 1) protect the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of patient
information and other sensitive information and assets of participants in the health
ecosystem, 2) ensure the continued and effective delivery of patient care and
coordination of care, 3) protect patient safety and privacy, and 4) further the delivery
of safe, secure, and effective care delivery and coordination.
HIMSS suggests leveraging the 2024 HIMSS Healthcare Cybersecurity Survey Report for
insights into the necessary cybersecurity safeguards. In addition, earlier this year, HIMSS
responded to Office for Civil Rights HIPAA Security Rule proposed rule. HIMSS
emphasized the importance of different approaches to make security, risk assessment
and documentation requirements more scalable to the security needs of small
practices and practices caring for rural and underserved communities and their
business associates.
6

Building upon HIMSS’s framework for assessing Digital Determinants of Health, CMS
should require any beneficiary-facing application or AI-enabled service to demonstrate
basic accessibility and clear usability. Conformance with Section 508 of the
Rehabilitation Act, plain-language materials validated at or below a sixth-grade
reading level, and culturally appropriate instructions will help ensure that no eligible
beneficiary is left behind because of disability or low digital literacy. Embedding these
requirements in coding or reimbursement criteria will advance consistent quality of care
and reduce avoidable program waste.
Consistent with the HIMSS AI/ML Public Policy Principles, specifically Principle 7 on
Human Oversight and Transparency, Principle 3 on Clinician and Consumer Trust, and
Principle 4 on Monitoring and Evaluation, CMS should require that any high-stakes
digital health functionality (e.g., diagnostic decision support, therapy modification, or
automated prior authorization) include a clearly documented human-in-the-loop
checkpoint and an auditable manual-override pathway. This safeguard preserves
clinician accountability and patient autonomy while permitting automation to
streamline administrative workflows and improve throughput. Moreover, by mandating
human oversight and systematic performance monitoring, CMS would operationalize
HIMSS’s call for explainable tools that maintain fair, robust performance across
beneficiary populations and provide a feedback loop for real-world safety signals.
CMS should move toward a policy that ties reimbursement to open, standards-based
APIs (FHIR R5, SMART on FHIR), adherence to information-blocking prohibitions, and
transparent data-portability metrics. Such requirements support competition, facilitate
beneficiary choice, and prevent costly data silos.
What are the current barriers to data interoperability that hinder the effective use of
digital health products and the sharing of health information among stakeholders?
While cost, workflow complexity, data quality, standard-variance, misaligned clinical
priorities, and even data-blocking behaviors are all real-world impediments, these are
symptoms of a deeper economic misalignment. Interoperability remains under-funded
precisely because private health systems and electronic health record (EHR) vendors
are not compensated for delivering what is a public good. These barriers include:
• Economic Misalignment Under current payment models, hospitals and health
systems absorb the full cost of implementing, maintaining, and operating data
exchange infrastructure with little corresponding revenue or subsidy. As
Medicare and Medicaid reimbursement rates drift farther below true cost of
care, the ability to invest in exchange platforms, data-mapping efforts, and care
coordination workflows is increasingly constrained, and CMS’ programs for
incenting interoperability lose their effectiveness. Without direct subsidies or a
sustainable business model for data exchange, true plug-and-play
interoperability remains commercially unattractive.
7

• Technical Barriers Are Nearing a Margin-Zero Problem Thanks to limitless cloud
compute, mature API-first platforms, and rapidly advancing AI-based
transformation tools, the “last mile” of unifying disparate documentation formats,
mapping legacy clinical codes, and normalizing structured and unstructured
data can now be largely automated at near-zero marginal cost. In the long run,
there is the potential that generative AI and natural-language-understanding
engines will render format translation and terminology reconciliation effectively
invisible, eliminating the need for data dictionaries or expensive custom
interfaces. Until such time, standards-based information access should be
promulgated by HHS.
Regarding the effective use of digital health tools, CMS should recognize that effective
use of digital tools among older Americans often hinges on live support. A 2021 study
shows that live, community-based coaching meaningfully improves older adults’ digital
engagement and cuts avoidable utilization. In HIMSS Member University of California,
San Francisco’s Video Visits for Elders Project, 18.7 percent of patients ≥ 65 requested
technical coaching and, once assisted, the share completing their appointment by
video more than doubled to 40 percent of those reached. A 2023 meta-analysis of
seven trials found face-to-face digital literacy training significantly improved eHealth
literacy among older adults. Complementing these engagement gains, a randomized
controlled trial in a Massachusetts safety-net system showed that 30-day patient-
navigator support reduced readmissions by four percentage points in patients over 60,
demonstrating tangible downstream savings from navigator programs. HIMSS urges
CMS and ASTP to pilot coverage for community-based “digital health navigators,” for
example, through expanded chronic-care management codes. Hands-on assistance
will help seniors adopt technology confidently, improving engagement and reducing
downstream costs from misuse or non-use.
What steps can be taken to improve data exchange and integration across different
health information systems?
HIMSS supports integration between all interoperability approaches, entities, and trusted
exchange frameworks, including current standards-based approaches, cloud scale
and AI-driven data harmonization when demonstrated to be effective, to reduce
technical barriers to interoperability. HIMSS urges CMS and ASTP to rebalance
incentives, through targeted subsidies, procurement policy, and AI-friendly certification
so that information exchange becomes both technically and economically self-
sustaining.
8

HIMSS recommends ASTP and CMS act on the following:
1. Introduce Targeted Exchange Subsidies: Consider a time-limited add-on to
existing quality or care-coordination payments that explicitly cover the costs of
data sharing, thereby removing the risk for vendor and provider investment.
2. Encourage Vendor Neutrality Through Procurement Rules: Incorporate data-
portability requirements and AI-readiness criteria into Certified EHR
reimbursement policy, so that future certification cycles reward open, cloud-
native, AI-compatible designs, at least for standard API and interface
implementations.
3. Leverage AI to Lower Technical Friction: Fund phased programs that
demonstrate how generative AI pipelines can automatically harmonize clinical
content across settings and use those pilots to validate financial models that tie
reimbursement directly to interoperable data exchange.
4. Monitor and Adjust Over Time: Establish clear outcomes metrics for data-
exchange uptake and ROI and commit to sunset dates on subsidies or add-ons
as the market matures and AI-enabled automation reduces per-transaction
costs.
5. Test and Certify: Programs should be incorporated into policy frameworks to
increase market confidence in the interoperability of health information. These
programs should be developed with extensive input from relevant subject matter
experts and impacted parties.
Information Exchange with Public Health
As part of the push to democratize data, public health needs to be included as a
stakeholder. Notably, the RFI requests feedback from multiple perspectives, but not
from public health. CMS and ASTP should continue to coordinate with the Centers for
Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and state, territorial and local agencies to
ensure public health data systems are modernized and public health and health care
organizations are exchanging critical information, to more efficiently improve
community health and wellbeing, prevent disease, and respond to emergencies. In
addition, HIMSS encourages ongoing investments in HIE and interoperability
infrastructure, exemplified by ASTP’s State HIE Cooperative Agreement and Medicaid
HITECH 90/10 Federal Financial Participation.
Many states are developing and remodeling their data governance and exchange
services to support broader community needs. Utilizing existing technical and
relationship infrastructures across states to bring together personally identifiable health
data, as well as population and public health data may support community-based
health interventions for chronic disease, and improve well-being based on the
application of robust analytics. HIMSS’s members have consistently shared that there is
a need for this; however, challenges remain, including low incentives and the need for
9

more resources. HIMSS leads a Public Health Data Modernization Advisory and
Community for members to share best practices and recommendations in this regard.
Interoperability with public health entities poses unique challenges that require novel
solutions. HIMSS recommends no longer prescribing one-size-fits-all standards for each
of the 4,000+ state, local, tribal and territorial public‐health agencies and 3,000+
hospitals to replace, modify, or reconfigure their systems. Instead, HIMSS urges HHS to
build on the success of the Pandemic Ready Interoperability Modernization Effort, by
deploying a lightweight, cloud-native normalization layer, like the AI-enhanced FHIR
translator the current DOGE Administrator, Amy Gleason, and the U.S. Digital Service
team prototyped for COVID reporting. With this kind of infrastructure, HHS can meet
every jurisdiction where they are, ingesting CSVs, HL7 v2 feeds, PDFs and more, and
dynamically harmonizing them into a single canonical format. This approach avoids the
prohibitively expensive and infeasible mandate of having thousands of disparate
organizations adopt identical APIs and instead creates a resilient, demand-driven
bridge to CDC/NIH analytics delivering low-marginal-cost interoperability without
waiting years for universal standard adoption.
