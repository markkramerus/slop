 June 16, 2025 Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services Department of Health and Human Services Attention: CMS-0042-NC 7500 Security Boulevard Baltimore, MD 21244-1850 RE: Request for Information: Health Technology Ecosystem Dear Administrator Oz: On behalf of The Wright Centers for Community Health (TWCCH) and Graduate Medical Education (TWCGME) (collectively referred to as “The Wright Center”), thank you for your leadership in strengthening America’s health technology ecosystem and working toward a future where health information truly serves patients, supports clinicians, and closes gaps in care. TWCCH is a Federally Qualified Health Center (FQHC) Look-Alike, an Essential Community Provider of safety-net primary health services, an Opioid Use Disorder Center of Excellence, and a Ryan White HIV/AIDS provider. TWCCH serves approximately 40,000 patients, operating thirteen primary care community health centers (CHCs) throughout Northeastern Pennsylvania, inclusive of a school-based health center and a mobile medical and dental unit (“Driving Better Health”). TWCCH serves as the cornerstone ambulatory whole person primary care delivery organizational member of our Teaching Health Center (THC) Graduate Medical Education Safety-Net Consortium (GME-SNC) operated by our affiliated entity, TWCGME, our Sponsoring Institution accredited by the Accreditation Council on Graduate Medical Education. Together with GME-SNC stakeholders, The Wright Center trains over 250 primary care residents and fellows in a community-based, needs-responsive, interprofessional workforce development model to advance our shared mission to improve the health and welfare of our communities through inclusive and responsive health services and the sustainable renewal of an inspired, competent workforce that is privileged to serve. Our Graduate Medical Education Safety-Net Consortium is community owned and governed, with a fiduciary responsibility for high-integrity stewardship of federal resources, including those from CMS, the Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) THC, and Veteran Affairs GME programs, as well as multi-payor clinical revenues. Certification Must Guarantee Real-World Usability Health centers like ours serve some of the nation’s most historically underserved and medically complex patients, many of whom face significant social and economic barriers. In this context, we see daily the gap between theoretical compliance with certification requirements and the 
 genuine usability of technology in a complex, high-volume ambulatory primary care environment. Technology that tests well in a vendor-controlled setting can and does break down in real-world use, where multiple care team members rely on it simultaneously to deliver whole-person, longitudinal, and integrated care. We want to emphasize our longstanding position that certification must include strong real-world surveillance and practical testing that demonstrates a system’s capacity to perform as advertised in frontline workflows. Systems must ingest, display, reconcile, and export structured data reliably; support high-volume population health reporting; and reduce, not increase, the cognitive burden on providers already operating under workforce shortages. The Wright Center urges ONC to enforce certification standards that prioritize usability in daily practice, ensuring that technology supports, rather than obstructs, the mission of a patient-centered medical home. A Truly Plug-and-Play Marketplace Must Be Affordable and Functional We appreciate that past information blocking rules and interoperability requirements have meaningfully advanced a culture of data sharing across the healthcare landscape. Many organizations are now more willing to exchange patient information. However, significant gaps remain– patient data remains fragmented across multiple systems, driven by countless differing definitions and internal workflows, often requiring providers to log in remotely to outside EHRs rather than accessing information seamlessly in one place. More work must be done to ensure that information is centrally available and universally accessible at the point of care. We strongly support the national commitment to Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) and a standards-based API ecosystem. However, in practice, health centers like ours continue to encounter costly licensing restrictions, proprietary gatekeeping, and incomplete functionality that undermines the vision of an open, competitive plug-and-play marketplace. This is not merely a technical frustration but a real barrier to our ability to implement innovative tools for care coordination, behavioral health integration, social needs screening, and population health management. For this reason, The Wright Center encourages ONC to require that certified systems deliver affordable, robust, production-ready APIs that can easily support third-party apps without hidden fees or legal obstacles. This plug-and-play capability must be verified in real-world conditions, particularly within safety-net delivery settings that have lean IT staffing and depend on modular solutions to meet complex community needs. Social Drivers of Health Data Should Be a Core Standard As a trusted provider of whole-person care, The Wright Center firmly believes that health technology must make it practical and seamless for providers to collect, code, share, and act on data about patients’ social drivers of health. Housing insecurity, food access, transportation, and other social needs shape outcomes far more than any single clinical encounter. Yet our experience has shown that most current systems treat social determinants as optional modules or bolt-on functions, adding cost and complexity instead of supporting integrated care. 
 We urge ONC to make robust support for social needs data exchange a baseline expectation of certification. Certified systems must allow care teams to document standardized social risk factors, integrate screening tools in multiple languages, and enable secure bidirectional exchange with community-based organizations and public health agencies. This is not only critical for patient-centered care but also for fulfilling our responsibilities as community partners addressing upstream determinants of health. Advance Transparent, Timely, and Fair Prior Authorization Processes Opaque and inconsistent prior authorization processes persist as one of the most damaging barriers to timely care for our patients and a major source of unnecessary administrative burden for our care teams. The Wright Center applauds the inclusion of prior authorization requirements in the scope of the Patient Access API and Provider Access API and strongly supports extending this to include prescription drugs, which are increasingly subject to restrictive and costly prior authorization hurdles. We emphasize that patients deserve clear, timely, and actionable information about their prior authorization status, reasons for denials, and pathways for appeal. Providers equally need visibility to plan appropriate care and prevent treatment delays. We urge CMS and ONC to maintain strong mandates for timely decision-making and require payers to publish key metrics such as the percentage of approvals and denials, average time to decision, and frequency of peer-to-peer reviews. Standardized timeliness expectations for both standard and expedited requests should apply consistently across all network types to ensure that a patient’s insurance coverage does not dictate how quickly they receive needed care. We continue to strongly support policies that promote gold carding for providers with a demonstrated history of compliance, which would reduce the unnecessary volume of prior authorization requests and free up precious provider time for patient care. Finally, we urge CMS to standardize the appeals process and provide clear guidance for how and when payers must respond to appeals so that patients do not suffer from preventable delays while navigating administrative barriers. Align Technology and Data with Patient Attribution and Value-Based Care Reliable, transparent patient attribution is foundational for health centers like ours that participate in accountable care organizations and other value-based payment arrangements. Misaligned panels and inaccurate attribution create confusion, undercut care coordination, and increase administrative burden for safety-net providers who already operate with limited resources. The Wright Center strongly recommends that ONC and CMS continue to align patient attribution processes across payers, establish clear attribution data standards, and ensure that providers have timely access to up-to-date patient panels. This alignment will enhance our ability to proactively manage population health, close care gaps, conduct targeted outreach for preventive services like Annual Wellness Visits, and succeed in value-based arrangements that reward quality. 
 The Wright Center remains fully committed to partnering with ONC, CMS, and other federal and state leaders to shape a health technology ecosystem that lives up to its promise. We believe deeply that certification must guarantee real-world functionality, that plug-and-play innovation must be affordable and practical, that social drivers of health must be visible and actionable, that prior authorization must be transparent, timely, and fair, and that patient attribution and data must support value-based care delivery. Our patients, care teams, and future physician workforce depend on a technology infrastructure that supports, rather than hinders, our collective mission to deliver whole-person care and close gaps in access. We thank you for the opportunity to provide these detailed recommendations and stand ready to continue contributing our practical insights as a national leader in community-based health care and graduate medical education.
