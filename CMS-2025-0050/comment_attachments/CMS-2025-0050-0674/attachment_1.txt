HMH is the largest, most comprehensive, and truly integrated health care network in New Jersey,

serves.

Overview of HMH’s Comments

HMH strongly supports the goals outlined in the CMS and ASTP/ONC RFI on digital health,
particularly its focus on leveraging innovation to enhance patient care, streamline administrative
processes, and promote efficiencies. As a leading integrated health network actively deploying
artificial intelligence (AI) and digital tools across clinical and operational settings, HMH recognizes
the immense opportunity to improve outcomes for millions of Medicare beneficiaries. We believe
that digital health innovations can be used to reduce provider burden and drive higher-quality, more
personalized care. Our comments reflect real-world experiences in using AI and digital technologies
to transform diagnostics, surgery, care coordination, and administrative workflows.

To unlock digital health’s full potential, HMH supports policies prioritizing seamless data

1. PC-3 & PC-5. Patient Needs, Health Management Apps, and CMS Role in Digital
Health Products

HMH uses AI to support personalized care pathways that reduce hospital readmissions and improve
chronic condition management. For example, Al-powered tools assist in identifying patients at risk
of nonadherence to medications, and help caregivers manage care plans with greater clarity and
predictability. These tools have demonstrated value in supporting Medicare beneficiaries with
complex needs and should be incentivized through reimbursement models and digital quality
measures that recognize patient engagement, care navigation, and caregiver relief.

Many patients and caregivers experience friction due to poor interoperability, identity authentication
hurdles, and digital literacy gaps.? While CMS should not directly endorse or require use of digital
products for clinical use, HMH believes that CMS can support the digital health ecosystem by
advancing user-friendly, secure digital identity solutions that are interoperable across systems and
easy to adopt. Federal policy should ensure these solutions support seamless access to Al-enabled
care tools across provider networks and are based on transparent criteria such as effectiveness in
improving outcomes and accessibility to seniors.

HMH also sees significant value in Al-powered virtual assistants that can help Medicare
beneficiaries and their families understand their care pathways, manage complex medication
regimens, and access timely support for symptom management. These tools not only empower
patients to take a more active role in their health but also free up provider resources for higher-value
clinical activities. CMS can accelerate adoption by establishing clear efficacy and safety benchmarks
for digital health products, incentivizing integration into care workflows, and supporting
reimbursement for digital interventions that demonstrate improved outcomes and reduced caregiver
burden.

2. PR-2& PR-7. Provider Workflows, Burnout, and Responsible AI Use

At HMH,, digital health tools have been pivotal in reducing clinician administrative burden and
optimizing workflow. For example, HMH uses AlI-driven documentation assistants that capture
real-time clinical notes and integrate directly into the EHR, significantly decreasing time spent
charting. Robotic surgery platforms, enhanced by AI, improve procedural precision while reducing
clinician fatigue and patient recovery time. HMH also leverages AI to streamline clinical
decision-making and manage patient flow. These solutions not only reduce burden but also
contribute to better outcomes. CMS should consider exploring ways to account for the measurable
impact of such tools on provider efficiency and well-being. Incentivizing AI applications that
demonstrably reduce screen time, documentation hours, and rework would accelerate their
deployment and uptake.

To integrate AI and digital tools more deeply into care delivery, HMH recommends that CMS
consider a multi-pronged policy approach:
e Establish standardized validation and certification pathways for clinical AI tools to ensure
safety and transparency;
e Provide specific billing guidance and reimbursement pathways for Al-enabled services such
as diagnostics, virtual assistants, and clinical decision support systems;
e Promote API-based EHR integrations using FHIR-native designs to enable plug-and-play
functionality across digital tools; and
e Fund ongoing training and continuing education for clinicians using AI tools, alongside
liability protections for good-faith use of certified systems.

Such policies would collectively reduce risk, improve interoperability, and encourage sustained use
of digital innovations in everyday care.

3. PR- 9. Data Privacy.

To effectively balance patient privacy with access to digital health products and services that offer
transformative health benefits, CMS must modernize existing privacy frameworks to reflect the
unique risks and realities of Al-driven care. Current standards, such as those under HIPAA, were not
designed to address the complexities of high-dimensional data use in AI applications. As AI systems
increasingly rely on large datasets, including data that is technically de-identified, there is a growing
risk that advancements in computing power could allow re-identification of individuals. HMH
strongly recommends modernizing HIPAA to explicitly address AI-related data flows, clarify
requirements for managing re-identification risk, and establish clear guidelines that permit
responsible data use while upholding strong privacy protections. These updates are critical to
ensuring that patients continue to benefit from AI-powered tools, such as predictive models, ambient
technologies, and personalized clinical decision support, without sacrificing the confidentiality of
their personal health information.

HMH further recommends that CMS take a proactive approach to safeguarding patient trust by
requiring enhanced privacy safeguards for AI developers. This includes implementing stronger
requirements for managing sensitive and hard-to-de-identify data types, such as voice recordings or
behavioral biometrics. For AI vendors using de-identified data in product development, privacy
frameworks should go beyond current “Safe Harbor” and “Expert Determination” methods,
requiring adherence to responsible data use agreements and audit mechanisms. In parallel, patients
should have transparent opt-in or opt-out choices regarding the use of their de-identified data for AI
training or research. This consumer choice framework would respect patient autonomy and ensure
that the benefits of digital health innovation are delivered in a way that is ethically sound and
publicly trusted. Taken together, these measures strike an appropriate balance between enabling
innovation and preserving the privacy rights of Medicare beneficiaries.

Conclusion

HMH strongly supports the Administration’s vision to modernize health care through digital
innovation, recognizing that prevention, wellness, and Al-enabled care are essential to building a
healthier America. We applaud efforts by CMS and ASTP/ONC to enhance infrastructure, streamline
data exchange, and reduce administrative burden. AI and digital tools can play a vital role in
improving outcomes, promoting access, and driving smarter care delivery. HMH would welcome the
opportunity to serve as a resource and look forward to continued collaboration to ensure the
responsible, patient-centered deployment of digital health technologies across the industry.
