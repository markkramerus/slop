Our comments to this RFI encompass three buckets of areas that impact Greenway and its

Quality Measurement
From a health IT perspective, frequent updates to quality measures and workflow expectations,
often with limited implementation timelines, create challenges for development and adoption.
More predictable schedules and alignment across programs would help vendors build
sustainable, usable solutions that providers can effectively integrate into care delivery.
PR-8. What are ways CMS or partners can help with simplifying clinical quality data
responsibilities of providers?
As Greenway has long advocated, simplifying clinical quality reporting by reducing duplication
across programs would be a long-awaited development for providers and developers. Alignment

and consolidation across programs on specifications, data requirements, and submission
methods will reduce burden and enable shared infrastructure investments.
Greenway recommends focusing on a smaller set of core quality measures so stakeholders can
more effectively test and refine the standards that support them. Aligning value sets, data
collection methods, submission processes, and how measures are calculated would reduce
unnecessary variation across programs and make reporting more efficient and reliable.
Standards and Interoperability
CMS posits questions about technical barriers for exchanging health data across stakeholders.
As the EHR Association notes in their comments, when considering the full scope of Electronic
Health Information (EHI), including externally referenced content, the technical barriers are
significant. Much data in EHRs is unstructured or lacks standard mappings, limiting its
usefulness in exchange. This is a universal challenge across care settings. Patient matching
and identity resolution also remain persistent obstacles, even within standardized exchange
frameworks.
PR-8a. What would be the benefits and downsides of using Bulk FHIR data exports from EHRs
to CMS to simplify clinical quality data submissions? Can CMS reduce the burden on providers
by performing quality metrics calculations leveraging Bulk FHIR data exports?
EHRA notes that Bulk FHIR exports have the potential to shift reporting burdens away from
providers, but deployment is still limited. While EHRs are certified to support Bulk FHIR, the lack
of industry-wide use, incentives, and operational experience means that uptake remains low.
CMS should focus on creating real-world use cases and policy drivers that compel adoption
before expecting it to be a primary mechanism for quality reporting. Greenway would agree with
this assessment.
PR-6. Is TEFCA currently helping to advance provider access to health information?
Greenway believes that TEFCA marks a major advancement in facilitating provider access to
health information by establishing a scalable and standardized framework. It holds the promise
of bringing together stakeholders from healthcare, public health, research, and technology,
fostering greater consistency and minimizing fragmentation.
TD-8. What are the most effective certification criteria and standards under the ONC Health IT
Certification Program?
Greenway points to several areas that the EHRA cited as effective standards and certification
criteria below – this is representative of the health IT development community at large.
• “Privacy and Security (P&S) criteria have been foundational, providing a consistent
baseline for safeguarding health information and serving as the closest operational proxy
to HIPAA Security Rule certification.
• C-CDA-based criteria and Direct messaging played a critical role in advancing early
interoperability. However, as the industry shifts away from document-based exchange,

the program should focus on modernizing these criteria toward RESTful API-based
exchange, particularly HL7 FHIR.
• Public health criteria, especially for immunization and electronic laboratory reporting,
have supported scalable reporting infrastructure. However, their effectiveness is limited
by the lack of reciprocal obligations on public health agencies (PHAs) to adhere to the
same standards, which leads to fragmentation.
• In recent years, FHIR API criteria, including patient and population services, have been
among the most impactful, supporting ecosystem-wide data access, app development,
and alignment with CMS programs.
The EHR Association encourages ONC to continue refining the certification program to support
modern, scalable, and bidirectional interoperability, with increased accountability across all
participants in the data exchange ecosystem.” Greenway agrees with this.
Health IT capabilities for value-based care arrangements
Effective health IT is essential to advancing value-based care. It must support care coordination,
performance measurement, patient engagement, and data-driven decisions. A major barrier is
the lack of standardization across CMS programs, particularly in quality measurement. Many
value-based care organizations work with multiple EHR systems, making it difficult to normalize,
map, and aggregate data. Inconsistent or overlapping reporting requirements add administrative
burden and can compromise data quality. Accelerating the adoption of digital quality
measurement (dQM), especially through standardized data elements and exchange formats like
FHIR and USCDI, is critical to ensuring timely, accurate insights into care quality and driving
system-wide improvement.
The EHRA cites that an often overlooked capability is bidirectional feedback between CMS and
provider systems, especially for quality performance data. As EHRs shift from being the
calculation engine to the data source in dQM models, providers still expect real-time visibility
into how they’re performing on quality measures. Establishing standardized APIs to display
performance feedback from CMS or third-party aggregators within the EHR dashboard will be
critical.
We strongly join with the EHRA in recommending CMS and ONC actively solicit provider
feedback to identify which capabilities they view as most valuable and actionable. Developer
capabilities are only impactful when aligned with provider priorities, workflows, and the
incentives that influence their behavior.
