2. DIGITAL IDENTITY
TD 3. Regarding digital identity implementation --
TD-3(a): What are the challenges and benefits?
Challenges
Equity and access must be proactively addressed via robust authorization controls and consent
management to effectively achieve patient/caregiver efficiencies. Digital identity systems must
accommodate individuals with limited connectivity, low digital literacy, language barriers, or lack of formal
documentation. Absent inclusive design, these systems risk deepening existing disparities.
Privacy and trust concerns must also be mitigated. Patients may resist identity systems perceived as
intrusive, particularly those using biometrics or centralized databases. Transparent governance, patient-
controlled sharing, and strong privacy protections are essential.
On the technical side, legacy systems often lack native support for OAuth or OpenID Connect, requiring
integration layers or modernization efforts. Small and under-resourced providers may lack the capital or
expertise for implementation, further widening the digital divide.

Governance challenges emerge when digital identity spans organizational boundaries. Credential Service
Providers (CSPs) may apply varying identity proofing standards, and healthcare providers relying on those
credentials may lack visibility into their rigor. This can create ambiguity in determining liability for
authentication errors or unauthorized access. Additionally, urgent care scenarios often require policy
exceptions, further complicating consistent enforcement.
Finally, regulatory alignment is lacking. HIPAA, 42 CFR Part 2, and state laws offer inconsistent guidance
on identity verification. Without federal standards or incentives, adoption remains fragmented and uneven.
Benefits
From a patient perspective, digital identity solutions eliminate the fractured access landscape currently
plaguing the system and eroding patient trust. Rather than managing disparate credentials across
providers, pharmacies, insurers, and health applications, patients could use a single, portable digital
identity throughout their care journey. This enables consistent authentication, delegated caregiver access,
and granular consent management. These features are especially impactful for complex patients or
those with family-based care coordination.
Clinically, accurate patient identification improves safety by reducing record mix-ups and enhances care
quality through access to complete histories. Identity verification during care transitions ensures accurate
medication reconciliation and timely treatment. These capabilities are foundational to safe, coordinated
care and foster maximum patient care impact.
Operationally, digital identity reduces the administrative burden of account recovery, duplicate record
correction, and manual verification. Workflow efficiency increases as patients arrive with pre-verified
identities. Accurate identity also improves revenue cycle performance by reducing registration and claim
processing errors.
From a cybersecurity standpoint, high-assurance credentials reduce vulnerabilities to phishing, credential
stuffing, and account takeovers. They enable better audit trails, fraud detection, and risk governance —
key for HIPAA compliance and protecting sensitive data.
At the system level, consistent identity supports longitudinal patient tracking for value-based care,
enhances public health surveillance, and improves data quality for research. Digital identity is a linchpin of
next-generation interoperability.
TD-3(b): How would requiring digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me,
other NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs) impact cybersecurity and data exchange?
Requiring standardized credentials, such as those issued by CLEAR, Login.gov, or ID.me, would elevate
healthcare’s cybersecurity and interoperability maturity.
Cybersecurity Impacts
AAL2-compliant multi-factor authentication would create a uniform identity security baseline, reducing
phishing, synthetic identity fraud, and unauthorized access. CSPs typically maintain stronger security
controls, centralized monitoring, and more rigorous patch management than many healthcare organizations
or IT system operators can implement independently. Standardization also enhances anomaly detection
and incident response.
Audit trails tied to verified identities improve attribution and forensic analysis. Federated identity models
support cross-organizational threat correlation. Consistent enforcement of access controls across
systems strengthens governance, compliance, and risk posture.

Data Exchange Impacts
The United States lacks a national patient identifier, relying instead on probabilistic matching methods that
are error-prone and contribute to data fragmentation. Federated identity frameworks, anchored in verified
digital credentials, offer a scalable alternative. By enabling cross-organizational identity resolution, they
improve the accuracy of patient matching and reduce redundant or conflicting records.
Trusted credentials eliminate the need for each organization to independently verify identity, streamlining
access to data and services. Consent artifacts and access permissions become more portable,
enforceable, and auditable across systems.
Directory services and patient lookup mechanisms benefit from higher-quality input, improving both
confidence and performance. Smaller providers, which often lack the resources to build identity
infrastructure, gain access to secure exchange networks by relying on federated credentials. Under a
common identity framework, provenance tracking, access auditing, and secure data routing are significantly
enhanced, supporting both interoperability and regulatory compliance.
Risks and Implementation Considerations
CSPs become high-value targets. A compromise could impact many healthcare entities, so risk must be
mitigated through isolation, redundancy, and breach protocols. Real-time verification adds performance
overhead. System migrations and interoperability across CSPs require coordination.
Despite these challenges, requiring standardized credentials would greatly enhance security and
streamline trusted exchange.
TD-3(c): What impact would mandatory use of the OpenID Connect identity protocol have?
Mandating OpenID Connect (OIDC) would unify authentication infrastructure across healthcare.
Operational Benefits
OIDC would standardize workflows, reduce integration costs, and simplify onboarding. Health IT vendors
could align with a consistent protocol, accelerating adoption. Patient experiences would improve with
familiar login flows and intuitive delegation options.
Security and Risk Management
OIDC's token-based authentication supports granular authorization, session control, and token revocation.
It aligns with zero-trust architecture and supports least-privilege enforcement. Threat models become more
consistent, and security testing benefits from established tools.
Governance and Implementation
Transition requires migration support for legacy systems. CMS should implement phased timelines,
reference and sample architectures, testing tools, and certification programs. Governance must define
responsibilities, handle disputes, and enforce compliance.
Stakeholder Engagement
Successful adoption hinges on inclusive planning. Patient advocates, identity federations, developers, and
provider networks should all have input. Collaboration with standards bodies will support healthcare-
specific protocol extensions.

TD 12. Should CMS endorse non-CMS data sources and networks, and if so, what criteria or metrics
should CMS consider?
Summary
CMS should establish a formal, standards-based endorsement framework for non-CMS data sources and
networks. By doing so, CMS can accelerate the development of longitudinal patient records that span
clinical and administrative domains — a foundational requirement for effective value-based care, public
health response, and program integrity oversight. Endorsement is not simply a signal of trust; it is a
mechanism to align market participants with federal priorities such as CMS-0057-F, Trusted Exchange
Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA), and the broader national interoperability agenda.
Strategic Rationale
Fragmented health data ecosystems continue to limit progress on care coordination, data-driven payment
innovation, error detection/reconciliation, and the consistent delivery of high-quality services across
populations. An endorsement framework helps overcome these limitations by encouraging alignment with
national standards, reducing variation in data access, and enabling smaller, under-resourced organizations
to participate on equal footing that fosters an inclusive ecosystem.
CMS should focus on endorsing networks and platforms that enable:
• USCDI (United States Core Data for Interoperability) and FHIR-based exchange (Fast
Healthcare Interoperability Resources), laying the foundation for shared semantics and
standardized payloads.
• Full EHI access, enabling providers and payers to construct rich, longitudinal views of patient
history — including problem lists, labs, medications, and encounter data.
• All-Payer Claims Database (APCD) integration, enhancing CMS’ ability to contextualize
Medicare and Medicaid data within broader population health and utilization patterns.
Together, these components allow CMS to shift from episodic, siloed data environments toward dynamic,
longitudinal records that support both real-time operations and retrospective analytics.

• Accelerates Longitudinal Data Creation
Endorsed networks that support USCDI, TEFCA routing, and payer data aggregation become
critical infrastructure for assembling comprehensive patient records. CMS leadership can bridge
the technical and policy divide between claims and clinical data silos.

TD 13. What new opportunities and advancements could emerge with APIs providing access to the
entirety of a patient’s electronic health information (EHI)?
New Opportunities and Advancements from Full EHI API Access
The transition from limited, siloed access to comprehensive, standards-based API access to a patient’s full
EHI represents a foundational leap in healthcare interoperability. While current standards such as USCDI
lay the groundwork, full EHI access unlocks a broader data fabric that can meaningfully reshape care
delivery, patient empowerment, and healthcare innovation. Core elements include:
1. Longitudinal Care Planning
Access to a patient’s complete EHI allows providers and care coordination systems to understand the full
arc of a patient’s medical history, providing a history across conditions, providers, settings, and time. For
patients with complex, chronic, or rare conditions, this longitudinal context is often essential to appropriate
decision-making. Care gaps, subtle contraindications, and care redundancies become easier to identify
when the full patient narrative is visible through API-connected platforms.
2. AI-Enabled Clinical Decision Support and Risk Stratification
With full EHI access, modern data science and AI tools can analyze unstructured data such as clinical
notes, discharge summaries, and imaging narratives to surface insights that go beyond structured fields.
These tools can flag early signs of deterioration, identify diagnostic inconsistencies, or recommend
personalized therapies based on a patient’s history, capabilities not feasible with a USCDI-only subset.

3. Precision Public Health and Population Risk Management
De-identified, complete EHI datasets accessed through APIs can support next-generation public health
surveillance and targeted population interventions. For example, complete medication histories and
behavioral health narratives can help predict opioid overdose risk or detect emergent chronic conditions at
the community level. This enables payers and regulators to direct interventions based on fine-grained, real-
world data.
4. Research and Real-World Evidence Generation
Full EHI access with patient consent supports scalable, privacy-preserving medical research. Investigators
can incorporate rich, real-world data. This includes lab trends, care team interactions, and historical
context, that would otherwise be inaccessible. This accelerates time-to-evidence in comparative
effectiveness studies and informs policy and coverage decisions with greater precision.
5. Patient Empowerment and Shared Decision-Making
True patient-directed access to full EHI enables individuals to manage their care proactively, share records
across providers, and serve as the orchestrators of their own health data. Tools that integrate with patient-
authorized APIs can consolidate fragmented records into personal health applications, eliminating the
burden of tracking histories manually or requesting PDFs from disparate portals.
TD-13(a): What are the primary obstacles to this?
The technical and policy shifts required to realize full EHI access are significant. While the promise is clear,
the following obstacles must be carefully managed:
1. Technical Barriers
• Legacy Infrastructure: Many EHR systems were not designed for full EHI export via API,
especially when dealing with large volumes of historical, unstructured, or scanned data.
• Unstructured Data Complexity: Notes, PDFs, and imaging metadata are inconsistently coded
and difficult to normalize across systems.
• Performance and Scalability: Delivering complete records on demand introduces bandwidth and
latency concerns, especially under concurrent use.
2. Privacy and Consent Complexity
• Granular Consent Requirements: Full EHI often contains sensitive information (e.g., behavioral
health, reproductive care, social risk factors). Current models struggle to support patient-friendly
consent at the level of individual data elements or contexts.
• Auditability and Risk Mitigation: Increased access must be met with robust audit trails and user
identity assurance to prevent misuse or unauthorized sharing.
3. Operational and Workflow Disruption
• Provider Adoption Challenges: Clinicians and administrators may be hesitant to release full EHI
to external parties without confidence in downstream handling, provenance, and liability coverage.
• Organizational Readiness: Upgrading infrastructure, training staff, and validating data mapping
across full EHI elements represent a significant burden, especially for small or under-resourced
organizations.

4. Data Quality and Governance Concerns
• Variable Quality of Legacy Data: Historical records may include inaccuracies, unverified patient-
reported items, or incomplete clinical context.
• Provenance and Lifecycle Management: As EHI spans years or decades, determining the
custodial source, context of creation, and expiration rules for every data element becomes more
complex.
5. Regulatory Ambiguity
• Overlap with Information Blocking: While the 21st Century Cures Act mandates broad
availability of EHI, definitions, enforcement boundaries, and exceptions, such as those related to
patient harm or privacy, are still evolving. This creates uncertainty for implementers balancing
access with legal risk.
• Interplay with Other Rules: 42 CFR Part 2, HIPAA, and state-level privacy laws may impose
stricter protections on specific data elements, particularly behavioral health and substance use
disorder (SUD) treatment records, than federal interoperability policy assumes. These protections
often include redisclosure restrictions and detailed consent requirements that challenge seamless
data exchange.
• Segmentation and Consent Challenges: To comply with regulations like Part 2, health IT
systems must support the ability to segment sensitive data (e.g., SUD, reproductive health,
behavioral health), manage consent directives, and enforce redisclosure limitations. However, most
FHIR-based interoperability implementations today do not offer native support for such
functionality. This leads to a compliance gap and discourages sharing, even when it is technically
permissible.
Legal and Operational Misalignment
Interoperability policies often assume uniform trust and disclosure frameworks across all health data. In
practice, variable obligations based on data type, origin, or jurisdiction create tension between federal
interoperability objectives and on-the-ground legal compliance.

Strategic Implication
USCDI should serve as a policy anchor and implementation starting point. It defines the floor, not the
ceiling, for interoperability. CMS should encourage phased expansion toward full EHI access through
incentivized pilots, prioritized use cases (e.g., care transitions, value-based payment models), and ongoing
alignment with TEFCA.
Modern data processing (e.g., AI/NLP pipelines, federated learning) now allows for scalable, secure
handling of unstructured EHI elements. As these capabilities mature, the cost-benefit curve is shifting —
with the marginal value of incorporating full EHI increasing while technical barriers are decreasing.
However, realizing the full value of expanded EHI exchange requires more than just payload size. It
demands robust, trustable, fine-grained access controls that honor complex legal, clinical, and patient-
driven restrictions. CMS should actively support implementation of emerging standards such as Data
Segmentation for Privacy (DS4P), OAuth-based consent frameworks, and UDAP Tiered Authorization,
which collectively enable compliant, context-aware data sharing at scale. These tools are critical to ensure
that sensitive data such as substance use disorder records or reproductive health information can be
exchanged in a manner that is both legally permissible and technically secure.
Conclusion
Full EHI API access represents a necessary evolution in health information policy. While USCDI provides a
valuable baseline for structured data exchange, it does not encompass the full scope of information
required for effective care coordination, risk stratification, or patient-facing applications.
CMS should lead in establishing a phased, standards-aligned roadmap for expanding beyond USCDI, one
that incorporates strong privacy protections, real-world operational use cases, and explicit technical
safeguards. This includes support for emerging frameworks such as DS4P, OAuth-based consent
management, and UDAP Tiered Authorization, which collectively enable compliant and patient-directed
exchange at scale.
Equally important is a forward-looking approach to metadata architecture. As data ecosystems grow in
complexity, the ability to assign rich, machine-readable metadata tags will be central not only to privacy
enforcement, but to data provenance, quality tracking, version control, access governance, and AI-
readiness. Metadata is the connective tissue that allows information to be understood, trusted, and reused
across systems and over time. Its thoughtful and consistent application is the next frontier in data
stewardship. CMS should take a leadership role in defining and incentivizing its adoption across health IT
infrastructure.

TD 17. Given operational costs, what role should CMS or ASTP/ONC or both have in ensuring
viability of healthcare data sharing networks, including enough supply and demand, that results in
usage and outcomes?
Healthcare data sharing networks remain essential infrastructure for value-based care, public health
surveillance, patient empowerment, and program integrity. Yet their long-term sustainability is uncertain
without aligned incentives and infrastructure support. CMS and ONC should take complementary, non-
overlapping roles that catalyze network effects, reduce participation barriers, and align payment models to
create persistent demand. The goal is not to operate networks directly, but to ensure the conditions are in
place for markets to function sustainably, consistently, and at scale.
The Nature of the Challenge
Health data networks face inherent market failures. Like roads or the internet backbone, they generate
positive externalities that exceed what individual participants can monetize. Left solely to private actors,
these networks often underperform due to fragmented incentives, lack of scale, or prohibitive onboarding
costs. CMS and ONC are uniquely positioned to correct these failures. This is done not through perpetual
funding, but by shaping markets, reducing friction, and aligning policy with operational realities.
CMS Should Shape Demand and Align Incentives
As the nation’s largest healthcare payer, CMS holds unique leverage to drive sustained network
participation by aligning its policies, payment models, and infrastructure investments. It should use this
position to advance payer-to-payer exchange as a core national capability — essential not only for
portability of coverage and continuity of care, but also for advancing real-time risk adjustment, beneficiary
protection, and program oversight.
To create durable market demand, CMS should:
• Embed data exchange expectations into value-based care models and implement differential
reimbursement to reward providers and plans that contribute complete, high-quality data.
• Incentivize beneficiary participation in longitudinal data sharing, particularly within Medicare
Advantage and Medicaid managed care, using trusted applications and credentialing pathways.
• Support the adoption of shared, standards-based infrastructure, such as directory services,
consent frameworks, and digital credentialing, by embedding them in program requirements and
guidance. While CMS may not operate patient-matching utilities directly, it can strengthen match
quality by advancing digital identity, demographic data standards, and interoperable metadata
practices, enabling local and federated approaches to improve accuracy over time.
• Ensure that CMS systems are designed to leverage common exchange infrastructure for
prior authorization, quality reporting, and coverage determination, thereby avoiding siloed builds
and duplicative data flows.

Shared Responsibilities and Sustained Implementation
The future of health data sharing depends not on federal operation of networks, but on a clear delineation
of responsibilities and sustained collaboration between CMS and ONC. CMS should shape the demand
environment by embedding data exchange into how healthcare is paid for and delivered. ONC should
reduce technical complexity, expand participation, and ensure standards remain fit for purpose as policy
and operational demands evolve.
Together, CMS and ONC must commit to a shared implementation approach that:
• Sets clear expectations for outcomes-based exchange performance
• Ensures that federal programs and technical standards evolve in sync, minimizing friction for
implementers
• Prioritizes alignment across payer and provider networks, so data can follow the patient through
transitions in both coverage and care
• Supports the technical and policy foundations for federated identity, consent enforcement, and
metadata tagging — key enablers of longitudinal record assembly
This model of joint leadership is particularly urgent as CMS begins implementing the Interoperability and
Prior Authorization Final Rule (CMS-0057-F), where payer-to-payer exchange is not only a compliance
obligation but a strategic opportunity to build a more connected, patient-centered system. CMS can lead
the charge in shaping demand and governance while ONC must ensure the infrastructure and standards
are ready for scale.
If implemented with discipline and coordination, this approach will allow networks to evolve from grant-
funded pilots to trusted, market-sustained utilities that support innovation and interoperability, reduce
administrative burden, and deliver meaningful value across public and private sectors.
