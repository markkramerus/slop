Building the Foundation The Financial Case for Universal Connectivity Prepared for Centers for Medicare/Medicaid June 2025
Universal connectivity among all healthcare providers delivers substantial financial benefits, generating billions in direct cost savings and unlocking additional value through improved provider satisfaction, enhanced patient outcomes, and operational efficiencies. Beyond these tangible gains, it paves the way for transformative digital innovation — enabling the U.S. healthcare system to overcome foundational challenges and adopt advanced care delivery models. These models integrate artificial intelligence, virtual care, and digital health solutions, marking a pivotal shift toward a more connected, efficient, and patient -centered future. Most healthcare professionals outside of health systems and physicians are not connected today . This is for many reasons, including lack of incentives and requirements to connect. Total Therapists: •Physical Therapists: 240,000 -250,000 •Occupational Therapists: 150,000 -180,000 •Therapy Assistants: 145,000 -150,000Physical therapy is fundamental to overall healthcare and value -based care because it provides non -invasive, cost - effective interventions that prevent expensive surgeries, reduce opioid dependence, and restore functional independence. Early physical therapy intervention can reduce total healthcare costs by 20 -50% for musculoskeletal conditions while significantly decreasing hospital readmissions and recovery times. For chronic diseases like diabetes, heart disease, and COPD, physical therapy improves cardiovascular fitness and prevents costly complications like falls and fractures, particularly in aging populations. From a value -based perspective, physical therapy addresses both treatment and prevention by maintaining patient mobility, reducing disability, improving workplace productivity, and enabling longer independence, ultimately keeping patients out of expensive acute care settings. When properly integrated into comprehensive care delivery, physical therapy serves as preventive medicine that delivers exceptional ROI while achieving the improved outcomes and reduced costs essential to value -based healthcare success.Why Include Physical Therapy

Total Eye Care Professionals: •Optometrists: 41,000 -47,000 •Ophthalmologists: 17,000 -18,000Eye care is fundamental to overall healthcare and value - based care because vision problems often serve as early indicators of systemic conditions like diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease, making regular eye exams critical for preventive medicine and early intervention. For diabetic patients specifically, timely eye care coordination can prevent blindness —a catastrophic outcome that not only devastates quality of life but also triggers cascading healthcare costs including vision rehabilitation, increased fall risks, depression treatment, and lost productivity. Universally connecting optometrists and incorporating their participation in co -managing patients assists in addressing our overall national provider/physician shortage. Optometrists often see patients more regularly than primary care physicians for routine eye care and can detect systemic diabetes complications through comprehensive eye exams and identify non - compliance through retinopathy progression patterns. Total Dental Professionals: •Dentists: 202,000 •Orthodontists: 10,904 •Endodontists: 5,000 •Periodontists: 6,000Dental care is fundamental to overall healthcare and value - based care because oral health serves as both a gateway and a mirror to systemic health conditions. Poor dental health is strongly linked to serious chronic diseases including diabetes, cardiovascular disease, stroke, and respiratory infections, with periodontal disease acting as both a risk factor and complicator for these conditions. For patients with diabetes, untreated gum disease can make blood sugar control significantly more difficult, while cardiovascular patients face increased risk of heart complications from oral bacteria entering the bloodstream. Dental professionals often serve as the first line of detection for serious conditions like diabetes, cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, and autoimmune disorders through oral manifestations, but without interoperability, these critical findings may never reach the patient's primary care physician or specialist. For diabetic patients, coordinated care between dental and medical providers is essential since periodontal disease can significantly impact blood sugar control, while uncontrolled diabetes increases infection risk and delays healing after dental procedures.Why Include Dental Providers •Hygienists: 215,000 •Assistants: 364,000
