1. Digital Health Applications and Provider Adoption
PR-2. Obstacles in Physician Workflows
A persistent barrier to the development and effective utilization of innovative applications for
physician workflows (such as quality reporting, clinical documentation, and billing) is the
complexity and fragmentation of existing health IT systems. Many healthcare organizations
must maintain large internal IT teams to manage disparate platforms, custom interfaces, and
siloed data repositories. As a result, even high-value digital tools often face significant adoption
hurdles, particularly in resource-constrained settings.
It is significantly easier and more efficient to integrate new functionalities into a single, unified
EHR platform than to manage a multitude of separate applications. However, the current lack of
standardized integration frameworks, inconsistent data definitions, and variable clinical
workflows across vendors severely limits scalability.
CMS can mitigate these challenges by promoting standards-based integration directly within
EHR platforms. Encouraging the adoption of standardized data formats, data quality protocols,
and clearly defined use cases would allow for faster and more consistent implementation of
digital tools. Reducing variability and ambiguity in data expectations would enable developers
and providers alike to focus resources on innovation, rather than custom integration.
PR-3. Importance of EHR Data Accessibility
Interoperable data is foundational to supporting workflows and reducing provider burden. This
requires targeted support for data quality improvement and standardization, which directly
reduces the need for costly and time-consuming data mapping across disparate systems. One
key opportunity is to invest in initiatives that promote consistent coding (e.g., LOINC, SNOMED),
structured clinical documentation, and adherence to national interoperability standards.
Machine learning may also help reduce the mapping and transformation workload, but its
effectiveness depends on a strong foundation of high-quality source data. Reducing the burden
of integration will allow programs to focus more resources on patient engagement and
proactive clinical intervention, rather than on technical infrastructure maintenance.
PR-9. Encouraging Adoption of Digital Identity Credentials
Digital identity is a critical foundation for secure and streamlined health data exchange,
particularly as more patients engage with digital health tools across multiple providers and
platforms. CMS can play a pivotal role in accelerating provider adoption of trusted digital
identity credentials, such as CLEAR, Login.gov, or ID.me, by addressing key operational and
regulatory barriers.
a. What would providers need help with to accelerate the transition to a single set of trusted
digital identity credentials for the patient to keep track of, instead of one for each provider?
Digital identity should be understood as three distinct components: identity verification, patient
matching, and authorization. While Credential Service Providers (CSPs) can effectively verify a
patient’s identity, they cannot—and should not—control patient matching or access

permissions. These steps must remain under the control of covered entities to ensure
compliance with HIPAA and ethical stewardship of protected health information.
For identity verification, Sutter supports an open, innovation-driven ecosystem that allows
providers flexibility in how they implement secure identity verification solutions. Rather than
mandating a single identity provider, CMS should promote standards-based credentialing
practices that integrate with existing provider workflows and health IT systems.
b. How might CMS balance patient privacy with convenience and access to digital health
products and services that may lead to significant improvements in health?
A trusted digital identity framework must be voluntary, secure, and interoperable. Patients
should be clearly informed about how their credentials will be used and how data access is
managed across systems. CMS can enhance adoption by supporting “single sign-on” capabilities
that allow patients to authenticate once and use their credentials across health plans, provider
portals, and approved third-party applications.
PR-10. Implementation and Implications of Digital Identity Credentials
a. What are the challenges and benefits for providers?
Challenges include lack of standardization among credentialing services, security concerns,
potential liability issues, and variability in patients’ ability to access necessary technology or
required documents. However, benefits include enhancing clinical and administrative efficiency
by reducing risk of identity fraud, improving patient matching, and streamlining onboarding
processes.
b. How would requiring their use improve access to health information?
Verified digital credentials could reduce the need for multiple logins and help ensure continuity
of access across provider systems and applications. However, CMS must recognize that identity
verification alone does not guarantee appropriate data access. Authorization workflows must
remain locally governed to protect patient rights and maintain accountability.
c. What are the potential downsides?
Mandatory credentialing could unintentionally exclude vulnerable populations, such as patients
without access to mobile devices or necessary documentation. Additionally, patients may not
fully understand the implications of credential sharing, which could increase privacy risks.
Authorization must remain the responsibility of the covered entity to ensure appropriate
oversight and avoid unintended disclosures or misuse of sensitive health information.
d. What impact would mandatory credentials have on a nationwide provider directory?
Mandatory digital credentials could improve the security and accuracy of interactions with a
national provider directory by enabling authenticated access and reducing the likelihood of
fraudulent submissions. However, this should not come at the cost of increased burden for
smaller providers or under-resourced organizations.
e. How could digital identity implementation improve provider data flow?
Standardized digital credentials can enhance identity matching and consent tracking—both of
which are critical to seamless, secure exchange of patient information. This supports more
efficient coordination of care across settings and systems.

f. Would combining FHIR addresses and identity improve data flow?
Yes. Linking validated digital credentials with FHIR endpoint directories could streamline routing,
improve traceability, and facilitate interoperability by connecting patients to the correct
provider access points.
PR-14. Encouraging Use of the Information Blocking Portal
Sutter Health believes CMS and ONC should prioritize collaboration and clarity over punitive
enforcement when addressing information blocking. Today, providers often face a regulatory
paradox: disclosing information too quickly may violate HIPAA, while holding it for contextual
review risks being labeled as information blocking. These unclear and conflicting expectations
generate significant compliance anxiety.
Sutter Health, which exchanges millions of medical records annually, is committed to
responsible health data sharing and supports the goals of increased transparency and
interoperability. However, the current enforcement approach—focused heavily on punitive
action—risks discouraging good-faith participation and slowing progress.
CMS should consider allowing a remediation window for good-faith efforts before enforcement
actions are taken. Such an approach would allow provider organizations to course-correct
without fear of immediate penalties. A more collaborative regulatory environment would better
support the goals of expanded interoperability.
Additionally, CMS and ONC should explicitly delineate the responsibilities of different actors—
such as providers, EHR vendors, and HIEs—regarding compliance. Clear role-based expectations
will help avoid confusion, improve accountability, and encourage more consistent adherence to
information sharing requirements. In this way, reporting can advance data exchange by
clarifying barriers and surfacing real-world issues rather than deepening mistrust in the
regulatory process.
2. Digital Health in Value-Based Care Models
VB-1. Incentives to Drive ACO/MSSP Adoption
Targeted financial and/or quality-based incentives could be designed to directly support
investment in and the use of digital health technologies that demonstrably advance value-based
care goals—such as remote patient monitoring, telehealth for specialty access, secure
messaging, and patient portals with advanced functionality.
In parallel, CMS could reduce administrative burden by allowing digital tools to facilitate more
efficient, consolidated, and potentially automated data collection and reporting for state and
federal programs. This would help align digital innovation with regulatory compliance and free
up resources for direct patient care.
VB-3. Essential Health IT Capabilities
Standardized data definitions and machine-readable quality metrics are essential to ensure
consistent performance measurement and reduce reporting friction across these tools.
Ubiquitous and reliable patient event notifications are also critical, enabling timely care
coordination and sustained patient engagement.

3. Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks
TD-5. Nationwide Provider Directory of FHIR Endpoints
Sutter Health strongly supports the development of a nationwide provider directory of FHIR
endpoints as a foundational infrastructure investment to improve access to health information
for patients, providers, and payers. To be effective, this directory must serve as a single source
of truth—consolidating, not duplicating, existing efforts across federal, commercial, and regional
systems. It must also include FHIR endpoints at both the organization and individual provider
levels, rather than limiting functionality to high-level or institutional endpoints.
Current tools such as the National Plan and Provider Enumeration System (NPPES) are
insufficient for this purpose and must be significantly enhanced or replaced. A modernized
directory should reflect the operational reality of interoperable care delivery and support clinical
data exchange with specificity at the point of care.
A centralized, accurate directory would enhance care coordination by streamlining referrals,
accelerating provider-to-provider communication, and supporting more efficient data exchange
workflows. It would also reduce administrative burden by simplifying credentialing, contracting,
and payment processing.
Responsibility for maintaining the directory should be shared across stakeholders. Payers should
be accountable for updating panel and coverage information, while providers should maintain
details related to practice locations and FHIR endpoint accessibility. Importantly, this burden
must not fall to individual physicians or frontline staff but rather be integrated into existing
organizational infrastructure.
Ultimately, well-designed and governed national provider directory can serve as a critical
enabler of interoperability, quality reporting, and patient-centered care delivery at scale.
