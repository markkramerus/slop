visualization. CREPES.AI is hosted on FedRAMP-compliant AWS infrastructure and is
engineered to automate labor-intensive processes and enable decision-making that improves

We are actively collaborating with the DHS Procurement Innovation Lab (PIL) on two of our

Our modular SaaS offerings are well-positioned to support CMS in achieving its vision of a

to digital tools for Medicare beneficiaries and their caregivers. CORMAC offers flexible
provisioning models—including standalone SaaS or integrated service-based deployments—to
meet agencies where they are on their modernization journey.
Our company’s core capabilities directly support CMS’s goals of improving data availability,
expanding interoperability, encouraging digital health innovation, and reducing provider and
patient burden. As CMS continues to lead efforts that foster trusted digital health ecosystems and
value-based care transformation, CORMAC stands ready to contribute solutions that meet today’s
challenges while supporting future policy frameworks and infrastructure needs.
2 Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks
2.1 Ecosystem
TD-1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to stimulate
developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers?
To foster innovation that improves the health and wellbeing of Medicare beneficiaries and their
caregivers, CMS must intentionally cultivate an ecosystem that attracts and supports developers.
CORMAC offers the following short-term and long-term recommendations that focus on
expanding data access, enhancing the developer experience, and incentivizing engagement through
strategic programs and partnerships. These steps will help CMS stimulate broad-based interest in
building impactful digital health tools.
Short-Term Actions (Next 2 Years)
1. Expand Access to Anonymized Data for Innovation:
CMS should establish a centralized, anonymized Data Lake to supplement the existing Blue Button
API. This environment will provide developers with broad, structured datasets in a standardized
and developer-friendly format. Doing so will reduce technical barriers, lower integration costs,

2. Launch Developer-Centric Innovation Events:
CMS is encouraged to host innovation-oriented events such as Hackathons, Developer Challenges,
and design competitions. These activities will spark interest, promote creative problem-solving,
and engage a wide range of technical talent. CMS could also distribute sample datasets and
problem statements at conferences or through platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk to further
widen participation.
3. Incentivize Through Gamification and Recognition:
To foster sustained engagement, CMS should implement gamification features that include
certifications, digital badges, and public recognition for developer contributions. CMS is also
encouraged to promote team-based participation by pairing senior and junior developers, thereby
facilitating mentorship and skill development within the community.
4. Build a Robust Developer Experience Layer:
CMS should enhance the usability of its development environment by building a comprehensive
Developer Experience Layer. This may include curated API bundles organized by use case (e.g.,
care navigation, medication management), sample FHIR resources, and pre-built consent flows.
These enhancements will simplify integration, reduce ramp-up time, and increase the appeal of
building on CMS platforms.
5. Provide Data Access Harnesses and Developer Sandboxes:
CMS should offer robust sandbox environments and test harnesses with high-quality synthetic data
that mimic real-world CMS systems. These tools would allow developers to simulate interactions
such as claims processing, provider transitions, and drug formulary changes. By providing realistic
testing conditions, CMS will help developers build and refine prototypes more effectively.
6. Data Access Test Harnesses and Developer Sandboxes:
Providing synthetic data environments that simulate real-world CMS responses, including claim
lifecycle events, provider transitions, drug formularies, would reduce developer ramp-up time and
increase prototype realism.
Long-Term Actions (Beyond 2 Years)
Over the long term, CMS should form partnerships with other federal agencies (e.g., VA, NIH),
state health systems, and private sector organizations to expand the scope and richness of
accessible datasets. These collaborations will enable the development of more comprehensive and
personalized digital tools that support improved care coordination, equity, and health outcomes for
Medicare beneficiaries and their caregivers.
By advancing these strategic actions, CMS builds a future-ready developer ecosystem that fuels
meaningful innovation. CORMAC stands ready to support this vision and work collaboratively to
foster solutions that elevate the health, wellbeing, and care experiences of America’s Medicare
population.

TD-3. Regarding digital identity implementation:
a. What are the challenges and benefits?
Challenges include inconsistent adoption of credentialing protocols across federal and commercial
systems, leading to fragmented patient experiences and limited reuse of digital identity credentials.
Many health IT systems still default to proprietary login models, requiring patients and caregivers
to maintain multiple credentials for different providers or portals. Benefits of standardized digital
identity credentials include improved trust between systems, streamlined patient access, and
reduced overhead for identity lifecycle management, particularly in use cases involving multi-
institutional data sharing, such as care coordination, TEFCA participation, or Blue Button API
authorization.
b. How would requiring digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov, ID.me, other
NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs) impact cybersecurity and data exchange?
Mandating identity credentials would strengthen end-user authentication without requiring each
provider or vendor to individually manage risk evaluation frameworks. This would reduce
credential sprawl, improve auditability, and standardize access control enforcement across
exchange ecosystems, especially those leveraging SMART on FHIR or bulk FHIR API endpoints.
Additionally, stronger credentialing would improve assurance during sensitive operations such as
claims-based access or patient-generated data submission.
c. What impact would mandatory use of the OpenID Connect identity protocol have?
OpenID Connect (OIDC) adoption would unify the identity abstraction layer across disparate
applications, particularly for FHIR-based API ecosystems. It would simplify authorization code
flow across vendors and enable broader reuse of authenticated identity assertions for delegated app
access, including enabling patients to consent to third-party access via a credentialed workflow.

TD-7. To what degree has USCDI improved interoperability and exchange and what are its
limitations?
The U.S. Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) has contributed meaningful advancements in
standardizing data exchange across healthcare systems; however, significant limitations persist in
both its definitional scope and practical implementation. While USCDI establishes a foundational
structure for interoperable health information, its current set of data elements falls short of fully
supporting critical use cases such as social determinants of health, mental health workflows, and

hinder true interoperability and lead to variability in data quality, completeness, and exchange
performance. To address these limitations, CORMAC recommends a hybrid approach that
preserves structured USCDI elements while introducing standardized semi-structured formats and
AI-friendly enhancements. This strategy balances the need for reliable, computable data with the
flexibility required to accommodate evolving data types, ultimately improving data accessibility
and clinical utility for all stakeholders.
a. Does it contain the full extent of data elements you need?
No, substantial limitations remain in USCDI coverage. Notable gaps include social determinants
of health with only approximately 30% capture rate, mental health workflows that create
implementation barriers, and care coordination data where automated exchanges often fail.
b. If not, is it because of limitations in the definition of the USCDI format or the way it is utilized?
The limitations stem from both definitional constraints in USCDI format and inconsistent
utilization across vendors. Implementation variability presents significant challenges, with
different EHR vendors interpreting USCDI requirements with 25-30% variance in code mappings,
inconsistent handling of optional versus required elements, and dramatic performance variations
ranging from 2 seconds to 45+ seconds for identical data requests.
c. If so, would adding more data elements to USCDI add value or create scoping challenges? How
could such challenges be addressed?
Adding more data elements could provide value but would create scoping challenges including
increased implementation complexity, vendor compliance burden, and potential performance
degradation. To address these challenges, a hybrid approach would be most effective: maintaining
current USCDI structured elements as the foundation while adding standardized narrative fields
with AI-extractable metadata tags and implementing machine-readable confidence scores for AI-
extracted data elements. An incremental implementation strategy featuring tiered requirements
with Core (mandatory), Enhanced (recommended), and Specialty (optional) categories would help
manage complexity.
d. Given improvements in language models, would you prefer a non-proprietary but less structured
format that might improve data coverage even if it requires more processing by the receiver?
Regarding less structured formats with language model processing, this approach could improve
data coverage but would introduce new challenges including processing variability, quality
assurance complexity, and potential inconsistency in data interpretation. A hybrid model
combining structured USCDI elements with AI-processable narrative components would likely
provide optimal balance between coverage and reliability while maintaining interoperability
standards. We have designed properly, semi-structured formats (JSON with coded segments and
free-text fields) allow NLP or large language models (LLMs) to extract meaning even when
structured inputs are lacking. This approach may be particularly useful in surfacing data from
PDFs, scanned forms, or clinical notes, which are often excluded from standard API responses but
carry high patient value.

TD-9. Regarding certification of health IT:
c. How could ASTP/ONC revise health IT certification criteria to require APIs to consistently
support exchanging data from all aspects of the patient's chart (for example, faxed records, free
text, discrete data)?
The government should introduce API coverage traceability requirements, where certified health
IT systems must demonstrate that their APIs expose all available clinical content, including:
• Unstructured documents (via FHIR DocumentReference and Binary resources)
• Clinical notes (ie:JSON-formatted narrative text)
• Scanned documents and structured imaging metadata
Certification test scripts should validate field-level completeness across resource classes, and
systems should be required to publish supported FHIR profiles, search capabilities, and known
limitations. This would ensure that even if EHRs store diverse formats, those formats are not
trapped from authorized external access.
d. What policy changes could CMS make so providers are motivated to respond to API-based data
requests with best possible coverage and quality of data?
CMS could introduce API response completeness benchmarks as part of VBC and interoperability
performance metrics, rewarding providers who maintain high coverage across clinical domains
including labs, encounters, vitals, and care plans via FHIR-based responses. Similar to quality
reporting incentives, this could be tied to:
• FHIR-based attestation fields submitted quarterly
• TEFCA participation thresholds
• Patient experience surveys measuring app access success rates
CMS could also align incentive programs to require participation in endpoint registries, allowing
seamless discovery of active APIs across provider organizations.

The tradeoffs between using USCDI and full EHI stem from the balance between standardization
and flexibility, as well as the technical, regulatory, and operational readiness of the healthcare
ecosystem.
USCDI is optimized for real-time, standards-based interoperability and low-friction data exchange
(e.g., patient access apps, EHR-to-EHR queries).. Full EHI opens the door to deeper, AI-driven
use cases like comprehensive risk prediction, litigation support, or advanced research—but with
greater complexity, liability, and compute costs. With today’s advanced NLP, cloud computing,
and data lakes, processing full EHI is more feasible than before, but still costly and uneven across
healthcare systems.
