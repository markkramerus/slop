Patients For Patient Safety US Patients For Patient Safety US | 251 Still Pine Bend, Smyrna, Georgia 30082 | 678.309.9605 Public Comment on CMS/ONC Request for Information; Health Technology Ecosystem Patients for Patient Safety US (PFPS US) is grateful for the opportunity to respond to this Request for Information. PFPS US is a national network led by patients and families, working alongside healthcare providers, systems engineers, researchers, educators, and other key stakeholders to elevate patient safety as a national public health priority. Our network includes individuals, as well as nonprofit and for profit organizations. Our shared goal is a healthcare system that centers the safety and well-being of patients in every setting, at every step of care. Our policy priorities align with CMS’s emphasis on transparency, measurement, and public reporting, fortified by patient/family engagement, as drivers of improvement. Improving safety and accountability for safety of care will also reduce unnecessary health care spending, waste, and associated taxpayer burden. PFPS US has a particular interest in improving diagnostic accuracy and timeliness. Diagnostic errors affect more than 12 million Americans each year and likely cause more harm to patients than all other medical errors combined. Diagnostic errors alone are responsible for an estimated 500,000 to 895,000 deaths and serious harms annually — numbers that rival or exceed other leading causes of death. Serious harms affect one third of these patients. Permanent disability or death can result from missed opportunities to treat dangerous diseases like stroke, infection, or cancer early on, before it is too late. Additional harms and costs result from inappropriate use of diagnostic tests and incorrect treatments for diseases not actually present. Diagnostic harm is now a leading driver of medical liability exposure. A 2015 study by the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, “Improving Diagnosis in Medicine”, called diagnostic error a massive "blind spot" in the national effort to improve health care quality and safety, and concluded that “improving the diagnostic process is not only possible, but also represents a moral, professional, and public health imperative.” Our responses reflect the lived experience of our members—individuals and families who have experienced preventable harm—and the collective mission that guides our work: to make harm visible, to ensure that patients’ voices inform policy, and to promote technologies and standards that serve patients—not just systems. We appreciate CMS and ONC for engaging the public and exploring how digital health technologies can better meet the needs of patients and caregivers. PC-1. What health management or care navigation apps would help you understand and manage your (or your loved ones’) health needs, as well as the actions you should take? a. What are the top things you would like to be able to do for your or your loved ones’ health that can be enabled by digital health products? Patients and caregivers in our network are calling for integrated digital tools that empower us to prevent harm, manage fragmented care, pursue timely and accurate diagnosis, recognize safety concerns before they escalate and alert and activate (real-time) clinicians when a patient has experienced unsafe care or is deteriorating. We need products that support both clinicians and patients to detect safety risks, such as dangerous drug interactions, duplicative testing, or lapses in necessary follow-up. Tools that can flag abnormal patterns—such as test results that were never addressed—can also help patients identify diagnostic errors or care delays. It is equally important to have visibility into whether referrals, imaging studies, or treatments have actually been completed, particularly across different portals or health systems. Patients have to manage so many logins to different portals that do not talk to each other and it is challenging, especially for older, complex, or vulnerable patients, to organize and manage the fragmented data for our care. Integration and interoperable data that can flow with patients through their various points of care - instead of siloed electronic health records (EHRs) is key to fluid care management. Further, patients want tools that can be proactive, not reactive, and meet them more easily in their daily life. This includes supporting telehealth, integration of data from wearables into the clinical EHR, and integration of patient concerns using LLM to help patients identify their concerns.
Patients For Patient Safety US Patients For Patient Safety US | 251 Still Pine Bend, Smyrna, Georgia 30082 | 678.309.9605 Specifically around safety, patients have long been frustrated about their inability to directly report patient safety events to a place where we can contribute to continuous learning. Providers can report the CDC’s National Healthcare Safety Net (NHSN), but patients cannot. Providers can also report to federally chartered Patient Safety Organizations (PSOs) but, again, patients are excluded. Missed and delayed diagnoses are especially under- reported, in part because lapses occur over more than one patient visit across the continuum of care, not as a single diagnostic lapse in a single treatment setting. Often providers do not learn of a missed or delayed diagnosis until a medical liability claim is filed. Diagnostic error is now a leading driver of liability exposure in both claims frequency and indemnity cost. (See Coverys, Insights from a Decade of Malpractice Claims, 2020, https://www.coverys.com/getmedia/cbb9b43a-c639-4d1a-b959-02c26ef92785/a-call-for-action-decade-of- malpractice-claims.pdf). Relying on the slow, costly and adversarial liability system is far from an effective or efficient way to learn from diagnostic safety errors. When harm cannot be seen, it cannot be fixed. Using digital tools, patients can help fill the gap in under-reporting. We should be able to easily report harm events, near misses or other safety concerns directly to healthcare systems or to a central learning engine, perhaps located at CMS, CDC or ONC. Studies show that patients actually do report patient safety events that providers do not. (Pozzobon LD, Rotter T, Sears K. The benefits and opportunities: Engaging patients in identifying and reporting patient safety incidents. Healthcare Management Forum. 2023;37(4):196-201. doi: 10.1177/08404704231203593). Being able to better advocate for and follow a loved ones’ healthcare journey is also important. Some tools allow for family members to be given access, but it’s often cursory. Do you have easy access to your own and all your loved ones’ health information in one location? While patient-accessible EHRs like EPIC MyChart are dominant they aren’t a luxury everyone has access to or can even get access to if they want it - it’s dependent upon the health system they use. Tracking referrals can be a challenge in this system. When patients, for example, have to seek care at an outside facility/health system due to an emergency, or because they want to see a specific provider that is a better match for their needs, the data from that encounter that may be very relevant to future care is now siloed elsewhere. While the patient can access both portals (assuming both have them and it’s not a paper record they have to request), the patient’s main providers are not privy to that new system’s information immediately and the patient bears the burden of communicating all that new information from that encounter back to their main providers. This is a difficult translation burden for patients to manage, that too often results in lapses in communication, missed lab results, etc. It creates work for the patient to have to get all that data from the new system, into their main portal. And now, the patient has two portals to manage - if they are lucky and not reliant on requesting paper medical records. From the perspective of a PFPS member in a very rural state, “orphaned” patients (patients with no designated primary care provider–a critical and worsening problem in rural America) are by default without digital services. An orphaned patient may utilize a medical service, e.g., a Critical Access Hospital emergency department, and notes from that encounter will have nowhere to go–beyond a paper discharge sheet given to the patient. Even if these notes are captured in a health information exchange, there is no medical professional gathering, integrating, and interpreting that patient’s data and coordinating a response to their evolving healthcare requirements. Visits to an emergency department or urgent care office by orphaned patients are undertaken without providers in those facilities having access to their medical history, beyond the patient’s memory. Missed opportunities for diagnosis of serious illness and prescription of contraindicated medications (polypharmacy) are among the problems that have arisen from this rural reality. Denmark offers a compelling model for nationwide health data access. Through its national health portal, patients can view lab results, imaging, prescriptions, hospital records, dental records, physical therapy, and more—from all providers—in one place. Family members can also be granted proxy access, making it easier to coordinate care, spot safety issues, and manage chronic conditions across settings. The Denmark model allows the patient results to follow the patient no matter which facility they are seen in supporting more continuity of records and care. a. We have the opportunity to develop proactive management of patient safety and improve healthcare quality in rural primary care, where the old physician and facility-centric model of primary care is in rapid collapse. New delivery system design is needed that takes advantage of AI-integrated knowledge management systems that support monitoring of patient health, and coordinate lay and professional care–including alerts to activate the interprofessional team. Some team members would be remote and accessible 24/7 (e.g., existing 24/7 nursing and tele-medicine services) and others would be available within the community through community paramedicine services. It is necessary to look to “human-machine teaming” design methodologies (common in other high-hazard domains) in developing, implementing, and continually improving such a system in practical use. There is prime opportunity in rural America to reverse deepening loss of access to primary care by developing, implementing, and refining a distributed primary care system, with proactive safety management capability–to pursue systemic safety management as it is done in other high-hazard domains–with the goal of proactively adapting care to avert patient harm as an “autonomic” monitoring, learning and coordination function, not as a reactive compliance-oriented approach to patient safety that dominates healthcare organizations, and which have yielded scant improvement in patient safety over the past twenty-five years. b. Patients For Patient Safety US Patients For Patient Safety US | 251 Still Pine Bend, Smyrna, Georgia 30082 | 678.309.9605 CMS should play a proactive role in evaluating digital health tools through a lens that prioritizes patient safety, usability, and trust. Rather than focusing solely on system efficiency or operational convenience, CMS should ensure that products are designed to improve patient outcomes and reduce preventable harm. This includes reviewing products for usability among populations often overlooked—particularly older adults, people with disabilities, and those with limited digital literacy. Tools must be designed for real-world use by Medicare beneficiaries, not just tech-savvy users. Additionally, CMS should require transparency around how digital tools function, including who benefits from their deployment. Patients need to know whether algorithms are reinforcing bias, omitting key risk factors, or overriding individual preferences. Digital tools should make care easier, better, and more affordable, not just add another billing code to increase the cost of our already too expensive American healthcare system. Digital health tools reviewed or recommended by CMS should meet high standards for safety and usability. Key evaluation criteria should include: •Proven impact on harm prevention and patient safety •Transparent and ethical data practices •Accessibility and ease of use for diverse users across all literacy levels •Data security and not selling patient data for profit b. What changes would enable better access to high quality CMS/provider-generated data? •Timely patient-facing summaries of key safety information. •Open APIs and standardized data formats across CMS programs and EHRs. •Patient alerts when new claims or risk scores are added. •Include patient-generated harm data in CMS data sets.
