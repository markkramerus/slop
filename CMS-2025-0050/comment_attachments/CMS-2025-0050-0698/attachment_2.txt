1. Clarify and Modernize Rules Governing Data Exchange for Labs

Laboratories must be able to access relevant clinical data to support timely, high-quality care,
particularly for operational needs such as prior authorization, claim adjudication, or test
appropriateness. Laboratories are often placed in a position where information is required by payors
that is not part of an electronic lab order or included with a paper requisition. This untenable position is
made more difficult due to HIPAA information access rules, which create barriers to technology
adoption and innovation. Laboratories do not determine medical necessity and do not treat patients
but are forced into provider situations due to payor activities. For example, labs have been asked to
provide signed orders or encounter notes to ensure payment for services when neither are available
through existing HL7 interfaces. Advanced interoperability technology exists to improve data access, but
labs are often not considered when interoperability policies are crafted. Lastly, CMS should consider
guidance and policy that promotes the technology for payers to engage providers when additional
documentation or billing information is needed for claims processing, rather than use laboratories as
interoperability middlemen.

In our attachment, we detail the journey of a cancer patient who needs prior authorization for a lab test
ordered by their oncologist. The prior authorization request contains key clinical data which is stored in
the provider’s EHR, such as medical history or previous cancer treatments. A modern FHIR-based
interface could enabie the lab to retrieve this information automatically — but only if current rules allow

it. Inconsistent interpretations of treatment versus operations use cases for labs have created confusion,
delays, and administrative burden.

Recommendation: CMS should clarify when a laboratory is acting in support of treatment — including
enabling prior authorization, establishing test appropriateness, or follow-up coordination — and access
to relevant patient information falls within the HIPAA treatment use case. This clarification would
streamline care, reduce unnecessary delays, and promote aligned clinical decision-making.

2. Use Lab Results to Empower Patients and Providers

Laboratory test results are often the starting point of a patient's care journey. However, current policies
fail to promote and often limit the ability of labs to pair lab results with advanced technology such as Al
or clinical decision support tools. We believe that updating anti-kickback rules and enhancing patient
communication standards to align HIPAA rules with patient preference are crucial steps towards
empowering patients and providers at the time of lab result delivery. By implementing policy changes
that prioritize patient engagement and encourage providers to follow evidence-based care through the
use of clinical decision support, we can improve healthcare outcomes and enhance the overail patient
experience. Leveraging emerging technology in this process will further drive positive patient outcomes
and ensure that patients are actively involved in their care decisions. We urge CMS to consider the
policy changes needed to promote and enable a more collaborative and informed healthcare
environment.

In our attached example, the patient receives their results along with educational materials that are
tailored to their personal history and comorbidities. The patient is also given access to an Al tool that
allows for questions to be asked and answered with relevant and accurate medical information, rather
than the patient resorting to the internet or social media. Simultaneously, the results delivered to the
provider leverage clinical decision support technology to help the provider identify the next best action
for the patient.

Recommendation: CMS should encourage the development and delivery of digital tools — including Al
— that support laboratories in providing clear, evidence-based education in conjunction with test
results. These tools should be recognized as part of the treatment process, not as marketing or
inducement, to allow broader use under current laws. Further, CMS, in coordination with OIG, should
explore safe harbors or regulatory clarification that allow laboratories to share clinically relevant
decision support and technology enhancements with patients and providers. These tools should be
recognized as part of care delivery — not inducement — when they are evidence-based, support quality
improvement, and follow best practices in compliance and transparency.

Responses to individual RFI questions

PC-14. Regarding digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov,
ID.me, other NIST 800-63-3 I[AL2/AAL2 credentialing service providers (CSP)):

b. What could be the benefits to patients/caregivers if digital identity credentials
were more widely used?

Laboratories could benefit significantly from digital ID credentials—particularly a national
patient [D— in the following ways:

I. Improved Patient Matching and Data Accuracy

One of the biggest challenges labs face is accurately matching test results to the correct patient,
especially when patients move across health systems or use different names, addresses, or
identifiers. Without appropriate patient matching, data sharing improvements like TEFCA will
be limited. A national patient ID would:

e Reduce errors caused by duplicate or mismatched records.

e Ensure continuity of laboratory data and services provided across providers and settings.

e Enhance safety, particularly in critical diagnostic testing.

Il. Faster, More Efficient Lab Workflows
Accurate patient identification reduces administrative burden:
e Less time spent manually verifying patient identity.
e Fewer test delays due to unclear or conflicting data.
e Lower rates of repeat testing due to lost or misattributed results.

III. Better Integration with Digital Health Tools
A national patient ID would enable labs to:
e Integrate securely with Al tools and apps that assist in test interpretation.
e Share test results with patient-facing platforms more efficiently and with proper consent.
e Support population health initiatives by reliably aggregating de-identified data across
systems.

IV. Enabling Innovation (Responsibly)
With stronger identity assurance:
e Labs can safely deliver educational content or Al-driven result explanations to patients.
e Patients could authorize labs to connect results with third-party tools (e.g. chronic
condition management apps).
e Regulatory compliance (e.g., HIPAA, CLIA) would be facilitated by verifiable consent
mechanisms.
e Create scalable patient consent tools to empower patients with their own data.

Vv. National Emergency and Public Health Response
During outbreaks or public health emergencies, digital ID credentials could allow labs to:
e Rapidly report and share test data tied to verified individuals.

e Coordinate across jurisdictions with less fragmentation.
e Improve contact tracing, surveillance, and follow-up testing logistics.

PR-7. What strategies can CMS implement to support providers in making high-
quality, timely, and comprehensive healthcare data available for interoperability in
the digital product ecosystem?

To support providers—especially laboratories—in making high-quality, timely, and
comprehensive healthcare data available for interoperability within the digital product
ecosystem, CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services) can implement a range of strategic
actions across policy, incentives, infrastructure, and oversight. Here are key strategies:

I. Align Payment Incentives with Data Sharing
CMS should tie data sharing performance to reimbursement:
e Include lab data interoperability metrics in programs like MIPS and value-based care
models.
e Provide bonus payments or reduced reporting burden for labs and providers that
participate in trusted exchange frameworks and maintain high-quality data feeds.
e Expand and Clarify Regulatory Guidance
CMS can reduce ambiguity by:
e Clarifying permissible data use cases under HIPAA, CLIA, and the Cures Act to ensure
labs feel confident in sharing data with patients and digital tools.
e Supporting clear governance for third-party access to lab data through APIs, including
consent management and audit trails.

II. Support Digital Credentialing and Identity Verification
To make lab data shareable across platforms with patient consent, CMS should:

e Promote digital JD infrastructure or patient-matching standards that enable secure,
accurate data linking.

e Encourage use of OAuth 2.0 / SMART on FHIR standards for secure app access with
patient authorization.

e Promote bi-directional communication and sharing of data between third party apps and
providers

II. Promote Public-Private Collaboration
CMS can convene stakeholders across healthcare, technology, and laboratory sectors to:
e Co-develop best practices for lab data interoperability.
e Encourage participation in public test beds or pilot programs for digital health integration
using lab data.
e Fund AI or decision-support initiatives that require lab data inputs, under appropriate
guardrails.
e Update guidance around anti-kickback statues that help laboratories and EHR vendors
collaborate on patient and provider innovation

By combining policy levers, technical standards, infrastructure support, and regulatory clarity,
CMS can play a transformative role in ensuring laboratory data is fully leveraged in a modern,
interoperable digital health ecosystem.
