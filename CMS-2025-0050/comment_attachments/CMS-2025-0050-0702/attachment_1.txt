Who We Are
HealthEx is a modern data rights management platform that enables secure, scalable, and
compliant data access. Backed by General Catalyst and collaborating with major institutions such
as MD Anderson, leading national payers, state HIEs, and ACOs, HealthEx has become a national
leader in enabling Individual Access Services (IAS) under TEFCA. Our system is built with robust
patient identity verification, digital consent, real-time data access and delivery, and endpoint
discoverability.
A Policy and Pilot Blueprint for CMS
We propose a CMS-sponsored pilot project in which CMS partners with HealthEx to activate
100,000 Medicare beneficiaries to access and exchange their longitudinal clinical data across sites
of care. This would enable CMS to close key care gaps that persist under claims-based
infrastructure, understand the value of care provided, and alongside address fraud, waste, and
abuse. Patients in turn are given the ability to access, retrieve, and direct access to their data to
drive better care outcomes. The project would also highlight practical examples of issues patients
run into while accessing their own data from the networks, and drive CMS-motivated change to
resolve these.
The pilot leverages existing TEFCA IAS protocols—including IAL2/AAL2 digital identity verification
and digitally enforceable consent—and will work in concert with CMS tools such as BlueButton 2.0
and MyMedicare.gov. By aligning policy with innovation, this pilot can serve as a springboard for
CMS’s digital health transformation goals, benefiting patients, providers, and plans alike.

six key policy recommendations (originally shared publicly via HealthEx in May 2025 as part of a
policy memo):
1. Incentivize or mandate TEFCA participation for CMS-regulated entities to ensure IAS
availability at scale.
2. Mandate responses to IAS requests and presume TEFCA-compliant verification meets
HIPAA identity and authorization requirements.

4. OUR UNDERSTANDING
CMS and ASTP seek to advance a patient-centered digital health ecosystem that is efficient,
built on modern technical infrastructure, and with the appropriate guardrails in place to build
trust and transparency. This includes expanding access to digital tools, reducing administrative
burden, and ensuring that patients, providers, and plans have timely access to relevant health
information to improve outcomes.
From the medicare beneficiary’s perspective, the current state of health data access is
frustrating and fragmented. Patients must log in to multiple portals, recall numerous
credentials, and manually assemble records from disparate systems. Even where IAS is live,
many consumers cannot yet access or exchange their data, because TEFCA participation is
voluntary, and legal concerns about identity and authorization verification still limit responses to
IAS requests. This makes patient-directed exchange inconsistent and inaccessible, especially
for vulnerable populations.
Today, the infrastructure available to CMS, including Medicare Fee-For-Service and Medicaid
programs, relies heavily on post-adjudicated claims and quality measures that can lag 12 to 18
months behind the actual patient experience. This delay in access to data hinders CMS’s
ability to support real-time care coordination, accurate risk adjustment, timely
identification of care gaps, and equitable benefit design. Moreover, current systems often
fail to deliver on the promise of interoperability for individuals—particularly those with chronic
illnesses, fragmented care, or limited digital literacy.
HealthEx believes the infrastructure and policy foundations now exist to solve these challenges.
HealthEx lays out the policy inputs that will enable a clear and secure framework for real-time,
patient-initiated exchange, via IAS. The combination of IAL2-verified, AAL2-authenticated digital
identity credentials, plus digitally enforceable and auditable IAS consent, provides a robust and
HIPAA-compliant method to verify patient identity and intent.
Our understanding is that CMS’s challenge is not technological feasibility—but operational
readiness, policy alignment, and demonstration of value at scale. CMS now has a historic
opportunity to lead the cultural and technical shift toward patient-centered data
exchange. This can be done by aligning incentives, clarifying compliance expectations, and
directly engaging patients as active participants in their care.
HealthEx is prepared to support CMS in this shift, both through concrete pilots and actionable
policy recommendations. Our proposed pilot effort offers CMS real-time access to 100,000
Medicare beneficiaries’ longitudinal clinical data. HealthEx can support CMS in reducing
administrative burden, increasing digital equity, and achieving its interoperability goals—while
ensuring patients themselves have the tools to manage and direct their own care.

The pilot HealthEx is advocating for advances of CMS’s goal to enable seniors and families to
make informed health decisions with modern, trusted digital tools. The proposed pilot
operationalizes the IAS exchange purpose under TEFCA—at national scale—to demonstrate
patient empowerment, value, and real-time information flow, benefiting patients, CMS, and the
entire health system.
HealthEx / CMS Pilot Proposal
HealthEx to deliver 100,000 patient records within 90 days to patients and to CMS, via a pilot effort that
provides CMS access to real-time records through patient-directed data exchange. Patients will
alongside have access to their comprehensive records, to drive their care.
Records will be made available, as below:
● Patients will receive outreach from MyMedicare.gov, and will have the opportunity to access
their comprehensive records, across all their points-of-care, and also make this data available to
CMS.
○ Real-time records will be made available via patient-directed data exchange, via IAS
under TEFCA, and falls back to available endpoint directories (potentially coordinated
with CMS’ own endpoint directory effort)
● Patient outreach, education and consent will occur via a joint HealthEx / MyMedicare.gov
experience used by patients.
● Patients have the opportunity to opt-in to share their data.
● Upon consent, HealthEx accesses comprehensive patient records on the national networks, and
delivers a copy of the records to the patient, and to Medicare, or the MA plan.
Key Metrics and measures of success include:
● Number of patients who opt-in to share their health records with CMS
● Volume of records made available to CMS
● Time to data access for CMS
● Patient satisfaction
● Number of care gaps closed for patients, degree of disease prevention / early diagnosis, ability
to enable closed-roop referrals and smarter care coordination

PC-1, A.
A patient’s health data drives their care: providers, care facilities, payers, and the right
teams having real-time access to health data is paramount to any individual managing
their health. With the challenges in US interoperability today, giving patients the control to
access, retrieve, and direct access of their health data & record is 100% aligned with
tech-enabled patient empowerment.
Today, the control over health data rests squarely with institutions: Organizations have
controlled access to the health data that they create or receive. As a result, patients and
individuals today face fragmented access to their records: They must log into multiple portals,
with unique usernames and passwords, and piece together their health history manually, since
organizations haven’t given patients the means or the agency to combine or manage their data
across institutions. This is especially burdensome for individuals with chronic conditions, or
complex care needs, particularly those who see multiple providers.
We believe giving patients direct control over their data is more than a user-experience
upgrade—it is a catalyst for systemic improvement. When individuals can easily retrieve and
share verified health records, care teams gain timely, complete information that reduces
duplicative testing, speeds diagnoses, and supports coordinated, value-based care. Patients
who see real transparency and autonomy are also more likely to trust digital tools, grant consent
for data use, and engage in preventive programs, ultimately lowering total cost of care while
improving care quality and outcomes.

The advent of patient-directed data exchange, enabled via IAS, signals a shift from
organizations controlling access to patient data, to now individuals having the power to do so
(see Steven Lane’s post that discusses the potential impact of giving patients more control over
interoperability, and Hemant Taneja and Teresa Carlson’s recent white paper on the role of
patient-data access, in the context of broader healthcare innovation).
Patient-directed exchange streamlines the experience of how individuals access and share their
health records; its key drivers of value for individuals are:
● Convenience: Individuals can request and receive their health records from across
providers through a single, secure workflow.
● Clarity: Individuals gain a consolidated view of their health information—essential for
coordinating care, managing conditions, and making informed decisions. There is also a
growing opportunity to utilize AI or other apps to support individuals’ understanding of
their information and to provide actionable insights upon this consolidated view.
● Control: Individuals direct where their data goes, enabling them to authorize trusted
third parties, like CMS, or caregivers, to use their data to provide better care. Individuals
should also be able to selectively share their data and be able to easily revoke access to
third parties.
When IAS functions as designed, it will enable patients to be active participants in their own
care, not passive recipients: Individuals will have the power to direct data to new providers to
allow them to easily gain a comprehensive view of their health history, to payers to support care
coordination and process prior authorizations more efficiently, to research organizations
enabling groundbreaking advances, or to any health app of their choice.

PC-2, A:
When patients and caregivers can access their complete health records through a single
platform or interface, the benefits are immediate and tangible:
● Faster, more accurate care: Providers gain access to a longitudinal record that includes
diagnoses, labs, medications, and prior imaging—leading to faster diagnoses, more
personalized treatment, and fewer duplicative tests.
● Improved care coordination: Especially for those managing multiple specialists or
caring for loved ones, having a unified view supports better preparation for appointments
and informed follow-up.
● Reduced administrative burden: Patients and caregivers can complete intake forms,
care summaries, or medication lists quickly and accurately using pre-filled data.
● Greater trust and engagement: Individuals who see and understand their own data are
more likely to engage in preventive care, ask questions, and participate in shared
decision-making.
● Empowered caregivers: For aging parents, children, or family members with
disabilities, consolidated access enables caregivers to support decision-making,
medication management, and care transitions effectively.
These benefits are being realized today by users of the HealthEx platform, which enables
real-time, patient-initiated data access across multiple care settings using TEFCA-aligned
protocols and verified digital identity. However, this type of experience is still not available to
most individuals today.
PC-2, B: For most Americans today, a single system that seamlessly aggregates health data
across all providers still does not exist. This absence is most acutely felt in the following
contexts:
● Transition of care scenarios: Patients discharged from hospitals or moving between
providers need timely access to recent labs, imaging, and medication lists.
● Caregiver support: Family members often coordinate care for loved ones across states
or health systems and need a central location to view, share, and act on records.
● Chronic disease management: Patients with complex conditions require data from
multiple specialties that are rarely accessible in one place.

A cornerstone of VBC is the ability to act on timely, complete, and continuously refreshed
patient data. Care teams need access not only to a patient’s historical record but to a real-time
stream of new health information across providers and care settings captured either
periodically or in an event-driven manner. Yet today, this foundational requirement—recurring,
real-time access to health data—is not reliably possible due to lack of a scalably implemented
Operations/Payments purpose use case on TEFCA and other national networks, and
operational as well as incentive alignment issues involved in negotiating Provider-to-Payer
release of sufficiently detailed clinical data.
This gap imposes a ceiling on VBC effectiveness. Capabilities like predictive analytics,
longitudinal care planning, event notifications, and proactive gap closure all depend on
repeated data access, not just one-time retrievals. Without a reliable way to maintain real-time
connections to the patient record, even the best VBC tools are forced to work with outdated or
partial data.
