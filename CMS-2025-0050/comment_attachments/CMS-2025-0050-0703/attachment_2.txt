Executive Summary
The Center for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) should consider an open innovation hub for the
safe and responsible development of artificial intelligence (AI) (Generative and Agentic) solutions that
address Medicare and Medicaid patient needs and alleviate provider administrative burden.
The healthcare sector represents 20% of GDP and is woefully inefficient with 40% of spend categorized
as waste, abuse or fraud. Hospital systems are on the brink of economic collapse due to such things
as rising labor costs, reimbursement pressure, and mix deterioration with no government bailout safety
net, so there is a real urgency to the problem. Access to care and health equity are particularly acute for
certain populations especially MA and Medicaid. Over the past five (5) years, total provider revenue tied
to risk contracts has grown from $50B to $200B and this value-based care (VBC) trend is expected to
continue growing in a few dimensions beyond primary care such as specialty focused models,
exporting VBC to other areas through payer partnerships (e.g. hospital VBC), and expansion into other
lines of business (LoBs) - traditional Medicare, Medicaid, and Commercial.
Chicago Pacific Founders (CPF) is a mid-sized, healthcare sector focused private equity firm with one
of the largest investments in VBC assets in the country with over 1.6M full risk lives. Our mission is
to accelerate health innovation which includes the following goals that are aligned with those of CMS -
improved patient engagement and access to care, lower total cost of care, improved quality of patient
care outcomes, and improved provider satisfaction.
AI is a foundational element of health innovation especially at a time when we will see an
unprecedented change in how business processes are executed in healthcare with the application of
generative and agentic AI. The purpose of the AI innovation hub is to identify the highest value and

1. Seamless Interoperability
Fragmented, delayed, and non-standard data throttle digital innovation and drive costly manual work, even
though Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) application programming interfaces (APIs) and
Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) connectivity are now mandated.
Seamless interoperability would be an avenue for CMS to test and prove new innovations prior to scaling
nationwide where we would pool de-identified clinical and claims data in a secure, certified platform,
expose that data through verified APIs, and let developers build once and deploy nationwide—giving

patients one digital front door and providers modern automation. Impact Advisors has substantial
experience implementing electronic health record (EHR) platforms and enabling interoperability among
myriad digital health tools such as Epic, Cerner and other core platforms including, but not limited to,
Workday, UKG, and Peoplesoft.
Key Interoperability Challenges
 Fragmented Encounter Visibility: Without a single, consent-aware feed of clinical and claims events,
providers and payers miss real-time admissions, procedures, and lab results performed elsewhere that
may undermine care coordination.
 Manual Payer-to-Payer Onboarding: New-member data still moves through labor-intensive chart
chase instead of standards-based APIs, leaving incoming plans without problem lists, medication
histories, or hierarchical condition categories for months, inflating risk-adjustment errors, and delaying
care-management interventions.
 Manual Prior Authorization: Clinicians still phone or fax requests and wait days for a decision which
may delay therapy starts and push patients to higher-acuity care settings without expedient resolution.
 Data Integration: The most significant challenge is integrating commercial MA payer files, EHR, health
information exchange (HIE), labs, and pharmacy data. There are over 11 different payer file types (e.g.
MAO-004, MMR, MOR) that need to be processed across multiple contracts and health plans in
different states. Integration of EHR data (one or more), HIE data (usually several different ones by
state) and operational data (e.g. CRM, call center, scheduling, etc.) with the claims data at a patient
level is challenging.
 Inconsistent Standards: Non-government claims data feeds, especially MA, are significantly more
complex than accountable care organizations (ACOs) (and formerly MSSPs). ACO data feeds come
directly from CMS and are delivered in a steady and consistent format. Commercial health plans often
send low quality data, including inconsistent file names, inaccurate membership counts, incorrect or
old files, changing data definitions, and add new columns and files that will pass basic audit checks
and can only be flagged for errors manually or with highly sophisticated automated rules logic.
Solution in Practice: MyCare Integrated Unified Member Database
MyCare, an MA full-risk VBC provider with over 35K patients, engineered a Unified Member Database
(UMD) that stitches together commercial Medicare Advantage claims, EHR, HIE, laboratory, and pharmacy
feeds to create a longitudinal, 360-degree profile of every member. Real-time identity resolution, data
normalization, and rigorous data-quality approaches reconcile millions of data points into a single source
of truth. These enriched datasets power a portfolio of AI-driven “next-best-action” engines that surface risk-
stratified, clinically prioritized interventions to help care teams close care gaps, prevent avoidable
admissions, and exceed value-based performance targets.
 Integrated Patient Access: A single, normalized data stream spanning CMS and all contracted
payers’ feeds and provider-facing tools in near real time, eliminating duplicative diagnostics and
accelerating clinical decision-making.
 Optimized Provider Operations: Automated integration of disparate data and reporting supports
clinicians managing at-risk panels with an AI-native next-best-action guidance while also removing low-
value clerical tasks with significant administrative burden that reallocates provider capacity to clinical
care. These functions integrate natively with EHRs through APIs, preserving established workflows.

 Responsive Population Management: Continuously refreshed, analysis-ready data keeps risk
scores, attribution, and quality metrics current, enabling timely interventions and more precise value-
based contracts. Governed data pipelines ensure auditability for regulators and partners.
 Comprehensive Cost Transparency: A patient-level ledger reconciles every claim, rebate, and high-
cost episode, establishing an accurate total-cost-of-care view for finance, actuarial, and benefit-design
teams. Granular insight into cost drivers strengthens contract negotiations and compliance reporting.
Recommendations for CMS
 (PR-7) CMS can mandate that all commercial insurers adopt the same reporting standards and formats
required by CMS for e.g., ACOs to ensure that administrative claims data are high quality and easy to
integrate into provider workflows.
 (PC-1, PC-3) Mandate SMART 2.0 patient-facing OAuth endpoints tied to a single verified digital
credential usable across payers, providers, and third-party apps
 (PR-1, VB-1) Require payers to accept and respond to FHIR prior-authorization requests within
seventy-two hours as a condition of participation in Medicare Advantage and Medicaid managed-care
programs.
 (VB-2, VB-3) Grant bonus credit in Medicare Advantage Star Ratings for monthly submission of bulk-
FHIR quality feeds through TEFCA to CMS.
 (VB-5, PR-3) Authorize Medicare Advantage plans to pool high-cost curative-therapy risk through a
CMS-administered reinsurance corridor. Require outcome-based annuity payments spread over five
years, triggered by real-world evidence captured via TEFCA event feeds.
 (PR-2, VB-4) Establish a three-year TEFCA regulatory sandbox that waives information-blocking
penalties for pilots in real-time bundled-payment adjudication using FHIR Payment Bundle resources.
 (PC-4) Require Innovation Hub participants to use IAL2 digital identity standards and operate under an
independent ethics and privacy board to ensure trust and auditability.
 (PC-5) Create a patient therapy ledger that travels with the enrollee across plan switches and
automatically reconciles payment obligations between the originating and receiving plans.
2. Patient-Centric Digital Solutions
Challenges
Delivering patient-centric digital solutions in healthcare is hindered by several persistent challenges that
disproportionately affect Medicare beneficiaries, caregivers, and providers:
 Digital Literacy and Accessibility: Many seniors and rural Medicare beneficiaries face barriers due
to limited digital skills, connectivity challenges, and complex health apps, reducing their ability to
effectively engage with digital care tools.
 Fragmented Data Access: Disconnected healthcare systems prevent unified patient data views,
complicating care coordination and patient engagement, particularly for those managing multiple
providers or chronic conditions.

 Poor Usability and Design: Digital health tools frequently emphasize clinical or administrative needs,
resulting in unintuitive interfaces that are difficult for seniors and other less tech-savvy users to navigate
effectively.
 Caregiver Overload: Caregivers often lack integrated digital tools to track medications, manage
appointments, and monitor health trends, adding complexity to their responsibilities.
 Provider Administrative Burden: Non-integrated digital tools require providers to handle manual data
entry and navigate multiple systems, increasing administrative tasks and reducing time available for
direct patient care.
These challenges underscore the need for digital solutions that are intuitive, equitable, and seamlessly
integrated into both patient and provider workflows.
Solutions In Practice
XSELL (a CPF partner company) has developed and is implementing innovative, patient-centric digital
solutions (with Atrio, a CPF MA Health Plan) that address these challenges, delivering measurable
improvements in access, engagement, and usability. See below in Figure 2 for an example of how an
agentic voice solution would optimize the overall Medicare Advantage member experience:

Here we can see agentic voice technology reshapes member outreach with tailored guidance from

Recommendations for CMS
To further advance patient-centric digital solutions, CMS should consider the following actions, directly
addressing the RFI’s focus areas:
 (PC-6) Establish guidelines requiring digital health tools to include simplified navigation, and
accessibility features, such as voice commands, screen readers, and multilingual support. as a
condition for CMS funding or certification.
 (PR-1a) Offer grants or reimbursement models for health systems that deploy caregiver-focused digital
solutions, such as consolidated health management apps or caregiver-specific portals.
 (PC-5a, PR-1) Require certified health IT systems to support patient-initiated data exchange (e.g., via
digital health wallets or APIs) and integrate patient-reported outcomes into provider workflows without
manual entry.
 (PC-6, PR-1) Update telehealth reimbursement policies to promote equitable accessibility and ease of
use for all healthcare consumers, ensuring equitable access for all Medicare beneficiaries.
 (PC-5a) Develop a single-consent (applicable across all provider processes and jurisdictions),
standardized, user-friendly framework for digital health tools, leveraging FHIR and API-driven solutions
to give patients control over their data with minimal complexity.
3. Digital Identity Management
Challenges
 Fragmented Digital Identity and Access: Clinicians must authenticate across multiple EHR, HIE,
and payer portals, creating credential fatigue, surging help-desk demand, and a larger attack surface.
Healthcare data breaches (affecting 500 or more individuals) are at an all-time high with over 700 in
2024 and each successful breach cost providers $9.8M on average, nearly double the cross-industry
average of $4.9M – signaling the sensitive nature of health data, regulatory penalties and remediation
complexities.
 Manual Privacy and Consent Workflows: Paper-based or ad-hoc e-signature processes slow
referrals and leave patients—especially those with limited digital literacy—without real-time control over
data sharing. Unmanaged copies of PHI also extend incident recovery where ransomware downtime
now averages 19 days but only 22% of organizations restore operations in under seven days, adding
significant costs, beyond the initial breach, for providers due to operational disruption.
 Inconsistent Data-Sharing Policies and Governance: Variable state regulations and payer
requirements fragment longitudinal records, impede care coordination, and heighten information-
blocking exposure. The sector has recorded the highest breach cost for 14 consecutive years, reflecting
both regulatory complexity and persistent adversary focus.
 Escalating Threats: Ransomware exploiting vendor connections or unpatched software forces 37 %
of providers to remain offline for more than a month during a ransomware attack meaning every third-
party integration represents a material seven-figure risk that must be actively addressed.

Solutions In Practice
Impact Advisors has deployed innovative solutions that address these challenges.
 Identity and Access Management / Trust Frameworks: Implemented federated digital identity and
single sign-on solutions at leading health systems. Leveraging NIST-compliant identity, continuous
biometric authentication, and adaptive access controls, we reduced login-related support calls and
improved clinician satisfaction by streamlining workflows, while multilingual interfaces and robust
training ensured accessibility.
 Privacy, Consent, and PHI Control: FHIR-based consent management platforms and modular API-
driven solutions empower patients to manage their data securely, increasing engagement and reducing
administrative burdens. App attestation registries and third-party audits further ensure privacy and
compliance for HIPAA and non-HIPAA actors.
 Information Risk and Blocking Behavior: Embedded mobile-optimized, multilingual complaint tools
and secured audit trails, reducing blocking incidents through automated policy detection and real-time
monitoring.
 Cybersecurity and Resilience: Full-lifecycle API security assessments, endpoint hardening, and
continuous monitoring, in collaboration with leading partners, have established real-time threat
detection, enhancing compliance with industry standards.
Recommendations for CMS
To build on these successes and advance the healthcare technology ecosystem, CMS should take the
following actions:
 (PC-14, PR-9, TD-3, TD-5) Expand federated digital identity (e.g., Universal Patient ID) and single sign-
on solutions, leveraging biometric authentication and adaptive controls to reduce credential fatigue and
improve access for patients and providers.
 (PC-6, TD-8, TD-10, PR-5) Support the deployment of FHIR-based consent platforms and API-driven
solutions, ensuring patients control their PHI while reducing administrative workloads, with a focus on
accessibility features for diverse populations.
 (PC-13, PA-7, PR-12, TD-14, TD-18) Encourage the adoption of mobile-optimized complaint tools and
audit systems to enhance transparency and compliance, addressing policy conflicts and improving
data sharing.
 (TD-15) Invest in full-lifecycle security assessments and continuous monitoring to protect against
threats and strengthen system resilience, collaborating with ecosystem partners to address supply
chain vulnerabilities.
 (TD-3, TD-5) Incentivize technology solution providers to develop standardized APIs and frameworks
to streamline data exchange, drawing from successful models like meaningful use to drive adoption.
 (PR-10, PR-11, PA-3) Encourage industry-wide efforts, including with payers and regulators, to align
on standards and incentives, ensuring scalable and equitable technology deployment.
 (PR-5, TD-13) Provide incentives for vendor-led training programs to equip healthcare organizations
with the skills needed to adopt and maintain advanced technologies securely.

4. Reducing Provider Burden
Challenges in Reducing Provider Burden
Providers across the healthcare landscape are grappling with an escalating burden that diverts attention
from patient care.
 Complexities of fragmented data access: Providers struggle to navigate records due to complex
systems of record creating significant workflow disruptions. Manual care coordination across care
settings remains labor-intensive, while crafting and updating personalized care plans demands
extensive manual effort.
 Administrative overload: Scheduling, notifications, and patient education further strain resources,
compounded by the challenge of monitoring at-risk patients and addressing social determinants of
health (SDoH) like housing, food insecurity or transportation.
 Technology adoption: One of the largest challenges is adoption lag, not technology. Providers are
inundated with technology point solutions which fail to integrate as a single source of truth, with HIPAA
regulations potentially stifling data-sharing progress.
Solution in Practice: MyCare / Impact Advisors / XSELL Case Study
MyCare (a CPF portfolio company that is an MA full-risk VBC provider with over 35K patients) partnered
with Impact Advisors and XSELL to develop an Agentic AI workflow and Generative AI solution designed
to alleviate these burdens. This AI-driven platform integrates comprehensive health records into standard
EHR workflows, enables a digital health wallet for real-time ADT notifications and enhance Blue Button
access to collectively address multifaceted challenges healthcare providers face. It progresses through
five interconnected stages, each fully interoperable through a single AI deployment platform, enhancing
operational efficiency.
 In Care Coordination, AI agents seamlessly manage communication and task distribution, reducing
manual effort through full integration with the EHR. The AI agent reaches out to the patient or caregiver
after a hospital admission to schedule a Transition of Care appointment and to address medication
adherence needs.
 Personalized Care Planning leverages Generative AI to generate tailored plans with real-time
updates, summarizing medical histories, from clinical and claims data, to save clinicians significant
time and minimize hospital readmissions.
 Patient Engagement employs conversational AI for FAQs, reminders, and education, while fostering
self-management.
 Proactive Monitoring and Follow-Up uses AI analytics to identify rising risk patients and automate
follow-ups.
 Addressing SDoH, the AI agent assesses social risks (e.g., food insecurity, transportation needs) and
coordinates community resources, automating interventions to lighten provider loads.

Value Demonstrated
 Improved Care Team Responsiveness: Reduced time between clinical handoffs and transitions
enhances patient and clinician satisfaction.
 Enhanced Patient Outcomes: Increased adherence rates and clinical effectiveness, with better
control of chronic conditions (e.g., HbA1c, BP).
 Financial Benefits: Reduced thirty (30) day re-admissions from 16% to 12%. Reduced administrative
costs by 8-12% and care coordination expenses by 25-35%.
 Staffing Efficiency: Reduced clinician time spent on care plan development by 20-30%, with a 15-
25% efficiency gain in patient education efforts.
 Proactive Health Management: Lower total cost of care by 10-20% through reduction in ER and
hospital utilization, driven by proactive agent-based outreach and targeted clinical interventions.
 SDoH Impact: Increased utilization of community support resources, reducing healthcare utilization
costs by addressing non-medical barriers.
Recommendations for CMS
MyCare’s Agentic and Generative AI approach offers a robust framework to transform provider workflows
by automating routine tasks, enhancing care coordination, and addressing SDoH, all while delivering
measurable efficiency gains and bending the medical cost curve. When paired with broader solutions like
digital health wallets it can significantly lighten the load on providers. CMS’s support through incentives
and policy reforms can amplify these benefits and as such CMS should consider the following
recommendations:
 (PR-1, VB-1) Incentivize the adoption of AI-driven platforms through reimbursement models, drawing
inspiration from the meaningful use program’s success in driving EHR adoption with a time-phased,
multi-stage incentive aligned to adoption, implementation, upgrade and consistent use.
 (PR-2, TD-1) Update CHERT policies to mandate default integration of records into EHRs, ensuring
seamless access without additional steps.
 (PR-1, PC-6) Simplify Blue Button data access by allowing providers to use treatment consent, easing
the burden on seniors.
 (PR-1, TD-1) Support the development of centralized ADT alert collectors and require hospitals to
publish standardized setup instructions, minimizing administrative complexity.
 (TD-4, PC-14, PR-2) Address adoption barriers by exploring a single, interoperable identity standard,
such as extending the Medicare Beneficiary Identifier (MBI), to streamline data sharing and overcome
participation hurdles, leveraging vendor interoperability expertise.
5. Supporting Value-Based Care Goals
Fragmented event feeds, duplicative consent processes, and closed EHR tables hold back accountable
care organizations. A single interoperability spine—built on TEFCA connectivity, FHIR services, and
reusable patient consent—can unlock real-time insight, lighten administrative load, and improve clinical
performance.

Challenges in Supporting Value-Based Care Goals
 Event and Quality Alerts: Patient identification services can flag and pass ADT, pharmacy, and
laboratory messages directly into existing provider workflows to ensure primary care providers are
alerted when e.g., patients are admitted or discharged from the hospital.
 Reusable Consent Service: Patients can provide consent once and permissions travel with the
patient across all sites, HIEs and state lines.
 EHR API: Integration, particularly with leading EHR platforms, is often complex and demands
significant technical expertise, creating substantial barriers to Value Based Care interoperability and
performance.
 EHR Data Egress: Certified EHR technology must clearly expose standard data and reporting tables
to facilitate integration with care navigation and analytics tools. Further, it must do so in a manner and
format that is accessible and easy to use. Rather than hide APIs behind a complex registration process,
advanced technical requirements, and out of date and incomplete documentation, API and bulk data
exports should be turn-key, and accessible / enabled via a user-friendly front end within the EHR itself
with no additional cost or intervention by the EHR vendor required.
 Provider Blue-Button Proxy: Expand treatment consent to allow access to Blue Button data and
bypass beneficiary portal enrolment, enriching longitudinal records.

 (VB-2, VB-3) Expand CEHRT to include additional tech capabilities to support overall goals of enabling

6. Real-World Evidence and Data Generation
Real-world evidence (RWE) is pivotal for advancing healthcare innovation, enabling better clinical
decisions, and developing policies that improve patient outcomes while managing healthcare costs through
insights derived from EHRs, administrative claims, patient-generated data, and other sources. CMS has
long supported real world data generation though programs such as the Medicare Standard Analytic Files
(SAF) and Chronic Conditions Warehouse (CCW), Qualified Entity (QE), and T-MSIS Analytic Files. CMS
generated RWD has been broadly leveraged to support program integrity, quality of care evaluations,
payment reform, and public safety initiatives for policy makers, payers, providers and other healthcare
stakeholders.
The CMS Request for Information (RFI) seeks to understand how technology can support this process,
particularly in expanding data access, improving data quality, and integrating data for value-based care
(VBC) organizations. This response enhances the perspective on RWE generation by leveraging the
capabilities of OMNY Health, a leader in providing comprehensive real-world data for researchers in health
systems and life sciences organizations.
Challenges in RWE Generation
Several barriers hinder effective RWE generation
 Data Quality and Completeness: Ensuring RWE is reliable, and representative of diverse patient
populations is challenging, especially when datasets lack standardization or completeness and RWD
sources cover specific patient populations, impacting its utility for clinical and policy decisions.
 Longitudinal Patient Linkage: People move between payers, providers, and states; record-linkage
breaks when identifiers change that erode multi-year follow-up that’s critical for safety surveillance and
comparative-effectiveness studies.
 Coding Evaluation and Misclassification: Mid-study shifts from ICD-9 to ICD-10, SNOMED updates,
or local lab-code re-mapping can re-label exposures and outcomes overnight and interfere with real-
world signal.

 Tools for Analysis: RWE in clinical data is voluminous and unstructured, especially clinical notes.
These notes contain potentially valuable insights around disease progression, drug switching etc. but
require advanced tools (such as LLMs trained on this data) to enable efficient analysis.
Real World Evidence Best Practices – OMNY Health Case Study
Centralized Data Access: OMNY Health provides a single, secure point of access to de-identified clinical
data from over 85 million patients, reducing the effort required for feasibility assessment and data
acquisition. OMNY has been expanding access to their data by partnering with other cloud and compute
providers, such as Snowflake and Datavant / AWS. This growing infrastructure allows broad access to
patient data, often linked to other sources of RWD, while providers, ASTP ONC, CMS and other
stakeholders progress on data sharing at the point of care.

7. Technology Ecosystem Guidance
Technology solution providers (e.g. EHR platforms) anchor healthcare’s data supply chain. Today’s
landscape reflects a compliance with the “letter of the law” versus the “spirit of the law”, resulting in
proprietary lock-ins, fragmented identifiers, and uneven standards, imposing hidden costs on providers,
researchers, patients and caregivers.
Challenges in the Tech Ecosystem:
 Closed EHR API: Major EHR platforms still gatekeep bi-directional read / write access of providers’
own data, forcing costly middleware and leaving smaller systems unable to surface longitudinal data
in near-real-time.
 Patchwork Patient Identification: Absent a universal identifier, providers juggle Medicare Beneficiary
Identifiers (MBIs), state IDs, and vendor keys.
 Misaligned Incentives: EHR vendors profit from custom interfaces, not openness; without regulatory
carrots or penalties, ROI favors proprietary modules over shared APIs researchers need.
 Opaque Data Stewardship: “Usage fees,” throttled export speeds, and unclear de-identification rules
raise legal risk, slowing datasets destined for analytics pipelines and quality reporting.
 Fragmented Standards Adoption: Despite TEFCA, inconsistent ADT messaging and optional
implementation guides leave edge cases unresolved; providers absorb integration rework instead of
focusing on clinical insights.
Solutions in Practice: Governance / Trust / Security / Compliance / QA – Allymar Health
Solutions
Robust governance is critical to ensuring that healthcare technology solutions are secure, compliant, and
trustworthy. The "AI Safe Standard of Care™" framework from Allymar Health Solutions, provides a
valuable model coupled with Impact Advisors responsible AI playbook. Vendors can support governance
by:
 Developing AI Responsibly: Establish the foundation and develop standards for responsible AI
development based on continuous reliability checks, safety simulations, fairness audits, penetration
testing, interoperability reviews, cultural competence assessments and robust stress tests sustain high
performance and public confidence.

 Build AI for Clinical Performance and Transparency: Mandate adherence to responsible AI
standards to develop products with real-world cross-validation, clinician-run safety trials, and
continuous live-dashboard monitoring guarantee the technology stays accurate, stable, and
interpretable; Maintain a human-in-the-loop and improve explainability for clinician case reviews to
accelerate adoption by skeptical end-users.
 Secure, Fair and Resilient Operations for Trustworthiness: Maintain access controls, perform
adversarial stress-tests, bias audits across demographics, and HIPAA-aligned data-governance
guardrails harden the technology and tools against misuse, ensure cultural competence, and lock in
end-to-end regulatory compliance as it scales.
 Providing Oversight Tools: Vendors can develop platforms that enable organizations to implement
roles outlined in the AI Safe Standard of Care™. These roles ensure responsible AI use, audit
readiness, and alignment with legal and ethical standards.
 Enhancing Compliance and Security: Solutions that simplify compliance with regulations like HIPAA
or address specific pain points such as streamlining Blue Button access for seniors via treatment
consent, help organizations meet regulatory requirements efficiently.
 Building Trust through Guidance: To raise the bar on trust—especially as AI solutions move closer
to clinical care and decision support—vendors should not only offer robust training, clear protocols,
and comprehensive support frameworks (such as the human oversight and compliance frameworks)
but also drive innovation through the establishment of a dedicated, measurable trustworthy AI
certification framework. This trustworthy AI certification, potentially developed and facilitated in
collaboration with Impact Advisors and Allymar as part of an overarching innovation program, would
standardize accountability, transparency, and quality assurance, significantly bolstering healthcare
organizations' confidence in adopting AI-driven clinical decision support solutions.
Recommendations for CMS
 (TD-1, TD-2, TD-4) Make full-write FHIR endpoints and TEFCA participation mandatory for vendor
certification and bonus pools. Ending multi-HIE contracting slashes interface costs, delivers
longitudinal data within days and can widen research cohorts.
 (PC-14, VB-1) Extend the Medicare Beneficiary Identifier to commercial plans and Medicaid. Pair the
rule with “Meaningful Use–style” incentives so providers that prove clean match rates receive higher
quality-measure payouts.
 (PR-2, TD-6, TD-1) Fund vendor-built, off-the-shelf tools—central ADT alert collectors, patient wallets
granting one-tap consent—that small practices can deploy in a weekend. Tie grant dollars and shared-
savings bonuses to active production use.
 (TD-7, PR-3, VB-8) Condition Medicare Advantage and ACO contracts on adoption of an “AI Safe
Standard of Care”: board-level oversight, compliance-risk mitigation, dispute containment, and incident
readiness. Require role-based dashboards (e.g., AI Safety Director) to document audits and model
drift.
 (PR-1, PC-6, TD-7) Replace account-creation hurdles with treatment-based consent, uniform UX
guidelines, and auto-renewing tokens. Provide vendor training grants so safety-net clinics master
privacy, audit prep, and rapid incident response.
