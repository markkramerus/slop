Opportunity Area : Enhancing ADT Notification Infrastructure (PC-8, TD -10, TD -12) Despite the 2020 CMS Interoperability and Patient Access Rule requiring hospitals to provide Admit, Discharge, and Transfer (ADT) notifications, the current implementation suffers from critical deficiencies that prevent healthcare stakeholders from accessi ng complete, usable data when it matters most. The fragmented delivery system creates non -standardized data with poor quality and limited usability, while disorganized, duplicative workflows across healthcare and government agencies waste resources and delay critical information sharing. There is inconsistent recognition that real- time event data sharing is a baseline requirement for effective treatment, operations, payment, and public health monitoring. Most

Recommendation: Establish a National ADT Network (PC- 8, TD -10, TD -12, PR -7) We recommend CMS facilitate the development of a standardized framework for clinical event notifications that enables more effective information sharing across care settings. This collaborative approach would complement TEFCA while addressing the specific needs for timely, event -driven notifications that support care coordination, quality improvement, and public health. Implementation considerations could include: • Encouraging industry adoption of standardized clinical event notification formats and protocols through incentive programs and technical assistance (TD -12) • Supporting development of implementation guidance and best practices for hospitals and health systems to optimize notification workflows with their community partners (PR -7) • Exploring opportunities for enhancing notification value through inclusion of additional care settings such as urgent care centers, skilled nursing facilities, and rehabilitation centers (PC -8) • Developing best practice guides for implementing comprehensive event notifications beyond basic admit and discharge events to support more robust care coordination (TD -10) • Facilitating industry consensus on recommended notification content elements and performance standards that balance comprehensiveness with implementation feasibility (TD -10) • Supporting state -led efforts to incorporate standardized clinical event notifications into their health information exchange strategies (TD -12) Similar notification frameworks have already demonstrated success in multiple states (Michigan, Maryland, Arizona, Nebraska, and others), helping hospitals reduce readmissions, improve care transitions, and better manage value -based contracts. By supportin g broader adoption of standardized approaches to event notification, CMS could help create more efficient care coordination while allowing hospitals to maximize the value of their existing investments in health information technology. Opportunity Area : Modernize Administrative Quality Measurement (PC -12, PA -5, TD -13, PR -8) The U.S. healthcare system is burdened by an outdated, fragmented quality measurement system that is expensive, retrospective, and often irrelevant to patients. The current quality model based on HEDIS, HCC coding, and payor charts rewards documentation ov er care delivery, wastes billions of taxpayer (patient) dollars, and fails to empower the patient as a consumer. This system creates excessive manual 
 3 Copyright 2025 | velatura.org | effort and compliance costs for providers (PR -8), relies on retrospective claims data that prevents timely interventions, implements measures that fail to reflect patient experiences or priorities (PC -12), and imposes fragmented data and reporting obligations across various payers (PA -5). The result is a quality measurement approach that diverts resources away from patient care while failing to drive meaningful improvements in healthcare outcomes or patient experience. Recommendation: Establish Patient -Centered Quality Measurement (PC -12, PA -5, TD -13, PR -8, VB -8) We recommend a fundamental shift toward real- time, patient- driven quality monitoring that leverages technological advances such as artificial intelligence (AI), patient private right of access, blockchain technology, modern payment models, and enhanced con sumer capabilities. CMMI should pilot AI - Enabled Consumer Payment Models that replace existing wasteful and administratively complex quality measurement approaches, encouraging providers and payors to focus on patients rather than chasing measures (PR- 8, PA-5). Implementation should include: • AI-Driven Quality Sensors that track care completion, follow -up, and compliance with best practices in real time, providing actionable insights rather than retrospective reports (TD -13) • Patient -Authorized Digital Data Access where patients control and release their data to trigger payment, enhancing privacy while empowering consumer choice (PC -12) • Smart Contracts that execute payment events when patient outcomes or interactions are confirmed, reducing administrative overhead (VB- 8) • Consumer Quality Trigger Events that release bonuses to health plans and providers when patients signal satisfaction, follow -up completion, or health status improvement (PC -12, VB -3) To implement this vision, CMS should: • Begin replacing claims -based measures with signal- based indicators from ADT notifications, APIs, and wearable data (PA -5) • Pilot consumer payment models where AI -driven quality events are verified by patient consent (PC-12, VB -8) • Incentivize organizations that integrate real- time data from APIs, HIEs, and patient apps into clinical and payment workflows (TD -13, PR -8) • Establish a nationwide, standardized ADT notification infrastructure (as detailed in our previous recommendation) The U.S. can lead the world in transforming the quality paradigm to reward what patients truly value — timeliness, respect, improvement, and care coordination —instead of perpetuating compliance -based scoring that fails to improve care delivery.

Opportunity Area: Modernizing Consent Management to Enable Seamless Information Sharing (PC -1, PR-12, PC -8) Despite significant investments in interoperability and electronic health systems, U.S. healthcare organizations continue to rely on outdated paper -based consent processes that create substantial barriers to information sharing at critical moments of care. Administrative inefficiencies in healthcare cost billions each year. The Council for Affordable Quality Healthcare (CAQH) noted that in 2020, the U.S. healthcare industry could have saved an estimated $16.3 billion, mostly from outdated manual processes. Administrative complexities contribute to around $372 billion spent annually by healthcare organizations. Paper consent forms frequently result in information blocking scenarios (PR -12) when providers cannot verify patient sharing preferences in real- time, leading to overly cautious withholding of information that compromises care quality. Traditional paper forms also fail to ensure patient comprehension of included material, with empirical studies published in the National Library of Medicine showing that nearly 50% of patients do not fully understand what they're authorizing (PC -1) 1. These disconnected consent processes create administrative burdens for providers, confusion for patients, and dangerous gaps in information availability precisely when coordination is most needed for value -based care initiatives (PC -8). Recommendation: AI -Enhanced Digital Consent Management (PC -1, PR -12, TD -4, PC -6) We recommend CMS prioritize solutions that transform consent from static documentation into dynamic, actionable information through digital transformation and artificial intelligence.
