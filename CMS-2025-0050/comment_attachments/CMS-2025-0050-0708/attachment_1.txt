Pair Team's Response to CMS Medicare RFI: Improving Technology to
Empower Medicare Beneficiaries
Submission Date: June 16, 2025
Submitted By: Neil Batlivala, CEO, Pair Team (Neil@PairTeam.com)
Introduction: Technology-Powered, Community-Integrated Care to Reduce
Spending and Improve Outcomes for the Highest Cost Populations
Pair Team is a technology-enabled medical group that contracts a network of
community-based organizations (CBOs) to find, engage, and manage high-need,
high-cost Medicaid and Medicare patients. Powered by Community Health Workers
(CHWs) and our proprietary technology platform, Pair Team enables a collaborative
care plan across the patient, care teams and CBOs. As a community-based care
team, we stabilize the patient’s medical and non-medical needs, close care gaps,
and coordinate longitudinal care with local primary care providers to improve
outcomes and reduce costs.
We have consistently demonstrated compelling outcomes, including:
● Significant Reductions in Acute Care Utilization: Reduction in Emergency
Department utilization by 52% and inpatient utilization by 26% for our patient
cohorts.
● Demonstrable Total Cost of Care (TCOC) Savings: Achieving approximately
$25,000 in annual savings on DSNP members and 20% savings on Medicaid
members.
● Improved Clinical Quality Metrics: For instance, the percent of diabetic
patients with Hemoglobin A1c > 9% or missing decreased from 63%
pre-intervention to 30% post-intervention (national median 40%).

We believe that true beneficiary empowerment hinges on a health technology
ecosystem that is interoperable, accessible, and intelligent across medical and
non-medical stakeholders. The following section outlines our core proposals that
drive our insights and recommendations in response to this RFI.
Pair Team's Core Proposals for an Empowered Medicare Ecosystem
Based on our extensive experience serving high-need populations across Medicaid
and Medicare, and leveraging technology for comprehensive care, Pair Team
proposes five key initiatives to improve technology, empower Medicare
beneficiaries, and foster optimal health outcomes for all:
1. AI-enabled Care Navigation Copilot and Patient Support
● Need: Administrative tasks like scheduling, transportation coordination,
redeterminations, or housing referrals often fall through—despite being
directly tied to access, adherence, and utilization. This is where high-needs
patients require the most support and accompaniment as these referrals end
up unsuccessful. Furthermore, patients often have a need of the moment for
navigation support, and if they don't receive timely assistance, they are prone
to seeking more costly emergency services or inefficiently searching across
hundreds of resources in real-time. This dynamic creates significant waste
within the healthcare system.
● Real-World Evidence (Pair Team's Experience):
1. Pair Team's care teams currently dedicate an estimated 15-20% of
their time weekly to high-frequency, complex administrative tasks
such as coordinating transportation, scheduling appointments across
multiple providers, or following up on intricate public benefit
applications. While crucial, the manual nature of these tasks limits the
number of patients each human care manager can effectively support.
www.pairteam.com

2. We often see that even with dedicated CHW support, administrative
complexities for housing or benefit renewals (e.g., missing documents,
navigating multiple agency portals) can lead to referrals "falling
through" for 30-40% of our most complex cases. This directly
contributes to care gaps and prevents our demonstrated 52%
reduction in ED utilization from being even greater. Patients awaiting
manual referral processing frequently resort to emergency
departments, adding to avoidable costs.
3. By automating routine aspects of these tasks, we estimate an
AI-powered copilot could free up 20 hours per CHW per week,
allowing them to focus on the most complex, nuanced, or
trust-building interactions, thus extending high-touch support to more
patients. For example, we see AI-enabled PCP scheduling on our
platform reduce CHW administrative time by >10%.
● Proposal: CMMI demonstration to deploy an AI-powered care navigation
copilot that automates common support tasks—appointment scheduling,
SNAP/Medicaid renewals, ride coordination, shelter intake forms—while
escalating edge cases to a human case manager. Current care management
(CCM) and chronic care integration (CHI) codes often incentivize human time
spent, rather than incentivizing efficiency. CMS should consider payment
models that directly incentivize using AI to drive efficiency and proactive
patient support. The CMMI demonstration would:
● Designate a temporary G-code (e.g., modeled on G0511) so copilot
work is billable under incident-to rules.
● Embed guardrails: required human review, minimum error rates, audit
logs accessible to patients.
● Measure impact: (a) task resolution rate (e.g., appointments scheduled
within 3 days), (b) 90-day avoidable utilization, (c) cost-effectiveness
relative to staffing.

This model extends high-touch, high-frequency support to patients at
scale—without adding administrative burden to clinical teams—and builds on
CMS’s goals around digital tools, access, and cost reduction.
● Implementation Considerations:
1. Design CMMI Demonstration: CMS, through CMMI, is encouraged to
design a demonstration model for the AI-powered care navigation
copilot. This design should incorporate clear performance metrics,
define billing mechanisms (like the proposed temporary G-code
billable under incident-to rules), and establish ethical AI use guidance.
To address uptake challenges, CMS should consider some payment
tied to utilization of the copilot. It is also vital to ensure patient
experience is a core component of the incentives.
2. Identify Launch Markets and Partners: CMS should choose a mix of
rural, suburban, and urban areas for the demonstration to ensure broad
applicability. A handful of private sector AI partners, selected via RFIs,
should be chosen, prioritizing both their technology capabilities and
healthcare experience. CMS should also consider upfront funding to
support ongoing research and development given the rapid pace of AI
development. Coordination with local plans, providers, CBOs, and state
agencies will be crucial to effectively market the AI navigation copilot
project.
3. Launch and Evaluate: The demonstration should be launched and
rigorously evaluated, assessing both process and outcomes-related
metrics. It will be important to address outcome attribution challenges,
and working with ACO partners may help in this regard to clearly link
AI-enabled navigation to improved health and cost outcomes.
2. Public-Benefit FHIR Endpoint: Housing & Eligibility
● Need: Nearly one-half of Medicare beneficiaries live on incomes < 200% FPL,
and millions rely on SNAP, SSI, LIHEAP, or HUD programs to stay healthy. The
current lack of clinician visibility into these public benefit programs creates
www.pairteam.com

systemic inefficiency and avoidable healthcare waste. When vital
non-medical needs like housing or food go unaddressed due to clinicians
being unaware of benefit eligibility or status, patients face preventable health
crises—driving up emergency department visits, inpatient stays, and overall
healthcare costs. Providing real-time information on benefit eligibility,
enrollment, and access would empower clinicians to preemptively support
these critical upstream drivers of health, thereby reducing costly
downstream utilization.
● Real-World Evidence (Pair Team's Experience):
1. Our CHWs often spend hours manually navigating siloed public benefit
systems or trying to obtain paper documentation to verify a patient's
eligibility for critical support like SNAP or housing vouchers. This
manual verification process reduces staff efficiency by more than half,
leading to system inefficiency, delayed service delivery and higher
costs.
2. For example, we worked with a patient experiencing homelessness
whose SNAP benefits lapsed. Without immediate visibility into this,
their prescription for diabetes medication went unfilled due to lack of
funds for food. This led to a preventable ED visit for uncontrolled blood
sugar. Real-time access to their SNAP status would have allowed our
team to proactively intervene, helping with re-certification before the
crisis. This kind of proactive support, enabled by holistic data access,
contributes to our demonstrated clinical improvements, such as the
significant reduction in diabetic patients with uncontrolled A1c (63%
pre vs. 30% post).
3. We consistently observe stronger patient engagement, trust, and care
plan adherence when our teams can proactively support patients in
maintaining or accessing public benefits. Patients feel seen and
understood when their care team acknowledges and helps address
these fundamental needs, demonstrating the direct link between social
stability and clinical outcomes.
www.pairteam.com

● Proposal: ONC should publish a “PublicBenefits” FHIR API. Helpful
components could include:
1. Eligibility and Enrollment Profile
■ BenefitType (e.g., Medicaid, SNAP, Housing Voucher)
■ EligibilityStatus (e.g., eligible, conditionally eligible, denied)
■ EffectivePeriod (start/end dates)
■ LastRedeterminationDate
■ NextRenewalDate
2. Program Participation and History
■ ProgramName (e.g., SNAP, Section 8, WIC)
■ EnrollmentDate
■ DisenrollmentDate
■ ParticipatingAgency (e.g., County social services office)
■ DeliveryMechanism (e.g., EBT card, voucher)
3. Needs Assessments / Non-Medical Risk Data
■ HousingStability
■ FoodInsecurity
■ TransportationNeeds
■ EmploymentStatus
■ CaregiverNeeds
4. BenefitDelivery Profile
■ Allotment amount
■ Last disbursement date
5. HousingStatus profile mapping HUD HMIS data elements, using ACA
§1561 enrollment-IT standards for security. Certifying this single
endpoint lets digital-health vendors (e.g., Pair Team) merge benefit and
health data under one consent, giving providers real-time insight into
financial barriers before they translate into ED visits.
● Implementation Considerations:
1. Define FHIR Implementation Guide for Public Benefits through
Public-Private Partnership: The Gravity Project may be best
positioned to define the FHIR Implementation Guide for Public Benefits.
www.pairteam.com

ONC could then oversee its certification, approval, and help ensure
alignment with US Core or USCDI expansion.
2. Federal HHS to host central API infrastructure with integration by
local agencies: HHS is encouraged to establish a national hub for
managing public benefits via the FHIR API. Centralizing a PublicBenefits
FHIR could create significant long-term leverage for HHS compared to
having each local state agency set up their own FHIR servers. This
would involve local State agencies standardizing and integrating their
existing eligibility and enrollment systems into the FHIR system. HHS
could consider funding a reference implementation with a single state

3. Utilize existing Patient Access Rules for Private Sector to Build
Solutions: With guidance from ONC, private sector solutions (e.g., Pair
Team, other digital-health vendors) can build solutions that leverage
Public Benefit FHIR APIs using existing Patient Access Rule standards to
drive utilization at the point of care. In addition to provider solutions
(e.g., EMR integration), new tooling could be established by the private
sector to support Community Based Organizations (CBOs) to pull and
push data.
4. Expand Provider Definition to Include Community Health Workers
(CHWs): Given the central role of CHWs in public benefits, CMS and
ASTP could consider updating guidance to include CHW authorized
access to consumer APIs as a recognized pathway for data access.
Alternatively, it is recommended that CHWs be considered a type of
provider for this API access given their direct role in care navigation
and non-medical support.

● Proposal: Add a “Community-Service Endpoint” type to the National
Directory and encourage QHINs to support Facilitated FHIR exchanges that
push service delivered events (e.g., meal box, shelter bed) and pull outcome

● Implementation Considerations:
1. Define Standard and Promote Across QHINs: ONC is encouraged to

2. Extend National Directory to Include CBOs and Staff: CMS has the

technology gaps.

CMS could update quality measures and reporting requirements to

stratification by CBO utilization and non-medical interventions. This

that incentivizes integration of CBO data and rewards improved

4. Authorized Representatives for Patient Access APIs
● Need: Current Patient-Access API requires beneficiaries to have access to
payer portals and high-needs beneficiaries, especially Duals, rarely activate
portal credentials, resulting in their data remaining inaccessible at the point
of care.
● Real-World Evidence (Pair Team's Experience):
1. Many of Pair Team's patients, particularly those experiencing
homelessness, severe mental illness, or cognitive impairments, lack
stable internet access, personal devices, or the digital literacy to
manage complex payer portal credentials (usernames, passwords,
multi-factor authentication). This leads to frequent situations where
critical health information (e.g., coverage changes, prior authorization
status, recent claims data) remains inaccessible to their care team,
delaying necessary medical interventions and impacting the efficiency
of care coordination that has yielded our 58% reduction in ED
utilization.
2. For example, one of our elderly, dual-eligible patients required a
specialist referral. Without the ability for their CHW to securely access
their payer's portal on their behalf (with consent), confirming referral
authorization became a multi-day manual process involving phone
calls and faxes, delaying access to specialized care. Had an
“authorized-representative” OAuth scope existed, the CHW could have
immediately obtained the necessary information, accelerating the
referral and preventing potential avoidable utilization.
● Proposal: Add an “authorized-representative” OAuth scope so caregivers,
CHWs, or accredited CBO staff can fetch data with a one-time beneficiary
e-consent—even when the member has no credentials. This preserves
beneficiary control while routing plan-held data to the clinicians, caregivers,
and even community-based staff who manage day-to-day care.
www.pairteam.com

● Implementation Considerations:
1. Define and Promote Standards Across Payers: ONC is encouraged to
revise existing API guidance to explicitly include and standardize the
"authorized-representative" OAuth scope for patient access APIs,
providing clear technical specifications. CMS can then promote
adoption across payers.
2. Establish Consent Process and Guidance: CMS could establish clear
guidance on acceptable forms of beneficiary e-consent for delegation,
including revisiting consumer protections to ensure the right to know
who requested records, the right to deny requests, and the right to
authorize third parties to access/use data.
3. Promote Private Sector Tooling Adoption: Payers are encouraged to
update their Patient Access APIs to include the
"authorized-representative" OAuth scope. Digital Health Vendors (e.g.,
Pair Team - private sector) should develop patient-facing applications
and care coordination platforms that securely leverage this scope.
Providers and CBOs can implement clear internal policies and train
staff on its use.
5. Z-code Inclusion in Risk Adjustment: Aligning Payment with Non-Medical
Needs
● Need: Current CMS-HCC risk adjustment does not include Z-codes — even
though homelessness drives 2-3x higher acute-spend patterns.
● Real-World Evidence (Pair Team's Experience):
1. Pair Team systematically screens for and addresses non-medical
needs, including homelessness (Z59.0), food insecurity (Z59.4), and
transportation barriers. Our internal analysis of patient cohorts
consistently reveals that beneficiaries with a documented Z59.0
(homelessness) status incur average healthcare costs 2.5 times higher
than comparable beneficiaries without this factor, primarily driven by
increased ED utilization and inpatient admissions for preventable
conditions. This directly impacts Total Cost of Care where we
www.pairteam.com

observe $25,000 annual savings on DSNP members and 20%
savings on Medicaid members when comprehensive non-medical
needs are addressed.
2. Our ability to improve health outcomes and reduce avoidable
utilization for these patients often stems directly from successful
non-medical interventions (e.g., connecting a patient to stable housing
reduces subsequent psychiatric ED visits). However, the current risk
adjustment model doesn't fully capture the clinical complexity and
associated costs driven by these non-medical factors, leading to an
inaccurate reflection of our work's value.
3. Because these non-medical cost drivers are not accurately captured,
health plans are less incentivized to enroll this population in Medicare
Advantage and less likely to invest in the non-medical interventions for
these complex enrollees, as the financial risk of caring for these
high-need individuals is not adequately acknowledged in their
capitated payments.
● Proposal: Explore inclusion of Z-codes in the CMS-HCC model to evaluate
their performance and align payment with need and re-evaluate
underweighted diagnosis codes of SMI and SUD. More precise codes + RAF
parity will encourage plans to enroll high-need beneficiaries in Medicare
Advantage where they will receive better care coordination.
● Implementation Considerations:
1. Quantify Impact of Non-Medical Z-codes: CMS could conduct
actuarial analysis to quantify the impact of specific non-medical
Z-codes (e.g., Z59.0, Z59.4) on healthcare utilization and costs for
Medicare and Medicaid populations.
2. Update CMS-HCC Model: Based on analysis, CMS could update the
CMS-HCC model to incorporate these Z-codes for risk adjustment,
aligning payment with true patient complexity.
3. Re-evaluate SMI/SUD Weighting: CMS is encouraged to re-evaluate
and adjust the weighting of diagnosis codes for Severe Mental Illness
www.pairteam.com

(SMI) and Substance Use Disorder (SUD) to ensure accurate risk
adjustment parity with physical health conditions.
4. Standardize Non-Medical Documentation: HHS should work with
clinical coding bodies and providers to ensure consistent and accurate
documentation of non-medical using Z-codes across all care settings,
and VBC Organizations should prioritize systematic non-medical
screening.
5. Adapt Financial Models and Care Strategies: Health Plans and VBC
Organization are encouraged to adapt their financial models and care
management strategies to reflect the updated risk adjustment,
allowing for greater investment in non-medical interventions and
comprehensive care for complex beneficiaries. They should advocate
for consistent coding practices across the industry.

3. Information Blocking and Digital Identity
● IB-2. What has been most helpful in addressing information blocking?
What additional actions should CMS take to address information
blocking? The 21st Century Cures Act information blocking rules have raised
awareness. However, for high-need populations, effective "information
blocking" often occurs not through malicious intent, but through structural
barriers to data access and digital literacy. Additional CMS actions include:
○ Mandate/Incentivize Expanded FHIR Endpoints: CMS can consider
requiring or strongly incentivizing the development of FHIR endpoints
for critical non-medical and public benefits data, as outlined in our
www.pairteam.com

Public-Benefit FHIR Endpoint proposal, addressing a current gap in
data availability that hinders comprehensive care.
○ Support for Authorized Representatives: Address the systemic
information blocking that occurs when patients cannot personally
navigate portals. Implementing Authorized Representatives for
Patient Access APIs would significantly empower caregivers and care
teams to access essential data with proper consent, breaking down
access barriers for vulnerable individuals.
○ Strengthen Consumer Data Protections: As more providers and third
parties access records, CMS is encouraged to implement robust
consumer protections to build and maintain trust. These should
include:
■ The explicit right to know who requested what records.
■ The right to deny such requests (with clear, understood
implications).
■ The right to authorize specific third parties (apps, people) to
access and use their data, ensuring granular control.
● DI-1. What methods of identity verification for patients and caregivers
should be considered by CMS to ensure that digital health products can
be accessed safely and securely? CMS should consider a tiered approach,
balancing security with accessibility. For high-need Medicare and Medicaid
beneficiaries, strict reliance on personal digital credentials is a significant
barrier. Methods should include secure, consent-based proxy access. Our
Authorized Representatives for Patient Access APIs proposal champions
this, allowing caregivers, CHWs, or accredited CBO staff to fetch data with a
one-time beneficiary e-consent. This allows for safe and secure access while
recognizing the practical realities of vulnerable populations who may lack
consistent digital access or literacy.

clinical activities.

teams to manage more patients effectively and deliver more proactive care.
The integration of Public-Benefit FHIR Endpoint and TEFCA
Community-Service Endpoints improves quality by giving providers a
complete, real-time picture of non-medical and community-based
interventions, enabling more precise and effective care planning. This allows
for proactive risk identification and intervention, moving beyond reactive,

● PY-2. What opportunities exist to simplify technology and reduce the
documentation burden associated with digital health products for payers

Public-Benefit FHIR Endpoint and TEFCA Community-Service Endpoints

● PY-4. What opportunities exist for improving the efficiency and quality of
care through health technology, beyond those related to patient access?
Payers can significantly improve efficiency and quality by leveraging
technology for more accurate risk stratification and proactive intervention.
Current CMS-HCC risk adjustment models ignore crucial non-medical
Z-codes, despite homelessness, for example, driving 2-3x higher

homelessness incur average healthcare costs 2.5 times higher than
comparable beneficiaries without this factor, primarily due to increased ED
utilization and inpatient admissions for preventable conditions.
This directly impacts Total Cost of Care, where we observe $25,000 annual
savings on DSNP members and 20% savings on Medicaid members when
comprehensive non-medical needs are addressed. Our ability to improve
outcomes and reduce avoidable utilization often stems from successful

6. Technology Vendors
● TV-3. What specific features and functionalities of health technology are
critical to successful technology vendor adoption by providers and
payers? Critical features include true interoperability (e.g., via FHIR APIs),
ease of integration with existing systems, and clear demonstration of value.
For Pair Team, a key functionality is the ability to securely and reliably
integrate disparate data sources across the full spectrum of patient needs.
This requires open standards for new data types, as proposed by our
Public-Benefit FHIR Endpoint and TEFCA Community-Service Endpoints.
Vendors will be more successful if they can build on a foundation of
comprehensive, standardized data, reducing bespoke integration work and
enhancing the value proposition of their solutions.
● TV-5. What role, if any, should CMS have in reviewing or approving digital
health products for technology vendors on the basis of their efficacy,
quality, or impact on health outcomes (not approving in the sense of a
coverage determination)? What criteria should be used if there is a
review process? CMS has an opportunity to establish or facilitate a
marketplace for digital health solutions for vendors, particularly focusing
on tools that directly impact patient outcomes and care coordination for
high-need populations. This marketplace could help providers and payers
identify vetted solutions. Criteria for inclusion in such a marketplace might
emphasize adherence to proposed standards like Public-Benefit FHIR

data accessibility. It could also consider a tool’s capacity for AI-enabled
Care Navigation, with clear guardrails for human oversight and error rates, as
well as its ability to enable access for Authorized Representatives for
Patient Access APIs, promoting accessible care.

7. Value-Based Care Organizations
● VBCO-1. What are the specific opportunities and challenges that health
technology brings to improving patient access to care for value-based
care organizations? Opportunities: For VBC organizations, technology offers
the ability to precisely identify, engage, and manage high-cost, high-need
patients. Our AI-enabled Care Navigation Copilot helps extend high-touch
support at scale, improving patient engagement and adherence. The
integration of non-medical and public benefit data (via Public-Benefit FHIR
Endpoint and TEFCA Community-Service Endpoints) allows VBCs to
proactively address root causes of poor health, rather than reactively
managing acute episodes, thereby reducing costs. Challenges: The primary
challenge is the lack of comprehensive, interoperable data across the clinical,
non-medical, and public benefit sectors. Without this data, VBC organizations
struggle to accurately stratify risk, coordinate truly holistic care, and measure
the ROI of non-medical interventions.

management tools, sophisticated care coordination capabilities, and deep
analytics. These functionalities are critically dependent on access to
comprehensive data, which necessitates:
○ Public-Benefit FHIR Endpoint: To understand a patient's full risk
profile tied to non-medical interventions and integrate this into care
planning.
○ TEFCA Community-Service Endpoints: To track and attribute
community-based interventions to health outcomes, demonstrating
value for upstream investments.
