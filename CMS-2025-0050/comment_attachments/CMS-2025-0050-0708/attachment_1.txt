 Pair Team's Response to CMS Medicare RFI: Improving Technology to Empower Medicare Beneﬁciaries Submission Date: June 16, 2025 Submitted By: Neil Batlivala, CEO, Pair Team ( Neil@PairTeam.com ) Introduction: Technology-Powered, Community-Integrated Care to Reduce Spending and Improve Outcomes for the Highest Cost Populations Pair Team is a technology-enabled medical group that contracts a network of community-based organizations (CBOs) to ﬁnd, engage, and manage high-need, high-cost Medicaid and Medicare patients. Powered by Community Health Workers (CHWs) and our proprietary technology platform, Pair Team enables a collaborative care plan across the patient, care teams and CBOs. As a community-based care team, we stabilize the patient’s medical and non-medical needs, close care gaps, and coordinate longitudinal care with local primary care providers to improve outcomes and reduce costs. We have consistently demonstrated compelling outcomes, including: ● Signiﬁcant Reductions in Acute Care Utilization: Reduction in Emergency Department utilization by 52% and inpatient utilization by 26% for our patient cohorts. ● Demonstrable Total Cost of Care (TCOC) Savings: Achieving approximately $25,000 in annual savings on DSNP members and 20% savings on Medicaid members. ● Improved Clinical Quality Metrics: For instance, the percent of diabetic patients with Hemoglobin A1c > 9% or missing decreased from 63% pre-intervention to 30% post-intervention (national median 40%). w w w.pairteam .com 
 We believe that true beneﬁciary empowerment hinges on a health technology ecosystem that is interoperable, accessible, and intelligent across medical and non-medical stakeholders. Pair Team's Core Proposals for an Empowered Medicare Ecosystem Based on our extensive experience serving high-need populations across Medicaid and Medicare, and leveraging technology for comprehensive care, Pair Team proposes ﬁve key initiatives to improve technology, empower Medicare beneﬁciaries, and foster optimal health outcomes for all: 1. AI-enabled Care Navigation Copilot and Patient Support ● Need: Administrative tasks like scheduling, transportation coordination, redeterminations, or housing referrals often fall through—despite being directly tied to access, adherence, and utilization. This is where high-needs patients require the most support and accompaniment as these referrals end up unsuccessful. Furthermore, patients often have a need of the moment for navigation support, and if they don't receive timely assistance, they are prone to seeking more costly emergency services or inefﬁciently searching across hundreds of resources in real-time. This dynamic creates signiﬁcant waste within the healthcare system. ● Real-World Evidence (Pair Team's Experience): 1. Pair Team's care teams currently dedicate an estimated 15-20% of their time weekly to high-frequency, complex administrative tasks such as coordinating transportation, scheduling appointments across multiple providers, or following up on intricate public beneﬁt applications. While crucial, the manual nature of these tasks limits the number of patients each human care manager can effectively support. w w w.pairteam .com 
 2. We often see that even with dedicated CHW support, administrative complexities for housing or beneﬁt renewals (e.g., missing documents, navigating multiple agency portals) can lead to referrals "falling through" for 30-40% of our most complex cases. This directly contributes to care gaps and prevents our demonstrated 52% reduction in ED utilization from being even greater. Patients awaiting manual referral processing frequently resort to emergency departments, adding to avoidable costs. 3. By automating routine aspects of these tasks, we estimate an AI-powered copilot could free up 20 hours per CHW per week , allowing them to focus on the most complex, nuanced, or trust-building interactions, thus extending high-touch support to more patients. For example, we see AI-enabled PCP scheduling on our platform reduce CHW administrative time by >10%. ● Proposal: CMMI demonstration to deploy an AI-powered care navigation copilot that automates common support tasks—appointment scheduling, SNAP/Medicaid renewals, ride coordination, shelter intake forms—while escalating edge cases to a human case manager. Current care management (CCM) and chronic care integration (CHI) codes often incentivize human time spent, rather than incentivizing efﬁciency. CMS should consider payment models that directly incentivize using AI to drive efﬁciency and proactive patient support. The CMMI demonstration would: ● Designate a temporary G-code (e.g., modeled on G0511) so copilot work is billable under incident-to rules. ● Embed guardrails: required human review, minimum error rates, audit logs accessible to patients. ● Measure impact: (a) task resolution rate (e.g., appointments scheduled within 3 days), (b) 90-day avoidable utilization, (c) cost-effectiveness relative to stafﬁng. w w w.pairteam .com 
 This model extends high-touch, high-frequency support to patients at scale—without adding administrative burden to clinical teams—and builds on CMS’s goals around digital tools, access, and cost reduction. ● Implementation Considerations: 1. Design CMMI Demonstration: CMS, through CMMI, is encouraged to design a demonstration model for the AI-powered care navigation copilot. This design should incorporate clear performance metrics, deﬁne billing mechanisms (like the proposed temporary G-code billable under incident-to rules), and establish ethical AI use guidance. To address uptake challenges, CMS should consider some payment tied to utilization of the copilot. It is also vital to ensure patient experience is a core component of the incentives. 2. Identify Launch Markets and Partners: CMS should choose a mix of rural, suburban, and urban areas for the demonstration to ensure broad applicability. A handful of private sector AI partners, selected via RFIs, should be chosen, prioritizing both their technology capabilities and healthcare experience. CMS should also consider upfront funding to support ongoing research and development given the rapid pace of AI development. Coordination with local plans, providers, CBOs, and state agencies will be crucial to effectively market the AI navigation copilot project. 3. Launch and Evaluate: The demonstration should be launched and rigorously evaluated, assessing both process and outcomes-related metrics. It will be important to address outcome attribution challenges, and working with ACO partners may help in this regard to clearly link AI-enabled navigation to improved health and cost outcomes. 2. Public-Beneﬁt FHIR Endpoint: Housing & Eligibility ● Need: Nearly one-half of Medicare beneﬁciaries live on incomes < 200% FPL, and millions rely on SNAP, SSI, LIHEAP, or HUD programs to stay healthy. The current lack of clinician visibility into these public beneﬁt programs creates w w w.pairteam .com 
 systemic inefﬁciency and avoidable healthcare waste . When vital non-medical needs like housing or food go unaddressed due to clinicians being unaware of beneﬁt eligibility or status, patients face preventable health crises—driving up emergency department visits, inpatient stays, and overall healthcare costs. Providing real-time information on beneﬁt eligibility, enrollment, and access would empower clinicians to preemptively support these critical upstream drivers of health, thereby reducing costly downstream utilization . ● Real-World Evidence (Pair Team's Experience): 1. Our CHWs often spend hours manually navigating siloed public beneﬁt systems or trying to obtain paper documentation to verify a patient's eligibility for critical support like SNAP or housing vouchers. This manual veriﬁcation process reduces staff efﬁciency by more than half, leading to system inefﬁciency, delayed service delivery and higher costs. 2. For example, we worked with a patient experiencing homelessness whose SNAP beneﬁts lapsed. Without immediate visibility into this, their prescription for diabetes medication went unﬁlled due to lack of funds for food. This led to a preventable ED visit for uncontrolled blood sugar. Real-time access to their SNAP status would have allowed our team to proactively intervene, helping with re-certiﬁcation before the crisis. This kind of proactive support, enabled by holistic data access, contributes to our demonstrated clinical improvements, such as the signiﬁcant reduction in diabetic patients with uncontrolled A1c (63% pre vs. 30% post). 3. We consistently observe stronger patient engagement, trust, and care plan adherence when our teams can proactively support patients in maintaining or accessing public beneﬁts. Authorized Representatives for Patient Access APIs ● Need: Current Patient-Access API requires beneﬁciaries to have access to payer portals and high-needs beneﬁciaries, especially Duals, rarely activate portal credentials, resulting in their data remaining inaccessible at the point of care. ● Real-World Evidence (Pair Team's Experience): 1. Many of Pair Team's patients, particularly those experiencing homelessness, severe mental illness, or cognitive impairments, lack stable internet access, personal devices, or the digital literacy to manage complex payer portal credentials (usernames, passwords, multi-factor authentication). This leads to frequent situations where critical health information (e.g., coverage changes, prior authorization status, recent claims data) remains inaccessible to their care team, delaying necessary medical interventions and impacting the efﬁciency of care coordination that has yielded our 58% reduction in ED utilization. 2. For example, one of our elderly, dual-eligible patients required a specialist referral. Without the ability for their CHW to securely access their payer's portal on their behalf (with consent), conﬁrming referral authorization became a multi-day manual process involving phone calls and faxes, delaying access to specialized care. Had an “authorized-representative” OAuth scope existed, the CHW could have immediately obtained the necessary information, accelerating the referral and preventing potential avoidable utilization. ● Proposal: Add an “ authorized-representative ” OAuth scope so caregivers, CHWs, or accredited CBO staff can fetch data with a one-time beneﬁciary e-consent—even when the member has no credentials. This preserves beneﬁciary control while routing plan-held data to the clinicians, caregivers, and even community-based staff who manage day-to-day care. w w w.pairteam .com 
 ● Implementation Considerations: 1. Deﬁne and Promote Standards Across Payers: ONC is encouraged to revise existing API guidance to explicitly include and standardize the "authorized-representative" OAuth scope for patient access APIs, providing clear technical speciﬁcations. CMS can then promote adoption across payers. 2. Establish Consent Process and Guidance: CMS could establish clear guidance on acceptable forms of beneﬁciary e-consent for delegation, including revisiting consumer protections to ensure the right to know who requested records, the right to deny requests, and the right to authorize third parties to access/use data. 3. Providers and CBOs can implement clear internal policies and train staff on its use. 5. Our internal analysis of patient cohorts consistently reveals that beneﬁciaries with a documented Z59.0 (homelessness) status incur average healthcare costs 2.5 times higher than comparable beneﬁciaries without this factor, primarily driven by increased ED utilization and inpatient admissions for preventable conditions. This directly impacts Total Cost of Care where we w w w.pairteam .com 
 observe $25,000 annual savings on DSNP members and 20% savings on Medicaid members when comprehensive non-medical needs are addressed. 2. Our ability to improve health outcomes and reduce avoidable utilization for these patients often stems directly from successful non-medical interventions (e.g., connecting a patient to stable housing reduces subsequent psychiatric ED visits). However, the current risk adjustment model doesn't fully capture the clinical complexity and associated costs driven by these non-medical factors, leading to an inaccurate reﬂection of our work's value. 3. Because these non-medical cost drivers are not accurately captured, health plans are less incentivized to enroll this population in Medicare Advantage and less likely to invest in the non-medical interventions for these complex enrollees, as the ﬁnancial risk of caring for these high-need individuals is not adequately acknowledged in their capitated payments. ● Proposal: Explore inclusion of Z-codes in the CMS-HCC model to evaluate their performance and align payment with need and re-evaluate underweighted diagnosis codes of SMI and SUD. More precise codes + RAF parity will encourage plans to enroll high-need beneﬁciaries in Medicare Advantage where they will receive better care coordination. ● Implementation Considerations: 1. Update CMS-HCC Model: Based on analysis, CMS could update the CMS-HCC model to incorporate these Z-codes for risk adjustment, aligning payment with true patient complexity. 3. Adapt Financial Models and Care Strategies: Health Plans and VBC Organization are encouraged to adapt their ﬁnancial models and care management strategies to reﬂect the updated risk adjustment, allowing for greater investment in non-medical interventions and comprehensive care for complex beneﬁciaries.
