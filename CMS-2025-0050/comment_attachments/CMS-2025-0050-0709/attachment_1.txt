I. Introduction
After a doctor's appointment, as we filled up my mother's weekly medication box I
watched her confusion when medications are removed from her medication list but
continue arriving via auto-refill. This disconnect between health IT systems and human
reality represents more than medication confusion—it's one symptom of a larger crisis:
the massive coordination gap between what medical providers document and what
family caregivers actually manage daily.
As a health informaticist and AI strategist with two decades of experience, I approach
technology like a surgeon approaches sutures—stitching people and processes together
with ones and zeroes to heal systemic gaps. My perspective is anchored in lived
experience—from faxing records for my late husband's complex care to navigating
today's fragmented digital landscape across multiple family members.
Through Mosaic Life Tech, I support organizations like the National Council for
Prescription Drug Programs (NCPDP) in understanding the impact of AI with governance
frameworks grounded in real-world impact and trust. My expertise spans SMART on
FHIR applications, HL7 standards, and value-based care principles, but what drives my
work is understanding the invisible health data that actually drives outcomes: caregiver
observations of daily functioning, transportation barriers, family communication
patterns, medication adherence challenges, and the countless coordination tasks that
fall to families when clinical systems don't talk to each other.
AI's greatest opportunity lies in making this invisible caregiving work visible and
actionable within health systems. The approach to this RFI committed to ensuring digital
health ecosystems don't just innovate—they recognize and integrate the essential
coordination work that family caregivers perform when medical providers' systems fail
to connect.

II. Patient and Consumer Responses
Patient Access to Comprehensive Health Information
This directly addresses RFI questions PC-1, PC-2, PC-8, PC-9, and PC-12, which focus on
health management applications, consolidated health information access, valuable
health data sources, Blue Button API expansion, and operational health data use cases.
Barriers
Patient and caregiver access to complete and understandable medical information
remains fragmented across systems, portals, and formats. Clinical notes—when
available—are often delayed, heavily templated, or full of clinical jargon that bears little
resemblance to what was communicated during the visit. This misalignment undermines
trust and makes it difficult for patients and caregivers to act on the information in
meaningful ways.
Current EHRs are structured for billing and clinical abstraction, not patient
understanding. This makes post-visit coordination difficult, especially for aging adults or
those with cognitive conditions. Caregivers are expected to piece together care across
multiple systems, despite not being formally included in care team structures. APIs and
SMART on FHIR apps are technically available, but inconsistent implementation and data
completeness make them unreliable for longitudinal patient insight.
Opportunities
Patients should have the right to access their full clinical history—including structured
and unstructured data—regardless of the system or vendor. This should include
imaging, referrals, and progress notes. CMS can promote consistent support for
standards-based interoperability through TEFCA and FHIR, but should also fund pilots
for ambient, AI-supported longitudinal care navigation tools for caregivers.
New rules should require that caregiver access and shared decision-making features be
standard in-patient portals and third-party app interfaces. AI development and
deployment is moving faster than traditional development and adoption cycles. To
advance usability and accessibility at scale, we must also move from protracted
innovation timelines to agile, rapid development cycles.
Example from Practice
As a caregiver, I routinely navigate a maze of patient portals and care silos to manage
medications and lab results for multiple family members. Even as a health informaticist
who built a SMART on FHIR app to retrieve my own health data, I struggled to connect
records across systems. For most patients, this is simply unmanageable.

An AI agent I designed now integrates CGM and wearables data to provide personalized
feedback and insight for self-management. Expanding access to comprehensive data
through FHIR-enabled apps and QHINs would make this kind of real-time, personalized
support possible for millions of patients and caregivers.
Policy Recommendation
CMS should require certified EHR vendors and QHINs to support a comprehensive
patient record API that includes structured and unstructured data. These APIs should
operate within a broader ecosystem that reflects not only technical capabilities but also
cross-sector accountability and governance. This API must adhere to a 'Portal-API Data
Parity' standard, ensuring that any data available to the patient or caregiver via a portal
is equally accessible and consumable via the API. This API must include not only
structured and unstructured clinical data (e.g., progress notes, imaging reports, referral
details), but also patient-generated health data (PGHD) from wearables, patient-
reported outcomes (PROs), and critical caregiver observations that are currently siloed
or uncaptured (e.g., medication adherence challenges, subtle behavioral changes, social
determinants of health insights).
While acknowledging potential resource constraints for smaller practices, CMS should fund and
require AI governance frameworks at state and organizational levels... *with tiered
requirements and accessible resources to ensure equitable adoption across all provider types.*"
Digital Health Application Accessibility and User Experience
This directly addresses RFI questions PC-3, PC-4, PC-6, and PC-14, which explore existing
digital health applications, missing features, accessibility needs, and digital identity
adoption.
Barriers
Current digital health applications often fail to account for the diverse needs of
Medicare beneficiaries, particularly those with limited digital literacy or accessibility
requirements. Many applications prioritize younger, tech-savvy users while overlooking
the specific workflow needs of family caregivers managing complex care coordination.
Digital identity systems remain fragmented and inconsistent across health systems,
creating barriers to seamless data access. The complexity of managing multiple logins
and authentication methods particularly burdens older adults and caregivers managing
multiple family members' care.
Opportunities

CMS should establish accessibility standards for Medicare-focused digital health
applications that go beyond basic Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)
compliance to address the specific needs of aging adults and family caregivers. This
includes support for voice interfaces, computer vision, simplified navigation patterns,
and caregiver proxy access capabilities.
Investment in standardized digital identity frameworks that work seamlessly across
health systems would significantly reduce barriers to patient and caregiver engagement
with digital health tools.
III. Provider Responses
Supporting Provider Adoption of Digital Health Innovation
This directly addresses RFI questions PR-1, PR-2, PR-7, and PR-9, which focus on provider
adoption strategies, workflow application obstacles, comprehensive data availability,
and digital identity adoption.
Barriers
Rapid adoption of AI tools is outpacing many health systems' readiness to evaluate,
govern, and implement these technologies responsibly. There is proposed limiting of
federal- state-level coordination, creating regulatory blind spots and duplicative efforts
that mirror HIPAA-era misalignment. Community-based organizations and safety-net
providers often lack resources to vet AI tools or build out governance infrastructure. In
addition, there are limited provider training and tools to effectively capture and
integrate caregiver-provided data or to seamlessly coordinate care with family
caregivers, leading to fragmented information flow. Many AI tools are marketed based
on features rather than actual function, performance, or clinical utility.
Opportunities
CMS should promote the development of state-aligned AI oversight structures that are
agile, responsive, and designed for local relevance, while adhering to federal guardrails.
CMS should fund and require AI governance frameworks at state and organizational
levels with tiered requirements and accessible resources to ensure equitable adoption
across all provider types, acknowledging potential resource constraints for smaller
practices.
Rather than relying on traditional, slow-moving pilot programs, CMS can accelerate
responsible AI deployment by modeling new grant structures after successful
community mobilization frameworks—such as the Drug-Free Communities (DFC)
Support Program. Due to the rapid development of foundational LLMs, a three-year,

community-driven funding model would enable local health coalitions—including
Medicaid agencies, safety-net providers, standards organizations, and caregiver
groups—to co-develop and test AI governance practices in real-world environments.
This approach encourages rapid-cycle learning and measurable outcomes with humans
in the loop, without the inertia of legacy pilot structures, and would also help address
implementation gaps by elevating practical, cross-sector solutions rooted in lived
experience and operational needs.
Additionally, CMS should promote and fund provider-facing digital tools that facilitate
secure, structured input from caregivers and integrate this 'invisible health data' into
clinical workflows (e.g., validated caregiver-reported outcome measures, shared care
plan platforms). AI tools used in care delivery—such as ambient listening, computer
vision, clinical summarization, and patient monitoring—should also be validated in
diverse settings, with special emphasis on Medicaid and dual-eligible populations to
ensure equitable outcomes.
CMS should further strengthen this work by partnering with organizations already
advancing AI governance and safety, including The Joint Commission and the Coalition
for Health AI (CHAI), to codify, adapt, and disseminate practical governance guidance for
AI algorithms that reflects both national standards and local realities.
Example from Practice: The Imperative for Human Oversight in AI
My daily work as an AI strategist and my personal experiences as a caregiver
consistently reinforce the critical need for human oversight in AI-driven health solutions.
For instance, in a recent demonstration using a large language model with computer
vision to identify medications, the AI initially misidentified a common drug, Dicyclomine,
and its purpose. It was only through direct human correction that the error was caught
and rectified.
This experience, and many others, underscores how even advanced AI can exhibit
"fragile performance" without robust governance frameworks and a human-in-the-loop
design.
Such direct, real-world interactions inform my work with national organizations like
NCPDP, payers, and vendors, helping them understand the complex intersection of AI
governance, standards-based data exchange, and the absolute necessity of human
verification.
Policy Recommendation
CMS should fund and require AI governance frameworks at both state and
organizational levels. These frameworks should include clear expectations for

implementation transparency, data lineage, human oversight, and performance
monitoring relevant to care coordination with families. A national registry of clinical AI
tools, modeled after the Joint Commission–CHAI partnership, could provide reference
implementation standards.
IV. Technology Developer Responses
Interoperability Standards and API Implementation
This directly addresses RFI questions TD-1, TD-5, TD-7, TD-9, TD-13, and TD-15, which
focus on developer incentives, FHIR endpoints directory, USCDI effectiveness,
certification changes, complete EHI access, and bulk FHIR API usage.
Barriers
Current API implementations lack consistency across vendors, making it difficult for
developers to create reliable applications that work across different health systems. The
absence of a comprehensive FHIR endpoints directory creates discovery barriers for
both developers and patients seeking to connect their data.
API designs often overlook the specific data access and functionality needs of third-party
applications designed for caregivers, such as bulk data access for longitudinal care
management or proxy access features.
Certification requirements focus heavily on technical compliance rather than real-world
usability and performance, leading to systems that meet regulatory requirements but
fail to deliver meaningful value to patients and providers.
Opportunities
CMS should mandate and fund a national Provider API Directory that is as accessible and
user-friendly as possible. This directory must include comprehensive metadata about
available data types, authentication requirements, and real-world performance metrics,
alongside clear documentation for developer access to caregiver-relevant data elements
and proxy functionality.
Furthermore, CMS should promote API design principles that prioritize caregiver app
interoperability within this directory.
Certification criteria should evolve to emphasize API usability, data completeness, and
real-world performance rather than just technical compliance. This includes requiring
"sandbox fidelity" where developer testing environments accurately reflect production
system capabilities.

Policy Recommendation
Implement mandatory "Portal-API Data Parity" requirements ensuring that any data
visible in patient portals is also available via certified FHIR APIs. Support dynamic client
registration protocols and verifiable credentials to enable real-time, secure application
authorization without lengthy pre-registration processes.
V. Value-Based Care Responses
AI Integration in Alternative Payment Models
This directly addresses RFI questions VB-1, VB-2, VB-3, VB-11, and VB-14, which focus on
APM digital health leverage, AI integration, critical health IT capabilities, interoperability
challenges, and digital identity benefits.
Barriers
Current value-based care models often lack the infrastructure to effectively capture and
utilize the comprehensive data needed for population health management. Many APM
participants struggle with data integration across multiple sources, limiting their ability
to identify high-risk patients and coordinate care effectively.
Opportunities
AI-powered population health analytics and risk stratification integrating AI-powered
population health analytics and risk stratification tools into APM requirements, with
specific focus on incorporating social determinants of health and caregiver-reported
outcomes and burden assessments. CMS should incentivize APM participants to adopt
comprehensive data integration platforms that include wearable devices, patient-
reported outcomes, and caregiver observations.
Policy Recommendation
CMS should create specific incentives for APM participants who demonstrate effective
use of AI governance frameworks and comprehensive data integration. This includes
recognition and payment adjustments for organizations that successfully incorporate
invisible health data—such as caregiver coordination activities—into their care
management processes.
VI. Conclusion
AI and interoperability are no longer future-facing challenges—they are present-tense
pressures reshaping care, cost, access, and trust. To ensure our health technology

ecosystem advances safely and equitably, we must ground innovation in the lived
realities of patients, caregivers, and community-based providers—not just the limits of
today’s technical infrastructure.
