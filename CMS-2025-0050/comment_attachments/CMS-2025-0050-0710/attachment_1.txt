1. Patient Needs
PC-1. What health management or care navigation apps would help you understand and manage your
(or your loved ones’) health needs, as well as the actions you should take?
A comprehensive enhanced digital health management platform can significantly improve how patients—and,
when necessary, their surrogates—interact with healthcare providers and manage their well-being. These
platforms offer numerous benefits, starting with the simplified scheduling and management of medical
appointments. Patients can easily book, adjust, and reschedule both in-person and telemedicine visits across
various providers, offering crucial flexibility and accessibility. Personalized reminders for appointments,
vaccinations, or health screenings also promote adherence to preventive care, tailored to individual needs.
Medication management is another core function. Users can securely reorder prescriptions, receive timely refill
notifications, and send requests directly to their providers. Many platforms also incorporate features for
checking drug interactions, greatly enhancing patient safety. Additionally, caregivers can leverage these tools
to oversee the medication schedules and proactive care of loved ones, simplifying family-based care
coordination.
The platforms also facilitate direct communication with healthcare providers through secure messaging or
email. This allows patients to ask questions, receive medical advice, or follow up on treatment plans without
needing an in-person visit. They also support the organization of health data, such as symptoms and
questions, to make provider interactions more efficient. Patients can track their health trends using data from
wearable devices—like heart rate, sleep patterns, or glucose levels—and share this information with their
doctors to proactively identify and address potential issues.
Access to medical information is a critical feature. Patients can promptly view lab and radiology results, often
with plain-language explanations to foster better understanding. The ability to track changes in results over
time empowers patients to engage more actively in their health management. Such platforms also help
interpret complex medical information, providing clear summaries and suggestions for next steps. Furthermore,
they send proactive health alerts, such as annual checkup reminders or preventive screening
recommendations, based on a patient’s age, medical history, and risk factors.
Finally, care coordination is vastly improved through centralized platforms that share and update medical
records across all involved providers. This interoperability ensures that every member of a patient’s care team
has access to the most current information, reducing errors and enhancing the continuity of care. In essence,
integrated health platforms not only streamline daily health tasks but also foster stronger patient engagement,
improve communication with providers, and lead to more proactive, personalized healthcare experiences.
PC-2. Do you have easy access to your own and all your loved ones’ health information in one location
(for example, in a single patient portal or another software system)?
Patient portals can offer significant benefits, including instantaneous access to critical health information, which
enhances patient engagement and care coordination. These platforms provide 24/7 access to lab results,
radiology reports, appointment schedules, and provider contact information, empowering patients to manage
chronic conditions effectively. Secure messaging improves communication with healthcare providers, reducing
unnecessary visits (Dendere et al., 2019; Kruse et al., 2018). What is more, portals streamline administrative
tasks like scheduling and bill payments, with 98% of users accessing test results in 2019 (Bizmatics, 2024).
Caregivers also benefit by monitoring loved ones’ health with authorized access, improving care coordination
for family members.
A key challenge is the fragmentation caused by multiple provider-specific portals. Each healthcare provider,
health system, or service often operates its own distinct portal, creating inconvenience for patients and
caregivers who must navigate separate platforms to access comprehensive health information. For example, a

PC-5. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in these digital
health products?
Establishing a Voluntary Validation Framework for Digital Health Tools
CMS is uniquely positioned to spearhead the development of a voluntary, evidence-based validation
framework for digital health technologies. Although not a regulatory body like the FDA, CMS's role as a
standards setter enables it to bring together key stakeholders—including patients, providers, and payers—to
foster a more transparent and trustworthy digital health ecosystem. In partnership with agencies such as the
National Institutes of Health (NIH) and the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ), CMS can
establish clinical and outcomes-focused benchmarks, provide quality assurance guidance in collaboration with
accrediting bodies, and develop secure validation environments using de-identified CMS data. These initiatives
would promote scientifically validated, user-centered digital tools while preventing the proliferation of unverified
products.
Criteria for Digital Health Product Evaluation
To establish a transparent and scalable review process for digital health tools, CMS can implement
standardized evaluation criteria across several critical areas. First, clinical validity must be demonstrated,
7

requiring tools to prove effectiveness through rigorous randomized controlled trials or validated real-world
evidence. Second, usability and accessibility are paramount; the design must effectively cater to all Medicare
and Medicaid beneficiaries, including individuals with disabilities. Third, data privacy is non-negotiable,
demanding strict adherence to HIPAA and 42 CFR Part 2 regulations to protect sensitive health information.
Fourth, a tool's health equity impact should be assessed, focusing on its ability to reduce healthcare disparities
or address social determinants of health. Fifth, interoperability is vital, necessitating compliance with HL7 FHIR
standards for seamless data exchange across the healthcare ecosystem. Finally, cost-effectiveness is
essential, with tools needing to demonstrate the capacity to lower overall healthcare costs without
compromising care quality.
Enabling Adoption by Patients and Caregivers
Achieving widespread adoption of digital health tools within Medicare and Medicaid populations requires a
multifaceted strategy. This includes expanding APIs and patient portals to simplify access to personal health
data, empowering beneficiaries to manage their information. It's also crucial to deploy Digital Health Navigators
in community-based aging and disability centers, offering hands-on support and training to bridge the digital
literacy gap. Furthermore, CMS can support robust reimbursement for remote care services, including
innovative AI-enabled tools, to incentivize providers. Concurrently, comprehensive outreach campaigns are
necessary to build trust and enhance overall healthcare literacy among beneficiaries. Finally, addressing
fundamental connectivity gaps through device and internet subsidies, potentially via Medicaid waivers and
Medicare Advantage plans, will ensure equitable access for all.
Challenges in Digital Health Implementation
Despite their promise, innovative digital health applications for physician workflows (e.g., quality reporting,
documentation, billing) face significant hurdles. These common obstacles include high financial costs due to
development and implementation expenses, uncertain return on investment, and inadequate reimbursement
models, especially for smaller or rural practices often lacking essential broadband infrastructure. Workflow
integration challenges and the burden of staff training disrupt established clinical routines, leading to user
resistance and increased administrative load. Pervasive interoperability issues and data silos continue to
impede seamless data exchange, fragmenting patient information and hindering comprehensive care
coordination. Furthermore, legitimate privacy and security concerns surrounding sensitive health data,
alongside the complexities of achieving and maintaining HIPAA compliance, add layers of risk and regulatory
burden. Issues like physician burnout, often exacerbated by the administrative demands of existing digital
tools, and a broader digital divide among patients with varying technological literacy and access, collectively
limit the effective adoption and full utilization of these promising innovations.

PC-7. If CMS were to collect real-world data on digital health products’ impact on health outcomes and
related costs once they are released into the market, what would be the best means of doing so?
CMS is strategically positioned to boost physician interoperability by embedding standardized data formats,
automation, and incentives into existing clinical workflows, all while minimizing administrative burden. By
aligning interoperability efforts with physicians' current motivations, such as quality reporting and care
coordination, CMS can facilitate seamless, high-quality data sharing that truly benefits patient care.
CMS can drive interoperability through a multitude of key strategies:

• Mandating Standards and APIs: Enforcing the use of Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources
(FHIR) and APIs (HL7 International, n.d.) ensures fluid data exchange. This can be supported by
enforcing EHR certification through the ONC Health IT Certification Program and providing funding
for API implementation (Office of the National Coordinator for Health IT, n.d.).
• Embedding Tools within EHRs: Integrating interoperability tools directly into Electronic Health
Records (EHRs) can automate data sharing during routine clinical tasks like charting or discharge.
CMS can update certification criteria and link these efforts to Merit-based Incentive Payment
System (MIPS) Promoting Interoperability measures (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services,
n.d.).
• Incentivizing Data Exchange: CMS can offer incentives for interoperability through value-based
programs like MIPS and Alternative Payment Models (APMs), rewarding metrics such as API-based
exchanges and funding training for Health Information Exchanges (HIEs) or the Trusted Exchange
Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services, n.d.;
Adler-Milstein & Jha, 2017).
• Empowering Patients: Enabling beneficiaries to share their health data via patient portals or apps,
as mandated by the 21st Century Cures Act (2016), can significantly reduce physician workload by
allowing patients to proactively manage and provide their information. CMS can further incentivize
education around portal use.
To effectively limit physician burden, CMS can streamline workflows by integrating single-click HIE sharing and
AI-driven data pre-population directly into EHRs. Aligning interoperability data with existing quality reporting
requirements, such as MIPS and HEDIS measures (National Committee for Quality Assurance, n.d.),
eliminates redundant documentation. CMS can also fund non-physician task delegation through programs
designed to transform clinical practice models (Kruse et al., 2018).
CMS can make interoperability intuitive by leveraging existing workflows. Reusing data points already collected
for MIPS or HEDIS, such as Continuity of Care Documents (CCDs), aligns with physicians' reimbursement
goals and promotes efficient care coordination (Holmgren & Adler-Milstein, 2021). Automating referral and
discharge data sharing (e.g., sending lab and radiology results to specialists via FHIR) improves patient
outcomes within value-based care models. Linking patient portal use to interoperability metrics taps into
physicians' existing MIPS tracking efforts. Furthermore, sharing chronic care data, such as A1c and Urine
Albumin Creatine Ratio results, via HIEs enhances chronic disease management for conditions like diabetes
and kidney disease, ultimately improving patient outcomes (Menachemi et al., 2018).
CMS can drive interoperability by standardizing formats, automating workflows, offering incentives, and
empowering beneficiaries. This can be achieved while reducing burden through streamlined processes,
decreased documentation, staff support, and clear vendor accountability. Integrating metrics like API exchange
success rates into MIPS directly aligns interoperability with physicians’ existing motivations for seamless, high-
quality data sharing.

a. What data is valuable, but hard for patients and caregivers, or app developers and other

While patients and caregivers are getting better access to some health data, significant challenges
remain for truly valuable use. It's tough to get comprehensive clinical data like detailed encounter notes
and operative reports, as this information is often unstructured and hard to interpret for most patients or
integrate. Claims data is another large obstacle; it's controlled by insurers and not easily accessible,
making it difficult for patients to see truly their full healthcare utilization and costs. Getting accurate,
personalized prices for healthcare services before treatment is also a major hurdle due to varying
negotiated rates. Moreover, managing appointment schedules across multiple different health systems
in one place is nearly impossible due to a lack of interconnectedness. Finally, accessing a complete,
unified health record for patients who've received care from various providers, and integrating social
determinants of health data, continues to be a struggle. These difficulties largely stem from technical
issues, privacy concerns, business models that can limit data sharing, and a lack of consistent data
standards across the healthcare industry also play into the equation.
b. What are specific sources, other than claims and clinical data, that would be of highest value,
and why?
Wearable technologies profoundly benefit patients, doctors, and providers by enabling continuous,
proactive health monitoring. For patients, they foster self-management and early issue detection, like
fall detection on smartwatches improving elderly safety. For providers, wearables deliver objective, real-
time data for enhanced diagnostics, personalized treatment, and remote management of chronic
conditions, potentially reducing in-person visits and emergencies. Conditions such as cardiovascular
diseases (e.g., continuous ECGs for arrhythmia detection), diabetes (Continuous Glucose Monitors for
real-time blood sugar management), sleep disorders, epilepsy (seizure detection alerts), and post-
surgical recovery (vital sign monitoring) are greatly improved through this constant oversight, leading to
better patient outcomes and peace of mind.
Envision a clinical application where a patient, 1) has an emergency onset of heart palpitations and is seen at
an Emergency Room, employs a point of care device that seamlessly and quickly incorporates the clinical data
for the immediate transmission to an EHR; or 2) is previously diagnosed with irregular heartbeats, employs a
smartwatch incorporating an ECG functionality to facilitate proactive self-monitoring and potential early
detection of significant cardiac events. How Wearable and Point of Care Data is Transmitted to an EHR (Using
a Smartwatch, Point of Care Provider Devices, and FHIR):
1. Data Collection: The provider application similar to OpMed used in JOMIS discussed in section
PC-3 collects the patient’s data in real-time quickly and easily. The smartwatch continuously
monitors the patient's heart rate and periodically performs an ECG recording (e.g., when the patient
initiates it or if it detects an anomaly). This raw data (heart rate, ECG waveform, time stamps) is
collected by the device.

2. Transmission to a Companion App/Cloud: The OpMed application and device can work offline
and also upload the data securely to any EHR with APIs (see Section PC-3). The smartwatch
typically syncs this data wirelessly (e.g., via Bluetooth) to a companion mobile application on the
patient's smartphone. This app then uploads the data securely to a cloud-based platform or the
wearable manufacturer's server. This can be easily integrated into the native mobile application
prototyped by Peraton discussed in section PC-4.
3. Standardization and API (FHIR): This is where interoperability comes in. The cloud platform or a
third-party data aggregator (like Validic or Human API) uses APIs (Application Programming
Interfaces) to extract this raw data. Crucially, they then standardize it into a common healthcare
data format, most commonly HL7 FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources). For instance,
a heart rate reading would be mapped to a FHIR "Observation" resource with specific codes for
heart rate, unit of measure, and the timestamp. An ECG recording might be represented as a FHIR
"DiagnosticReport" or "Observation" containing the waveform data.
4. EHR Integration: The standardized FHIR data is then sent from the cloud platform to the hospital
or clinic's Electronic Health Record (EHR) system (e.g., Epic, Cerner) using FHIR APIs. The EHR's
interface receives these FHIR resources and ingests them into the patient's digital chart.
5. Provider Access: Within the EHR, the cardiologist can now access a dashboard or a specific
section of the patient's chart that displays the continuous heart rate trends, recent ECGs, and any
alerts generated by the wearable. This allows the doctor to monitor the patient's condition remotely,
identify patterns, and make informed decisions about medication adjustments or further diagnostic
tests, all without the patient needing to visit the clinic.
This streamlined process, significantly facilitated by interoperability standards such as FHIR, ensures
the seamless integration of valuable real-time physiological data from wearable devices directly into the
formal medical record. This capability effectively bridges the gap between a patient's daily life and their
clinical care, enabling a more comprehensive and continuous understanding of their health.

Health Information Exchanges (HIEs) are fundamentally reshaping patient access to health information by
enabling the secure, electronic sharing of medical data among diverse healthcare providers and directly with

reducing administrative burdens and lowering healthcare costs. Furthermore, HIEs are vital for public health
initiatives, facilitating disease surveillance and reporting. By breaking down data silos between disparate EHR

2.1.3 How might CMS encourage providers to accept digital identity credentials from patients and
their partners instead of proprietary logins that need to be tracked for each provider relationship?
CMS can take several strategic steps to encourage healthcare providers to accept digital credentials from
patients and their partners. First, CMS can establish national standards and accreditation for digital identity by
endorsing or helping to define a consistent and secure framework for federated digital identity, such as those
compliant with NIST SP 800-63-3. This would mirror the approach taken by the solution implemented by
Peraton, using the Okta Commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) product, for accessing Department of Defense (DoD)
applications. The tool is a secure, user-friendly way to access DoD applications, allowing eligible individuals
and beneficiaries affiliated with the DoD, a single sign-on (SSO) to log into partner applications. Peraton's
solution can federate with multiple digital identity providers, promoting an interoperable identity framework that
could be used across Medicare services for providers and beneficiaries.
Second, CMS could incentivize adoption through reimbursement or participation models, similar to how the
Meaningful Use program drove EHR adoption. Providers who accept digital credentials from trusted sources—
such as ID.me, Login.gov, or healthcare-specific equivalents—could be rewarded for their participation.

Lastly, CMS can support identity federation through policy guidance and technological support, encouraging

2.1.4 What would providers need help with to accelerate the transition to a single set of trusted
digital identity credentials for the patient to keep track of, instead of one for each provider?
Providers will likely need assistance in several key areas to successfully implement federated identity services.
Technical integration support will be crucial, as providers will require help integrating with federated identity
services through APIs, single sign-on (SSO) protocols like SAML and OpenID Connect, and attribute
exchanges. CMS can facilitate this by offering implementation toolkits, comprehensive technical
documentation, and integration grants. Additionally, education and training initiatives from CMS can inform
providers about the advantages and mechanics of federated identity, including how Attribute-Based Access
Control (ABAC) can provide enhanced, role-specific access to patient data. To address concerns about HIPAA
and cybersecurity liability, legal and compliance guidance from CMS will be essential; this could involve
publishing guidelines on how federated identity aligns with privacy laws and offering safe harbor policies for
providers accepting trusted third-party credentials. Finally, change management and user experience design
support will be vital, as transitioning from proprietary logins to federated identity will necessitate workflow
redesign and introduce patient onboarding challenges. CMS can assist by funding user-centered design pilots
and providing change management resources to ensure a smooth adoption process.
2.1.5 How might CMS balance patient privacy with convenience and access to digital health products
and services that may lead to significant improvements in health?
CMS can implement a tiered, attribute-based approach to enable granular access to patient data based on
specific attributes like condition, eligibility, or consent status, rather than an all-or-nothing approach.
Furthermore, CMS can use consent-driven federated identity models, integrating explicit patient consent flows
into health service access to empower patients with control over their shared information, much like how
federated identity systems incorporate user approval during login. To bolster privacy, CMS can also promote
Privacy-by-Design frameworks, supporting the development of privacy-preserving authentication technologies
such as zero-knowledge proofs and minimal disclosure tokens that verify identity without fully exposing
credentials. Lastly, CMS can align with interagency efforts by partnering with the ONC, VA, DoD, and HHS to
establish reliable and consistent cross-agency identity services for patients.
Peraton’s SSO solution with the DoD, serves as a strong case study for CMS, demonstrating how federated
identity, a seamless user experience, attribute-based access, and interagency coordination can lead to
scalable, secure, and inclusive authentication systems. By adopting these principles, CMS can spearhead the
transition to trusted, reusable digital identity in healthcare, effectively balancing privacy, efficiency, and
accessibility for millions of Americans.

2. Digital Identity
TD-3. Regarding digital identity implementation:
See Peraton’s response is section PR-9 for solutions on this topic.
a. What are the challenges and benefits?
Digital identity implementation in healthcare offers transformative potential but faces notable
challenges for all stakeholders. For patients, key hurdles include the digital divide, limiting
access for digitally underserved populations, alongside pervasive privacy and data security
concerns that erode trust. The complexity of fragmented identity processes can also lead to
frustration and hinder engagement. Healthcare providers grapple with the high costs and
technical complexities of integrating new digital identity solutions with their diverse legacy
systems, leading to workflow disruptions and requiring extensive staff training. Navigating the
intricate and evolving landscape of regulatory compliance (e.g., HIPAA) further adds to their
burden. Payers confront challenges in data standardization and quality from various provider
sources, alongside the technical complexities of achieving interoperability across vast and
diverse networks. Robust fraud prevention mechanisms must be developed, and a clear return
on investment must be demonstrated to justify significant financial outlays for these new
systems.
Despite these challenges, the benefits of robust digital identity solutions are substantial.
Patients gain enhanced access and convenience, securely managing their health information
and engaging with care services more easily. This also leads to improved care coordination,
increased patient safety through more accurate data, and greater control over their personal
health information, fostering trust. For healthcare providers, digital identity streamlines
operations by automating administrative tasks, reducing errors, and cutting costs. It ensures
more accurate patient matching, bolstering data security and compliance with stringent
regulations. Furthermore, it is foundational for expanding telehealth and remote care, improving
clinical decision-making. Payers benefit from improved member identification and data quality,
leading to better fraud detection and reduced abuse. This translates into more efficient claims
processing, enhanced member engagement, and more effective population health management
strategies, ultimately fostering a more secure, efficient, and patient-centric healthcare
ecosystem.
b. How would requiring digital identity credentials (for example, CLEAR, Login.gov,
ID.me, other NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 CSPs) impact cybersecurity and data
exchange?
Requiring digital identity credentials compliant with NIST 800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 standards would
profoundly benefit healthcare cybersecurity and data exchange.
For cybersecurity, these high-assurance digital identities would significantly reduce fraud and
strengthen access controls. IAL2 ensures rigorous identity proofing, making impersonation
difficult, while AAL2 mandates strong multi-factor authentication, thwarting account takeovers.
This creates a much more resilient defense against cyber threats and unauthorized access to
sensitive health information.

For data exchange, these verified digital identities would enable secure, reliable, and efficient
information sharing. They instill confidence that individuals exchanging data are legitimate,
facilitating seamless access to fragmented health records across disparate systems. This would
streamline patient access to their data, improve data accuracy for providers (reducing errors
and duplicates), and enhance payers' ability to identify members and process claims, ultimately
leading to a more connected, efficient, and secure healthcare ecosystem.
c. What impact would mandatory use of the OpenID Connect identity protocol have?
The widespread adoption of OpenID Connect (OIDC) as a foundational identity protocol holds
the potential to transform the healthcare industry, delivering substantial positive impacts. By its
very design, OIDC would fundamentally streamline authentication processes, significantly
enhance cybersecurity postures, and markedly improve the efficiency and security of data
exchange and interoperability across the complex healthcare ecosystem. This shift would
provide a standardized, robust, and user-friendly framework for digital identity, paving the way
for a more integrated and secure future in healthcare.
