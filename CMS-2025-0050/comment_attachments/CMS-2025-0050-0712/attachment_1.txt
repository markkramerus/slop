Executive Summary:
Walrus Health appreciates the opportunity to respond to CMS’s Request for Information
on strengthening the Medicare health technology ecosystem. As a digital health
company delivering clinically minded, tech-enabled medication therapy management
and care coordination, we’ve demonstrated measurable impact on outcomes and costs
across diverse populations.
Our approach addresses common fragmentation in digital health by uniting clinical
interventions, behavioral support, and real-time data into one integrated experience for
patient and provider. We support CMS’s goals and offer specific, field-tested medication
therapy and self-care recommendations across patient engagement, interoperability,
data standards, and real-world outcomes.
Introduction
Medication errors are the third leading cause of death in the U.S.1—a preventable crisis
made worse by fragmented care and poor medication oversight. Walrus Health is a
digital solution built to solve this, combining clinically driven logic with AI-powered
engagement to drive better outcomes at scale. The Walrus platform pre-populates each
member’s profile with active medications and chronic conditions, enabling real-time
tracking of adherence, side effects, and therapeutic effectiveness. AI-generated
prompts, shaped by clinical pharmacists, identify high-risk members and trigger timely
alerts to the care team. Walrus integrates directly into the EHR, giving providers
continuous visibility into how medications are working—while improving safety, reducing
costs, and closing gaps in care.
Section B: Patients and Caregivers
PC-3 / PC-4 / PC-5 Health Management and Care Navigation Solutions
Walrus Health brings a clinically minded, AI tech-enabled care model designed to
improve outcomes and reduce costs for medically complex populations. Our platform
goes beyond basic app navigation by delivering tailored clinical interventions, real-time

engagement, and measurable value—aligned with CMS’s goals for digital health
advancement. Many Medicare beneficiaries—particularly those with multiple chronic
conditions and limited caregiver support—struggle to coordinate care across providers,
benefits, and therapies. Current point solutions offer fragmented experiences, often
lacking medication management, care coordination and deprescribing support. For
example, a patient living in rural South Carolina may struggle knowing which care team
member to contact for a replacement sensor for their continuous glucose monitor. They
may get tossed around the system and told several different points of contact only to
end up more confused and still without a replacement. Walrus can connect patients to
providers and provide a clear path to coordinate care and manage medication therapy
for patients who need assistance with transportation, medical supplies, as well as other
medication related issues.
Self care is a critical part of chronic disease management 2, such as in chronic heart
failure (CHF). Over 68% of the Medicare population has been diagnosed with CHF 3 and
can benefit from frequent touch points with the care team as well as easy to understand
education materials. While remote patient monitoring (RPM) can help with this role,
many providers may not be able to respond in a timely fashion to these alerts, or the
RPM devices may not have seamless integration into the current workflow. Walrus
provides a direct and clear channel to have emergent communication from RPM
devices to all members of the care team. When all members of the care team are
alerted, it increases accountability for the care team to provide care to the patient in a
timely fashion. Walrus AI is able to triage patients into the provider work queue and
assign priority to those who need it most. Walrus offers a customizable API that can
integrate with any PBM, Health Plan and EHR.
Walrus provides an integrated experience combining pharmacist-created medication
interventions and AI coaching, automated and self-induced patient and provider
outreach based on risk and claims triggers, deprescribing and therapeutic interchange
support, adherence and side effect tracking with member self-reporting. It can also tailor
specific education material related to the patient’s current disease state - all in an easy
to understand digital format.
Nearly ¼ of older adults reported medical care avoidance¹ which oftentimes increases
risk of hospitalization. Walrus can proactively identify potential causes of hospitalization
and alert the entire care team of the issue. This allows for seamless communication
between all members of the care team as well as the member. Whenever a new
opportunity is presented, each care team is given the opportunity to accept or reject the
opportunity. The member is also able to see this in real time and all messages are
visible to any member of the care team.

Walrus Health’s capabilities have been successfully deployed in partnership with
ClareMedica, a high-touch network of physicians offices servicing Medicare members.
Walrus seamlessly integrated into the workflow of ClareMedica’s clinics.

PC-6: Accessibility and Usability
To make digital health products truly accessible and easy to use for Medicare
beneficiaries and their caregivers—especially those with limited digital

experience—solutions must prioritize simplicity, inclusivity, and provide a channel for
available human support. Key features include intuitive design with large fonts, high
contrast, and minimal navigation steps, as well as voice-enabled interfaces and
compatibility with screen readers or assistive devices. Tools should be available in
multiple languages and reflect culturally relevant design elements to foster trust and
usability across diverse populations. Caregiver access must be built-in, with clear
delegation options. Importantly, users must have real-time access to their care team,
which guides them through medication decisions and technical challenges. Finally,
strong privacy protections and transparent consent flows help build confidence among
older adults, especially those unfamiliar with digital systems. At Walrus Health, these
principles shape every aspect of our member experience, ensuring digital engagement
leads to meaningful outcomes—not frustration.
Section C: Providers
PR-1: CMS and its partners can play a pivotal role in encouraging providers—including
those in rural and underserved areas—to adopt approved digital health tools by
reducing implementation friction, offering financial incentives, and establishing trust in
the technology.
● CMS should create clear reimbursement pathways for digital health engagement,
including clinically driven virtual care and medication management, which are
often underutilized despite proven effectiveness.
● CMS can expand grant funding or technical assistance to rural practices,
federally qualified health centers (FQHCs), and critical access hospitals (CAHs)
to help integrate digital tools into existing workflows.
● Endorsing a vetted list of "approved" digital health solutions—based on clinical
outcomes, usability, and accessibility—would give providers confidence in the
tools' safety and effectiveness. Additionally, embedding digital health use within
existing quality measures or value-based care frameworks would align incentives
and reinforce adoption. CMS should consider the addition of a new quality
measure titled “Member Health Technology Adoption rate” to further drive use
with CMS approved vendors.
● CMS should support broadband and device access initiatives, as digital equity is
a prerequisite for digital health success in rural populations. Walrus Health is
designed to support low-bandwidth environments, multilingual users, and
patients who need high-touch virtual engagement—making it a strong fit for these
settings.

PR-7: CMS can support providers in making high-quality, timely, and comprehensive
healthcare data interoperable by focusing on streamlined standards adoption, aligned
incentives, and minimizing administrative burden.
● Continue to mandate and enforce use of standardized APIs—such as FHIR and
SMART —that enable seamless, secure data exchange across EHRs, PBMs,
and digital health platforms.
● Promote bulk data exchange solutions that allow population-level data sharing
without repeated individual queries, integrating smoothly into existing workflows.
● Aligning interoperability efforts with current quality reporting and value-based
care metrics (i.e. MIPS) enables providers to leverage data sharing as part of
goals they are already incentivized to meet, reducing duplicative work.
● CMS could fund technical assistance and shared services that simplify data
integration for smaller or rural practices, who often face the greatest resource
constraints due to a low provider to patient ratio4
● Walrus Health’s platform exemplifies how interoperable data access combined
with clinical decision support can enhance care while minimizing workflow
disruption yet still creating an “all-hands on deck” approach for the care team,
especially for medication management and chronic disease monitoring. By
prioritizing scalable standards, incentivizing aligned metrics, and supporting
provider capacity, CMS can foster a digital ecosystem where data flows efficiently
to improve patient outcomes without overburdening clinicians.
Section D: Payers
PA-5: Payers can significantly reduce the clinical quality data burden on providers by
shifting responsibility for quality measurement to downstream systems that leverage
bulk data extraction from EHRs and other health IT systems. By using these standards,
payers can assume responsibility for generating standardized, timely quality
metrics—freeing providers to focus on delivering care rather than managing fragmented
reporting workflows.
Walrus Health supports this approach by integrating real-time patient-reported data and
remote patient monitoring (RPM) device inputs into a shared digital platform that
connects patients with all members of their care team—including primary care,
specialists, pharmacists, and care coordinators. This real-time, bi-directional
communication channel allows Walrus to identify gaps in care, medication-related risks,
or adherence challenges proactively. These insights not only support clinical
decision-making but also streamline reporting by producing continuous, high-fidelity
quality data that can be accessed by payers and providers alike.

By aligning technology like Walrus with payer-led efforts to consolidate quality data
infrastructure, CMS can support a dual-purpose investment that simplifies provider
reporting, enhances care coordination, and accelerates progress toward value-based
care goals.
Section E: Technology Vendors
TD-1: To stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare
beneficiaries and caregivers in the short term, CMS should provide targeted grants and
innovation prizes that lower financial barriers and encourage development focused on
high-impact areas like medication therapy management, chronic disease support, and
allow for caregiver engagement. CMS can accelerate innovation by requiring PBMs,
health insurers, and other payers to share standardized claims, medication data, lab
and imaging metadata and patient risk scores with approved digital health vendors via
secure, interoperable APIs. Access to this data is critical for developers to build
effective, easy to use, personalized solutions that integrate seamlessly into beneficiary
care and prioritize high-risk populations.
TD-5: A nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints would significantly enhance
access to health information by enabling real-time, standardized data exchange among
patients, caregivers, providers, and payers. For patients and caregivers, it supports
seamless record portability, improved care continuity, and greater engagement through
third-party digital solutions. Providers benefit by being able to provide more
patient-centered care, have reduced administrative burden, and more effective care
coordination. Payers gain timely access to clinical data for more efficient claims
processing, prior authorizations, and broader population health management.
Walrus suggests that this directory be published by the Office of the National
Coordinator for Health IT (ONC), to ensure broad adoption, transparency, and
interoperability standards compliance.
End users should not bear the direct cost of accessing or contributing to this directory.
Instead, funding should come from the payor side with costs equitably distributed across
the patient population through minimal, widely shared increases in insurance premiums.
This approach avoids placing financial or operational burden on any single stakeholder
while supporting the public good of a connected, interoperable healthcare system.

Section F: Value-Based Care Organizations
VB-1 / VB-2 / VB-3: To encourage APMs such as ACOs and MSSP participants to more
effectively adopt digital health management and care navigation products, CMS should
offer performance-based incentives tied to engagement with approved digital platforms
that demonstrate improvements in patient outcomes, medication adherence, and cost
savings. For example, granting credit within quality or shared savings calculations for
digital interventions—such as medication optimization, remote monitoring, or pharmacist
engagement—would directly align incentives with value-based goals. Upfront funding or
technical assistance for digital infrastructure would also lower barriers, particularly for
small or rural ACOs.
One key obstacle is fragmented data access, particularly from PBMs and external
monitoring tools, which makes it difficult for providers to act on a complete picture of a
patient’s health. Walrus Health addresses this gap by integrating real-time
patient-reported outcomes and remote patient monitoring device data, creating a
unified, continuous feedback loop between patients and the entire care team—including
primary care, pharmacists, specialists, and caregivers. This improves coordination and
enables timely clinical interventions.
To enhance APM frameworks, CMS can further integrate technologies such as AI, risk
stratification, and population health analytics by embedding them into performance
measurement and care coordination expectations. Walrus uses AI-driven algorithms to
identify high-risk patients and trigger proactive engagement, enabling earlier
interventions that drive measurable quality improvements. By encouraging use of
platforms that combine real-time monitoring, data integration, and patient engagement
tools, CMS can make care navigation a central function of value-based care rather than
an afterthought. Embedding usability, equity, and real-time quality metrics into APM
requirements ensures these technologies are not only adopted—but used effectively to
drive better outcomes at scale.
VB-13: To better support value-based care (VBC) capabilities while reducing provider
burden, CMS should modernize existing criteria and standards to focus on
interoperability, delegation of administrative tasks, and integration of real-time data from
digital health platforms like Walrus Health.
Walrus operates at the intersection of care coordination, medication optimization, and
real-time patient engagement. By leveraging pharmacist-created interventions and
remote patient monitoring (RPM) integration, Walrus reduces provider workload while
improving clinical outcomes. Current VBC standards often require physicians to be the
primary data reporters and intervention drivers, which is not scalable—especially in
settings with workforce shortages. CMS can revise these criteria to allow credentialed
care team members (e.g., pharmacists, care coordinators) to contribute directly to care

plans, documentation, and quality metrics. This shift would reduce provider
administrative burden and enable team-based care delivery.
Additionally, existing standards should require and enforce real-time data sharing from
PBMs and EHRs with approved digital solutions via standardized APIs (e.g., FHIR),
allowing for accurate risk stratification, care gap identification, and proactive
engagement. Recognizing digital tools like Walrus as legitimate contributors to quality
outcomes (e.g., in MIPS and ACO performance scores) would validate their role in
driving value-based care.
Lastly, CMS should streamline overlapping reporting requirements across programs,
ensuring that a single digital data capture can serve multiple regulatory
needs—maximizing provider time spent on patient care rather than compliance.
Conclusion
Walrus Health recommends a multi-pronged strategy to modernize Medicare’s digital
health ecosystem, rooted in real-world outcomes and equity. To improve patient access
and engagement, Walrus calls for digital tools that prioritize intuitive design, cultural
relevance, caregiver access, and real-time support for medication therapy management
be mandatory as a part of the care process for medicare beneficiaries. CMS should
reduce implementation barriers for providers by offering financial incentives, technical
assistance, and clear reimbursement pathways for clinically minded digital interventions,
especially in rural settings.
To strengthen interoperability and data availability, Walrus supports widespread
FHIR-based APIs, bulk data sharing capabilities, and aligning interoperability goals with
quality measures to reduce provider burden. Payers should take a more active role in
extracting and managing quality data from EHRs, enabling providers to focus on care
delivery while maintaining thorough reporting.
Walrus urges CMS to fund and require PBMs and insurers to share critical data—such
as claims and risk scores—with trusted digital health vendors, unlocking new care
coordination and risk stratification capabilities.
Finally, value-based care models should reward digital engagement through shared
savings credit, encourage adoption of AI-powered tools for risk stratification and
population health, and integrate patient-generated and RPM data into coordinated
workflows. Walrus demonstrates how these strategies yield better health outcomes,

lower costs, and higher engagement—delivering a 2.6:1 ROI, 70% repeat engagement,
and over $1,600 in annual savings per drug change across complex patient populations.
