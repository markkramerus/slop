Dear CMS, ASTP/ONC and HHS Teams:
The Digital Medicine Society (DiMe) is a global non-profit and the professional home for digital
medicine. Together, we drive broad adoption of digital approaches to advance the practice of
medicine to enhance public health. Our mission is to advance the safe, effective, and equitable
use of digital approaches to redefine healthcare and improve lives. As CMS, ASTP/ONC and
HHS seek input from the public regarding the market of digital health products for Medicare
beneficiaries as well as the state of data interoperability and broader health technology
infrastructure, DiMe appreciates the opportunity to respond to the Request for Information, to
inform efforts to lead infrastructure progress to cultivate this market, increasing beneficiary
access to effective digital capabilities needed to make informed health decisions, and increasing
data availability for all stakeholders contributing to health outcomes.
A core component of our commitment to accelerating the identification and informed adoption of
high-quality, trustworthy digital health software products is "The DiMe Seal." With over 350,000
digital health software products available, including more than 30,000 for clinicians and
healthcare administrators, the challenge of identifying high-quality, trustworthy options is
substantial. The DiMe Seal, developed with an independent Governance Committee, provides a
standardized framework to assess digital health software products (DHSPs) across critical
domains of usability, privacy and security, and evidence. By simplifying this assessment
process, the DiMe Seal enables adopters and buyers to confidently select products that are
well-made and meet rigorous standards, ultimately improving health outcomes for patients.
We offer the following responses herein specifically from the perspective of the DiMe Seal and
referring to sections PC-5, PR-1, TD-1, and VB-1 of the RFI, as requested. The Digital Medicine
Society is also submitting a separate response, representing our broader perspective across
digital medicine.
PC-5: What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver
interest in these digital health products?
Encouraging patient and caregiver interest in digital health products requires building
fundamental trust, ensuring accessibility, and demonstrating clear value. The DiMe Seal directly
addresses these needs by assessing products for quality and trustworthiness, making it easier
for patients and caregivers to quickly make confident and informed decisions.
To encourage patient and caregiver interest, CMS and its partners can:

● Promote awareness of trusted resources: Actively embrace and endorse
independent, private sector, transparent, assessment programs like the DiMe Seal.
Leverage accessible lists or directories of awarded products, clearly indicating that these
products have met rigorous standards for evidence, privacy and security, and usability.
This helps patients and caregivers cut through the noise and identify reliable solutions.
● Invest in digital health literacy: Promote and partner with independent organizations to
develop and disseminate educational materials (e.g., plain language guides, webinars,
community workshops) for Medicare beneficiaries and their caregivers on the
identification, benefits and safe use of digital health products. These could include
explaining what to look for in a quality app, how to protect their data, how digital tools
can complement traditional care, and how to leverage independent, private sector
programs like the DiMe Seal to find those digital health products meeting these benefits.
● Ensure accessibility and usability: Digital health products must be designed with the
diverse needs of Medicare beneficiaries in mind, including those with varying levels of
digital literacy, cognitive abilities, technology access and physical impairments. CMS
should emphasize and potentially incentivize adherence to robust usability and
accessibility standards (e.g., WCAG guidelines) as part of product selection or
reimbursement. The DiMe Seal's "Usability" domain explicitly assesses these aspects.
a. What role, if any, should CMS have in reviewing or approving digital health products
on the basis of their efficacy, quality or impact or both on health outcomes (not
approving in the sense of a coverage determination)? What criteria should be used if
there is a review process? What technology solutions, policy changes, or program
design changes can increase patient and caregiver adoption of digital health products
(for example, enhancements to data access, reimbursement adjustments, or new
beneficiary communications)?
CMS has a critical and appropriate role in influencing the adoption of high-quality digital health
products, even outside of coverage determinations, by acting as a trusted convener and
information disseminator. Given the rapid evolution and sheer volume of digital health products,
it is impractical and potentially stifling for CMS to directly "approve" every product for efficacy or
quality. Instead, CMS's role should focus on elevating and endorsing non-regulatory, private
sector, robust frameworks for independent, third-party assessment and creating incentives
for the market to reward assessed high-value solutions.
CMS's Role in Review/Approval (beyond coverage):
● Endorsement of Independent Assessment: Instead of direct approval, CMS should
formally recognize and promote assessments like The DiMe Seal. The DiMe Seal,
through its independent Governance Committee and rigorous evaluation process,
assesses products based on their efficacy (evidence generation), quality (usability,
privacy & security), and impact on health outcomes (evidenced through clinical
studies). This leverages private sector expertise and capacity while providing a credible
signal of quality to patients, caregivers, and providers.

● Setting the Standards for "Trustworthy": CMS can collaborate with organizations like
DiMe to define what constitutes a "high-quality, trustworthy" digital health product. This
could involve contributing to and endorsing evaluation frameworks that address:
○ Clinical Evidence: Clear demonstration of a product's efficacy and impact on
health outcomes through appropriately designed studies, stratified by relevant
demographic factors (e.g., age, race, socioeconomic status) to ensure
generalizability and equitable impact. The DiMe Seal's "Evidence" domain,
enhanced by the collaboration with Elevance Health for clinical evidence
assessment, is built precisely for this purpose.
○ Usability and Accessibility: Products must be intuitive, easy to navigate, and
accessible to individuals across the full spectrum of digital literacy and
physical/cognitive abilities, especially for Medicare beneficiaries.
○ Privacy and Security: Adherence to robust data privacy and security standards
(e.g., HIPAA compliance, strong encryption, transparent data use policies) is
non-negotiable for building trust.
By focusing on these areas, and elevating the role and work of the private sector such as what
we’ve developed with the DiMe Seal, CMS can empower patients and caregivers to confidently
embrace digital health products, fostering a market that prioritizes and rewards high-quality,
impactful solutions.

PR-1. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage providers, including
those in rural areas, to leverage approved (see description in PC-5) digital
health products for their patients?
To encourage providers, particularly those in rural areas, to leverage assessed digital health
products for their patients, CMS and its partners must address existing barriers and clearly
articulate the value proposition of these tools. The DiMe Seal provides a crucial mechanism for
identifying "awarded" (i.e., independently assessed as high-quality and trustworthy) digital
health products, streamlining provider decision-making.
CMS and its partners can encourage adoption by:
● Promoting and Integrating Third-Party Certifications: CMS should formally recognize
and promote independent, evidence-based assessment programs like The DiMe Seal.
By doing so, CMS provides a clear signal to providers that products bearing the DiMe
Seal have been rigorously assessed for efficacy, privacy and security and usability. This
reduces the burden on individual providers to assess the vast number of available
products.
● Creating Incentives within Value-Based Care Models: Link the adoption and
appropriate use of assessment awarded digital health products to existing or new
value-based care initiatives. This could involve quality measures that incentivize the use
of digital tools for remote monitoring, patient education, or care coordination, particularly
for chronic disease management or preventive care.
● Providing Education and Training: Contribute to accessible and practical education
and training programs for providers on how to effectively integrate awarded digital health
products into their clinical workflows. This should include best practices for selecting,
prescribing/recommending, and monitoring patients using these tools. Tailored programs
for rural providers, potentially delivered remotely, are essential.
● Addressing Connectivity and Infrastructure Gaps: For rural areas, robust broadband
infrastructure is foundational. CMS, in collaboration with other federal agencies, should
continue to invest in and advocate for policies that expand high-speed internet access in
underserved regions, recognizing that digital health adoption is impossible without it.
a. What are the current obstacles?
Current obstacles to provider adoption of digital health products, especially in rural areas, that
the DiMe Seal framework addresses, include:
● Lack of Trust and Information Overload: Providers face a bewildering array of digital
health products, many without robust evidence or transparent claims. It takes
approximately 75-150 hours for providers to review a single product. Without a reliable
way to differentiate high-quality from low-quality solutions, they are hesitant to
recommend them.

● Integration Challenges: Many digital health products do not seamlessly integrate with
existing Electronic Health Records (EHRs) or clinical workflows, creating additional
administrative burden rather than alleviating it. This is a significant pain point for busy
clinicians.
● Reimbursement Uncertainty: Lack of clear and consistent reimbursement pathways for
digital health services or the use of specific digital health products creates financial
disincentives for providers.
● Digital Divide in Rural Areas: Limited broadband access, insufficient digital literacy
among both providers and patients, and a lack of technical support infrastructure can
severely hinder adoption in rural settings.
● Provider Burnout and Time Constraints: Providers are already stretched thin.
Learning new technologies and incorporating them into practice requires time and effort,
which can be a significant barrier without clear benefits or supportive infrastructure.
● Patient Engagement and Adherence: Even if a provider recommends a digital product,
ensuring patient engagement and consistent use can be challenging, impacting the
perceived value of the product.
● Unclear Clinical Utility and Evidence: Many providers are rightly skeptical of products
without strong clinical evidence of efficacy and impact on health outcomes.
The DiMe Seal, through its comprehensive evaluation across these domains, serves as a
meta-assessment that integrates and harmonizes these critical standards, offering a single,
trustworthy symbol for adopters and buyers. It reduces the burden on individual stakeholders to
assess each product against a myriad of individual technical standards.
TD-1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS
take to stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for
Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers?
To stimulate developer interest in building high-quality digital health products for Medicare
beneficiaries and caregivers, CMS must create a more predictable, transparent, and rewarding
market environment. Developers are driven by clear market signals, streamlined pathways to
adoption, and opportunities for sustainable growth. The DiMe Seal, by defining and recognizing
"quality," plays a crucial role in shaping this market.
Short-Term Steps (Next 2 Years):
1. Clearly Articulate Market Needs and Opportunities:
○ Publish Priority Areas: CMS should clearly delineate specific health challenges
or unmet needs within the Medicare beneficiary population that digital health
products are well-suited to address (e.g., chronic disease management for
specific conditions, caregiver burden, fall prevention, medication adherence).
This provides developers with targeted opportunities.

○ Host Developer Challenges/Hackathons: Sponsor short-term challenges or
hackathons focused on these priority areas, offering prizes and opportunities for
mentorship or pilot project funding.
2. Establish a Clear "Trusted Technology" Pathway:
○ Formally Endorse Independent Assessments: CMS should officially recognize
and promote credible, independent third-party assessment programs like The
DiMe Seal. This provides developers with a clear "quality bar" to aim for and a
signal that their investment in robust development, evidence generation, and
user-centric design will be recognized. This reduces market ambiguity and
accelerates adoption for assessment awarded products.
3. Pilot Incentive Programs for "Approved" Products:
○ Demonstration Projects: Fund and publicize demonstration projects where
healthcare organizations (especially those serving rural or underserved Medicare
populations) pilot the use of DiMe Seal-awarded digital health products,
showcasing their impact on outcomes and ROI.
○ "Innovation Waivers" for Value-Based Models: Explore targeted waivers or
flexibilities within existing value-based care models that incentivize the use of
independently assessed digital health products.
Longer-Term Steps:
1. Integrate Assessment into Reimbursement and Quality Frameworks:
○ Performance-Based Reimbursement: Develop long-term strategies to directly
link reimbursement for certain digital health-enabled services or products to the
product holding a recognized, outcome-focused assessment award (like the
DiMe Seal, which emphasizes evidence of impact). This creates a direct financial
incentive for developing high-value, high quality, effective products.
○ Quality Measure Inclusion: Incorporate the appropriate use of independently
assessed digital health products into quality measures for providers within
Medicare programs.
By implementing these short-term and longer-term steps, CMS can create a robust and
attractive ecosystem that encourages developers to build high-quality, impactful digital health
products specifically tailored to the needs of Medicare beneficiaries and their caregivers,
ultimately advancing health outcomes across the nation.
VB-1. What incentives could encourage APMs such as accountable care
organizations (ACOs) or participants in Medicare Shared Savings Program
(MSSP) to leverage digital health management and care navigation
products more often and more effectively with their patients? What are the
current obstacles preventing broader digital product adoption for patients
in ACOs?

Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs) and participants in the Medicare Shared Savings
Program (MSSP) are uniquely positioned to benefit from digital health management and care
navigation products due to their focus on population health, care coordination, and value-based
outcomes. Incentivizing their effective use requires a multi-faceted approach that addresses
both financial drivers and operational barriers. The DiMe Seal serves as a critical enabler by
identifying trustworthy, high-quality products that ACOs can confidently adopt.
Incentives to Encourage Leveraging Digital Health Products in APMs:
1. Integrate Assessed Digital Health Use into Quality Measures:
○ Short-Term: Introduce optional or bonus quality measures within APMs that
reward the appropriate adoption and patient engagement with independently
assessed digital health management and care navigation products (e.g., those
with the DiMe Seal) for specific conditions (e.g., chronic disease management,
post-discharge follow-up, preventive care). This incentivizes ACOs to use tools
that are proven to be high-quality.
○ Long-Term: Develop core quality measures that directly reflect improved
outcomes or reduced costs attributable to the effective use of digital health
products, allowing ACOs flexibility in choosing awarded solutions that best fit
their patient population.
2. Financial Incentives and Flexibilities:
○ Shared Savings Attribution: Clarify how the costs associated with
implementing and supporting assessed digital health solutions, including
associated personnel (e.g., digital navigators, care coordinators using digital
tools), can be factored into ACO expenditures for shared savings calculations.
○ Innovation Investment Funds: Create or expand specific funds or grants within
APMs dedicated to investing in and deploying assessed digital health
technologies, particularly for ACOs serving rural or underserved populations.
○ Flexibility in Benefit Design: Allow greater flexibility for ACOs to cover or
subsidize patient access to assessed digital health products as part of their care
management strategies, especially for products that reduce high-cost utilization
(e.g., hospital readmissions).
3. Streamlined Procurement and Implementation:
○ CMS Endorsement of Certification: By formally endorsing and promoting a
standardized, comprehensive assessment like the DiMe Seal, CMS significantly
reduces the due diligence burden on ACOs. This signal of "awarded" products
streamlines their procurement process, allowing them to confidently select
solutions.
○ Best Practice Sharing: Facilitate platforms and collaborative networks where
ACOs can share best practices, lessons learned, and successful implementation
strategies for digital health products, particularly those that have achieved
positive outcomes.
