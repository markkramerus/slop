Request for Information Response: CMS-0042-NC Submitted by: Catherine Costa RN MTM Date: 06/16/2025 Subject: Enabling Whole Person Care Through Relationship-Based Infrastructure and Policy Alignment I. Introduction As a healthcare strategist working at the intersection of clinical transformation and digital innovation, I appreciate the opportunity to respond to CMS-0042-NC. My career has centered on designing technologies and workflows that support care models aligned with the whole person—models that prioritize trust, continuity, and individualized care planning over transactional documentation. My perspective draws from both direct experience designing functional medicine EMRs and strategy work supporting value-based transformation in health systems. It is also shaped by the belief that nurses are essential to whole person care—not only because of their clinical training, but because of their unmatched ability to build relationships that drive behavioral change. II. Background My comments respond broadly to the sections on Provider workflows , Patient and Caregiver experiences , and Value-Based Care Organizations , with particular emphasis on data standards, care navigation apps, and quality frameworks that reflect whole person needs. While technology has improved data liquidity, it has not yet transformed care delivery. Diagnosis, treatment, and billing remain structured around siloed codes rather than the lived experience of health. As a result, current digital products reinforce rather than rethink the legacy care model. III. Analysis The Problem: Technology Built for Billing, Not for Healing Despite advances in APIs and interoperability, the core data captured in most EHRs is still episodic, reductionist, and driven by reimbursement logic. As outlined in Whole Person Care in Practice and Policy (Costa, 2025), the reliance on ICD, CPT, and DRG codes leaves no 
structured space for capturing patient goals, relationship continuity, or the nuanced behavioral insights that nurses routinely generate. Even value-based care models are hindered by this constraint. While APMs aim to reward quality, their measures often fail to account for the non-encounter-based interventions—education, coaching, trust-building—that truly move the needle on outcomes. Similarly, TEFCA’s progress on data exchange is important but insufficient unless it includes whole person, longitudinal insights. The Untapped Asset: Nurses as Relationship Builders and Behavioral Catalysts Nurses are already trained in whole person assessment, health education, and continuity-based care. But our infrastructure and metrics don’t reward these strengths. As a result, we underutilize one of the most scalable, evidence-based tools for improving health: relationships. IV. Recommendations 1. Expand Data Standards to Reflect Whole Person Narratives ● Evolve USCDI and Blue Button APIs to include behavior-change readiness, self-management milestones, and patient-defined goals. ● Ensure interoperability initiatives (including TEFCA) prioritize not just clinical data exchange, but social-behavioral context and care continuity documentation. 2. Incentivize Nurse-Led Relationship Models ● Fund CMMI pilots that position nurses as primary continuity providers for high-need populations. ● Track outcomes based on patient engagement, behavioral change, and longitudinal progress—not just utilization or biometric targets. 3. Align APMs with What Drives Outcomes ● Require or reward Z-code capture and follow-up in APM quality measures. ● Redefine “value” to include metrics like trust, adherence to care plans, and patient confidence—all of which are directly influenced by relational care. 4. Support Whole Person Digital Infrastructure 
● Enable care navigation and health management tools that center on relational continuity—allowing nurses and patients to co-manage evolving care goals. ● Move beyond encounter-based EHRs toward platforms that integrate social, emotional, and behavioral health in real time. V. Conclusion The future of health isn’t in more codes—it’s in deeper connections. Technology should serve relationships, not replace them. By reimagining what we measure, what we reward, and how we define interoperability, CMS can lead the transformation toward whole person care. Summary Recommendations: ● Broaden data standards to include behavioral and relational health insights. ● Fund nurse-led continuity pilots and capture outcomes beyond utilization. ● Align APM quality metrics with real-world drivers of health. ● Prioritize infrastructure that supports trust, not just transactions. Thank you for your leadership in building a more person-centered, connected future for care.
