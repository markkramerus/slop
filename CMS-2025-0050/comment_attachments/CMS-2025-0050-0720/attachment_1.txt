Addressing Critical DMEPOS System Inefficiencies Through Direct Physician Engagement
I. Executive Summary
Rx Redefined respectfully submits these comments regarding documented inefficiencies in the
Durable Medical Equipment, Prosthetics, Orthotics, and Supplies (DMEPOS) sector, which are
associated with adverse impacts on Medicare beneficiary access to timely care and increased
costs to the healthcare system. The Medicare Comprehensive Error Rate Testing (CERT)
program's identification of a 21.4% improper payment rate, representing $1.9 billion in projected
improper payments in DMEPOS—close to triple the 7.6% rate for Part A provider
services—demonstrates the need for comprehensive reform through direct physician engagement
and integrated technology solutions.1
Analysis of current data suggests that the current third-party supplier model may perpetuate
systemic inefficiencies that could persist, highlighting a potential need for structural updates to
DMEPOS delivery mechanisms to better support Medicare beneficiaries and reduce instances of
fraud, waste, and abuse.
II. Background: Critical Problems in Current DMEPOS Delivery
A. Patterns Consistent with Fraud, Waste, and Abuse
(Responses to VB-11, TD-18(a)(c), TD-2(d))
The DMEPOS sector demonstrates vulnerabilities that impact both the integrity of patient care
delivery and fiscal management. Recent data demonstrates the severity of these challenges:
Concerning Billing Patterns: The National Association of Accountable Care Organizations
(NAACOS) reported that payments for urinary catheters increased from $153 million in 2021 to
$2.1 billion in 2023—representing a 1,273% increase that may account for nearly one-fifth of all
Medicare spending on medical supplies in 2023. Some suppliers demonstrated billing patterns
identified by regulatory authorities as possibly indicative of improper billing, with total claims
approaching $3 billion, including seven high-volume suppliers charging $2 billion for catheters
alone.2,3,4

Patterns consistent with improper HCPCS code upgrading: Suppliers have at times upgrade
prescriptions from lower-reimbursed codes (e.g., A4351 at $2 per catheter) to higher-reimbursed
codes (e.g., A4353 at $7.50 per catheter) by soliciting medical histories to identify any record of

Provider Burden and Uncompensated Work: Physicians and clinical support staff frequently
perform uncompensated administrative activities related to DMEPOS, including:
● Comprehensive patient evaluation encompassing medical history, insurance coverage
criteria, and social determinants of health
● Product selection and patient training on optimal DMEPOS utilization

IV. Proposed Solutions
A. Direct Physician DMEPOS Provision Model
(Responses to PC-1,PC-4(a)(c), PC-5, PC-12(a)(b)(c)(d), PR-1(a)(c)(d))
Recommendation: We recommend CMS evaluate options to support and further streamline the
established pathway for physicians to directly supply DMEPOS to their patients via integrated,
compliant technology platforms. Such an approach has the potential to reduce reliance on
third-party intermediaries and support timelier patient care.
Page 8 of 17 CMS-0042-NC

Implementation Framework: Physicians would obtain required DMEPOS supplier enrollment
and licensure by complying with the requirements in 42 CFR § 424.57 (supplier standards) and
42 CFR §§ 424.500–424.570 (enrollment process), including submission of the CMS-855S
application and adherence to the CMS Publication 100-08 (Medicare Program Integrity
Manual).8,9,10,11 Certified technology platforms could be leveraged to manage billing, logistics,
and compliance, ensuring adherence to applicable statutes such as Stark Law and the
Anti-Kickback Statute.12,13 This approach places clinical decision-making with the physician
while supporting regulatory oversight.
B. Streamlining Payer-Provider Workflows for Value and Savings
(Responses to PA-1, PA-2, PA-5(c), VB-1(a), VB-4)
Reducing Plan Costs through Direct Physician Supply: Direct physician provision has the
potential to reduce unnecessary utilization, fraud, and administrative overhead by minimizing
third-party involvement. CERT data shows DMEPOS improper payments are nearly triple those
of physician services, at $1.9B annually. NAACOS reports that direct physician oversight
prevents increased utilization and billing not aligned with clinical needs, resulting in substantial
savings for payers.14
Lowering Grievance and Administrative Workload: Digital-first, physician-led workflows
have the potential to streamline claims, reduce appeals, and expedite resolution of coverage and
eligibility matters. Centralizing compliance within clinical teams can lessen communication
burdens between parties.
Alignment with CMS Interoperability and Quality Initiatives: Implementing FHIR-based
APIs and real-time data exchange supports CMS’s interoperability and digital quality reporting
objectives. This approach enables seamless, standards-based data sharing, reduces reporting
burden for providers and payers, and ensures readiness for evolving CMS requirements.

C. Technology Integration and API Enhancement
(Responses to PA-1, PA-2, PA-5(a)(b), PR-5(g), PC-8(c)(d), PC-9(a)(c)(d), PC-10(b)(c), PC-14(e)(f),
PR-6(b), PR-9, TD-2(a)(b)(c), TD-3, TD-15(a), VB-4)
Blue Button 2.0 API Expansion: We recommend expanding the Blue Button 2.0 API to
include:
● Real-time DMEPOS supplier network status and coverage verification
● Product-specific coverage criteria and prior authorization requirements
● Clinical documentation templates and submission tracking
● Patient education materials and training resources
Interoperability Standards: Technology platforms certified to facilitate licensed physicians in
the management of DMEPOS would be required to:
● Support interoperability by making available DMEPOS processing data, as facilitated by
its participating licensed physician users, via FHIR R4 endpoints. FHIR resources, such
as Device, SupplyDelivery, Claim, and DocumentReference and relevant profiles, such as
US Core Device Profile must be utilized to standardize data exchange with authorized
entities, including bulk access for ACOs.
● Integrate with TEFCA QHINs as a FHIR-enabled participant, using QHIN-facilitated
APIs for DMEPOS data exchange while preparing for advanced stages of the FHIR
Roadmap.
● Implement IAL2 and AAL2 standards from NIST Special Publication 800-63 regarding
trust and authentication.
Requiring use of standards-based authentication protocols (such as OIDC) will enhance
cybersecurity and data exchange by enforcing consistent, government-standardized identity
proofing and authentication, leaving less room for interpretation and incident. In particular,
specific solutions such as Login.gov would further improve cybersecurity by eliminating
interpretation and offloading trust entirely to the Login.gov platform.
D. Value-Based Care Integration
(Responses to PC-14(e), VB-1(a), VB-2, VB-3, VB-4, VB-5, VB-9, VB-13, VB-14, VB-15)
Incentivizing Digital Health Adoption in VBC: Direct physician DMEPOS provision,
integrated with digital health platforms, aligns incentives for ACOs and other VBC entities by
reducing unnecessary utilization, enabling real-time population health analytics, and supporting
care coordination. This model empowers clinical teams to act on timely, accurate data, which is
essential for shared savings and quality improvement.
Essential Health IT Capabilities and Data Types: The approach leverages FHIR-based APIs
and USCDI v3-aligned data exchange to ensure seamless integration with EHRs and payer
systems. This supports core VBC functions such as risk stratification, quality measurement, and
remote device monitoring, while meeting ONC Health IT Certification Program standards.

Certification, Reporting, and Interoperability: We recommend CMS recognize direct
physician DMEPOS provision as meeting enhanced care coordination and reporting
requirements for Advanced APMs. Interoperable, API-enabled platforms reduce provider
burden, simplify quality reporting, and address common interoperability challenges in VBC
programs.
Digital Identity and Nationwide Directories: Adopting IAL2/AAL2 digital identity credentials
and participating in FHIR endpoint directories can further strengthen security, streamline patient
matching, and improve data access for VBC organizations.
Direct physician DMEPOS provision aligns perfectly with Advanced APM objectives by:
● Reducing unnecessary product utilization through clinical oversight (studies demonstrate
up to 30% reduction in product usage)15
● Minimizing the frequency of product substitution and non-justified HCPCS code
upgrades
● Enhancing care coordination for chronic disease management
● Improving population health outcomes through appropriate product selection
For Advanced APMs, we recommend establishing specific certification criteria that recognize
direct physician DMEPOS provision as meeting enhanced care coordination requirements under
CEHRT standards.

V. Expected System Improvements and Potential Savings
A. Financial Impact Analysis
(Responses to VB-1(a), VB-14,TD-9(a), VB-3(c) VB-13, VB-15(a))
Fraud Reduction: Based on historical CERT data, the improper payment rate for DMEPOS is
21.4%, totaling approximately $1.9 billion in projected improper payments annually.16 If
transitioning to physician-led DMEPOS services were to reduce improper payments to the rate
observed for physician-provided services (approximately 0.5%), modeling indicates the potential
reduction would be:
$1.9 billion × ((21.4%−0.5%) / 21.4%) ≈ $1.82 billion
For illustrative purposes, if improper payments decreased by 97.5% (from 21.4% to 0.5%), the
potential annual savings would be:
$1.9 billion × 0.975 = $1.85 billion
This estimate reflects the possible impact of expanded professional oversight in physician
practice, which may support a reduction in improper billing opportunities compared to current
third-party supplier licensure requirements.

Utilization Optimization: Clinical oversight by physicians supports appropriate utilization and
product selection based on clinical need. This approach helps align incentives by addressing
patterns such as non-justified HCPCS code upgrades, recommendations for alternative products
not consistent with current clinical standards, or the promotion of quantities that exceed patient
requirements.
A 2022 pilot study of catheter utilization demonstrated a 30% reduction in median utilization
under physician-led models compared to third-party supplier workflows, with no increase in
adverse events. While further research is needed to confirm generalizability across DMEPOS
categories, these findings suggest a potential for reducing overuse while maintaining care quality.
Savings Calculation (Illustrative):
● Total DMEPOS payments (2024): $9.0 billion (CERT Table A1)
● Proper payments (non-fraudulent): $9.0B – $1.9B (improper) = $7.1 billion
● Potential savings from 23–30% utilization reduction:
$7.1B × 0.25 (conservative estimate) = $1.78B annually
Physician oversight may reduce inappropriate code upgrades (e.g., A4351 to A4353) by aligning
coding with clinical documentation. CERT data shows incorrect coding contributed to 9.2% of
DMEPOS improper payments in 2024, within the overall 21.4% DMEPOS improper payment
rate.17
Direct provision may also lessen the incidence of alternate product substitutions not aligned with
current clinical standards, supporting appropriate item delivery.
Reduced Risk of Adverse Medical Events: More timely DMEPOS delivery and product
continuity may reduce avoidable adverse events due to lapses in supply or improper use of a
product the patient wasn’t trained to use. Evidence from a recent quality improvement project
found that optimizing delivery systems decreased treatment delays by 72% and improved clinical
outcomes for patients requiring timely access to medical equipment.18 For value-based entities,
even a modest reduction in adverse events such as catheter-associated urinary tract infections
(CAUTIs) can yield measurable savings. For example, if a value-based care organization serving
1,000 Medicare beneficiaries with chronic catheter use achieved a 50% reduction in CAUTI
rates, this could prevent approximately 10–20 hospitalizations annually, resulting in an estimated
$100,000–$200,000 in cost avoidance based on CMS’s cost models for avoidable

VI. Regulatory and Compliance Framework
A. Certification Standards
(Responses to TD-8, TD-9(c)(d), VB-5)
We recommend CMS establish specific certification criteria for physician DMEPOS platforms
requiring:
● Automated regulatory compliance monitoring and reporting
● Real-time fraud detection and prevention capabilities
● Clinical need-driven product access assurance
● Patient access API compatibility consistent with CMS Interoperability rules
B. Information Blocking Prevention
(Responses to PC-12(a)(b)(c)(d), PR-12, TD-18(c))
Direct physician provision naturally reduces information blocking by:
● Eliminating third-party supplier gatekeepers who may restrict patient access to product
information
● Maintaining transparent clinical decision-making processes
● Ensuring patient access to complete medical records including DMEPOS prescriptions
and delivery status

VII. Implementation Recommendations
A. Pilot Program Development
Phase 1 (12-18 months): Establish pilot programs for direct physician DMEPOS provision in
high-fraud, waste and abuse categories including urological supplies and diabetes management
products. Focus initial implementation on practices already participating in Advanced APMs to
leverage existing infrastructure and performance measurement capabilities.
Phase 2 (18-36 months): Expand pilot to additional DMEPOS categories and provider types,
incorporating lessons learned and refining compliance frameworks. Develop comprehensive
training and certification programs for participating physicians.
B. Long-term Integration
TEFCA Alignment: Integrate physician DMEPOS platforms with TEFCA infrastructure to
enable seamless data exchange and care coordination across provider networks.
Quality Measurement: Implement outcome-based reimbursement models that reward quality
metrics including patient satisfaction, clinical appropriateness, and cost-effectiveness.
VIII. Conclusion
The current DMEPOS system exhibits inefficiencies that require continued evaluation and
improvement. Evidence indicates that some prevailing practices in the sector are associated with
patterns of improper billing, increased utilization, product substitutions not aligned with initial
clinical prescription, and potential delays in delivery of necessary equipment.
Facilitating direct physician provision of DMEPOS through compliant, integrated technology
platforms offers a viable strategy for addressing these challenges. This model has the potential to
improve care timeliness and appropriateness, reduce the occurrence of patterns consistent with
fraud, waste, or abuse, and support providers in delivering clinically appropriate care.
The proposed reforms are consistent with CMS’s broader digital health transformation goals and
address specific vulnerabilities that affect the care of Medicare beneficiaries. By reallocating
resources from administrative intermediaries toward physicians responsible for clinical care,
CMS can support cost optimization as well as improved quality and timeliness in DMEPOS
delivery.
We respectfully encourage CMS to consider prioritizing these reforms and welcome the
opportunity to collaborate on implementing solutions that enhance service to Medicare
beneficiaries and promote system efficiency and integrity.
