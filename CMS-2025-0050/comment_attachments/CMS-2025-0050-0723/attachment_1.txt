RE: CMS–0042–NC – Health Technology Ecosystem RFI
Submitted by: Samsung Electronics America – US Digital Health
Submitted by: Senior Vice President Hon Pak, Head of the Digital Health Team
Date: June 16, 2025
Executive Summary
Samsung Electronics America appreciates the opportunity to respond to the Centers for
Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS), the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy/Office of
the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ASTP/ONC), and the Department
of Health and Human Services (HHS) Request for Information (RFI) on the Health Technology
Ecosystem. As one of the largest global digital health platforms, Samsung reaches tens of
millions of U.S. consumers and offers advanced capabilities on wearable rings and watches
including real-time biomarker monitoring, sleep and fitness coaching, and FDA-cleared features
such as Electrocardiogram Monitoring, Irregular Heart Rhythm Notifications, and Sleep Apnea
Risk detection. Recognizing the sensitivity of a user’s health and wellness data, Samsung
places the highest priority on privacy and security, embedding rigorous data protection and
governance practices into the design of our technologies.
We strongly support CMS and ASTP/ONC's vision to accelerate access, interoperability, and
innovation in digital health, and commend the agency's commitment to reducing friction in data
exchange, empowering patients and caregivers and supporting a thriving digital health
ecosystem. Our comments focus on enhancing the integration of consumer health technologies
into the health system and reimbursement frameworks, expanding interoperability standards to
encompass real-world and patient generated health data, and enabling the scalable use of
digital identity and patient access APIs.
In alignment with Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy’s Make America Healthy Again (MAHA)
initiative emphasizing prevention and patient empowerment, Samsung is uniquely positioned to
engage consumers proactively. Our proven capabilities in digital health innovation and
consumer-driven health technology seamlessly complement the objectives of MAHA, fostering a
healthier, more empowered American population.
Samsung has a strategic focus on enabling care in the home. We are actively developing
solutions that not only make it easier for individuals to manage their own health, but also to
empower caregivers to support loved ones more effectively. The home is increasingly
becoming the center of care, and policy decisions must support the changing landscape to meet
the needs of both patients and those who care for them.
Samsung urges CMS and ASTP/ONC to:

1. Expand the scope of Blue Button and FHIR APIs to include certified consumer device
data.
2. Prioritize TEFCA and other interoperability frameworks that accept and route data from
digital health apps and wearables.
3. Create policy pathways for recognizing and reimbursing digital health tools that support
consumer device generated patient data, patient engagement, and information care
giving.
4. Incentivize the use of standardized digital identity credentials to streamline patient
access to their own health data across payers and providers.
Detailed Comments
PC–1. Health management and care navigation apps
Samsung Health provides evidence-based digital tools for managing sleep, stress, weight,
nutrition, activity, and mental health. Additionally, Samsung Health has integrations with b.well,
a leading consumer-centric health data platform that consolidates payer, provider, and
individual-generated health information. Samsung Health has also partnered with Xealth, a
Digital Health platform that enables providers to prescribe, monitor, and manage digital tools
and services directly within the EMR workflow. CMS should consider formalizing the role of
such digital health solutions in care navigation policies, recognizing it as essential for Medicare
beneficiaries seeking to manage care across multiple sources. This data, combined with the
other wearable sensor data Samsung provides, empowers users to understand trends, identify
issues, and act independently or in concert with their care team. As CMS promotes care
navigation tools, we recommend the agency establish clear criteria and formal recognition
frameworks (such as a “CMS Digital Health Navigation app” designation) to promote and
incentivize apps that:
● Integrate both clinical and patient-generated data
● Offer coaching or guided workflows to interpret health data
● Are accessible through existing consumer-grade platforms
● Include features that facilitate caregiver engagement and shared access to health
insights for trusted individuals
Establishing such a formal designation would allow CMS to measurably assess and highlight
applications that effectively improve patient outcomes and reduce care fragmentation.
PC–5. Encouraging adoption
In order to encourage adoption, CMS should consider offering designations (e.g., "CMS Digital
Health Trusted App") for products that meet data interoperability and patient impact

benchmarks, particularly those that enable care coordination among family members and
informal caregivers.
Samsung also supports the inclusion of solutions like Xealth to drive adoption. Xealth
seamlessly embeds digital health tool access and activation within the electronic health record
workflow. By enabling clinicians to prescribe and monitor digital health apps and devices as
part of their regular clinical documentation process, platforms like Xealth improve uptake,
reduce clinician burden, and tie digital health engagement directly to measurable quality
outcomes. CMS could explore incorporating this into the Alternative Payment Model or clinical
quality improvement programs.
CMS should also consider forming strategic partnerships with major consumer health platforms
(e.g., Samsung Health) to enable real-time insurance eligibility verification, benefit coverage
checks, and prior authorization workflows direction with digital health applications as well as
data-sharing integrations with Medicare. A significant barrier to patient engagement and
adoption of digital health interventions is uncertainty about insurance coverage. By partnering
with platforms like Samsung Health, CMS can integrate seamless payer eligibility and prior
authorization processes, empowering Medicare beneficiaries with immediate clarity on covered
services. This integration would significantly accelerate the implementation of frictionless digital
enrollment pathways, encouraging patients to engage proactively with personalized health
recommendations that are verified as reimbursable by insurance, thereby reducing uncertainty
and promoting widespread adoption of beneficial digital health interventions.
PC–9. Expansion of Blue Button 2.0
Blue Button 2.0 is a beneficial technology that could be made even stronger by:
● Adding real-time or near-real-time encounter data, not just claims data
● Including structured device data (e.g., steps, heart rate variability, sleep scores, etc)
from digital health tools
● Facilitating authorized proxy access so that caregivers can manage a loved one's data
within compliant digital health tools
PC–10. TEFCA and real-world access
To fully realize TEFCA’s potential, we encourage CMS and ONC to clarify the eligibility and
participation pathways for consumer technology platforms that are not traditional health
information networks, but are increasingly acting as critical conduits for patient generated data.
The Samsung Health app, for example, collects behavioral data and real time biometric data
from wearables that can enhance care coordination, identify health issues sooner, and support
remote monitoring when integrated with clinical systems. It’s important that policies align with
emerging use cases around caregiver coordination, such as proxy access to real-time health
data or medication adherence insights. This is particularly important for Medicare beneficiaries
that are supported by informal caregivers.

CMS and ONC should also consider establishing a formal TEFCA certification pathway for
consumer health platforms that meet data quality, consent, and security requirements. This
would help bridge the trust gap between consumer and clinical environments and ensure
compliant inclusion of patient data in national networks.
TD–1. Stimulating developer interest
CMS can stimulate innovation in Medicare relevant digital health apps by creating condition-
specific "developer challenges" that invite solution design (e.g., hypertension, fall prevention,
sleep apnea) and opening pathways for remote therapeutic monitoring (RTM) and remote
physiological monitoring (RPM) reimbursement for non-traditional platforms.
In partnership with a major health system, Samsung developed a home-based cardiac
rehabilitation program that served 20,000 patients.1 Despite evidence of improved outcomes
compared to center-based programs, expansion was curtailed due to a lack of reimbursement
codes. Samsung urges CMS to explore reimbursement for digital health tools for prevention
and rehabilitation.
We further recommend that CMS encourage innovation among platform providers that can
reduce friction caused by the fragmentation of point solutions and instead orchestrate multiple
services through a unified, patient-centric interface. Today, patients and caregivers often
navigate a disjointed digital experience – using separate applications for continuous glucose
monitoring, insulin management tools, medication adherence, and coaching – each with its own
login, data silos, and learning curve. This fragmentation creates cognitive and operational
burdens, especially for patients managing chronic conditions. Tools like Samsung Health can
serve as orchestration platforms that unify these services into one interface, streamline the
experience, and enable seamless data sharing with caregivers and providers. Providing
development incentives and introducing “orchestration reimbursement codes” would stimulate
the creation of more scalable, integrated solutions while addressing the current inefficiencies
and complexity in managing multiple apps and device.
TD–2. CMS Data APIs
To unlock greater value, CMS should expose via FHIR:
● Prior authorization status
● Prescription fill history
● Real-time eligibility and benefits
● Plan formularies and cost-sharing tiers
● Annual wellness visits and preventive screening status
● Vaccination records

TD–5. National FHIR endpoint directory
Samsung strongly supports a national provider directory containing verified FHIR endpoints and
digital identity credentialing methods. This infrastructure would reduce patient burden by
allowing digital health tools to seamlessly connect to verified provider and payer endpoints
without requiring the user to manually locate, input, or manage the information. For example, a
caregiver using Samsung Health to coordinate care for an elderly family member could use the
directory to automatically find the correct FHIR endpoint for the member’s provider or insurer.
This could enable secure data sharing for medication updates, care plan adjustments, and
benefit verification without the need for repeated logins thereby expanding adoption.
VB–1. Incentivizing digital tools in APMs and ACOs
CMS should consider a formal reimbursement or quality measure adjustment for ACOs and
APM participants that use consumer-facing platforms that enable real time adherence and
biometric monitoring data and provide incentive payments for documented improvement in
outcomes attributable to patient generated data. Enabling this incentive will help bridge the gap
between traditional care and digital-first strategies.
Currently, RTM and RPM are often tied to specific billing codes for hardware devices or
synchronous check ins. However, much of the value lies in passive monitoring, digital coaching,
asynchronous alerts, and non-billable coordination activities that contribute significantly to
clinical outcomes, especially for high-risk patients. CMS could provide clearer guidance on how
such activities can be documented and credited under existing codes, value-based payment
models, or new care coordination incentives.
We also encourage CMS to expand the definition of eligible provider types for preventive
interventions, particularly for high-risk populations. Virtual care touchpoints such as pharmacist-
led medication reviews, behavioral coaching for stress, anxiety, or sleep, or dietitian-led nutrition
and chronic disease education could play a vital role in early intervention, treatment adherence,
and hospitalization avoidance.
VB–3 / VB–4. Essential IT capabilities and data types
These data types should be recognized alongside EHR and claims data to reflect a complete
picture of patient status in a value-based care arrangement:
● Remote device data (steps, oxygen saturation, sleep staging, stress tracking, heart rate)
● Passive monitoring for fall detection, heart rhythm, inactivity, and sleep apnea
● Self-reported outcomes and real-time behavioral engagement signals
● Proxy support functionality for caregivers

Conclusion
Samsung supports CMS and ASTP/ONC’s commitment to building a more open, interoperable,
and patient-centered digital health ecosystem. As a leading global health technology platform,
we are eager to partner with the federal government to improve the Medicare beneficiary
experience, deliver more personalized care, enable information care giving, and unlock better
outcomes through data-driven digital engagement.
We strongly believe that enabling care in the home, and empowering both individuals and their
loved ones with tools to manage care, is vital to realizing the promise of digital health. CMS
policy can and should support this transformation.
We appreciate this opportunity to provide input and are available for follow-up discussion.
