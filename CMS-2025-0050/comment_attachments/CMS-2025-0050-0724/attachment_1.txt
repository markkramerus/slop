About Imagine Pediatrics
Imagine Pediatrics is a pediatrician-led medical group delivering 24/7 virtual and in-home care
to children with special health care needs. We currently serve more than 35,000 children and
their families across Texas, Florida, and the Washington D.C. metro area through value-based
arrangements with six Medicaid managed care organizations. Our multidisciplinary team
of 400+ staff members, approximately 70% of whom are clinical team members, works in
partnership with existing providers to deliver expanded access to care that helps children spend
more safe days at home.
Since launching care delivery for our first patients in January 2023, we have demonstrated
remarkable outcomes:

families and it has the power to transform the health industry
● 86+ Net Promoter Score from patients/caregivers
● Prevention of nearly 10,000 unnecessary emergency and urgent care visits through
timely primary interventions
● 76% of those diverted ED visits required no acute care in the following 30 days

1. Behavioral Health Data Integration Remains a Critical Gap

imaginepediatrics.org
The Challenge: Despite advances in interoperability, behavioral health data remains largely
inaccessible. Many behavioral health facilities do not utilize certified EHRs and are therefore
absent from health information networks. In addition, many large health systems that perform
behavioral health care keep such data carved entirely out of the HIE networks to which they are
otherwise connected for the risk of inappropriate disclosure. This forces families caring for
children with complex needs to repeatedly share their child's behavioral health history with
each new provider. Expanding TEFCA or other HIEs to include behavioral health is critical to
advancing provider access as detailed in PR-6 and PR-7.
Our Experience: As noted in our introduction, our 24/7 care model requires real-time
coordination across fragmented systems to bridge gaps in access and care. This challenge is
most acute with behavioral health data. Recently, a 13-year-old patient in the foster care system
with PTSD and mood disorders was discharged from a behavioral health facility while
transitioning between foster placements. Despite our care coordination efforts, we only learned
of the discharge through live discussion with the new foster facility. The discharge medication
regimen was never communicated to the new caregivers, and our team discovered the patient
was on duplicate doses of one medication and needed an urgent refill for another. Our
Behavioral Health and Pharmacy teams spent hours piecing together this critical
information—work that could have been avoided if the facility were connected to an HIE. This
gap in real-time data sharing directly impacts our ability to prevent unnecessary ED visits and
potential patient harm.

Leverage existing authorities to incentivize EHR adoption through:

provide technology grants; 1115 demonstration waivers for health IT
infrastructure; CCBHC expansion grants with IT requirements; 90/10
administrative matching funds for HIE connectivity
○ Medicare: Use Innovation Center (CMMI) demonstrations to test behavioral
health IT adoption models; incorporate IT requirements into Medicare Shared
Savings Program behavioral health integration tracks; leverage Conditions of
Participation updates to require data sharing capabilities
○ Cross-program: Align Medicare and Medicaid requirements through integrated
care models for dual-eligibles; use existing SAMHSA block grants with enhanced

2. Text Messaging as an Essential Digital Health Tool
The Challenge: Despite strong evidence that patients and caregivers readily adopt and benefit
from SMS-based care, texting remains under-recognized as a digital tool that supports care
coordination. Requiring an app download introduces significant friction: many phones have
limited storage or data plans, and unreliable broadband can make large downloads and updates
impractical.
Our Experience: Our Patient & Family Advisory Committee (PFAC) consistently identifies text
messaging as a critical communication preference. Families love to interact with us digitally, and
SMS is their preferred method of communication with our teams. We also often hear that
families struggle with app storage on their phones, remembering login credentials for phones
that don't support biometrics, occasionally older caregivers who struggle with the app
download process, and some households that are not able to complete downloads and updates
due to poor connectivity.
SMS eliminates these barriers entirely - no downloads, no passwords, no storage requirements.
This accessibility is why texting has proven so effective for appointment reminders, quality gap
closure nudges, care coordination, and disease management programs - including studies
showing a 14.6% reduction in no-show appointment rates when patients were texted
appointment reminders vs. those who were not texted (Link), and 35% higher rates of primary
care visits at 12-month follow up for children with asthma who received in-person and
text-based educational programming vs. those who did not (Link).
Recommendations:
● Issue non-binding guidance recognizing SMS as a qualified digital health tool: CMS
should clarify that text messaging programs qualify as digital health products when used
for care-related purposes, including appointment reminders and scheduling, quality gap
closure, care plan education and adherence, and disease management programs [PC-6:
Features for accessibility]

imaginepediatrics.org
This recognition would give providers confidence to invest in SMS-based care programs that
meet families where they are, reducing barriers to engagement for the millions of
Medicaid-covered children and their caregivers who need accessible, evidence-based digital
touchpoints.
3. National Digital Identity Infrastructure Would Transform Patient Access
The Challenge: Families caring for children with special health care needs must manage dozens
of provider portal credentials. As a result, we are strongly supportive of advancing PC-14: Digital
identity challenges from the RFI.
Our Experience: Our success in bridging digital divides comes from meeting families where they
are. However, even our simplified approach faces barriers. In the case of pediatrics (and more
specifically, care for foster children), a national patient identity structure would ease the burden
of children moving between caregivers and medical consenters. Our caregivers frequently must
reset digital health application account credentials, particularly in highly sensitive moments
such as acute presentations to hospitals.
For example, a foster child in our care recently transferred to a new foster setting. The prior
caregiver was unable to identify the new foster placement. The prior caregiver lost access to our
app, as it was no longer appropriate for them to have access to the child's PHI. So we had to
regularly follow up with the foster system to 'find' the patient who was enrolled in our care.
A national identity inclusive of consenter responsibilities would have prevented delays in care
and allowed us as a longitudinal care team to follow the patient across contexts. The current
fragmented identity system forces families to choose between security (unique passwords) and
access (remembering credentials during crisis moments). This directly contradicts our
caregiver-centered design principle and CMS’ own goals in the RFI for caregivers that technology
should reduce, not increase, family burden.
Recommendations:
● Support adoption of standards like OpenID Connect across all CMS programs: Lead by
example and require Medicare, Medicaid, and Exchange systems to function as identity
providers, enabling single sign-on across federal health programs. Implement NIST
800-63-3 IAL2/AAL2 standards allowing patients to use verified credentials (CLEAR,
ID.me, Login.gov) across all CMS systems [PC-14(a): Implementation approach]

imaginepediatrics.org
● Establish national digital identity credentials as an option (not requirement) for
patients: Require payers and providers receiving federal funds to use digital identity for
health information exchange and patient portal workflows [PC-14: Digital identity]
○ Organizations should accept the aforementioned credentials for health
information exchange by 2026 via TEFCA or an equivalent network
○ Organizations should allow for account creation, password reset, and other
identity-sensitive moments using digital identity while maintaining traditional
authentication options by 2027
● Design identity solutions specifically tested with Medicaid populations and caregivers
managing multiple dependents: Create roadmap and glidepath for identity providers to
support harder edge cases and move towards a digital-only future [TD-3:
Implementation considerations]
○ Authorized representative/guardian access with role-based permissions
○ Mobile-first authentication (no desktop/scanner requirements)
○ Multiple linked profiles under one caregiver account
○ Low-literacy interface options and video-based verification alternatives
○ Integration with state Medicaid eligibility systems for auto-verification
● Ensure identity solutions work across state lines: Establish federal identity federation
requirements allowing credentials issued in one state to access records nationwide.
Prohibit state-specific identity requirements that block interstate care coordination.
Create reciprocity agreements similar to driver's license compacts [PC-14(e): Cross-state
functionality]
● Maintain strong privacy protections while improving access: Implement
privacy-preserving technologies [PC-14(c): Privacy concerns]
○ Selective disclosure (share only necessary identity attributes)
○ Consent management APIs tracking data sharing preferences
○ Audit logs accessible to patients showing who accessed their identity
○ Prohibition on selling or sharing identity verification data
○ Right to delete identity credentials while maintaining care access
4. Provider Directory Accuracy Impacts Care Access
The Challenge: Inaccurate provider directories create barriers to care, particularly for specialty
services. Our care coordinators report that provider directories are more often wrong than
right, impacting our ability to connect families to timely specialist care.

imaginepediatrics.org
Our Experience: Our caregiver-centered design philosophy means we measure success by how
much time and stress we save families. Yet, provider directory inaccuracy undermines these
efforts daily.
We face systemic provider mapping challenges. Recently, we attempted to coordinate care with
a multi-specialty practice in Florida, where we believed we shared 100 patients based on
patient-provider mapping from our MCO partner. After extensive manual reconciliation, we
discovered we actually shared 350 patients. Without accurate mapping of providers to their
practice affiliations, we had no way of knowing the overlap was this large—a missed
opportunity to scalably approach patients jointly with the practice and ensure coordinated care
across PCPs and digital health providers.
Most critically, directory failures directly harm the continuity of care. We recently discovered
that a primary care provider had moved out of the area months earlier, but this change was
never reflected in any directory. Several of our patients who weren't seeing her regularly were
lost to care entirely. If we had been made aware of the provider's departure through up-to-date
listings, our model is specifically built to intercept these transitions and ensure continuity by
bridging patients to new providers. Instead, these vulnerable children experienced gaps in care
that could have been prevented.
Additionally, the existing Medicaid provider enrollment landscape presents substantial
inefficiencies. Healthcare providers must navigate 50+ different state enrollment systems, each
with unique requirements, timelines, and documentation standards. This fragmentation creates
several critical issues:
● Providers face enormous administrative costs in maintaining separate enrollments
across multiple states, particularly affecting multi-state healthcare systems and
telemedicine providers.
● The redundant credentialing processes delay patient access to care as providers await
approval in each jurisdiction.
● Data inconsistencies across state systems create gaps in provider directories, making it
difficult for patients to locate participating providers.
● States bear significant costs in maintaining separate enrollment infrastructures, and the
lack of standardization complicates oversight and fraud prevention efforts.
This wide variety of directory failures, from individual availability to practice affiliations to
provider departures, underpins the breakdowns in care we work every day to prevent.

○ Provider status changes (departures, moves, practice changes)
○ Provider-to-practice organizational mappings
○ Accurate specialty taxonomy with standardized service descriptions
○ Real-time Medicaid acceptance status

○ New patient acceptance status by insurance type updated in real-time

5. Variability in Quality Measure Data Reporting and Measurement Hinders
Innovation
The Challenge: While quality requirements are essential for ensuring whole-population care,
today's fragmented quality measure stewardship creates significant operational burdens for
innovative at-risk providers. Currently, both payers and providers must independently calculate
(or pay third parties to calculate) patient- and population-level quality statuses over time. This
duplicative system leads to year-end reconciliations that leave both parties uncertain about true
performance, while score calculations that require consolidation of data across many
stakeholders and sources make it nearly impossible to maintain a real-time view of the quality
gap in care closure in order to ensure proper and personalized care.
Our Experience: Our value-based care innovation—achieving 15% medical cost ratio
reduction—requires us to navigate not just contract variability but disagreements about quality
performance across our six Medicaid Managed Care partners. Each plan uses different
processes and data sources to capture quality metric information for the same HEDIS measures,
forcing us to maintain six parallel quality submission and tracking systems. Additionally, the lack
of timely claim and encounter information across a fragmented care delivery ecosystem creates
lagging information related to which patients are still in need of care gap closure.
Our care teams huddle each morning to plan their visits for the day. One key component of the
daily huddle is preparing to assist with the closure of open quality gaps for patients on the
schedule. Our teams review our own internally calculated measure status, but then also must
check payor portals and other HIE-based external clinical documentation to gain the best
picture of “true” gap status. Mismatches between sources create frustration for care teams and,
more importantly, abrasion for caregivers. For example, we may ask a caregiver to see their PCP
for their well-child visit or to complete a social needs screener, when in fact that visit or
screener has already been completed for the measurement period. These barriers erode trust in
the healthcare system.
In order to provide the highest quality preventive care, we encourage CMS to address today's
system of duplicative calculations and divergent data submission criteria between payers and

imaginepediatrics.org
providers via a unified reporting mechanism viewable by all relevant stakeholders. We'd be
excited to contribute data to such a mechanism.
Recommendations:
● Create a unified quality registry accessible to all stakeholders: Implement a real-time
registry where every provider can see a patient's current preventive care status, enabling
gap closure at every encounter [PR-8(c): Registry requirements for real-time data]
● Create real-time FHIR-based quality APIs to augment existing flat file gaps list sharing:
Transition from static file exchanges to dynamic, standardized APIs that provide current
quality gap information [TD-9(e): Bulk FHIR for quality]
● Implement the DaVinci Data Exchange for Quality Measures: Incentivize use of this
FHIR-based standard to ensure consistent data formatting and real-time updates across
all stakeholders [TD-11: Standardized export capabilities]
● Provide financial incentives for unified reporting: Offer enhanced payments to
organizations that contribute real-time quality data to the unified registry [VB-1:
Incentives for digital adoption]
6. Alternative Payment Models Require Clear Regulatory Boundaries for
Technology Innovation
The Challenge: Current regulations create uncertainty about when clinical technologies cross
into population health management decision-making that could be construed as benefiting
some patients over others based on insights from analytics that rely on incomplete underlying
data sets. This regulatory ambiguity chills innovation, as APM participants fear that
implementing capabilities such as AI-driven risk stratification or population health analytics
could trigger additional compliance requirements or litigation. With recent scrutiny and lawsuits
against payers and providers across the industry for various uses of AI, providers in value-based
arrangements need clear regulatory boundaries that provide clarity on clinical care delivery
informed by innovative technologies.
Our Experience: Our value-based care model enables our clinicians to identify children at risk of
hospitalization and intervene proactively. For example, our algorithms flag when a child with
special health care needs shows diagnostic signs of clinical deterioration, allowing us to surge
our efforts to that child through proactive outreach. We have structured AI tools that assess
rising risk to ensure the right care is delivered at the right time to the right patient, across our
whole population. AI tools have the opportunity to identify who needs more support, not less.

imaginepediatrics.org
Recommendations:
● Explicitly clarify that clinical population health management informed by analytics and
innovative technologies is appropriate in APM contexts: CMS should clarify that AI and
analytics tools used for risk stratification, care planning, and population health
management constitute clinical care when used by providers in value-based
arrangements [VB-7: Reducing complexity while preserving flexibility]
● Create a "safe harbor" for APM technology innovations: Establish clear criteria that
exempt clinical decision support tools from utilization management regulations when
they:
○ Are used to enhance care delivery rather than deny services
○ Support proactive interventions rather than retrospective denials
○ Are transparent to patients and their care teams
● Provide technical assistance for APM participants: Help organizations understand the
boundaries between permissible clinical innovation and perceived patient discrimination
activities [TD-1: Stimulating innovation]
● Support a standard healthcare-specific framework for developing and using AI models,
similar to HITRUST and SOC for information security, that would provide stakeholders
with confidence that business associates are using AI responsibly, safely, and with clinical
accuracy.
This regulatory clarity would unleash innovation in value-based care, allowing more
organizations to deploy the technologies that have enabled our success in keeping vulnerable
children healthy at home.
7. General Interoperability Considerations
The Challenge: Despite significant progress in health data exchange, fundamental
interoperability gaps persist that impact daily care delivery. CCDA documents often arrive with
inconsistent formatting and missing critical data elements like upcoming appointments.
Cross-state care coordination remains fragmented, with state-based HIE models creating
barriers for patients seeking specialty care across state lines. Additionally, evolving privacy
concerns post-Dobbs have reignited debates about opt-in versus opt-out consent models,
potentially restricting data availability for care coordination.
Our Experience: As a provider serving children with special health care needs across Texas,
Florida, and the Washington D.C. metro area (with plans to rapidly scale to new states), we

imaginepediatrics.org
encounter these interoperability challenges daily. When receiving CCDAs from various health
systems, we find that critical information is often missing or inconsistently formatted. We are
proud to offer upcoming appointment reminders to families under our care, including reminders
about appointments with external specialists or therapists. Children with special health care
needs routinely see multiple providers, placing a large burden on families to take time away
from work and children out of school. Value-based providers like us could support families by
aligning frequent appointments to the same day and ensure coordinated transportation, but the
burden often falls on the family to accomplish this. Without complete information about
upcoming external appointments, these capabilities remain nascent and fragmented.
Our appointment reminder program is just one of many capabilities that would be enhanced by
deeper and clearer flow of patient data across contexts. Members of our PFAC maintain physical
‘care binders’ as the best way to understand their child’s full health picture, as digital medical
records do not consolidate well across the many specialists our patients encounter throughout
their journey of care. A member of our PFAC recently told us: "Care coordination should not
depend solely on caregiver capacity. We need tools that treat families as partners — not data
entry clerks — and shift part of the system’s responsibility back to those being paid to deliver
care. A collaborative care binder is not just a tech upgrade; it’s a lifeline for caregivers and a
critical piece of the interoperability puzzle."
Each time a child encounters a new provider, caregivers must re-explain years of surgeries,
therapies, and medication changes. This storytelling is time consuming, and with particularly
complex pediatric patients, reliving past medical escalations can be traumatizing for caregivers.
A member of our PFAC who cares for two children with severe behavioral health issues tells us:
"It’s very frustrating to continue to have to repeat the same information. Not only that, it’s
triggering. I have to have my boys with me and my boys have to sit with me and revisit the same
traumas over and over. Coming out of these appointments in the past, the boys have had
episodes specifically because we have had to revisit the past over and over and not just focus on
healing.”
Despite requirements in the ASTP’s certified health IT program via the §170.315(b)(1)
Transitions of care criteria that EHRs should support USCDI v1 in their C-CDA documents and
USCDI v3 in their documents after December 31, 2025. Our experience has been that this is not
the case across providers exchanging information for transitions of care on major networks -
they are consistently lacking in providing the full expected USCDI dataset.

imaginepediatrics.org
The state-based HIE model particularly impacts our most vulnerable patients. Children with rare
conditions often need to see specialists in neighboring states, but state HIE boundaries create
artificial barriers to data sharing. We've found TEFCA and Carequality networks more effective
for cross-state coordination, as they operate on consistent technical standards and governance
frameworks rather than the patchwork of state-specific rules and extremely variable data
capabilities.
Recommendations:
● Enforce USCDI completeness for CCDAs on national networks: Make a requirement of
TEFCA, Carequality, and other national networks that all CCDAs transmitted through
them include all applicable USCDI data elements, with technical validation at the point
of transmission [TD-7: USCDI coverage and compliance]
● Expand CCDA content requirements: Mandate inclusion of upcoming appointments,
care team contact information, and active care plans in all CCDA transmissions [TD-7:
USCDI limitations and improvements]
● Prioritize national frameworks over state-based models: Support TEFCA and
Carequality as primary vehicles for cross-state data exchange, reducing reliance on state
HIE boundaries that don't align with patient care patterns [PC-10: TEFCA advancing
patient access]
● Maintain opt-out consent models for treatment purposes: Preserve the ability to share
data for treatment, payment, and operations without requiring explicit opt-in consent,
which creates barriers to timely care coordination [PC-10]
● Address post-Dobbs privacy concerns without compromising care: Develop clear
guidance that protects sensitive health information while ensuring providers can access
complete medical histories for treatment decisions
These improvements would enable seamless care coordination across state lines and care
settings, ensuring that children with special health care needs receive timely, informed care
regardless of where they seek treatment.
