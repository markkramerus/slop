US Healthcare Current State – Evidence Gap:
While healthcare data is now abundant, it remains siloed and difficult to transform into actionable
evidence. Today, only 14% of daily medical decisions are based on high-quality evidence—a disconnect
known as “The Evidence Gap.” Traditional clinical trials are limited and exclude nearly 70% of real-world
patients, including those with comorbidities, as well as underrepresented populations such as minorities,
children, and seniors, among others.
Generating high-quality insights typically takes months and significant resources, confining the process to
expensive, academic research settings. The current system places significant strain on U.S. healthcare:
many patients do not receive evidence-based care, leading to avoidable adverse outcomes, under or
delayed treatments, and increased costs.

PC-8 I n your experience, what health data is readily available and valuable to patients or their
caregivers or both?
- For patients and caregivers to get the most value out of their health data, having a longitudinal
record inclusive of open and closed claims, EMR data, labs, vitals, registries, and both inpatient
and outpatient encounters is critical for a full picture of a patient's health and care needs. These
datasets are available, including rich clinical and administrative coding—spanning RxNorm, CPT,
ICD-9/10 - however, very few providers have access to these insights in an actionable format
today at the point-of-care.
- Laboratory data such as hemoglobin, A1c, lipid profile, blood pressure is readily available and
has high value to patients and caregivers.
PC-8-A What data is valuable, but hard for patients and caregivers, or app developers and other
technical vendors, to access for appropriate and valuable use (for example, claims data, clinical
data, encounter notes, operative reports, appointment schedules, prices)?
- Impactful health data tracked outside of EHRs from wearables/implanted devices such as
continuous glucose monitor, daily step account, active or move minutes, sleep duration and
quality, nutrition information including alcohol use. These health metrics have a profound impact
on a patient's overall health beyond medications/visits and are sparsely represented into health
models.
- Clinical scales in a variety of conditions such as PHQ2/9 for depression to global functioning and
disease activity scores in dementia, rheumatoid arthritis, or ulcerative colitis are non-discrete and
present in notes.
- Oncology data in pathology, radiology, and oncology reports such as mutation/biomarker status
and tumor size, stage and response to therapy.
PC-B. What are specific sources, other than claims and clinical data, that would be of highest
value, and why?
- Data from patient wearables such as continuous glucose monitor to Apple & Samsung watches,
Oura rings, Fitbits etc. Data from wearables includes a number of parameters such as activity
(total step count, move minutes), heart rate, respiratory rate, estimated oxygen saturation,
abnormal heart rhythms, glucose levels, potential fall (from accelerometers). While wearable
devices do lack their hospital/clinic grade device in some metrics, they have been validated for
use in a variety of important outpatient monitoring scenarios and often give a more complete and
accurate picture of the patient in their usual home setting rather than a point view in a clinic or
during an inpatient stay.
- Data from pathology (biopsies and complex labs outside of complete blood count & routine
chemistries) and radiology reports are part of the clinical record but often present in a format that
is poorly accessible.

PR-7. What strategies can CMS implement to support providers in making high-quality, timely, and
comprehensive healthcare data available for interoperability in the digital product ecosystem?
How can the burden of increasing data availability and sharing be mitigated for providers? Are
there ways that workflows or metrics that providers are already motivated to optimize for that
could be reused for, or combined with, efforts needed to support interoperability?
1. From an engineering and analytics viewpoint, the following actions would help ensure easier
sharing of de-identified healthcare data for RWE/D purposes, while retaining protection of privacy
through valid de-identification.
a. Outline a commonly accepted mathematical formula or definition for actors in the
healthcare system to follow to achieve a “very small” risk of re-identification, and
especially one that could be implemented programmatically and automatically instead of
requiring cumbersome processes of manual assessment.
b. Provide clarity to ecosystem actors that once a set of data has been certified as
de-identified by expert determination, then all subsets or ‘cuts’ of that data are also
considered to be de-identified and do not require recertification or re-analysis.
c. Provide guidance that the addition of derived, non-patient-related fields based on a
lookup to a de-identified dataset should not trigger a recertification or re-analysis.
- For example, if a de-identified dataset contains ZIP-3 information, then the
addition of the fields for State and US Census Region associated with each ZIP-3
should not require a re-analysis of the entire dataset. These fields 1) are of a
higher grain than ZIP-3 2) do not pertain to patients directly and could not contain
PII and 3) are a mechanical lookup based on another field and are not derived
from additional data about patients.
d. Publish or identify an existing free, open-source, and validated PII redaction NLP model
that, if used, should be considered to reduce the amount of PII in free text fields to a level
that it is guaranteed to constitute a “very small” risk of re-identification.
- This would remove the need for very time consuming and expensive validation of
the results of scattered NLP models that are used for this purpose.
- This model could be validated in clinical text taken from a wide variety of
datasets once, and the results published.
- This could act as a “Safe Harbor” method of PII redaction in clinical text.
e. Publishing a validated prompt and a list of accepted LLM models and versions that
achieve the same ends would also work to achieve the same results.
f. Take steps to provide as much reassurance and clarity to healthcare providers that their
good faith attempts to share de-identified data for the purposes of collecting RWE/D do

TD-18. Information blocking: a. Could you, as a technology vendor, provide examples for the types
of practices you have experienced that may constitute information blocking. Please include both
situations of non-responsiveness as well as situations that may cause a failure or unusable
response? b. What additional policies could ASTP/ONC and CMS implement to further discourage
healthcare providers from engaging in information blocking practices? c. Are there specific
categories of healthcare actors covered under the definition of information blocking in section
3022(a)(1) of the Public Health Service Act (PHSA) that lack information blocking disincentives?
Information blocking remains a persistent challenge across healthcare technology, with several patterns
emerging that impede legitimate data access and research efforts. Technology vendors frequently
encounter situations where large healthcare IT systems create artificial barriers to data sharing, often
through overly restrictive interpretation of privacy regulations, unreasonable technical requirements, or
deliberate system design choices that make interoperability difficult. These practices become particularly
problematic when dominant market players use their position to control data flows in ways that serve their
business interests rather than advancing healthcare outcomes or supporting critical research initiatives.
Key examples of information blocking practices include:
● Selective API limitations - Implementing application programming interfaces that provide only
partial data sets or impose restrictive rate limits that make comprehensive data access
impractical, particularly affecting smaller research organizations that lack the resources to
navigate complex technical barriers.
● Proprietary format requirements - Mandating data exports in vendor-specific formats that
require expensive licensing or specialized software to access, creating unnecessary financial
barriers for healthcare providers and researchers seeking to use their own data.
● Excessive security protocols - Implementing authentication and authorization processes that
go beyond reasonable security requirements, creating administrative burdens that effectively
discourage legitimate data sharing while providing legal cover for restrictive practices.
● Non-responsive technical support - Systematically delaying or providing inadequate responses
to interoperability requests, particularly from smaller organizations or those seeking data for
research purposes rather than direct patient care.
● Contractual restrictions - Including terms in licensing agreements that limit how healthcare data
can be used, shared, or analyzed, even when such restrictions exceed regulatory requirements
and impede legitimate research activities.
To address these challenges, ASTP/ONC and CMS should consider developing comprehensive
guidelines that clearly define acceptable interoperability practices, establish standardized response
timeframes for technical support requests, and provide detailed frameworks for what constitutes
reasonable data sharing requirements. Additionally, guidance should explicitly address situations where
market-dominant vendors use their position to restrict data access in ways that limit competition and
innovation in healthcare research and analytics, offering clear pathways for organizations to navigate
these complex relationships while maintaining compliance with legitimate privacy and security
requirements.
