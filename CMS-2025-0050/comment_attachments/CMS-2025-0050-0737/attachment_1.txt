1 General Observations Our organization supports advancing patient access, data transparency, and system interoperability. We emphasize five core guiding principles: • Patient primacy and empowerment: Patients should have full access to their health data and control how it is used. • Comprehensive and performant data access: All data, including structured and unstructured, should be accessible and computable. • Open innovation and participation: systems should accommodate individual and organizational involvement. • Transparent and accountable networks with federal oversight: Networks such as TEFCA should be transparent, patient -centered, and federally governed. • Fostering competition through open and fair market foundations: Public infrastructure should lower barriers to entry and ensure equitable access. Patient Access PC-2: Easy Access to Health Information • Our organization believes that having one portal for a patient to access all of their information should be the gold standard. Trying to manually reconcile data across multiple sources is frustrating and reduces the value of the data to the patient. The value of a specifi c type of data depends on the patient’s health conditions. Also, a single type of data may have little value unless a second type of data is also available. The form in which the data is provided has an impact on its value, e.g., a 300-page PDF of a pat ient’s medical record has little or no value. Tools for accessing the data should go beyond collating and presenting the data to include information to assist patients in interpreting the data. Access and proficiency in using technology is also a barrier for older, low -income, and rural patients. Steward USCDI development to pragmatically expand standardized elements. • Establish a TEFCA Patient -Developer Credential for individuals to aggregate their own health data across sources. PC-5: Encouraging Patient Adoption of Digital Tools CMS should partner with the FDA and ASTP to develop programs to review, assess, and approve digital health products. Efficacy, quality and impact on health outcomes should all be addressed in the approval process. The approval process should have mechani sms to enable small developers to navigate and afford to participate in the approval program, e.g., navigators to support small firms in understanding and meeting the requirements of the approval process and a fee schedule that is adjusted based on firm si ze. We do not support CMS developing its own standalone review program. CMS could take the following actions to encourage patient and caregiver interest in digital health products: • Educate providers on the value of a product including validated health impacts • Incentivize providers to incorporate data from digital health products into their EHRs

In the current environment large and complex process and programs are required to align, validate, and c leanse data from multiple sources. More rigorous and consistent use of terminologies in source systems can reduce the burden of integrating data across sources. This will also reduce barriers to entry for app developers. CMS can further interoperability of digital health products by working with FDA and ASTP to include conformance with data standards as a criterion for approving digital health products and products that support source systems. Product conformance is insufficient alone. Implementers of source systems must invest in resources to ensure that data is accurately represented in the terminologies that they apply. CMS may want to consider having a program that awards stars or rankings of data from source systems. PC-8: Data Valuable to Patients and Caregivers Many if not most EHRs provide patients with access to their records including lab results, prescriptions, visit notes, and appointments. The limitation of this data is that it resides in and must be accessed within each provider’s portal. Making access com plex and time consuming and arraying the data across sources difficult to impossible. As we stated above having a single source to access data across providers has the potential to offer significant benefits to patients, their caregivers, and their provide rs. Claims data will need to be used with some caution since the content of claims is driven by reimbursement policy rather than clinical care requirements. Access to claims data is most value in assisting patients in determining the services they have receive d and costs that have been incurred. The value of other data sources will often be dependent on the health conditions of the patient. Patient generated health data can have high value if it is accurate, e.g., sleeping heart rate. Outside the realm of clinical data an easy access to informatio n on approvals by payers for referrals, medical devices, or procedures can reduce the time and frustration associated with obtaining this information and possibly expedite access to care. Research using clinical, claims, and/or patient generated health data are all challenged by interoperability, data quality, and data access. Data can be exchanged using existing standards, but if the underlying data that is shared is of poor quality or poo rly coded it will be limited in its value. Processes and procedures for obtaining data access vary significantly and add to the complexity and burden of creating the data bases needed by researchers. Developing standardized and repeatable processes and pol icies related to data sharing has the potential to unlock valuable data for research. These barriers inhibit data sharing across government and private sector organizations. As stated in previous responses full implementation of a common set data standards and vocabularies with accurate mapping to local coding systems is fundamental to making high quality data available for clinical and research uses. 
3 PC-10: TEFCA and Patient Access TEFCA’s role in supporting patient -initiated access is still emerging and would benefit from further development. Key Recommendations: • Provide patients with transparency, control, and visibility into TEFCA data sharing. • Create a TEFCA Patient -Developer Credential for direct, secure patient -initiated data aggregation. • Promote a trustworthy, accountable architecture for individual access services. • Broaden TEFCA’s scope to progressively include full EHI in addition to USCDI. PC-14: Digital Identity Credentials High-assurance digital identity (e.g., IAL2) is essential for secure and streamlined access but must be coupled with strong authorization credentials. Key Recommendations: • CMS/ONC should mandate CEHRT support for IAL2 -based credentialing. • Providers and payers should offer IAL2 login options. • App developers should integrate IAL2 support. • Leverage identity frameworks to support broader policy recommendations, including Patient -Developer Credentials and trustworthy IAS architecture. Providers PR-3: Importance of Comprehensive EHR Data Exchange Complete access to all EHR data (structured, unstructured, scanned documents, images, notes) is essential to ensure safe, effective patient care. Key Recommendations: • Promote API-accessible, computable full EHI export aligned with industry specifications to overcome format -based barriers. Payers PA-1: TEFCA Limitations for Payers TEFCA should continue to evolve toward: • Greater individual control and transparency. • Lowered innovation barriers for patient -facing tools. • Robust identity verification and authorization architecture. • Full EHI scope inclusion. • Establishment of public foundational infrastructure. PA-4: National Provider Directory A nationwide, free, API -accessible directory with credentialing and endpoint information would: • Streamline provider data management. • Enhance interoperability for prior auth and quality reporting. • Improve value -based care operations and network management. 
4 Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks TD-1: Stimulating Developer Interest Short-Term Priorities (2 years): • Promote computable full EHI export via standardized APIs. • Advance bulk data and single -patient FHIR APIs. • Pilot the TEFCA Patient -Developer Credential. • Establish public foundational discovery infrastructure. Long-Term Priorities: • Embed patient control into TEFCA. • Expand API certification (CDS Hooks, FHIR Subscriptions, Bulk Data API performance). • Shift from gatekeepers to open standardized interfaces. TD-5: Provider Directory Benefits The costs of a nationwide provider directory should be guided by the principle that patient financial resources should not be a barrier to using the provider directory. For this reason we recommend that the costs of the provider directory should be borne by organizations with the financial resources and associated benefits to enable them to bear the costs. TD- 6: TEFCA’s Unique Role • LOINC and the SNOMED vocabularies are complementary standards. To enable developer and users to optimize these vocabularies the steward s for LOINC ® and SNOMED CT® are working together to develop an ontology that that integrates SNOMED -CT and LOINC into a single solution . This initiative will enable users of SNOMED to more easily integrate LOINC codes into their applications. Funding and support for this work would accelerate progress and further interoperability. TD-7: USCDI’s Role and Limitations USCDI in its current form is limited by the time lag between updates to USCDI and the timeframe for adoption. This often leads to the implementation of standards that have advanced and improved beyond those included in the USCDI regulation. We recommend that USCDI require that implementers use the most current versions of standards that are available at the time that implementation is required. TD-10: Cures Act API Requirements Key Recommendation: Promote computable, API -based full EHI export as the primary mechanism for fulfilling the Cures Act’s "without special effort" requirement. Supporting elements include: • Modern SMART App Launch and Backend Services. • Self-service electronic EHI request initiation. • Programmatic access to medical images. • Secure messaging APIs. • Electronic pathways for patient -initiated amendment requests.
