Re: Request for Information—Health Technology Ecosystem (RFI 2025-08701)

We appreciate the opportunity to respond to CMS and ASTP/ONC's Request for Information on
the health technology ecosystem, digital health, and interoperability. Our comments are focused
on specifically addressing macro-structural reform, payment, certification, and Al-enabied
interoperability. As a leading EHR vendor and clinical workflow solution serving independent
primary care organizations nationwide. including 36,000 clinical users caring for 20 million
patients, Elation Health is uniquely positioned to provide insights and recommendations that
marry frontline clinical experience with technical expertise and a deep understanding of policy
and financial incentives.

Our recommendations to CMS for transforming the health technology ecosystem focus on
aligning incentives to create market demand, reforming certification, streamlining quality
reporting through administrative simplicity, modernizing interoperability to support innovation,
and, most importantly, empowering primary care. The current regulatory approach fails to align
with genuine clinical needs—especially the needs of primary care. The following comments
synthesize our views:

Reform Market Incentives through Primary Care and Value-Based Models

The foundational problem of the current technology ecosystem is not technical but incentive
misalignment. Technological progress alone will not solve data exchange and interoperability
deficits. The real barrier is misaligned market incentives. especially payment structures that fail
to reward longitudinal, coordinated, whole-person primary care and instead incentivize a system
which benefits from an unhealthy population. Technology, no matter how sophisticated, won't
yield meaningful information exchange uniess there's a true market demand for interoperability.
The most powerful lever CMS controls is payment, and payment reform, particularly for primary
care. is the critical lever CMS can pull to promote the kind of robust interoperability that
underpins overall health improvement at a lower total cost of care.

Certification Reform and Outcome Focus

Today's CEHRT requirements mandate many low-value features (e.g., clock synchronization),
consuming vast vendor resources for elements that clinicians do not demand. The result is an
ecosystem where real interoperability lags, and true clinical or customer needs are deprioritized.
CMS should reframe certification to prioritize end-to-end utility for clinicians—delivering what
matters most for care coordination and patient outcomes—rather than focusing on technical
features without clear specific use cases.

Recommendations:

e Shift certification from prescriptive, technical checklists to clear, outcome-based
mandates that focus on real-world, functional data interoperability.

e Discard requirements that would be “market-corrected” (e.g., basic system functions
whose failure would render products unsellable).

e Consider certifying the real-world performance and reliability of APIs for end-to-end,
high-value clinical use cases (e.g., successful referrals), not just theoretical availability of
data.

This would align market incentives, empowering vendors and clinicians to strive for and be
recognized for tangible advances in interoperability and care coordination, not just compliance
and certification.

Modernize interoperability Standards: Al, FHIR, Proprietary APis

Al is reshaping the conversation about standards fast, and the debate between FHIR and
proprietary APIs is quickly losing relevance. While the FHIR standard remains an important
“minimum viable” baseline and proprietary APIs support more complex use cases, advances in
Al are flattening the differences. Soon, Ai will serve as an instant translator across standards,
creating structured actionable data out of “blobs of text”. True interoperability can then focus
less on rigid adherence to a single standard and more on simply ensuring the fnctionless
exchange of rich data - expanding the horizon for deeper interoperability and more innovative
care tools. CMS should provide regulatory fiexibility that supports innovation in interoperability.
Standardized FHIR endpoints for regulatory reporting, such as quality measurement as
described above, along with Al to translate clinical data to FHIR endpoints, remains an
important pathway to reducing burdensome variation in documentation and reporting
requirements at the point of care, reducing waste and freeing clinicians to promote health and
tackle chronic disease.

Recommendations:

e Recognize the accelerating ability of Al to translate between standards and to structure

Reduce Red Tape, Clarify Liability, and Improve Digital Identity

Administrative friction—fear of liability, endiess paperwork, and confusion around
consent—slows genuine data sharing and is even being used to create proprietary moats
around technology and delivery systems. CMS can make a tangible difference by explicitly

Recommendations:

e Streamline, clarify, standardize, and enforce regulations on patient consent, identity
verification, and liability.

e Invest in provider and stakeholder education to ensure rules are well understood and
friction is minimized.

Conclusion

The greatest opportunities for U.S. health technology policy lie in aligning certification and
payment incentives with genuine clinical needs—especially the needs of primary care. The
current system-wide failures are not technology failures, but failures of economic alignment and
systemic incentive fragmentation. The way CMS incentivizes care delivery is as
important—perhaps more important—than how it regulates technologies. To get to a primary
care—driven, health-promoting future. CMS must lead with incentives aligned for longitudinal,
patient-centered care. Certification and technology policy should follow this north star, shedding
distractions that do not serve clinical outcomes, and industry will respond to meet that demand.
