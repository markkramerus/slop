I. Executive Summary KuduCare functions as a digital health product in several categories defined by CMS: - As a **health management app**, it supports patient and caregiver behavior tracking, adherence logging, and real -time triage. - As a **care navigation tool**, it enables providers to respond to behavioral risk events between visits, preventing unnecessary escalation. - As a **personal health record extension**, it collects structured logs and symptom reports from both patient and caregiver, viewable within RTM dashboards. 
KuduCare is a digital health company advancing a CMS -aligned Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) platform that supports Remote Therapeutic Monitoring (RTM), behavioral coaching, and caregiver engagement across chronic and mental health conditions. Our core innovation is dyad -centered care: enabling scalable support for both Medicare patients and the family caregivers who shoulder the burden of home - based care —without increasing entitlement costs or requiring new legislation. While CMS has expanded reimbursement through RTM and Chronic Care Management (CCM), the tools and guidance to make these programs scalable, caregiver -inclusive, and digitally streamlined remain fragmented. KuduCare bridges this gap with a Class I, audit - ready platform that integrates structured behavioral nudges, CBT -informed self - management, caregiver input, and clinical oversight —all mapped to existing CPT codes. We are submitting this response because current CMS rules and legacy fraud safeguards inadvertently block broader adoption of these innovations. Providers face legal ambiguity, documentation burden, and billing uncertainty when trying to support caregivers —even when it prevents deterioration and reduces system costs. KuduCare offers CMS a low -risk path to accelerate value -based care delivery. Our model uses: • No new entitlements – RTM, CCM, and BHI codes already exist • No additional staffing mandates – General supervision and automation via SaMD • No fraud risk expansion – Built -in compliance safeguards and caregiver identity protocols The model addresses three major goals CMS outlined in this RFI: 1. Modernize fraud and abuse frameworks – by defining caregiver engagement and dyadic monitoring as legitimate, trackable uses of RTM 2. Enable scalable digital tools – by encouraging interoperability, SMART -on-FHIR certification, and caregiver access through identity delegation 3. Lower system cost while improving outcomes – by reducing ER visits and avoidable admissions through caregiver education, behavior tracking, and early escalation 
This is not a request for more government —it is a roadmap to modernize what already exists. With thoughtful regulatory clarification, CMS can unleash a wave of private innovation that finally supports how care is delivered in real life: not just to patients, but to families. II. In unanswered questions, deteriorating mental health, and a creeping sense of failure. What we needed —what millions of caregivers still need —is not heroic intervention, but something quieter and more sustainable: • A way to monitor how things are going without having to guess • A channel to escalate concern without calling 911 • Reassurance that someone else is watching, too KuduCare was born out of that need. It’s not just a software tool. It’s a practical model that empowers providers to get paid for what they’ve already been doing informally — and for what families desperately need them to do more intentionally. It’s our way of turning experience into infrastructure. III. Human Impact: James, Rosa, and Dr. Patel James is 76. Regulatory and Payment Barriers While CMS has taken meaningful steps to support remote care through codes like RTM, CCM, and BHI, the current regulatory framework still presents three major barriers to widespread adoption —especially for solutions that center around the patient -caregiver dyad. 1. Fraud and Abuse Ambiguity (Anti -Kickback + Beneficiary Inducement) Providers are uncertain whether engaging caregivers through digital tools, education modules, or feedback loops could trigger regulatory scrutiny —even when these tools are part of the patient's care plan and supervised under RTM or CCM. The lack of clarity discourages innovation and deters even compliant, medically beneficial uses of SaMD platforms. 
✅ Policy need: CMS should confirm that caregiver -facing features within RTM -eligible software are allowable when the service supports the patient’s treatment plan and meets supervision requirements. 2. Undefined Role of the Caregiver in Reimbursable Care Caregivers often provide critical context, support behavior change, and enable adherence —but current RTM definitions don’t formally include them. As a result, clinicians hesitate to document or act on caregiver input, and SaMD platforms built for family -centered care operate in a legal grey area. ✅ Policy need: CMS should explicitly state that caregiver engagement is permissible within RTM and CCM workflows when: • The caregiver is acting on behalf of the patient • The caregiver’s actions are supervised or reviewed by the billing provider • The software and documentation link caregiver actions to patient outcomes 3. Device Classification + Documentation Ambiguity CMS states that RTM must use a medical device, but many digital tools offering CBT - informed coaching, journaling, and behavioral prompts fall into a gray zone between “monitoring” and “treatment.” This creates unnecessary uncertainty for SaMD developers and providers alike. ✅ Policy need: CMS should reaffirm that: • Class I SaMD tools that pass general controls and facilitate RTM -eligible monitoring are sufficient • Documentation can include digital activity logs, patient/caregiver -reported outcomes, and structured behavioral feedback • RTM is not limited to physical devices or sensors 
V. Policy Recommendations To reduce legal uncertainty, expand equitable access, and accelerate the use of Remote Therapeutic Monitoring (RTM) and Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) for Medicare patients and caregivers, we respectfully submit the following recommendations. Each is actionable under CMS’s current authority. Clarify What’s Reimbursable 1. Affirm that CBT -informed behavioral coaching and self -management (e.g., breathing techniques, journaling, stress tracking) delivered via SaMD may qualify under RTM when linked to a chronic condition. 2. Clarify that RTM programs can include caregiver -facing tools (e.g., structured feedback logs, alerts, or education modules) when the caregiver is supporting the patient’s plan of care under general supervision. 3. Specify that a formal DSM -5 diagnosis is not required for behavioral -focused RTM if the intervention is targeting symptom management and engagement in a chronic disease context (e.g., anxiety with COPD). 4. Reaffirm that SaMD platforms are valid “medical devices” for RTM purposes when they meet Class I general controls, support monitoring, and escalate to treatment only through clinical review. Simplify Documentation and Supervision 5. Publish sample documentation templates showing how to record time spent reviewing RTM data, caregiver input, or coaching logs —especially in primary care and mental health. 6. Affirm that general supervision applies to RTM behavioral coaching and software -facilitated check -ins across all qualified specialties (e.g., PCPs, PTs, mental health), including use of auxiliary staff or third -party partners. 7. Enable caregivers to participate under "incident to" structures, provided documentation shows they are supporting patient adherence and care continuity. 
 Promote Dyad -Centered Interoperability 8. Encourage RTM and CCM vendors to support SMART -on-FHIR integration for caregiver - and patient -facing modules, improving usability and audit -readiness. 9. Define caregiver digital identity standards that align with TEFCA and Blue Button 2.0 for secure access, consent, and communication between providers and caregivers. 10. Allow caregiver -reported observations to populate RTM dashboards as part of legitimate medical monitoring under provider supervision. ✅ Summary None of these recommendations require new legislation. Each represents a clarification of current codes, authority, and intent —and they would remove unnecessary friction that currently blocks scalable, caregiver -inclusive care. To support future regulatory evaluation and reimbursement modeling, CMS could pilot a **Real -World Evidence (RWE)** reporting framework for SaMD -enabled RTM platforms. Metrics may include: - ER visit avoidance - Days at home following hospitalization - Caregiver -reported burden reduction - Behavioral task completion rate (via SaMD logs) This would allow CMS to assess the comparative effectiveness of digital interventions across patient subtypes without requiring full rulemaking.

Enabling PCPs to Address Behavioral Health Through RTM —Without Requiring Referral or DSM Diagnosis Primary care physicians (PCPs) and general practitioners (GPs) are often the first —and sometimes only —touchpoint for patients struggling with behavioral health concerns linked to chronic conditions. Yet under current CMS billing rules and legacy care models, these providers face a confusing reality: • They can prescribe anti -anxiety medication, • They can document depression symptoms, • But they often feel constrained or uncertain about using RTM or BHI to support behavioral change unless a formal DSM -5 diagnosis is made or a referral to a mental health provider is initiated. This deters action, delays support, and increases avoidable events. 
🧠 What RTM Enables That PCPs Already Do PCPs regularly guide patients through behavior -linked management: • "Track how your breathing changes with stress." • "Keep a log of when you skip your meds." • "Let your daughter tell me what she’s seeing at home." These activities are perfectly aligned with RTM —but are underutilized due to fear of billing errors or audit risk. ✅ What CMS Can Do 1. Clarify that a DSM diagnosis is not required to use RTM for behavioral reinforcement tied to chronic conditions (e.g., anxiety with COPD, depression linked to diabetes). 2. Affirm that primary care clinicians can deliver CBT -informed self -management coaching (e.g., breathing, journaling, reflection prompts) using SaMD tools under general supervision. 3. Promote RTM as a non -stigmatizing, early -intervention path for behavioral support in patients who are not yet ready —or clinically appropriate —for psychotherapy. 4. Encourage EHR macro templates that link common chronic disease symptoms with behavioral patterns and prequalify patients for RTM eligibility. 🌱 Why This Matters Many patients, particularly seniors, are hesitant to engage in formal mental health treatment. But they’ll talk to their PCP. They’ll use a breathing app. They’ll track how stress affects their sleep or breathing. If CMS affirms PCPs can lead that work under RTM, it opens scalable access to behavioral care —without expanding entitlements, increasing stigma, or waiting for specialty referrals. 
XI. Clarifying RTM as a Behavioral Monitoring vs. Treatment Tool for Mental Health Support As RTM evolves to support behavioral health use cases, CMS and providers must distinguish between monitoring and treatment —not just for regulatory classification, but for billing clarity, SaMD compliance, and patient safety. Today, many RTM platforms offer CBT -informed content: • Journaling prompts • Mood or symptom trackers • Breathing exercises • Thought reframing techniques These tools do not provide autonomous diagnosis or clinical treatment, but they do promote behavior change and early intervention. Without clear boundaries, providers fear misclassification, and SaMD developers hesitate to innovate. 🛠️ What CMS Can Clarify 1. RTM is for monitoring —not treatment —when: o The tool tracks adherence or response to a care plan o The platform does not independently diagnose or prescribe o Escalation decisions remain with a qualified provider 2. Class I SaMD tools delivering behavioral nudges and symptom logs fall within RTM scope when: o Supervision is provided by a qualified billing provider o The purpose is behavior monitoring, not psychotherapy o Clinical decisions are based on reviewed data, not software automation 3. Documented use of structured digital content (CBT -based or otherwise) qualifies under RTM if: o It supports a chronic condition care plan 
o Time spent reviewing logs or triaging results is captured o The SaMD facilitates, but does not replace, clinical oversight ⚖️ Why It Matters This clarification protects CMS against fraud, ensures SaMD compliance, and gives providers confidence to deliver early -stage behavioral support at scale. It also ensures that behavioral RTM use does not inappropriately encroach on psychotherapy codes, while still enabling meaningful engagement for mild -to-moderate mental health needs. Most importantly, it reinforces CMS’s commitment to modern digital tools, clinical accountability, and flexibility without confusion. We also recommend that CMS explicitly encourage the use of trusted digital identity providers -such as Login.gov, ID.me, and NIST 800 -63-3 IAL2/AAL2 credentialing services - to facilitate caregiver access to patient -approved SaMD platforms and RTM data. These identity standards will enable scalable delegated access and maintain data integrity across care navigation, documentation, and billing workflows. To further support transparency and digital equity, CMS should increase awareness and ease of use of the ONC Information Blocking Portal. Patients and caregivers should be able to quickly report blocked access to digital records when acting in compliance with TEFCA or delegated credentials. CMS could consider a streamlined caregiver -specific complaint path to increase appropriate reporting. XII. Supporting Interoperability for Dyad -Centered Care While CMS continues to lead on digital modernization through Blue Button, TEFCA, and FHIR standards, caregiver interoperability remains underdeveloped. Most interoperability efforts are designed for providers and patients —not for the people who are actively managing care at home. As family caregivers take on more medical responsibilities, including medication administration, behavioral monitoring, and care escalation, CMS must ensure that the tools and standards surrounding digital health explicitly support the dyad . 
 Recommended CMS Actions for Interoperable Dyadic Engagement 1. Caregiver Access Standards Establish caregiver -specific identity, access, and consent protocols under Blue Button 2.0 or TEFCA to allow secure, delegated caregiver access to SaMD platforms and RTM logs. This ensures privacy compliance without blocking real - world care support. 2. Bidirectional Dyad Data Feeds Encourage SaMD vendors to support structured caregiver -reported inputs (e.g., observed distress, skipped medications, environmental stressors) that flow into provider RTM dashboards via FHIR APIs —under provider supervision. 3. Cross -Specialty RTM Plan Integration Promote or require RTM SaMD platforms to support unified care plans across specialties (e.g., PCP, mental health, pulmonology). This avoids duplicative data entry and improves care coordination for patients with overlapping chronic and behavioral conditions. 4. SMART -on-FHIR Certification for Dyadic Usability Support certification pathways or incentives for RTM platforms that meet: o SMART -on-FHIR integration o Dyad -specific UX standards (multi -user logins, caregiver alerts) o Plain language design and accessibility for Medicare populations Why It Matters Without caregiver integration, digital health remains incomplete. The dyad is the true unit of care for millions of Medicare households. Ensuring caregivers can securely engage, report, and receive feedback is not just a technology upgrade —it’s an equity and outcomes imperative. These changes require no legislative authority —just intentional design and policy alignment. CMS can lead this evolution by making sure the modern interoperability conversation includes the very people holding care together between visits.
