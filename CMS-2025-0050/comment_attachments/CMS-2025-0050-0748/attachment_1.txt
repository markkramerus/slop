Patients and Caregivers
• PC-5. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in digital
health products?
Patients are most likely to be receptive to digital health products adopted and/or recommended
by a trusted clinician.
CMS and its partners can facilitate broader interest in, and appropriate use of, digital health
solutions by eliminating obstacles to accountable care across payer types—allowing providers to
embrace high-quality, low-cost interventions across their patient population—and by ensuring
seamless integration of digital health data into clinical workflows so that patients and caregivers
can see and appreciate the value of digital health products at the point of care.
Fee-for-service payment models and misaligned incentives undermine the shift to a patient-
centered digital health ecosystem. Some payers will pay for digital health, some will not. These
factors have a direct impact on both patient and provider uptake. Payers should be mandated to
support increased healthcare access via a digital health solution to enable a cohesive patient and
provider experience and help to facilitate a national, cultural shift toward tech-enabled patient
empowerment.
• PC-6. What features are most important to make digital health products accessible and easy to
use for Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers, particularly those with limited prior experience
using digital tools and services?
Core attributes of a more person-centered digital health care ecosystem should include:
― User experience and design that accommodates diverse patient needs, including digital
literacy and culturally competent care.
― Tools that support shared decision-making, longitudinal care planning, and meaningful
patient empowerment.
― Integration of medical, behavioral, and social data to support care for the whole person,
rather than episodic, condition-focused interventions.
― Integration of clinical, coverage, cost, and quality information to equip patients with the full
picture of their care options to make informed decisions.
― Abilities to support patients and providers in proactively understanding and managing one’s
health to prevent illness, as well as support navigating and accessing care options when
needs arise.
― A person-centered digital health care ecosystem would not be person-centered if individuals
are not the true owners of their health data. A patient’s health information should be easily
portable throughout their own lifelong health journey, across insurers, states, and settings of
care.

• PC-9. Given that the Blue Button 2.0 API only includes basic patient demographic, Medicare
coverage, and claims data (Part A, B, D), what additional CMS data sources do developers view
as most valuable for inclusion in the API to enable more useful digital products for patients and
caretakers?
A. What difficulties are there in accessing or utilizing these data sources today?
The primary difficulty today is that access requires an OAuth sign-in from the patient. The
target population can find it confusing to complete and understand the entire process, from
creating a CMS Login then logging into a partner website with the CMS credentials.
B. What suggestions do you have to improve the Blue Button 2.0 API experience?
An easier way to log in or access the data as well as population-level access (e.g., DPC).
C. Is there non-CMS data that should be included in the API?
Upcoming appointments, medication adherence data (e.g., prescription fill rates), Medicare
rider plan information, social resource ecosystem.
Providers
• PR-2. What are obstacles that prevent development, deployment, or effective utilization of the
most useful and innovative applications for physician workflows, such as quality measurement
reporting, clinical documentation, and billing tasks? How could these obstacles be mitigated?
Today, most digital tools are not well integrated into clinical workflows, leading to clinician
frustration and underutilization. Obstacles are largely the result of business practices rather than
technical limitations.
Many digital health solutions operate as overlays or “bolt-ons” to EHRs based on limits to third-
party vendors, increasing complexity. EHRs limit or block a third-party vendor from building
applications the EHR vendor determines could be a competing product.
Financial barriers—such as charging providers to access or generate specific data reports within
EHRs—disproportionately impact smaller groups and should be addressed through stronger
interoperability and Information Blocking enforcement.
CMS can support burden reduction by certifying a broader ecosystem of interoperable tools and
explicitly addressing business practices that create artificial friction. Improved API
infrastructure—including advancing open APIs and improved API certification—and more flexible
health IT certification requirements could enable better workflow integration.
• PR-8. What are ways CMS or partners can help with simplifying clinical quality data
responsibilities of providers?
Organizations struggle with overlapping, inconsistent, or evolving quality reporting requirements
across CMS programs, state Medicaid, and commercial payers. This burdens providers and
complicates measurement-driven improvement.
3

To simplify clinical quality reporting, CMS should align measure requirements and data formats
across programs such as MIPS, MSSP, and MA Stars.
Additionally, greater flexibility in accepted data sources and submission formats—beyond CEHRT
and QRDA—would reduce administrative burden, particularly for smaller or under-resourced
providers.
Supporting trusted intermediaries and establishing a vendor certification pathway could
streamline reporting and accelerate adoption of digital quality measures.
Technology Vendors, Data Providers, and Networks
• TD-4. How can CMS better encourage use of open, standards-based, publicly available APIs
over proprietary APIs?
CMS should fund multi-stakeholder FHIR grants for state-based or program-focused projects that
involve multiple payers, providers, and digital health groups to implement FHIR APIs from rules
like CMS-0057-F, to increase adoption.
Create a centralized public resource and/or leverage industry working groups to offer open-
source tools, testing sandboxes, implementation case studies, and a forum for sharing thinking
and development of FHIR APIs.
Consider financial incentives for high-volume and open APIs and enforce clear penalties for failing
to meet API mandates.
Value-Based Organizations
• VB-1. What incentives could encourage APMs such as accountable care organizations (ACOs) or
participants in Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) to leverage digital health management
and care navigation products more often and more effectively with their patients? What are the
current obstacles preventing broader digital product adoption for patients in ACOs?
Addressing the barriers to interoperability and workflow integration described in earlier responses
would also serve ACO and other APM participants to better leverage digital health solutions.
For ACO-specific use cases:
― Care navigation products should be able to help beneficiaries to identify which providers
are working collaboratively to coordinate their care (i.e., ACO participants), while still clearly
indicating that beneficiaries have a choice. In addition to transparency in cost and quality
information, navigation tools should better support patients in understanding which
providers are accountable for their health. This will also help to encourage value-oriented
competition.
― Digital health management tools for chronic care are more frequently adopted by ACO
participants. Increasingly, ACOs are seeking digital health vendors who are willing to enter
into value-based arrangements in which a portion of the vendor’s fees are tied to outcomes.
CMS, likely via the Innovation Center (CMMI), could better support ACOs and/or other APM

participants to better navigate these novel downstream risk-bearing arrangements by
providing contracting guidance, data for performance monitoring, learning systems, etc. This
would also allow CMMI to more systematically inventory and measure digital health
interventions used within their models.
Current obstacles preventing broader digital product adoption for patients in ACOs include
beneficiary co-pays for some services supported by digital tools, cash flow constraints inherent to
FFS-based shared savings models that limit provider investment in digital health infrastructure
without a clear and immediate ROI, as well as having a limited portion of their patient population
under value-based arrangements with total cost of care accountability. Without multi-payer
alignment, provider organizations cannot reach the “tipping point” of truly transforming clinical
care pathways, operations, and culture to embrace tech-enabled care at scale. CMS, CMMI and
other government agencies must use their authority and influence to finally achieve multi-payer
adoption of accountable care.
• VB-11. What specific interoperability challenges have you encountered in implementing value-
based care programs?
While some progress has been made, most systems still struggle with seamless data exchange
across EHR vendors and organizational boundaries, impeding real-time information sharing.
Proprietary EHR systems, inconsistent use of national standards, like FHIR, and lack of universal
technical specifications contribute to persistent data silos and hinder coordination across the
continuum. Behavioral health and community-based organizations—important stakeholders for
value-based care—frequently lack access to core interoperability infrastructure, creating blind
spots in whole-person care.
Many health systems are hesitant to open data access due to concerns over revenue loss in
competitive markets, even when participating in value-based arrangements.
Similarly, even when engaged in value-based arrangements, many payers fail to provide
participating practices and delivery entities with the necessary data. Essential information should
include, at a minimum, accurate and comprehensive list of attributed patients, patient-level
risk scores, information on cost and quality outcomes to inform high-value specialty
referrals (e.g., raw claims feeds, prepared reports), and VBP contract-specific performance
information (e.g., financial reporting reconciliations, status on performance relative to key targets
on quality, etc.). Critically, these essential data should be provided in a timely manner and a
usable format for ingestion and analysis. Far too often, payers who are holding providers
accountable for cost and quality outcomes do not equip providers with the information required
to be successful.
Where CMS has an ability to directly or indirectly influence payer behavior, public and private
health plans must be expected to modernize and invest in data sharing capabilities that enable
success under accountable care. Standard format across all payers should be mandated.
