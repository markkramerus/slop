Unreliability of Manual Respiratory Rate Measurements in Hospitalized Patients The accuracy of respiratory rate measurements in hospitalized patients is generally poor, with substantial evidence of both systematic and random error. Multiple studies demonstrate that manual respiratory rate (RR) measurements by healthcare professionals are frequently inaccurate, often due to estimation, value bias (notably clustering at 18 or 20 breaths per minute), and use of shortcut techniques such as counting for less than 60 seconds and multiplying, which increases error and reduces detection of abnormal rates.[1-6] Quantitatively, only about 36–58% of manual RR measurements fall within ±2 breaths per minute of a gold-standard measurement, even after targeted interventions to improve accuracy.[7] Interobserver agreement is moderate at best (intraclass correlation coefficient [ICC] ≈ 0.64), and 78% of measurements are within 4 breaths per minute of the true rate, but this margin is clinically significant for many scoring systems.[1] The use of shorter observation periods (e.g., 15 or 30 seconds) further reduces accuracy, with limits of agreement as wide as ±7 breaths per minute compared to a full 60-second count.[5] Continuous electronic monitoring (e.g., via capnography or wearable sensors) shows better agreement with reference standards, with mean differences typically <3 breaths per minute and narrower limits of agreement, but these technologies are not yet universally implemented.[8-10] In summary, manual respiratory rate measurements in hospitalized patients are frequently inaccurate and subject to both systematic and random error, which can impact clinical decision-making and early warning scores.[1][7][8-10] 1. Relying on inaccurate respiratory rate measurements in hospitalized patients can lead to missed or delayed recognition of clinical deterioration, inappropriate risk stratification, and misclassification within early warning and sepsis screening systems. Manual respiratory rate errors—both systematic (such as value bias and clustering at 18 or 20 breaths per minute) and random—result in both underestimation and overestimation of abnormal respiratory rates, directly impacting the sensitivity and specificity of clinical prediction rules such as SIRS, qSOFA, NEWS, and MEWS. This can cause delays in escalation of care for deteriorating patients or unnecessary interventions for those incorrectly identified as high risk.[1-5] For example, inaccurate respiratory rate measurement can result in failure to identify tachypnea, a key criterion for sepsis and early warning scores, thereby delaying sepsis recognition and treatment. Conversely, overestimation may trigger false alarms, leading to alarm fatigue and unnecessary resource utilization.[2-4] Studies have shown that up to 37% of early warning score calculations are affected by respiratory rate measurement errors, with both under- and overestimation occurring.[2]Furthermore, inaccurate respiratory rate 
documentation persists even in patients with cardiopulmonary compromise or impending ICU transfer, undermining the reliability of vital sign–based surveillance.[3][5] In summary, the clinical consequences of inaccurate respiratory rate measurement include delayed or missed detection of patient deterioration, inappropriate triage, and compromised patient safety.[1-5] 1.
