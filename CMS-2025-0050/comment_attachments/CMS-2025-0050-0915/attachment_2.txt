As a patient advocate for over 25 years, each day I work with patients and their families as they navigate life-altering, life-limiting diagnoses, such as cancer, while navigating our complex healthcare system. Each day, I connect patients and their loved ones to the information, tools, and technology they need to make informed decisions about their care. What features are missing from apps you use or that you are aware of today? •Interoperability that actually works- These tools rarely talk to each other. A patient’s medication list might live in one app, advance directives in a Dropbox folder, appointments in a paper calendar and/or patient portal, and lab results in four different portals. The burden of weaving it all together falls squarely on the shoulders of the patient or caregiver. •UX design for aging populations- Too many apps are designed by and for tech-savvy professionals, not for people living with cognitive decline, visual impairment, tremors, or limited data plans. Accessibility and simplicity need to be built in from the start and co- created with patients and their carepartners. •Support for carepartners and caregivers- Most apps do not account for the fact that many Medicare beneficiaries rely on adult children, spouses, or other carepartners and caregivers to help coordinate their care. Role-based access and shared task management needs to be standard. •Contextual relevance- We need tools that understand the full context of patient’s life’s circumstances: what treatments is someone is on, what coverage do they have, what do they value, and what are they’re experiencing day to day. Think of the Waze driving app and how it automatically reroutes a driver when delays are detected, hazards are reported, or speed traps are flagged. a. What apps should exist but do not yet? Why do you believe they do not exist yet? In general, the apps that are missing are the ones that would shift power toward patients, carepartners, and caregivers, reduce unnecessary suffering, and center human realities over institutional convenience. They don’t exist yet not because we don’t have the technology, but rather because we lack the incentives to rewire where value accrues. We need policy, funding models, and ethical frameworks that support the development of tools people actually need, not just what is reimbursed, and protect against exploitative ones that masquerade as help while extracting value and data from the most vulnerable and needy.
1. A true, portable, longitudinal health record owned and controlled by the person. We should have one digital home where every piece of our health data—EHRs, lab results, imaging, genomics, meds, device data, social determinants, preferences, and advance care planning, etc., resides together. It should move with us across providers, payers, and time. It should be patient-directed, not provider-constrained. Why it doesn’t exist yet: Profit incentives still sit with data holders, not data generators. EHR vendors, health systems, and payers all benefit from information asymmetry. CMS, ONC, and HHS have made progress on interoperability rules, but the resistance is deeply entrenched. Until patients are recognized as rightful stewards of their data, this will remain a patchwork. 2. Waze for healthcare: An AI-powered care navigation app that doesn’t just schedule appointments but guides real-life decision-making across treatment, finances, and logistics. Imagine an assistant that could surface treatment options, help weigh them against personal goals and insurance coverage, sync appointments around fatigue and caregiver availability, and flag duplicative tests or unfilled prescriptions. This is what patients and caregivers actually need. Why it doesn’t exist yet: It’s technically feasible, but there’s no aligned financial model. Navigation is considered “non- reimbursable” or “non-essential” by many payers, despite being the connective tissue of the care experience. Patients and families are routinely turning to ChatGPT and other open access LLMs to navigate the current fragmented system. Also, many tools in this space are locked into enterprise contracts with health systems or payers, not designed for or accessible to individual people. 3. A consent management tool that lets people see, grant, and revoke data-sharing permissions across all their health interactions. Think of it as a digital privacy command center, like what we have for app permissions on a phone, but for health data, AI models, clinical trials, and third-party analytics tools. Why it doesn’t exist yet: Because our system was never built for patients to actually own their choices. HIPAA’s minimum necessary rule is misused, consent forms are buried, and most apps don’t give patients personalized, segmented, granular control. This kind of transparency is viewed as a liability by entrenched players, not a right. 4. A caregiver coordination platform that enables shared tasking, secure communication, and collaborative documentation across family members and clinicians. Being a carepartner and caregiver is sometimes more than a full-time job. We need a tool that centralizes appointments, to-dos, medication changes, home care observations, and care team communications in one place, accessible by whoever the patient designates.
Why it doesn’t exist yet: The caregiver is still largely invisible in our system. They’re not consistently documented in the medical record, let alone given access. Most HIPAA workflows are built to gatekeep, not empower. And caregiving is grossly undervalued in our reimbursement models. 5. A side effect and symptom management app that actually does something. There are apps that let people track their pain, nausea, fatigue, et but very few that help us understand patterns, push insights to clinicians, suggest validated interventions, or adjust care in real time. This is critical for chronic illness, cancer, rare disease, and disability. Why it doesn’t exist yet: Many of these apps were designed to collect data for research or billing, not to improve quality of life. There’s fear about liability if an app makes a “recommendation,” so most default to passive logging. Clinical teams are also overwhelmed and don’t want another inbox. The burden falls on the patient to figure it out. 6. A values-based decision-making tool for major care choices. In an ideal world, we need an app that helps people and their families think through what matters most, goals, fears, trade-offs, before facing a surgery, treatment change, or end-of-life decision. Something evidence-based, compassionate, and non-coercive that can inform shared decision-making. Why it doesn’t exist yet: Talking about death and potentially dangerous consequences is taboo and sometimes culturally unacceptable. It doesn’t reduce billing friction. And it forces us to grapple with the emotional realities of serious illness and death. But it’s one of the most transformative digital interventions we could offer and we’re ignoring it. One of the most important spaces in innovate in is the end of life care and active death care space. b. What set of workflows do you believe CMS is uniquely positioned to offer? CMS is well positioned to offer a Waze for healthcare. Many of its members are already familiar with Waze conceptually for driving purposes and could now understand a deployment of Waze for their healthcare journeys. We do not need more generic symptom checkers or portals. We need real-time, dynamic, context-aware guidance, just like how Waze reroutes drivers based on traffic, construction, or accidents ahead. A patient- and carepartner-facing, intelligent health navigation system that: •Maps the entire care journey in real time: Based on a person’s diagnoses, medications, labs, coverage, location, and preferences, it would chart the most efficient and person-
centered path through the system. Think: referrals, tests, pharmacy pickups, follow-ups, durable medical equipment, home care, all sequenced and timed optimally. •Crowdsources insights from others on the same path: Just like Waze gathers data from other drivers, this would gather patterns, pitfalls, and best practices from others with similar health conditions. What side effects tend to emerge with this treatment at week 3? What delays typically happen with this prior auth? How long is the wait for an in- network endocrinologist in your area? What’s the fastest way to get to the right provider, to get to the right treatment at the right time? •Offers intelligent rerouting and proactive alerts: If a lab result is missing, a prior auth is delayed, or a critical medication refill is about to lapse, the system flags it and guides you to act. It can suggest better alternatives, such as telehealth instead of waiting 3 months for a visit, or financial assistance programs based on your payer and condition. •Coordinates communication across care team members: Notifies primary care when a specialist prescribes something new. Finds duplicate orders. Flags errors in your medical records. Ensures your cardiologist knows your nephrologist just changed your meds. Alerts you when something needs follow-up, not three weeks later. Alerts you if your blood pressure is trending too high. •Centers patient and caregiver values: The route is not just the “fastest” or “cheapest” but the one that aligns with your priorities. For example, fewer in-person visits if transportation is a barrier, or appointments clustered together to reduce time off work. •Includes real-world guardrails and stop signs: Alerts users if a treatment pathway or diagnostic delay is common with negative outcomes. Warns if a drug interaction might be missed or if out-of-pocket costs could lead to financial toxicity. Connects to social needs data (food, housing, caregiving) and flags if gaps might jeopardize health. •Learns and adapts over time: It becomes more personalized and precise the more it is used, and it draws on data from others like you, just like Waze becomes more powerful the more people contribute. Why Waze for Healthcare Doesn’t Exist (Yet) •Fragmented data systems: No single source aggregates claims, clinical data, social determinants, preferences, and real-time access info (yet) •Misaligned incentives: Navigating care more efficiently reduces revenue in many fee- for-service models. Payers and health systems aren’t motivated to simplify access. Health systems don’t want to keep the “healthier” patients out of their facilities because it’s “bad for their margins”. •Lack of reimbursement for navigation and coordination: Even though it improves outcomes and reduces cost, navigation is underfunded and often left to patients or overburdened caregivers. PC-5. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in these digital health products?
When CMS asks how to encourage patient and caregiver interest in digital health tools, the answer isn’t in more apps, but rather it’s in how we show up, how we listen, and how we build trust. People aren’t disinterested in digital health. They are exhausted by systems that constantly ask them to adapt, input, troubleshoot, and give feedback, without ever showing that it matters, while labelling us “non-compliant, non-adherent, poor historians, hysterical, difficult, senile, drug-seeking, and frequent-flyers”. If CMS wants to build meaningful engagement, it must start by recognizing patients and carepartners as experts, investing in co-creating tools that reflect real life, and supporting the full range of navigational ability, from first-time users to those already navigating our healthcare system at a level of mastery. Here’s how we do that: 1. Co-create digital health tools with patients and caregivers, and compensate them fairly for their expertise. People support what they help build. Patients and carepartners are not just informants or passengers in their care, they are co-designers, strategists, and subject matter experts. CMS should require that anyone receiving federal funding to develop or deploy digital health tools engages patient and caregiver contributors from diverse communities and compensates them using a standardized framework. The National Health Council’s Fair Market Value (FMV) Calculator offers a trusted, transparent guide for determining appropriate compensation based on the level of engagement. No one should be asked to share their story, review a design, or test a tool without being paid for their time, labor, and lived experience. 2. Pair technology with trusted, human support. Tools do not build trust. People and human connections do! CMS should expand the Principal Illness Navigation (PIN) program to include reimbursement for trained navigators who offer tech-enabled, person-centered support. These navigators should be equipped to guide patients who are not digitally native, who have limited English proficiency, who are managing disability or cognitive challenges, or who are overwhelmed by the sheer complexity of their health situation. Peer mentors, community health workers, and digital health coaches should all be part of the engagement ecosystem. 3. Remove structural barriers that keep people from accessing digital tools. CMS must treat internet access, devices, and tech literacy as foundational to care participation. Patients cannot use what they cannot reach. This includes subsidizing smartphones, tablets, broadband access, and hands-on setup and training, especially for people receiving PIN-aligned navigation services. Co-pays for navigation should be waived for patients with complex needs or limited resources. CMS should also partner with public libraries, community centers, and retail health hubs to deliver on-the-ground support in accessible, familiar environments. Barbershops, beauty salons, and laundry mats as well as places of worship are key. 4. Highlight what digital health makes possible. CMS should invest in sharing stories, not polished marketing stories, but real stories about how digital health tools helped someone understand their diagnosis, avoid an emergency, reduce confusion, or finally 
get the support they needed. Patient and carepartner-created videos, toolkits, and explainer guides should be funded and widely shared. These stories should reflect diverse communities, conditions, and lived realities. 5. Build transparency into every tool, especially those that use AI or automation. Patients and carepartners need to know when algorithms are influencing their care, benefits, or access. CMS should require plain-language disclosures when tools use AI, and ensure there are options to opt out, appeal, or get a human review. Patient and caregiver representatives should sit on governance panels for these tools, and be compensated for their contributions using frameworks like the NHC FMV calculator. Transparency isn’t a threat to innovation, but rather it’s a prerequisite for trust. 6. Design for a full range of navigational ability, from an early beginner to a digitally savvy expert. Not all patients need a simplified tool. Many are already operating at an advanced level. There are patients and carepartners who understand insurance coding, track their own labs and biomarkers, coordinate multiple specialists, and manage complex insurance appeals. CMS should support tools that can scale in complexity, offering entry-level guidance for some, and advanced dashboards, data syncing, and customizable features for others. These expert users should also be supported to serve as navigators, peer mentors, or system design collaborators. Let’s stop designing for the mythical “average user” and instead create tools that are flexible enough to meet people where they are and grow with them. A positive innovation feedback loop from real lived experience is key! 7. Make co-creation and accessibility part of procurement and payment. CMS has enormous influence through the way it funds and reimburses innovation. That power should be used to uplift tools that are co-created, that compensate patient and caregiver contributors fairly, and that are accessible in multiple languages, literacy levels, and functional abilities. Co-creation should not be optional. It should be embedded in the criteria for CMS-supported innovation, and the people doing this work should be paid accordingly. If CMS wants to boost patient and carepartner interest in digital health, the answer is not in more features. It’s in more fairness, more humility, and more respect. Digital health cannot succeed if we ignore the real-world expertise of the people navigating care every day. We need to design tools that reflect the people carrying the system on their backs—and we need to start compensating them for their expertise, not just thanking them for their feedback. a. PC-7. If CMS were to collect real-world data on digital health products' impact on health outcomes and related costs once they are released into the market, what would be the best means of doing so? Here’s what CMS should do to collect meaningful real-world data that actually reflects how digital tools are shaping care, cost, and quality for Medicare beneficiaries and their carepartners: 1. Prioritize patient-reported outcomes and caregiver-reported outcomes. You cannot evaluate the value of digital health products if you don’t ask the people using them how it impacted their lives. CMS should build into every digital health evaluation: •Patient-reported outcomes (PROs) and experiences (PREMs) tied to symptom management, access, communication, and emotional well-being •Caregiver-reported outcomes (CROs), especially related to stress, time burden, clarity, and support in managing care
•Real-world feedback on how digital tools made a difference—or didn’t—across diverse demographics, abilities, and conditions 2. Fund community-led evaluation networks. Stop relying solely on academic health systems or private vendors to evaluate impact. CMS should fund and partner with patient-led organizations, caregiver coalitions, and disability rights groups to co-design and lead impact assessments. •These communities know what to ask, how to ask it, and what matters •Compensation must be standard for all contributors, guided by the National Health Council’s FMV Calculator •Co-create frameworks that reflect real-life definitions of success: less confusion, fewer ER visits, better shared decision-making, smoother transitions—not just lab numbers and utilization rates 3. Use longitudinal claims and clinical data to track downstream effects. CMS has unique visibility into long-term care trajectories. It should analyze whether digital health tools: •Reduce avoidable hospitalizations, redundant testing, or care delays •Improve medication adherence, follow-up visit completion, or preventive screening rates •Shift utilization patterns (e.g., from ER to urgent care, from inpatient to outpatient) But don’t stop at utilization. Track whether digital tools correlate with better functional status, home safety, caregiver retention, and hospice or palliative engagement. 4.Track cost impact, but center value to the person. Cost savings alone do not equal value. A tool that prevents one hospital admission but increases daily confusion for a caregiver is not a success story. CMS must balance hard cost data with: •Patient and carepartner time saved •Mental health impact •Reduction in unnecessary bureaucracy or paperwork •The emotional cost of using (or not being able to use) digital health tools 5.Measure equity, inclusion, and who gets left behind. Every CMS-funded evaluation must include gaps in care analysis: •Who is benefitting most from digital health tools? •Who isn’t able to access them—and why? •Are there disparities in impact by race, language, disability status, income, or geography? If a tool works best for affluent, digitally fluent English speakers with fast internet, that’s not innovation. That’s exclusion.
6. Make participation easy and accessible. If you want real feedback from real people, meet them where they are. •Offer multiple feedback channels: SMS, phone, paper, in-person, caregiver-reported, voice memos •Avoid long, burdensome surveys that require portal logins or tech fluency •Make sure feedback can be shared in the moment, not just months later in a structured interview 7.Create a public dashboard of impact, with patient- and carepartner-facing language. People deserve to know what’s working, what’s not, and how decisions are being made. CMS should maintain a living, transparent dashboard that shows: •Which tools are being used across programs •What impact they’re having •What beneficiaries and carepartners are saying Make it searchable, readable, and accountable. 8. Close the loop: share back what was learned, and what changed. If you ask for input, show how it shaped outcomes. •Every evaluation process should end with a public report and plain-language summary sent back to the communities who contributed •Highlight what improved, what was adjusted, and what new questions are being asked This builds trust—and shows people that their voice wasn’t just collected. It mattered.
