Response to CMS/ONC Request for Information on Digital
Health, Data Access, and Value-Based Care
Topic: Advance Care Planning (ACP) as a Digital Health Use Case for Patient
Empowerment and Value-Based Care
Thank you for the opportunity to comment on how digital tools can expand patient
empowerment and advance value-based care. This response highlights Advance Care Planning
(ACP) as a critical, underutilized strategy that advances both goals.
Advance Care Planning: A High-Impact, Patient-Centered Use Case
When patients lose the ability to make or communicate their own healthcare decisions, the
absence of clear, well-documented preferences can cause significant harm. Families are often
left to make agonizing decisions in moments of crisis, which has been associated with
increased rates of anxiety, depression, and PTSD among surrogates (Wendler & Rid, Ann
Intern Med, 2011). Care teams may default to delivering high-intensity interventions that are
unwanted, non-beneficial, or even harmful—simply because the patient’s goals are unknown.
This dynamic contributes to moral distress among clinicians (Epstein et al., J Gen Intern Med,
2019), emotional trauma for loved ones, and care experiences that are deeply misaligned with
what patients would have chosen.
Most patients, when asked, prefer less aggressive treatment near the end of life. The landmark
SUPPORT study found that seriously ill patients overwhelmingly desired comfort-focused care,
yet often received intensive interventions inconsistent with those preferences (SUPPORT
Principal Investigators, JAMA, 1995). More recent data show that over 70% of older adults
would opt to avoid hospitalization or life-prolonging treatment if outcomes are poor or uncertain
(Silveira et al., NEJM, 2010).
Yet in the absence of structured Advance Care Planning (ACP), the healthcare system defaults
to the most intensive care pathway. This default is driven by institutional habits, defensive
medicine, and billing structures—not patient values. As a result, individuals frequently receive
ICU stays, mechanical ventilation, and other invasive procedures they would not have chosen if
given the opportunity to plan ahead.
ACP offers a practical and scalable solution. It enables individuals to reflect on their values,
document care preferences, and designate a surrogate decision-maker. When implemented
effectively, ACP improves patient and family experience, relieves moral distress among
clinicians, and aligns care delivery with patient goals—not with system defaults.
ACP also generates significant cost savings by reducing unwanted, non-beneficial interventions.
Studies have shown that high-quality ACP can reduce total costs by up to an average $14,000
per patient, without denying care—only by delivering the care people truly want in their final
years. Across all Medicare beneficiaries, this amounts to up to $40 billion in annual waste.
Acknowledging CMS's Role and the Need for Modernization
1

CMS has long recognized the importance of ACP in supporting high-quality, person-centered
care. The creation of CPT codes 99497 and 99498 represented an important step in
acknowledging the clinical, emotional, and financial value of these conversations—for patients,
families, care teams, and the healthcare system as a whole.
However, the use of these codes in practice remains limited. Despite growing awareness of
ACP’s benefits, documentation rates and frequency of billed ACP conversations are persistently
low. This disconnect reflects a critical policy gap: while CMS has provided a path to
reimbursement, the structure of that reimbursement does not yet align with the realities of
modern clinical practice.
To close that gap, policy must evolve to reflect:
• The time constraints and competing demands faced by physicians in primary and
specialty care;
• The proven effectiveness of team-based ACP delivery models, including the central role
of nurses, social workers, and care managers; and
• The value of digital tools and asynchronous engagement in gathering patient goals,
preferences, and surrogate decision-maker documentation—often before the clinical
encounter takes place.
These tools and care models expand access, improve documentation quality, and make ACP
more feasible within the operational limits of today’s healthcare settings. But they are not
adequately supported under the current billing framework.
Policy Barriers and Recommendations
Despite its clinical and financial benefits, ACP remains underutilized due to outdated policy and
billing frameworks. CMS can address these barriers and unlock the full potential of ACP through
the following policy actions:
1. Modernize Billing to Reward Completion and Quality
Current CPT codes (99497, 99498) reimburse ACP based on time spent, not on
outcomes or documentation quality. This approach discourages efficiency and
innovation in ACP delivery.
Recommendation: Adopt a value-based billing model that reimburses for completed
ACP components—such as surrogate designation, preference documentation, and EHR
integration—regardless of duration.
2. Permit Indirect Supervision to Enable Team-Based Models
ACP is often best initiated by nurses, social workers, and care managers. However,
current requirements for direct supervision limit flexibility and scale.
Recommendation: Allow indirect supervision of ACP services, similar to Chronic Care
Management (CCM) and other population health services, to enable team-based
delivery.
2

3. Allow Social Workers and Nurses to Bill Directly for ACP
Licensed clinical social workers and registered nurses play a critical role in ACP
conversations, especially in underserved and community-based settings. They often
have more training and experience in supporting ACP conversations – many of which
are not explicitly clinical - than physicians.
Recommendation: Permit direct billing for ACP by qualified social workers and nurses
consistent with their scope of practice.
4. Eliminate Cost Sharing for ACP Services
Treating ACP as a specialist service with required cost sharing undermines its
preventive value and discourages uptake—particularly among older adults and those
with serious illness.
Recommendation: Remove Medicare cost sharing requirements for ACP, aligning it
with other preventive services that support patient autonomy and care alignment.
5. Ensure telehealth authority includes ACP
Telehealth is typically a better modality than in-office, enabling longer conversations to
explore goals of care tradeoffs, include family members, and better utilize experts in
ACP.
Digital Tools as a Catalyst for Scalable, Effective ACP
Technology plays a pivotal role in preparing patients and enhancing the quality of ACP
conversations. Digital tools—including AI-enabled previsit platforms—can guide individuals
through goal-setting and values clarification asynchronously, at their own pace and in their own
language. These tools generate structured outputs that can be shared with care teams and
embedded in the electronic health record, improving both documentation and downstream
communication.
Far from replacing clinicians, these tools augment their effectiveness. They make limited
clinician time more meaningful, enable proactive patient engagement, and ensure that ACP is
delivered equitably and efficiently. To realize these benefits, CMS must evolve reimbursement
frameworks to support asynchronous engagement, virtual delivery, and team-based models that
integrate digital platforms.
Conclusion
Advance Care Planning exemplifies how policy and technology come together to deliver care
that is more person-centered, equitable, and fiscally responsible. CMS has an opportunity to
modernize reimbursement, support technology advancement, align care delivery with patient
values, and expand access to a service that improves outcomes across clinical, financial, and
human dimensions. Thank you for the opportunity to contribute to this important policy
conversation.
