Health Technology Ecosystem RFI
Building on Blue Button's Success: Exploring a VA
Lighthouse-Inspired API Platform for Medicare Innovation
Questions Addressed:
● TD–1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to
stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare beneficiaries
and caregivers?
● TD–2. Regarding CMS Data, to stimulate developer interest—
○ a. What additional data would be most valuable if made available through CMS
APIs?
○ b. What data sources are most valuable alongside the data available through the
Blue Button 2.0 API?
○ c. What obstacles prevent accessing these data sources today?
○ d. What other APIs should CMS and ASTP/ONC consider including in program
policies to unleash innovation and support patients and providers?
Understanding the Opportunity: CMS laid important groundwork with Blue Button 2.0, giving
Medicare claims data to over 60 million beneficiaries through a FHIR API. Ad Hoc helped to build
this foundation as prime contractor for Blue Button 2.0 in 2018 and continues to see opportunities
to expand developer engagement across other CMS programs, ultimately to enable even more
impactful applications for Medicare beneficiaries. Current claims data, while valuable for financial
and administrative insights, has inherent limitations—it reflects services from 60-90 days ago and
doesn't include the clinical information needed for real-time care coordination, medication
management, or preventive health applications that beneficiaries need.
Our experience tells us the developer community wants to build more comprehensive tools for
Medicare but they seek additional data avenues, clear return on their time investment, and
trustworthy APIs. Blue Button 2.0 and the few other APIs on developer.cms.gov leave a lot to be
desired, especially considering what’s available on similar platforms like developer.va.gov, which
is the essence of this comment. By evolving beyond the current limited APIs to a more
comprehensive platform strategy, CMS could unlock significant innovation potential. The
Department of Veterans Affairs' journey with their Lighthouse platform (which Ad Hoc has
stewarded since 2018) offers valuable lessons and a potential model worth exploring. The VA
publishes 20 public APIs supporting a vibrant developer ecosystem, as well as a mature internal
developer platform as further enablement. Including FHIR APIs for claims, the VA public portal
includes the Facilities API (where to get treatment), Eligibility API (status of various program
eligibilities), Veteran Status API (similar to a Medicare MBI), and many more.
Exploring a Platform Approach: Learning from VA Lighthouse: CMS should consider adopting
elements of the VA Lighthouse model. Interestingly, VA's Lighthouse program initially drew
inspiration from CMS's Blue Button project, then enhanced it with additional capabilities. This
creates an opportunity for mutual learning between agencies.
Currently, CMS's developer resources are scattered across multiple portals—Blue Button has its
own site, QPP APIs live on qpp.cms.gov, Medicaid APIs reside on data.medicaid.gov, and other

APIs lack clear homes. This fragmentation creates confusion for developers who have to navigate
different registration processes, documentation standards, and technical implementations. In
contrast, VA's unified developer.va.gov portal provides a single entry point where developers can
discover all available APIs, register once for access, and find consistent documentation and tools.
This streamlined approach has attracted over 243 active developers and enabled innovations like
the successful submission of 68,000+ digital claims through the Benefits Intake API alone.
The technical and organizational infrastructure behind VA's success is equally instructive. Ad Hoc
and the VA learned that a thriving external developer ecosystem requires robust internal
systems. Our implementation of an internal developer platform using modern tools like Spotify
Backstage created a culture where teams can easily share and reuse APIs, maintain consistent
standards, and accelerate development. Most impressively, VA's continuous Authority to Operate
(ATO) process reduced security approval times from over 400 days to just 70 days—addressing
one of the biggest barriers to government API innovation. CMS could achieve similar efficiencies
by adopting these proven practices while maintaining the high security standards essential for
protecting beneficiary data.
The contrast in developer experience drives this idea. A developer who wants to build a
Medicare application would need to register separately for Blue Button, navigate to different sites
for provider data, and often cannot find APIs for critical functions like prior authorization or
real-time eligibility. Meanwhile, a developer in the VA space can visit a single portal, immediately
access documentation for all 21+ APIs, test in a unified sandbox, and build applications that
combine health, benefits, and administrative data seamlessly. By consolidating
developer.cms.gov into a unified platform modeled on VA's approach, CMS could unlock similar
innovation for the 60 million Medicare beneficiaries who deserve modern digital tools.
Potential High-Value Data Sources: Building on Blue Button 2.0's claims data, CMS could
consider making additional data sources available to enable new types of beneficiary-focused
applications. Prior authorization information combined with Part D event data could help reduce
treatment delays and improve medication adherence. A unified provider directory (we agree
wholeheartedly with this initiative) combining existing NPPES and PECOS data with quality
metrics could help beneficiaries make more informed care decisions.
Other valuable additions might include real-time medication data for safety checking and
identifying cost-effective alternatives, clinical quality measures that help identify care
opportunities, and eligibility information that supports the 12 million beneficiaries who qualify for
both Medicare and Medicaid. These data sources, when thoughtfully combined with existing
claims data through well-designed APIs, could enable developers to create holistic health
management tools. The goal would be to present these various data sources through consistent,
well-documented interfaces that reduce the complexity developers currently face when trying to
access multiple CMS systems.
Potential Benefits and Positive Outcomes: A platform approach could yield significant benefits
for Medicare beneficiaries and the healthcare ecosystem. This could lead to dozens of innovative
applications addressing beneficiary needs in areas like medication management, care
coordination, and healthcare cost transparency. Streamlined technical processes could reduce
the time needed to launch new APIs while maintaining security standards.

The broader impacts could include reduced administrative burden through automation of
processes like prior authorization, improved beneficiary satisfaction through better digital
experiences, and support for value-based care models through enhanced data accessibility. This
approach would also position CMS to leverage emerging technologies like artificial intelligence
and machine learning in service of Medicare beneficiaries. Following patterns seen at VA and
other agencies, these improvements often lead to increased trust and doubled adoption of digital
services among beneficiaries.
Our Experience and Suggested Next Steps: Ad Hoc has had the privilege of contributing to the
VA Lighthouse platform's development, as well as the VA Flagship mobile app with over 2 million
downloads. Our team has also worked extensively with CMS data systems including Blue Button
2.0, Part D systems (Medicare Coverage Tool), the Marketplace and its APIs, Quality Payment
Program infrastructure, and Medicaid systems. We've seen firsthand how thoughtful platform
approaches can transform government service delivery while maintaining security and
compliance.
We would be eager to have exploratory conversations with CMS, ASTP/ONC, or other HHS
leadership to share lessons learned and identify applicable practices. A pilot program with 2-3
high-value APIs could demonstrate the platform approach's potential while allowing for learning
and adjustment.
