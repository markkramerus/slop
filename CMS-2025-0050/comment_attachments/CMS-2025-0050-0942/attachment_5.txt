Commercial electronic health records (EHRs) were first devel-
oped to automate business processes. As EHRs developed,
design principles focused on transferring existing paper-based
documentation to comparable electronic forms. In addition, a
strong industry focus on adult healthcare settings and quality
measures has limited attention and resources for high priority
EHR functionality needed for the unique health care of children.
The objective of this paper is to provide a review of innovation
in the ERR, that includes a variety of established and emerging
technologies that may help realize a more effective EHR in child
health settings. A more effective EHR would serve as an elec-
tronic hub. Existing EHR infrastructure could provide the

foundation upon which new technologies and approaches
branch and extend, enabling more rapid and customizable
innovation to better meet shifting stakeholder and end-user
needs. Among many areas for improvement, key goals of inno-
vation could include technology that relieves ambulatory pri-
mary care clinician documentation burden, identifies needs,
and supports improved care coordination and outcomes,
focused on the following key areas: identification of child and
family care needs, decision support, documentation, care coor-
dination, and family communication.

Identification of Child and Family Care Needs

Collecting information directly from parents and/or
children in anticipation of their visit with the primary
care team will continue to expand, optimizing the abil-
ity to identify children’s needs and concerns, as well
as maximizing the efficiency of the time spent with
the healthcare team. Distribution of time spent in the
visit can shift from data collection to problem solving,
an approach more focused on improving health. Pre-
visit materials, including checklists and question-
naires, are valued by parents to facilitate better use of
limited time with the clinician.'’ Materials in the form
of screeners, via internet-based tools or native EHR
applications, are already widely used to screen for
early developmental or adolescent mental health
concerns.'*'°?° Pre-visit screeners may expand to
new health domains relevant to pediatric primary
care, including physical activity and sleep.*!°? Both
parents and child health clinicians report high satisfac-
tion with these approaches. They help guide discus-
sion, promote in-depth information exchange, and
increase visit efficiency.” Pre-visit screening for ado-
lescent-specific care also offers benefits, including
potentially increasing disclosure of sensitive health
risk behaviors, priming the teen for the confidential
visit portion with the clinician, and improving the
clinical discussion and tailoring health communica-
tion to the adolescent’s individual needs.***° By
improving patient-centered care and clinician respon-
siveness to patient and family identified needs, these
tools have the potential to support healthy behaviors
and improve health outcomes.*°*’** Technologies
that integrate these services could support clinicians
in their efforts to provide tailored guidance and inter-
ventions through clinical decision support (CDS) sys-
tems and connecting children and families to relevant
resources based on information gathered for ambula-
tory or office-base encounter.

Emerging and evolving standards are facilitating the
integration of third-party tools into EHR systems
using web service approaches. A web service, at the
most basic level, is a method for two electronic devi-
ces to communicate over a network.”? Integrating
these external tools into the EHR by web services pro-
vides a new mechanism for exchanging information in
numerous useful modes. For example, web services
enable the complex computations necessary to imple-
ment algorithms developed using machine learning
techniques to be moved from the EHR into more suit-
able high performance computing environments. Web
services also enable information primarily stored or
maintained outside the EHR (for example, lists of
community resources) to be delivered to clinical staff
via the EHR. Notably, in light of the COVID-19 pan-
demic, these approaches can improve healthcare sys-
tem responses to emerging public health crises by
integrating the most current public health information
into the EHR.’°! These integration efforts have
benefited significantly from ongoing efforts to refine
the Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR)
standard by Health Level Seven (HL7).° ? FHIR

supports applications access to clinical data in a stan-
dardized fashion regardless of the underlying EHR
data model. Other HL7 standards are increasingly
being adopted by healthcare settings and EHR vendors
to support the scaling and sharing of CDS efforts
among health systems. These standards include the
Clinical Quality Language for expressing CDS rules
and electronic clinical quality measurement logic
using a standard language,’ the CDS Hooks standard
for integrating external CDS prompts into EHRs,”™*
and the Substitutable Medical Applications and Reus-
able Technologies (SMART) standard.°° Examples of
what can be accomplished using web services can be
found via the SMART on FHIR app gallery.°°

Leaders of most Internet-based businesses have real-
ized that using this open approach expands the reach
and effectiveness of their organizations. Organizations
such as Amazon, IBM, Salesforce.com, Facebook, and
Google have all leveraged application programming
interfaces (APIs) — programming routines or proto-
cols that enable software applications to share data —
to permit developers to access information so they can
build new applications or businesses.°’ Using open
APIs companies can extend their reach (for example,
the ability to access Netflix on any internet-connected
screen) or open up new cus-

innovative user interfaces and analytics platforms that
truly support clinical decision-making. Finally,
researchers could have easier access to detailed data
to identify problems, test hypotheses, and generate
new solutions. Thus, EHRs could eventually support
companies or innovative teams within health care sys-
tems to build more tailored applications that improve
the end-user experience and the quality of care
through better knowledge dissemination.

Family Communication

Finally, the EHR as electronic hub could support
direct child and family engagement. EHR-based
patient portals, through which children or caregivers
access the child’s health information online, hold
potential to increase connectedness to the medical
home, through improved communication and schedul-
ing,” as well as support management of chronic
health conditions.°° A portal designed to support

parents of children with asthma, for example,
improved parent-reported child health and reduced
lost time from work by connecting families to their
child’s medical home and monitoring ongoing symp-
toms.*” Better integration of these systems into EHRs,
with particular attention on improved health literacy
and supporting the unique needs of underserved popu-
lations,°° will help overcome barriers and support dis-
seminating effective interventions. Text messaging
can complement patient portal use, with potential
applications for a wide range of child health care
needs.*' Text messaging holds particular potential for
further implementation and maintenance of vaccina-
tion reminder systems,*” with various studies suggest-
ing this approach may be more equitable with
improved reach compared to patient portal-based
messaging.*~*? Finally, integrating direct patient gen-
erated data is likely to become increasingly important
and requested by families, with devices like auto-
mated asthma inhalers capable of reporting on adher-
ence** and various wearable devices supporting both
remote monitoring of daily activities, such as sleep
and physical activity, and potential disease diagnosis
and management. 89.80.87 EHR- integrated apps and
services may help to collect and synthesize data from
these devices, presenting information in such a way to
offer a more complete picture of a patient’s activities

Conclusions

To help realize a more effective EHR in child
healthcare settings, existing EHR infrastructure will
be the foundation upon which new technologies and
approaches will branch and extend. This will enable
more rapid and customizable innovation to meet shift-
ing stakeholder and end-user needs. Key areas for
focus include identification of child and family care
needs, decision support, documentation, care coordi-
nation, and family communication. A vital area of
need is addressing the child and family privacy con-
siderations unique to child healthcare settings. Innova-
tion in these areas will help achieve the ultimate goal:
technology that relieves clinicians’ documentation
burden, identifies needs, and supports improved care
coordination and health outcomes.
