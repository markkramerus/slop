Health Technology Ecosystem RFI
Making Digital Health Work for People: A Human-Centered
Design Approach
Questions Addressed: PC–5. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and
caregiver interest in these digital health products?
Understanding the Opportunity: Medicare beneficiaries often struggle with using health apps: "I
have six different apps just to manage my diabetes," "each works differently, and I can never find
what I need," and "the print is too small, the buttons too tiny, and when something goes wrong, I
can't understand the error message." These aren't edge cases—they're the daily reality for
millions trying to manage their health digitally.
One key area of fulfilling the promise of digital health is designing for the people who need it
most. Medicare beneficiaries—who often manage multiple conditions and medications—struggle
with tools that ignore their needs. Poor design isn't just frustrating; it's dangerous when patients
can't access critical health information or miss important care reminders.
But we've seen what's possible when government prioritizes user needs. There are federal health
design success stories across CMS (healthcare.gov, the Medicare Coverage Tool, and others), the
VA, and others. The VA Flagship mobile app in particular, with over 2 million downloads and a
4.8-star rating, proves that older Americans will eagerly adopt well-designed digital tools. CMS
can spark this same transformation across Medicare digital health.
Solution(s): CMS could provide incentives to encourage app developers to leverage
user-centered design in a standard method when developing apps for Medicare beneficiaries
without having to go through extra regulatory hurdles. For example, apps from the Medicare
Connected Apps Directory could have a special designation on the website indicating that they
followed certain user-centered design principles, the CMS design system, or display a rating that
indicates the ease of navigating the app based on testing by users.
As part of an app certification process, CMS can that great user-centered design is part of the
app development process to make it easier for patients and caregivers to navigate and find
information that is needed. CMS could collaborate with ASTP/ONC to include apps for Medicare
beneficiaries on the Certified Health IT Product list and add testing criteria related to
user-centered design. Some guidelines that could be included in the testing criteria are:
consistency, plain language / clear info, warning, and error messages, ease of updating
preferences, providing a personalized experience (see
https://design.cms.gov/guidelines/design-principles), and 508 accessibility. To test that these
apps followed these guidelines, automated testing could be conducted using existing testing
tools (e.g., for 508 accessibility) or new tools could be developed for specific design principles.
Patients and caregivers should be included in the app development process for suggestions
regarding how to improve adoption and navigation of digital health apps, and for health
information that would be useful. CMS could sponsor hackathon-like challenges where
developers would design and improve apps that address top priorities identified by CMS based
on feedback from Medicare beneficiaries. For example, apps could include consistent messaging

Dependencies and/or Constraints: We believe that success will require balance between
standards specific enough to ensure quality but flexible enough for innovation. The certification
should be fast and simple, using automated testing where possible with human review only for
user experience elements. CMS and ONC need to align efforts, building on the existing Certified
Health IT program to include patient-facing apps. Industry groups and accessibility advocates
should help shape achievable standards.
The approach should be based on carrots and not sticks. Rather than mandating design
requirements that might exclude smaller developers, create market incentives where
beneficiaries naturally choose certified apps and providers recommend them.
Potential Benefits and Positive Outcomes: When health apps work for people, amazing things
happen. Beneficiaries who can easily check their benefits make better care decisions. Caregivers
who get clear medication reminders prevent dangerous drug interactions. Seniors who
understand their coverage avoid surprise bills and get preventive care on time.
The ripple effects extend throughout healthcare. Providers spend less time explaining how to use
patient portals and more time on care. Call centers see fewer confused beneficiaries unable to
navigate digital tools. Emergency rooms see fewer patients who missed critical medications
because reminder apps were too confusing to set up.
For app developers, clear design standards level the playing field. Small companies can compete
with tech giants by focusing on user needs rather than flashy features. The "Medicare Design
Certified" badge becomes a competitive advantage, driving a race to the top in usability. Success
in the Medicare market—with its 60 million users—becomes a launching pad for broader
healthcare innovation.
Most importantly, good design builds trust. When beneficiaries see that apps actually work for
them, they're more likely to try other digital health tools. This creates a virtuous cycle where
better design leads to more adoption, which attracts more developers, which drives even better
design.
Suggested Next Steps: Ad Hoc brings proven expertise in government human-centered design.
We've transformed digital experiences across federal agencies and seen how good design
improves outcomes for vulnerable populations.
We suggest that CMS/ONC start by listening and convening beneficiary focus groups to identify
top app frustrations and wishes. Use this research to shape certification criteria. Then pilot with
willing developers on high-impact use cases like medication management and appointment
scheduling. Test and refine the process based on real experience. We also recommend launching
design challenges around beneficiary priorities—celebrations where developers compete to
serve seniors well, with beneficiaries as judges to show that users determine success!
By making human-centered design a competitive advantage rather than a burden, CMS can
transform Medicare's digital landscape. The technology exists and users are ready. They just
need apps that work for them. When we design for those who need help most, we create tools
that work better for everyone.
