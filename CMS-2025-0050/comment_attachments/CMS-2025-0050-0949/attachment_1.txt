Health Technology Ecosystem RFI
Streamlining Quality Reporting Through Direct EHR-to-CMS
Data Submission
Questions addressed: TD–9. Regarding certification of health IT: e. How could EHRs capable of
bulk data transfer be used to reduce the burden on providers for reporting quality performance
data to CMS? What capabilities are needed to show benefit? What concerns are there with this
approach?
Understanding the Opportunity: Providers today face a high administrative and technology
burden that pulls them away from patient care. A family doctor in MIPS might waste hours each
quarter pulling data that's already in their computer system, then pay thousands of dollars yearly
to a registry just to send it to CMS. They rightfully ask: "Why do I need to pay someone else to
send data that's already in my EHR?"
The current quality reporting ecosystem requires providers to choose between multiple
pathways—qualified registries, Qualified Clinical Data Registries (QCDRs), or manual
attestation—each with different costs, capabilities, and complexities. Small and rural practices
particularly struggle with these requirements, often lacking the resources to engage
sophisticated registries or the technical expertise to navigate submission options. Meanwhile, the
EHR systems that providers use every day already capture the clinical data needed for quality
measurement during routine patient care.
This disconnect is both a challenge and enormous opportunity for the new Administration to
make a valuable change. By having EHRs submit quality data directly to CMS, we could get rid of
redundant data handling, reduce costs for providers, and improve the timeliness and accuracy of
quality measurement. Most importantly, we could return precious hours to providers.
Exploring an EHR Direct-to-Government Platform Approach: Modern EHR systems already
possess the technical ability for direct quality reporting. The missing piece is a streamlined,
secure pathway for these systems to transmit data directly to CMS without intermediaries.
This direct submission model would work through standardized, automated processes. EHRs
would extract required quality measures using established formats like QRDA (Quality Reporting
Document Architecture) or emerging FHIR-based quality measure specifications. The data would
then flow through secure channels—either through bulk file uploads or real-time API
submissions—directly into CMS quality reporting systems. Providers could simply log in to a
common portal and review and approve submissions within their familiar EHR interface,
eliminating the need to log into separate registry portals or manage multiple vendor relationships.
The approach builds naturally on existing infrastructure and standards. ONC's certification
program already requires EHRs to support quality measure calculation and reporting. The
technical standards exist through QRDA and the emerging FHIR-based quality measures. What's
needed is the connective tissue—a secure, standardized submission pathway that vendors can

Importantly, this model preserves choice while reducing burden. Providers who prefer their
current registry relationships could continue using them, while those seeking simplification could
use direct EHR submission. Over time, as the direct pathway proves its value, organic adoption
would drive industry transformation without mandating disruptive changes.
Potential Benefits and Positive Outcomes: Direct submission would transform healthcare
reporting. Practices save $3,000 to $10,000 per year by cutting registry fees and it enables small
and rural practices access to the same powerful reporting tools as large health systems. The time
savings would also lead to staff spending fewer days on quarterly reports and instead focus on
their non-technologist roles. Fewer manual steps also mean fewer errors, as data comes straight
from patient care.
CMS benefits too. Instead of waiting months for registry batches, CMS could get near real-time
updates, reduce its burden by having one standard submission method to replace many different
registry formats, and speed the feedback to providers. FHIR adoption may accelerate as well.
Bottom line, everyone moves from looking backward at old reports to improving care in real time.
Dependencies and Constraints: Success requires careful attention to technical, regulatory, and
market dynamics. The technical foundation must ensure robust security and privacy protections,
leveraging modern authentication methods and encryption to protect sensitive quality data
during transmission. EHR vendors would need clear, stable technical specifications that allow
them to invest confidently in development.
From a regulatory perspective, ONC certification requirements might need updates to include
direct CMS submission capabilities and creating adoption incentives. CMS would need to make
clear data validation and audit processes to ensure submitted data meets quality standards
without the intermediate validation currently provided by registries. Privacy regulations must be
carefully navigated to ensure proper patient consent and de-identification where required.
The transition must also consider existing stakeholders. Qualified registries and QCDRs provide
valuable services beyond data transmission, including measure selection guidance, performance
feedback, and improvement coaching. The direct submission model should preserve
opportunities for these organizations to provide value-added services.
Our Experience and Suggested Next Steps: Ad Hoc brings deep expertise in both quality
reporting programs: our multi-year experience with the Quality Payment Program and Hospital
Quality Reporting program provides intimate knowledge of current pain points and opportunities.
We've witnessed firsthand how administrative burden impacts provider participation and have
developed solutions that simplify complex healthcare data workflows.
We recommend starting with a pilot with a few willing EHR vendors and diverse practices. Begin
with common measures like diabetes care where data capture is standard. Include big and small
practices to ensure it works everywhere. We should also bring together CMS quality teams, ONC
