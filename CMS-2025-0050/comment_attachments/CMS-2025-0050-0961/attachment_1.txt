Comment Response
Below, we have categorically outlined our supporting comments by the original questions posed
by CMS.
Patient Needs
PC-1. What health management or care navigation apps would help you understand
and manage your (or your loved ones’) health needs, as well as the actions you
should take?
Digital health-forward care navigation apps should make it effortless for patients, caregivers, and
clinicians to share data, coordinate services, and act on clear, personalized insights. To do so, apps
must (1) adopt common interoperability standards (HL7 FHIR, DICOM, LOINC, ICD-11, SNOMED
CT) so information flows wherever care happens, and (2) pair that technical backbone with
human-centered design (HCD) that puts the patient experience first—from the moment an
appointment is booked to long-term condition management.
• Friction-free scheduling – One interface to book, reschedule, or cancel visits without
phone calls or multiple log-ins.
• Cross-provider data exchange – Real-time interoperability (e.g., via Mirth Connect, Redox,
Google Cloud Healthcare Application Programming Interface [API]) that links clinics, labs,
transport, and tele-triage in a single workflow.
• Personalized, data-driven guidance – Standards-based integrations that turn real-time
vitals and records into concrete nutrition, activity, and medication prompts.
• Patient-friendly AI touchpoints – Passive/active AI delivered through channels users already
trust (secure text, voice assistants) to refill meds or message clinicians without extra apps.
• Blockchain-grade privacy – Decentralized, immutable ledgers that give patients key-based
control over who sees what, slashing breach risk and admin overhead.
• Unified health dashboards – Consumer-grade hubs (think Apple Health-style) that aggregate
wearables, Electronic Health Records (EHR) data, and physician-ordered tests into shareable,
easy-to-read trends.
• Insight-centric trackers – Apps like Levels, Gentler Streak, and Ada that translate glucose,
activity, and symptom data into plain-language cause-and-effect lessons users can act on.
• AI care advocates – Conversational agents that reframe medical jargon, surface “why”
behind decisions, and bridge language or literacy gaps for patients and families.
PC-5. What can CMS and its partners do to encourage patient and caregiver interest in
these digital health products?
CMS can wield its purchasing power to accelerate digital health adoption—both by rewarding
data-sharing and by hard-wiring patient-centered metrics into every contract. Doing so will not
only modernize Medicare, Medicaid, and the Children’s Health Insurance Program (CHIP) but also
push the wider market toward integrated, value-based care.

• Open-data Medicare contracts – Require every Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC)
to make claims and encounter data openly available to providers and payers via standardized
APIs.
• Mandatory data exchange conditions – Make EHR sharing with patients, Qualified Health
Information Networks (QHINs), and Health Information Exchanges (HIEs) a new condition
of Medicare participation, separate from payment rules.
• Patient-centric quality metrics – Adopt frameworks such as the National Academy of
Medicine’s to track beneficiary sentiment, access, and service satisfaction inside value-
based payment models.
• ICD-11 modernization – Lead the U.S. transition to the fully digital ICD-11 platform so
codes align with today’s science, link seamlessly to SNOMED CT, and feed richer analytics
for care improvement and research.
• Exploding self-tracked data – With 53% of U.S. adults wearing at least one connected
device and 54% digitally tracking a health metric, consumer data now rivals EHR volume
and must be harmonized1.
• Creating opportunities to incentivize patients and caregivers – Enhance Accountable
Care Organization (ACO) financial rewards by focusing on incentives that promote health
data cross-sharing between provider groups and their patients through innovations such
as health data from wearables and health improvement programs such as rewards for
patients adhering to episodic immunization.
Data Access and Integration
PC-8c. What specific opportunities and challenges exist to improve accessibility,
interoperability and integration of clinical data from different sources to enable
more meaningful clinical research and generation of actionable evidence?
Healthcare data still lives in too many walled-off systems, each with its own schema and coding
rules making large-scale analytics slow and costly. Modern ontology-discovery and mapping tools
can break those walls down by creating shared vocabularies and data models on the fly, letting
clinicians and analysts focus on insights instead of integration.
• On-demand ontologies and taxonomies – Auto-generate flexible, extensible data models
that align granular elements to the right medical specialties, then splice in terms from
related ontologies for quick cross-walks.
• ML-driven concept fusion – Combine rule-based mapping, machine learning, and large
language models (LLMs) to spot equivalent codes across sources, slash manual work, and
surface novel analytic groupings.

Ecosystem
TD-1. What short term (in the next 2 years) and longer-term steps can CMS take to
stimulate developer interest in building digital health products for Medicare
beneficiaries and caregivers?
An immediate jolt of “developer-friendliness” plus a clear runway for scaling can draw new talent
and capital into solutions that serve Medicare beneficiaries and their caregivers. Below are near-
term actions CMS can execute within two years, followed by longer-horizon moves that lock in
momentum and create durable market signals.
Short-term (next 24 months)
• Launch a Super-charged Blue Button Sandbox
Ship synthetic FHIR R4 datasets, consent-flow examples, and a command-line test harness
so teams can prototype integrations in hours.
• Run Problem-Backlog Micro-Grants and Hackathons
Post tightly scoped challenges (e.g., fall prevention, medication adherence) with prizes
and offer live API support at annual FHIR hackathons.
• Introduce Provisional CPT/G-codes for Digital Pilots
Allow billing for app-enabled monitoring or coaching during limited trials, giving startups
a revenue path while CMS gathers evidence.
• Publish an Open-Source Reference Stack
Host sample apps, SDKs, and unit tests in a CMS GitHub org, all mapped to certification
criteria to slash redundant dev work.
• Offer Value-Based Care App Bonuses
Provide small upward adjustments to bundled or shared-savings payments when providers
prescribe CMS-certified apps and submit outcomes.
Longer-term (3–5 years and beyond)
• Maintain a De-identified Outcomes Registry
Create a “data corridor” where developers can study longitudinal effectiveness without
new IRB hurdles, accelerating iteration and evidence generation.
• Elevate Beneficiary Portals to a Unified “Health Home”
Fund User Experience/User Interface (UX/UI) revamps and cross-provider data unification,
making portals the single front door for scheduling, records, and caregiver access.
• Expand AI Research and Deployment Grants
Focus on practical wins—diagnostic support, prior-auth automation, personalized med
regimens—and embed voice/chat agents where seniors already engage.

• Pioneer Smart Tele-Health Interfaces
Partner with consumer-electronics firms to bring tele-visits, med reminders, and
appointment alerts to the living-room screens familiar to older adults.
• Institutionalize Open-Source First
Reduce licensing costs, avoid vendor lock-in, and cultivate a public developer community

These parallel tracks (quick wins that remove friction today and structural moves that reward
innovation tomorrow) can transform CMS from a payer into the nation’s most powerful catalyst
for age-friendly digital health.
In Conclusion
Noblis appreciates the opportunity to help shape CMS and ONC’s vision for a patient-centered,
data-driven health-technology ecosystem. Drawing on three decades of public-interest research
and deep, hands-on work with CMS, we have outlined practical, near-term actions—super-charged
developer sandboxes, open-data contracts, provisional billing codes—as well as longer-horizon
investments in unified beneficiary portals, AI-enabled care, and open-source infrastructure.
Together, these steps can accelerate market innovation, reduce administrative waste, and deliver
measurable value to Medicare, Medicaid, and CHIP beneficiaries and their caregivers.
Noblis agrees with all the recommendations herein this reply and many recommendations made
by Leavitt Partners, an HMA Company, in their white paper, “Kill the Clipboard! A Federal Policy
and Industry Roadmap to Accelerate Innovation and Cut Administrative Waste.”2 We stand ready
to collaborate with CMS, ONC, and industry stakeholders to translate these ideas into scalable
solutions that improve outcomes and lower costs.
Thank you for considering our comments.
