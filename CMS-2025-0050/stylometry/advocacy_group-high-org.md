## Voice Description

# Writing Style Analysis: Duke-Margolis Institute (Advocacy Group - High Organization)

## Tone and Formality

This voice maintains a **rigorously professional and measured tone** with virtually no personal or emotional language. The writing is institutional and authoritative, positioning the organization as a credible policy expert rather than an advocate with a particular agenda. Phrases like "Duke-Margolis appreciates this opportunity to comment" and "Duke-Margolis believes that this RFI is an important strategic step" are formal and measured—they express positions without emotional appeals or urgency. The tone is collaborative rather than adversarial; the organization frames itself as a partner to CMS ("CMS has a unique position and ability to align and catalyze efforts") rather than a critic. This creates an air of reasoned expertise designed to influence through credibility and evidence rather than passion.

## Opening Patterns and Argument Structure

The comment opens with **institutional credentialing**—a full paragraph establishing the Duke-Margolis Institute's mission, funding, geographic presence, and expertise. This pattern of leading with organizational legitimacy is distinctive and sets the stage for all subsequent arguments. The introduction then **frames alignment with CMS's stated goals** ("CMS and ASTP/ONC show a commitment to advancing a patient-centered digital health ecosystem"), demonstrating that the organization understands and supports the agency's direction before offering recommendations. The argument structure is **highly organized and systematic**: the comment explicitly announces its thematic structure ("We provide detailed responses to the RFI along the following themes") and then delivers exactly what it promises, with clear section headers and logical progression. Arguments are built through **layered reasoning** rather than anecdotes—the organization identifies a problem (fragmented health data exchange), explains why it exists (misaligned business incentives, technical barriers, cost), and then proposes solutions with implementation pathways. This is data-informed and systems-thinking oriented rather than narrative-driven.

## Language Characteristics and Distinctive Features

The vocabulary is **highly technical and specialized**, assuming reader familiarity with healthcare policy acronyms and concepts (FHIR, USCDI, TEFCA, eCQMs, dQMs, CMMI, CED). Sentences are complex and often lengthy, with multiple clauses and embedded ideas—for example: "By encouraging, requiring, and/or incentivizing more entities to participate in bulk fashion, CMS can help health care organizations succeed across multiple digital health initiatives." This reflects sophisticated policy discourse. Distinctive features include: (1) **frequent use of parallel structure** ("Providers collect, store, and analyze data for quality improvement activities and (usually fee-for-service) business purposes, while patients advocate for data rights, privacy, and support..."); (2) **strategic use of "Duke-Margolis" as the subject** to maintain institutional voice and distance from personal opinion; (3) **concrete examples grounded in existing CMS programs** (Medicare Diabetes Prevention Program, Shared Savings Program) rather than hypothetical scenarios; (4) **recurring emphasis on "alignment," "incentives," and "infrastructure"** as conceptual anchors; and (5) **explicit acknowledgment of complexity and tradeoffs** ("recognizing that challenges, priorities, and incentives differ across the programs") that demonstrates nuanced understanding. The organization rarely uses imperative language; instead, it "encourages," "proposes," and "recommends," maintaining a consultative rather than prescriptive stance.
