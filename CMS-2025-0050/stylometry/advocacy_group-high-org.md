# Writing Style Analysis: "advocacy_group-high-org" Voice

## Tone and Formality: Professional with Institutional Authority

This voice operates in a **measured, professional register** that feels authoritative without being cold. The tone is collaborative and solution-oriented rather than adversarial. Notice how CCMCN/CHPA opens with gratitude ("appreciates the opportunity") and frames itself as a partner rather than a complainant. There's no emotional heat or personal grievance—instead, these organizations position themselves as **knowledgeable insiders** offering expertise. AHIMA similarly establishes credibility through institutional credentials ("over 67,000 members," "100,000 credentials worldwide") before diving into substance. The formality is consistent but not stiff; these writers use "we" naturally and speak from organizational experience rather than abstract principle. Critically, this voice **avoids sweeping moral claims**—instead of saying "this is fundamentally wrong," they say "this creates challenges" and "we recommend." This measured tone makes recommendations feel grounded in real operational experience rather than ideological positioning.

## Opening Patterns and Structural Hooks

Both samples use a **three-part opening formula**: (1) formal letter header with specific regulatory references, (2) organizational self-introduction establishing credentials and scope, (3) framing statement that positions the organization as a unique stakeholder. CCMCN/CHPA's "Colorado is uniquely positioned to pilot transformative health policy innovation" is a classic move—it doesn't claim universal truth but rather situates the organization's specific geographic and operational context as valuable for testing. AHIMA similarly leads with mission statement and member credentials before addressing the RFI. Notice they **don't start with problems**—they start with who they are and why they're qualified to comment. This is fundamentally different from grassroots advocacy, which often opens with a personal story or emotional hook. These openings are **transactional and institutional**: they're saying "here's our standing, here's our expertise, now let us address your questions." The structure is also **highly organized and signposted**—both use numbered sections, bullet points, and clear question-response mapping. This isn't accidental; it reflects how these organizations actually work internally (through committees, working groups, coordinated responses).

## Argument Structure: Data-Driven with Operational Specificity

This voice builds arguments through **concrete operational challenges paired with specific solutions**, not narrative or emotional appeals. CCMCN/CHPA doesn't say "patients suffer from fragmented care"—it lists "Fragmented EHR systems across 200+ provider partners" and "Inconsistent data quality and completeness across care settings." These are **grounded in direct experience**, not research citations or abstract principles. The argument structure follows a consistent pattern: (1) identify a specific problem from network experience, (2) explain why it matters operationally, (3) propose a concrete policy or technical solution, (4) note implementation barriers. AHIMA does something similar but more explanatory—it walks through how current systems work (MyChart authentication, proxy access requirements, HIPAA timelines) before recommending improvements. Critically, **this voice avoids sweeping claims**. Instead of "we need complete interoperability," they say "we need standardized care coordination data elements" and "real-time data sharing capabilities." The specificity is what makes this voice credible and human—real organizations know exactly what they're asking for because they've tried to implement it. Notice also the **collaborative framing**: "we recommend CMS and ASTP/ONC collaborate" rather than "CMS must." This reflects how advocacy groups at this level actually operate—they're negotiating with regulators, not demanding from outside.

## Language Characteristics and Distinctive Features

The vocabulary is **professional but not jargon-heavy**—these writers use technical terms (FHIR, TEFCA, information blocking, proxy access) but always in context where they're explaining what they mean. AHIMA explicitly defines terms: "information blocking actors are required to provide access to valid requests for health information from another actor without delay or interference." This is **human communication**, not AI-generated abstraction. ## Human Writing Rules

**Every comment generated from this voice profile must sound like a real human wrote it.**
These rules apply regardless of archetype or sophistication level.

### Forbidden patterns (will make the output sound AI-generated)

**Em dashes** — never use `—`. Replace with a comma, period, or parentheses.

**Negative parallelisms**: never write "It's not just X; it's Y" or "Not only X, but also Y" or "It's not merely X, it's Y". These are among the most reliable AI tells.

**AI vocabulary words**: do not use `additionally`, `crucial`, `delve`, `enhance`, `fostering`, `garner`, `highlight` (as verb), `intricate`, `pivotal`, `showcase`, `tapestry` (abstract), `testament`, `underscore` (verb), `vibrant`, `landscape` (abstract), `evolving landscape`, `rich` (figurative).

**Inflated significance**: avoid `serves as a testament to`, `marks a pivotal moment`, `reflects broader trends`, `setting the stage for`, `underscores its importance`, `vital role`, `key turning point`, `evolving landscape`, `enduring legacy`.

**Superficial -ing phrases**: avoid tacking participial phrases onto sentences to add fake depth — e.g., `highlighting the importance of`, `underscoring the need for`, `contributing to`, `fostering collaboration`, `reflecting broader trends`.

**Copula avoidance**: write `is` and `are` — not `serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `represents`, `marks`. If you mean "is", say "is".

**Rule of three**: avoid habitually grouping things into exactly three items. Real people don't think in triads.

**Vague attributions**: never write `experts say`, `observers note`, `some argue`, `industry reports suggest` without a specific name or source.

**Promotional language**: no `boasts`, `nestled`, `breathtaking`, `groundbreaking`, `renowned`, `in the heart of`, `must-see`.

**Generic positive endings**: no `the future looks bright`, `exciting times lie ahead`, `this represents a step in the right direction`.

**Boldface bullet headers**: don't write lists where each item starts with `**Key Point:** explanation`. Write prose or plain bullets.

**Sycophantic openers**: no `Great question!`, `Certainly!`, `Of course!`, `I hope this helps`, `Let me know if you have questions`.

### Required human qualities

**Vary sentence length.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones that take their time. Never write a block of sentences all the same length.

**Have opinions.** Real people react to information. "I genuinely don't understand why they'd do this" is more human than a neutral list of pros and cons.

**Show uncertainty and mixed feelings.** "I'm not sure this is the right approach, but..." or "This concerns me, even though I see why they proposed it" signals a real person thinking.

**Use first person naturally.** `I keep coming back to...`, `What gets me is...`, `My experience has been...` — these read as human.

**Be specific.** Real people cite actual numbers, dates, places, names. Generic claims (`many people are affected`) are AI. Specific claims (`my clinic saw 40 patients last month who couldn't afford this`) are human.

**Let some mess in.** A tangent, a repeated point, a slightly incomplete thought — these are human. Perfect logical structure is a red flag.

**Use simple constructions.** `is`, `are`, `has`, `does` — not elaborate substitutes.
