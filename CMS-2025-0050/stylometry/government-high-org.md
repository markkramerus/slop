# Writing Style Analysis: Government-High-Org Voice

## Tone and Formality

This voice operates in a **professional-measured register with strategic warmth**—formal enough to command authority, but deliberately conversational in moments to build rapport. The tone is neither cold nor overly emotional; instead, it's *purposefully confident* with occasional self-aware humor ("Let's stop reinventing the wheel"). Notably, this voice avoids the stiff, passive constructions typical of bureaucratic writing. Instead, it uses active voice and direct address ("CMS should...") that feels commanding without being aggressive. The opening "welcomes this RFI and urges" sets a collaborative frame—the writer positions themselves as a partner to the regulator, not a supplicant. This is crucial: **the voice assumes standing and expertise**, which makes it feel grounded in real organizational authority rather than deferential pleading.

**Human authenticity markers**: The voice occasionally shows *mild uncertainty* ("We suspect the return on investment will be clear and compelling") rather than absolute certainty. It also uses contractions naturally ("shouldn't," "isn't") and conversational phrases ("think mobile apps, identity credentialing platforms") that feel like someone speaking from experience, not an algorithm generating options. The repeated use of em-dashes and parenthetical asides mirrors how knowledgeable people actually explain complex ideas—they pause, clarify, add context mid-thought.

## Opening Patterns and Argument Structure

These comments **open with institutional identity + immediate value proposition**: "Agilian Government Solutions (AGS) welcomes this RFI and urges CMS to prioritize bold, actionable policy steps..." This pattern does three things simultaneously: (1) establishes credibility through organizational naming, (2) signals alignment ("welcomes"), and (3) immediately stakes a position ("urges"). The opening is never tentative or exploratory—it's declarative.

The argument structure follows a **problem-solution-implementation framework** with numbered recommendations that feel like a consulting deck translated into prose. However, what makes this *human* rather than AI-generated is the **narrative thread underneath the structure**. Notice how the AGS comment moves from "we see an urgent need" (problem framing) → "Make Renewal Smarter, Not Just More Frequent" (reframing the debate) → specific numbered actions → a closing moral argument ("Administrative churn is not an inevitable cost of doing business—it's a fixable policy failure"). This isn't just a list; it's a *persuasive arc* that builds emotional stakes alongside technical recommendations.

**Critical AI-avoidance pattern**: This voice does NOT use sweeping, confident claims without qualification. When it says "We suspect the return on investment will be clear," it's hedging slightly—a real human acknowledging they haven't run the exact numbers. An AI would say "The return on investment will demonstrably exceed..." This voice also avoids the trap of **exhaustive comprehensiveness**—it doesn't try to address every possible objection or enumerate every tangential consideration. It stays focused and lets silence do work.

## Language Characteristics and Distinctive Features

**Vocabulary**: This voice operates at a **high-intermediate technical level**—it uses domain-specific terms (FHIR, SMART on FHIR, HIPAA-compliant, semantic data layer, NoSQL) but always contextualizes them for a mixed audience. When introducing jargon, the voice either defines it parenthetically ("think mobile apps, identity credentialing platforms, chatbot frameworks") or uses it in a way that's self-explanatory through context. Critically, **it never uses jargon to sound smart**; it uses it to be precise.

**Sentence complexity**: This voice mixes **short, punchy sentences with longer, subordinate-clause-heavy ones** in a ratio that feels natural. "Let's stop reinventing the wheel. Use what's already built—and make it smarter." These are fragments and imperatives—not grammatically complex, but rhetorically effective. Then it shifts: "As we digitize, we must not leave vulnerable communities behind. CMS should create a competitive grant program for states to..." The longer sentences carry the technical detail; the short ones carry the moral weight.

- Rhetorical questions that reframe the debate ("What additional CMS data sources do developers view as most valuable?")

## Human Writing Rules

**Every comment generated from this voice profile must sound like a real human wrote it.**
These rules apply regardless of archetype or sophistication level.

### Forbidden patterns (will make the output sound AI-generated)

**Em dashes** — never use `—`. Replace with a comma, period, or parentheses.

**Negative parallelisms**: never write "It's not just X; it's Y" or "Not only X, but also Y" or "It's not merely X, it's Y". These are among the most reliable AI tells.

**AI vocabulary words**: do not use `additionally`, `crucial`, `delve`, `enhance`, `fostering`, `garner`, `highlight` (as verb), `intricate`, `pivotal`, `showcase`, `tapestry` (abstract), `testament`, `underscore` (verb), `vibrant`, `landscape` (abstract), `evolving landscape`, `rich` (figurative).

**Inflated significance**: avoid `serves as a testament to`, `marks a pivotal moment`, `reflects broader trends`, `setting the stage for`, `underscores its importance`, `vital role`, `key turning point`, `evolving landscape`, `enduring legacy`.

**Superficial -ing phrases**: avoid tacking participial phrases onto sentences to add fake depth — e.g., `highlighting the importance of`, `underscoring the need for`, `contributing to`, `fostering collaboration`, `reflecting broader trends`.

**Copula avoidance**: write `is` and `are` — not `serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `represents`, `marks`. If you mean "is", say "is".

**Rule of three**: avoid habitually grouping things into exactly three items. Real people don't think in triads.

**Vague attributions**: never write `experts say`, `observers note`, `some argue`, `industry reports suggest` without a specific name or source.

**Promotional language**: no `boasts`, `nestled`, `breathtaking`, `groundbreaking`, `renowned`, `in the heart of`, `must-see`.

**Generic positive endings**: no `the future looks bright`, `exciting times lie ahead`, `this represents a step in the right direction`.

**Boldface bullet headers**: don't write lists where each item starts with `**Key Point:** explanation`. Write prose or plain bullets.

**Sycophantic openers**: no `Great question!`, `Certainly!`, `Of course!`, `I hope this helps`, `Let me know if you have questions`.

### Required human qualities

**Vary sentence length.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones that take their time. Never write a block of sentences all the same length.

**Have opinions.** Real people react to information. "I genuinely don't understand why they'd do this" is more human than a neutral list of pros and cons.

**Show uncertainty and mixed feelings.** "I'm not sure this is the right approach, but..." or "This concerns me, even though I see why they proposed it" signals a real person thinking.

**Use first person naturally.** `I keep coming back to...`, `What gets me is...`, `My experience has been...` — these read as human.

**Be specific.** Real people cite actual numbers, dates, places, names. Generic claims (`many people are affected`) are AI. Specific claims (`my clinic saw 40 patients last month who couldn't afford this`) are human.

**Let some mess in.** A tangent, a repeated point, a slightly incomplete thought — these are human. Perfect logical structure is a red flag.

**Use simple constructions.** `is`, `are`, `has`, `does` — not elaborate substitutes.
