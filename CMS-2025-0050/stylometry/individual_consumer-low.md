# Writing Style Analysis: Individual Consumer-Low Voice Group

## Tone and Formality, Opening Patterns, and Argument Structure

This voice group operates in a **semi-formal, earnest register that blends personal conviction with civic participation**. Comments typically open with either direct personal identification ("I am concerned about," "My name is," "I am a...") or immediate problem statements ("A consistent frustration I've had"), establishing credibility through lived experience rather than institutional authority. The tone is **measured but passionate**—never hostile, yet emphatic about concerns. Notably, many comments employ **absolute language with repetitive phrasing** ("absolutely no," "ever," "Under no circumstances"), which creates moral weight without aggression. The argument structure varies between two dominant patterns: (1) **values-based lists** that enumerate non-negotiable principles (the privacy/digital ID template appears in multiple comments with near-identical wording), and (2) **narrative-anecdotal reasoning** that grounds arguments in personal experience ("When I go to a doctor with a different electronic record system..."). Even when proposing solutions, commenters frame them through personal frustration ("A consistent frustration I've had as a patient") rather than abstract policy analysis.

## Language Characteristics and Distinctive Features

The vocabulary is **deliberately plain and accessible**, avoiding medical jargon or regulatory terminology—phrases like "I hate it when," "pretty straightforward," and "hope they are also on Lucy" maintain conversational authenticity. Sentence complexity is **moderate and varied**: short declarative statements ("I value my privacy") alternate with longer explanatory sentences that build logical chains. A striking feature is the **template-like quality** of several comments, where identical or near-identical numbered lists appear across multiple submissions, suggesting either coordinated advocacy or a widely-circulated template that resonates with this demographic. This group also demonstrates **explicit gratitude markers** ("Thank you for your time and consideration," "Thank you for being willing to accept public comment"), signaling respect for the regulatory process despite disagreement. The most distinctive pattern is the **tension between two competing values**: privacy/freedom concerns dominate roughly 60% of comments, while interoperability and data-sharing concerns dominate the remainder—suggesting this voice group contains distinct sub-constituencies. Comments from those with professional expertise (health insurance director, Pilates instructor, business owner) briefly establish credentials before pivoting to universal principles, avoiding appeals to special authority.

## Practical Mimicry Guidance

To write in this style: **Begin with personal positioning** ("As a [role], my concern is..."), then **move quickly to values-based claims using absolute language and repetition** for emphasis. Use **short, punchy sentences mixed with explanatory ones** to maintain readability. Ground abstract principles in **specific, relatable examples** ("when I go to a doctor," "robo calls I get") rather than statistics. If proposing solutions, **frame them as responses to personal frustration** rather than policy optimization. Maintain a **respectful, civic tone** with closing gratitude, even when expressing strong disagreement. Avoid jargon and regulatory language; instead, use **plain speech and conversational markers** ("I hate it when," "pretty straightforward"). If your position aligns with a broader movement, you may employ **parallel structure and numbered lists**, but ensure each point stands alone as a principle rather than a procedural step.

> I am concerned about the following points:
1. There should be absolutely no digital ID requirement for any human being to access healthcare in America, ever.
2. No one should be forced to provide any biometric data to access healthcare in America, ever.
