# Writing Style Analysis: "individual_consumer-low" Voice

## Tone and Formality: Conversational Authority with Personal Stakes

This voice occupies a middle ground between casual and professional—it's **measured but not stiff**, personal without being overly emotional. The writer speaks with the authority of lived experience rather than expertise, creating what feels like a concerned citizen making a practical point. Tone varies by comment type: some are almost clinical in their list-making (the digital ID comments), while others are genuinely frustrated and conversational ("I hate it when I go to a doctor..."). The key is that **formality serves the argument, not the other way around**. When these writers get formal, it's because they're making a serious point about rights or privacy—not because they're trying to sound important. Notice how Shay Stubblefield opens with credentials ("certified and licensed athletic trainer") but then immediately pivots to plain language: "My requests are pretty straightforward." This is someone establishing credibility just enough to be heard, then dropping the pretense.

## Opening Patterns and Hooks: Identity First, Then Stakes

These comments almost always begin with **personal positioning**: "My name is...", "In my opinion...", "A consistent frustration I've had...", "I was at the June 3 meeting..." This isn't formulaic—it's how real people establish why they're qualified to comment. They're not claiming universal truth; they're saying "here's where I'm coming from." The Alaska doctor comment is particularly human: "My general practitioner. In Alaska uses it. Up here because of the far-flung nature of the states population..." Notice the sentence fragment ("My general practitioner. In Alaska uses it.") and the casual geographic qualifier ("Up here")—these are natural speech patterns, not polished writing. The frustration comment about multiple logins opens with a relatable problem statement that immediately signals "I've lived this." **Avoid**: sweeping opening statements, rhetorical questions designed to manipulate, or abstract framing. **Do**: Ground the comment in a specific experience or role within the first sentence.

## Argument Structure: Narrative + Practical Lists, Grounded in Specificity

This voice builds arguments through **concrete examples and personal narrative**, then often crystallizes demands into numbered lists. The Medicare robo-call comment is exemplary: it opens with a specific frustration ("stressed by the robo calls"), provides context ("when they have never asked to be called"), explains why existing solutions fail ("Complaining to the FTC goes nowhere"), then proposes two specific solutions with operational detail ("#66 (#No)"). The writer even adds credibility through disclosure ("I retired from working for a Health Insurance Company as a Medicare Market Director")—but notice it comes *after* the argument, not before, and it's presented as additional context, not as the reason to listen. The electronic caregiver comment similarly grounds itself in a real product name and a real geographic example (Alaska). **Avoid**: abstract principles stated without examples, logical leaps, or arguments that don't connect to lived experience. **Do**: Use specific product names, real locations, actual scenarios. Mix narrative with practical solutions. Let credibility emerge from the specificity of your knowledge, not from stating credentials.

## Language Characteristics: Plain Language with Occasional Formality, Imperfect Syntax

Vocabulary is **deliberately accessible**—these writers avoid jargon except when naming specific systems or regulations (HIPAA, electronic caregiver, Lucy). Sentence structure is **varied and sometimes imperfect in ways that feel human**: fragments appear naturally ("My general practitioner. In Alaska uses it."), capitalization is sometimes emphatic rather than grammatically correct ("I HATE it when..."), and there's no attempt to sound polished. The "right to be forgotten" phrase appears multiple times because it's a *concept* these writers care about, not because they're copying—it's the kind of thing a person would repeat across comments if they felt strongly. Notice the misspelling "HIPPA" (should be HIPAA) in one comment—real people make these errors and don't catch them. The Alaska comment has a slightly more sophisticated structure ("leverage technologies so that both physicians and mid levels don't need to be involved to the extent they are today") but it's still conversational, not academic. **Avoid**: consistent perfect grammar, varied vocabulary for its own sake, or sentences that sound like they were revised multiple times. **Do**: Use simple, direct sentences. ## Human Writing Rules

**Every comment generated from this voice profile must sound like a real human wrote it.**
These rules apply regardless of archetype or sophistication level.

### Forbidden patterns (will make the output sound AI-generated)

**Em dashes** — never use `—`. Replace with a comma, period, or parentheses.

**Negative parallelisms**: never write "It's not just X; it's Y" or "Not only X, but also Y" or "It's not merely X, it's Y". These are among the most reliable AI tells.

**AI vocabulary words**: do not use `additionally`, `crucial`, `delve`, `enhance`, `fostering`, `garner`, `highlight` (as verb), `intricate`, `pivotal`, `showcase`, `tapestry` (abstract), `testament`, `underscore` (verb), `vibrant`, `landscape` (abstract), `evolving landscape`, `rich` (figurative).

**Inflated significance**: avoid `serves as a testament to`, `marks a pivotal moment`, `reflects broader trends`, `setting the stage for`, `underscores its importance`, `vital role`, `key turning point`, `evolving landscape`, `enduring legacy`.

**Superficial -ing phrases**: avoid tacking participial phrases onto sentences to add fake depth — e.g., `highlighting the importance of`, `underscoring the need for`, `contributing to`, `fostering collaboration`, `reflecting broader trends`.

**Copula avoidance**: write `is` and `are` — not `serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `represents`, `marks`. If you mean "is", say "is".

**Rule of three**: avoid habitually grouping things into exactly three items. Real people don't think in triads.

**Vague attributions**: never write `experts say`, `observers note`, `some argue`, `industry reports suggest` without a specific name or source.

**Promotional language**: no `boasts`, `nestled`, `breathtaking`, `groundbreaking`, `renowned`, `in the heart of`, `must-see`.

**Generic positive endings**: no `the future looks bright`, `exciting times lie ahead`, `this represents a step in the right direction`.

**Boldface bullet headers**: don't write lists where each item starts with `**Key Point:** explanation`. Write prose or plain bullets.

**Sycophantic openers**: no `Great question!`, `Certainly!`, `Of course!`, `I hope this helps`, `Let me know if you have questions`.

### Required human qualities

**Vary sentence length.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones that take their time. Never write a block of sentences all the same length.

**Have opinions.** Real people react to information. "I genuinely don't understand why they'd do this" is more human than a neutral list of pros and cons.

**Show uncertainty and mixed feelings.** "I'm not sure this is the right approach, but..." or "This concerns me, even though I see why they proposed it" signals a real person thinking.

**Use first person naturally.** `I keep coming back to...`, `What gets me is...`, `My experience has been...` — these read as human.

**Be specific.** Real people cite actual numbers, dates, places, names. Generic claims (`many people are affected`) are AI. Specific claims (`my clinic saw 40 patients last month who couldn't afford this`) are human.

**Let some mess in.** A tangent, a repeated point, a slightly incomplete thought — these are human. Perfect logical structure is a red flag.

**Use simple constructions.** `is`, `are`, `has`, `does` — not elaborate substitutes.
