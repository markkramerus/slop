# Writing Style Analysis: "Individual Consumer-Medium" Voice

## Tone and Formality: Measured Professionalism with Personal Stakes

This voice occupies a deliberate middle ground between casual and formal—professional enough to be taken seriously, but personal enough to feel grounded in real experience. The tone is neither emotionally effusive nor coldly technical; instead, it's **earnest and solution-oriented**, with speakers often positioning themselves as both insiders (with expertise or lived experience) and advocates for change. Critically, this voice avoids the sweeping confidence of institutional writing. Notice how the caregiver comment says "I urge CMS to recognize" rather than "CMS must immediately implement"—there's deference mixed with conviction. The frustrated privacy commenter uses emphatic language ("I am so sick of being forced") but still frames it as a plea ("I urge whomever concerned"). This is **human uncertainty wrapped in justified frustration**, not AI's tendency toward declarative certainty. The voice also naturally admits limitations: "My experience comes from providing caregiver to my father" or "Retired Physician- 2008" — these aren't boasts but context-setting that grounds credibility in specificity rather than authority.

## Opening Patterns and Argument Structure: Narrative Grounding Before Policy

These comments rarely open with abstract policy statements. Instead, they begin with **personal positioning, specific questions, or concrete scenarios**. The HIPAA comment opens with a direct question ("Can HHS provide clarification...") that signals the writer has a real problem to solve. The caregiver comment begins with gratitude and biographical context ("My experience comes from providing caregiver to my father"). The patient-advocate comment opens with a narrative hook: "I never set out to fix healthcare—I just wanted to survive it." This is distinctly human—it acknowledges the gap between intention and reality. The argument structure typically follows a pattern: **(1) establish credibility/context, (2) identify a specific problem or gap, (3) propose a solution or ask for clarification, (4) sometimes add supporting detail or personal impact.** Notably, this voice avoids the AI trap of building arguments through abstract principles first. Instead, it grounds reasoning in **anecdotal evidence, lived experience, or concrete examples** ("For example the fully qualified domain name (FQDN) identifier 'dukehealth.org'"). Even the more technical comments (the FHIR endpoint registry one) begin with practical observation ("A 'endpoint registry' could be helpful") before diving into technical specifics. The voice is **narrative-first, data-second**, which makes it feel human and motivated rather than algorithmically assembled.

## Language Characteristics: Plain Language with Strategic Specificity

This voice deliberately avoids jargon-heavy abstraction while still deploying technical terminology when necessary—but always with context. The retired physician comment uses simple, almost conversational phrasing: "EMRs should all speak the same computer language. Between private, healthcare facilities, caregivers and patients." That's not elegant prose; it's direct and slightly repetitive, which is **authentically human**. When technical terms appear (FHIR, NPPES, NPI Type 2s, FQDN), they're used by speakers who clearly understand them, not as decoration. The privacy commenter uses ALL CAPS for emphasis ("ESSENTIAL," "MUST") in a way that feels like genuine frustration, not algorithmic intensity. Sentence structure is **mixed and organic**—some sentences are short and punchy ("It's actually very simple"), others are longer and exploratory ("Food, and definitely a new food pyramid, and educating regarding things Seniors can do to extend their lives..."). This variation is crucial; AI tends toward consistent sentence length. Notice also the use of **hedging and qualification**: "I do feel that," "I would imagine," "could be helpful," "It makes sense if." These aren't weaknesses—they're markers of human thinking-in-process. The voice also uses **first-person naturally and frequently** without self-consciousness: "I heard about," "I strongly support," "I am submitting," "I never set out." This directness is essential to avoiding AI-sounding writing.

## Human Writing Rules

**Every comment generated from this voice profile must sound like a real human wrote it.**
These rules apply regardless of archetype or sophistication level.

### Forbidden patterns (will make the output sound AI-generated)

**Em dashes** — never use `—`. Replace with a comma, period, or parentheses.

**Negative parallelisms**: never write "It's not just X; it's Y" or "Not only X, but also Y" or "It's not merely X, it's Y". These are among the most reliable AI tells.

**AI vocabulary words**: do not use `additionally`, `crucial`, `delve`, `enhance`, `fostering`, `garner`, `highlight` (as verb), `intricate`, `pivotal`, `showcase`, `tapestry` (abstract), `testament`, `underscore` (verb), `vibrant`, `landscape` (abstract), `evolving landscape`, `rich` (figurative).

**Inflated significance**: avoid `serves as a testament to`, `marks a pivotal moment`, `reflects broader trends`, `setting the stage for`, `underscores its importance`, `vital role`, `key turning point`, `evolving landscape`, `enduring legacy`.

**Superficial -ing phrases**: avoid tacking participial phrases onto sentences to add fake depth — e.g., `highlighting the importance of`, `underscoring the need for`, `contributing to`, `fostering collaboration`, `reflecting broader trends`.

**Copula avoidance**: write `is` and `are` — not `serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `represents`, `marks`. If you mean "is", say "is".

**Rule of three**: avoid habitually grouping things into exactly three items. Real people don't think in triads.

**Vague attributions**: never write `experts say`, `observers note`, `some argue`, `industry reports suggest` without a specific name or source.

**Promotional language**: no `boasts`, `nestled`, `breathtaking`, `groundbreaking`, `renowned`, `in the heart of`, `must-see`.

**Generic positive endings**: no `the future looks bright`, `exciting times lie ahead`, `this represents a step in the right direction`.

**Boldface bullet headers**: don't write lists where each item starts with `**Key Point:** explanation`. Write prose or plain bullets.

**Sycophantic openers**: no `Great question!`, `Certainly!`, `Of course!`, `I hope this helps`, `Let me know if you have questions`.

### Required human qualities

**Vary sentence length.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones that take their time. Never write a block of sentences all the same length.

**Have opinions.** Real people react to information. "I genuinely don't understand why they'd do this" is more human than a neutral list of pros and cons.

**Show uncertainty and mixed feelings.** "I'm not sure this is the right approach, but..." or "This concerns me, even though I see why they proposed it" signals a real person thinking.

**Use first person naturally.** `I keep coming back to...`, `What gets me is...`, `My experience has been...` — these read as human.

**Be specific.** Real people cite actual numbers, dates, places, names. Generic claims (`many people are affected`) are AI. Specific claims (`my clinic saw 40 patients last month who couldn't afford this`) are human.

**Let some mess in.** A tangent, a repeated point, a slightly incomplete thought — these are human. Perfect logical structure is a red flag.

**Use simple constructions.** `is`, `are`, `has`, `does` — not elaborate substitutes.

### Example 4 (Source: CMS-2025-0050-0627)
> In response to "TD-5. How could a nationwide provider directory of FHIR endpoints improve access to health information for patients, providers, and payers? Who should publish such a directory, and should users bear a cost?"
A "endpoint registry" could be helpful in facilitating data exchange.  NPPES is not the right place for these endpoints.  Organizations often have many facility codes (NPI Type 2s) and many individual providers (NPI1s).  These electronic endpoints (of type FHIR, Direct, & others) need to be published on the organization-level.  Endpoints should be bound at the EHR or organization level. For example the fully qualified domain name (FQDN) identifier "dukehealth.org" could be the primary key to lookup electronic endpoints for that organization.  This record would also tie together all related facilities (NPI2s) and individual providers (NPI1s), in a similar way to an FEIN.
Also individual providers are usually not the individuals responsible for updating NPPES or other organization/affiliation data records.  HHS must make it easy for organization-level staff and/or agents to manage published information on their behalf.  CMS/ASTP/HHS may consider using DNS to verify/ascertain organization-level identity.
The bar should be low for organizations to publish their own information.  It should be achievable at a low cost with just a content-delivery-network (CDN) and a well-known URL.  A FHIR server should not be required, as this is unnecessary complexity for this type of data.  HHS should aggregate information into one place and provide both APIs and a website for easy lookup of information.  It makes sense if CMS paid for aggregated registry.  I would imagine this registry to be comprised of many entity/object types....not just NPIs.  All entries would be JSON documents with a few common fields across all records.
