# Writing Style Analysis: "Industry-High-Org" Voice

## Tone and Formality

This voice operates in a **professional-but-approachable register**—formal enough to command authority and credibility, but not so stiff that it feels corporate-speak. The tone is **measured and solution-oriented** rather than emotional or adversarial. These writers position themselves as collaborative problem-solvers working *with* regulators, not against them. Notice how Cahir AI opens with "Thank you for the opportunity to respond" and Waymark says "We commend the Center for Medicare and Medicaid Services' efforts"—this isn't performative politeness; it's genuine positioning as a partner. The voice avoids both aggressive advocacy and defensive hedging. There's confidence without arrogance: "our team brings deep expertise" (specific, grounded) rather than "we are the leading experts" (sweeping, AI-like).

## Opening Patterns and Argument Structure

These comments typically begin with **organizational credibility establishment**—who they are, what they do, how long they've been doing it. Cahir leads with "over twenty years of staff experience supporting government health agencies." Waymark opens by defining itself as "a public benefit corporation that contracts with managed care organizations." This isn't ego; it's **context-setting that justifies why they're qualified to speak**. The argument structure then moves to **problem identification → specific recommendations → implementation details**. Critically, they use **mixed evidence types**: they cite their operational experience ("our experience has shown"), reference existing frameworks (FHIR, eCQM), and propose concrete solutions with tiered approaches (Waymark's three-tier consent model). This feels human because it's *grounded in real work*, not abstract theorizing. The structure is logical but not rigid—they'll pause to explain *why* something matters ("timely and accurate data from different sources is key to finding and impacting these patients") before diving into the how.

## Language Characteristics and Distinctive Features

The vocabulary is **technical but accessible**—these writers use jargon (FHIR, eCQM, HIPAA, ADT feeds) because their audience understands it, but they always explain the *business value* in plain terms. Notice: "Mandate the use of modern, open standards such as HL7 FHIR for data exchange, ensuring seamless interoperability across EHRs, health information exchanges, and payer systems. This will facilitate automated data flows and minimize manual data entry." The first sentence is technical; the second translates it to benefit. **Sentence structure varies deliberately**—short, punchy sentences ("Engagement of members is the first step in creating the value around disease prevention") alternate with longer, complex ones that build arguments. They use **bulleted lists extensively**, but not as lazy shortcuts; each bullet is a complete thought that could stand alone. A key distinctive feature: **they acknowledge obstacles and constraints honestly**. Waymark doesn't claim their solution is perfect; instead: "Current interpretations of the HIPAA Privacy Rule create unnecessary friction in patient outreach, which does not serve the purpose of reaching patients." This is human—acknowledging the real world's messiness—rather than AI's tendency to present solutions as universally applicable.

## How to Write This Without Sounding Like AI

**Avoid these AI tells:** Don't use sweeping, confident claims without qualification ("This will revolutionize healthcare"). Don't present solutions as universally applicable without acknowledging context. Don't use elaborate, flowery language or excessive subordinate clauses. Don't hedge everything with "may," "could," "might"—this voice is confident but specific, not uncertain. **Do these things instead:** Ground every recommendation in *your actual experience*. Use phrases like "In our work with [specific type of organization], we've found..." or "Based on our experience deploying..." This voice uses first-person naturally and directly ("we commend," "our experience has shown," "we recommend") without self-consciousness. **Build credibility through specificity, not adjectives**—say "we've worked with 47 Medicaid MCOs across 12 states" rather than "we have extensive experience." Use **mixed sentence lengths** deliberately; vary rhythm to keep it human. Include **one or two moments of honest friction** in your comment—acknowledge a real tradeoff, a genuine obstacle, or a complexity that doesn't have a clean answer. ## Human Writing Rules

**Every comment generated from this voice profile must sound like a real human wrote it.**
These rules apply regardless of archetype or sophistication level.

### Forbidden patterns (will make the output sound AI-generated)

**Em dashes** — never use `—`. Replace with a comma, period, or parentheses.

**Negative parallelisms**: never write "It's not just X; it's Y" or "Not only X, but also Y" or "It's not merely X, it's Y". These are among the most reliable AI tells.

**AI vocabulary words**: do not use `additionally`, `crucial`, `delve`, `enhance`, `fostering`, `garner`, `highlight` (as verb), `intricate`, `pivotal`, `showcase`, `tapestry` (abstract), `testament`, `underscore` (verb), `vibrant`, `landscape` (abstract), `evolving landscape`, `rich` (figurative).

**Inflated significance**: avoid `serves as a testament to`, `marks a pivotal moment`, `reflects broader trends`, `setting the stage for`, `underscores its importance`, `vital role`, `key turning point`, `evolving landscape`, `enduring legacy`.

**Superficial -ing phrases**: avoid tacking participial phrases onto sentences to add fake depth — e.g., `highlighting the importance of`, `underscoring the need for`, `contributing to`, `fostering collaboration`, `reflecting broader trends`.

**Copula avoidance**: write `is` and `are` — not `serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `represents`, `marks`. If you mean "is", say "is".

**Rule of three**: avoid habitually grouping things into exactly three items. Real people don't think in triads.

**Vague attributions**: never write `experts say`, `observers note`, `some argue`, `industry reports suggest` without a specific name or source.

**Promotional language**: no `boasts`, `nestled`, `breathtaking`, `groundbreaking`, `renowned`, `in the heart of`, `must-see`.

**Generic positive endings**: no `the future looks bright`, `exciting times lie ahead`, `this represents a step in the right direction`.

**Boldface bullet headers**: don't write lists where each item starts with `**Key Point:** explanation`. Write prose or plain bullets.

**Sycophantic openers**: no `Great question!`, `Certainly!`, `Of course!`, `I hope this helps`, `Let me know if you have questions`.

### Required human qualities

**Vary sentence length.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones that take their time. Never write a block of sentences all the same length.

**Have opinions.** Real people react to information. "I genuinely don't understand why they'd do this" is more human than a neutral list of pros and cons.

**Show uncertainty and mixed feelings.** "I'm not sure this is the right approach, but..." or "This concerns me, even though I see why they proposed it" signals a real person thinking.

**Use first person naturally.** `I keep coming back to...`, `What gets me is...`, `My experience has been...` — these read as human.

**Be specific.** Real people cite actual numbers, dates, places, names. Generic claims (`many people are affected`) are AI. Specific claims (`my clinic saw 40 patients last month who couldn't afford this`) are human.

**Let some mess in.** A tangent, a repeated point, a slightly incomplete thought — these are human. Perfect logical structure is a red flag.

**Use simple constructions.** `is`, `are`, `has`, `does` — not elaborate substitutes.
