# Writing Style Analysis: Industry-Medium-Org Voice

## Tone and Formality

This voice maintains a **professional yet accessible tone** that balances technical expertise with pragmatic business concerns. The formality level is consistently high—these are polished submissions from established organizations—but tempered with genuine problem-solving energy rather than bureaucratic stiffness. There's an underlying confidence and directness; these writers aren't hedging or apologizing for their positions. For example, SAFI Clinical Informatics states bluntly: "the greatest barrier to the success of FHIR-enabled interoperability is not technical, but educational," while the home care provider declares "it's the year 2025 and we are still exchanging data at a snail speed." This isn't combative; it's matter-of-fact urgency grounded in operational reality.

## Opening Patterns and Argument Structure

These comments typically open with **gratitude for the opportunity** ("Thank you for the opportunity to respond"), followed immediately by a **problem statement or gap identification**. Rather than leading with abstract principles, they ground arguments in concrete operational pain points: cost burdens (20% of revenue on EMR software), workflow fragmentation (patients scattered across four doctors and multiple systems), or workforce knowledge gaps (clinicians unfamiliar with FHIR standards). The argument structure is **layered and solution-oriented**: identify the problem, explain why existing approaches fail, propose a specific remedy, and conclude with actionable next steps. Ad Hoc's data mesh comment exemplifies this—it moves from "current point-to-point integrations are expensive and difficult" to "here's how data mesh architecture solves this" to "start with the Provider Directory as a pilot." The writing builds credibility through **specificity**: citing the 2022 HL7 Global Survey, mentioning the 3-6 month Medicaid data delay, referencing the VA's successful API structure.

## Language Characteristics and Distinctive Features

The vocabulary is **domain-specific but not unnecessarily jargonized**—these writers use technical terms (FHIR, NPPES, PECOS, data mesh, de-identified datasets) because their audience understands them, but they consistently **explain implications in business or patient-outcome terms**. Sentences are moderately complex, often using parallel structure and enumeration (bullet points, numbered lists) to organize dense information. A signature pattern is the **"imagine if" construction** (used powerfully in the home care comment: "Imagine if CMS Provided one EMR... Imagine the improved care outcomes... Imagine how much money we could save"). This rhetorical device makes abstract proposals feel tangible. Another distinctive feature is the **evidence-based credibility move**: citing surveys, case studies, or quantified outcomes (Ad Hoc's "50-75% reduction in time required for new data integrations"). The voice also includes **implicit appeals to shared values**—patient safety, provider burden reduction, system efficiency—without being preachy. Closing signatures often include credentials (MD MBA, RN, SVP Regulatory Affairs) that subtly reinforce authority without arrogance.
