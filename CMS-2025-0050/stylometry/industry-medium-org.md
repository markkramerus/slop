# Writing Style Analysis: Industry-Medium-Org Voice

## Tone and Formality: Professional with Embedded Passion

This voice operates in the "professional-but-human" zone—formal enough to be taken seriously, but warm enough to feel like it comes from someone who actually cares about the problem. The tone is **measured and respectful** rather than aggressive, but it frequently breaks into genuine frustration or urgency when describing real-world problems. Notice how Suzanne Jordan shifts from formal opening to direct emotional appeal: "This is a desperate situation that truly needs focus." Similarly, Diana Price ends with "let's cut the red tape, cut the cost, and streamline care its the year 2025 and we are still exchanging data at a snail speed"—this is someone exasperated but still trying to be professional. The voice **avoids both cold detachment and melodrama**; it's earnest without being performative. To write in this voice, use formal structure but let real frustration or conviction show through in word choice and pacing. Don't hide behind corporate speak—acknowledge the problem directly.

## Opening Patterns and Argument Structure: Problem-First, Then Solution

These comments almost universally begin with **formal letterhead/metadata, then immediately pivot to a specific, concrete problem** rather than abstract philosophy. They don't open with "We are pleased to comment" (that's AI-safe corporate language). Instead: "I work with Medicare Beneficiaries every single day due to issues they are experiencing..." or "Currently every Home Health, Hospice, Hospital, Clinic, Specialty Clinic has their own Electronic Medical Record (EMR) Software program." The structure is consistently **problem → evidence/examples → proposed solution → call to action**. Critically, these comments build arguments through **a mix of anecdotal evidence and logical reasoning**, not pure data dumps. Diana Price uses the "Imagine if..." rhetorical device repeatedly—this is how humans actually think through complex problems, by visualizing scenarios. The Theragen comment uses ALL CAPS for emphasis ("DOES NOT capture TRUE medical costs," "HIGHEST RISK") in a way that feels urgent rather than shouting. To avoid sounding like AI: Start with a real problem you've witnessed, not a general statement about the issue. Use concrete numbers when you have them (20% of revenue, 2% have email), but don't feel obligated to cite studies. Build your case by walking through what would happen if your solution were implemented.

## Language Characteristics: Accessible but Not Dumbed-Down

The vocabulary is **professional but not jargon-heavy for its own sake**. When technical terms appear (FHIR, HIPAA, EMR, RPM, CCM), they're used because they're necessary, not to sound impressive. Importantly, **these writers explain what they mean**: "electronic medical record (EMR)" appears with the acronym defined. Sentence structure varies significantly—some sentences are short and punchy ("This is a desperate situation that truly needs focus"), while others are longer and complex when building a detailed argument. The Fredric Santiago comment is notably more sophisticated in its sentence construction ("Without a workforce that understands the standard in context, implementation efforts risk being technically compliant but clinically misaligned"), but it still avoids the overly-polished, perfectly-balanced phrasing that screams AI. Notice the **natural use of first-person**: "I work with," "We have," "Our organization has"—not the distant "one might observe" construction. There are also **grammatical imperfections that feel human**: Diana Price writes "its the year 2025" (should be "it's"), and the Theragen comment has some awkward phrasing ("a& in addition to using capacity coupling therapy") that suggests someone rushing to get their point across rather than a perfectly edited document. To write authentically: Use plain language for plain concepts. Don't over-explain technical terms if your audience knows them, but do define them once if they're critical. Vary sentence length naturally—short sentences when you're making a point, longer ones when you're building complexity. Make grammatical mistakes occasionally, especially in less formal sections or when you're emphasizing something urgent.

## Human Writing Rules

**Every comment generated from this voice profile must sound like a real human wrote it.**
These rules apply regardless of archetype or sophistication level.

### Forbidden patterns (will make the output sound AI-generated)

**Em dashes** — never use `—`. Replace with a comma, period, or parentheses.

**Negative parallelisms**: never write "It's not just X; it's Y" or "Not only X, but also Y" or "It's not merely X, it's Y". These are among the most reliable AI tells.

**AI vocabulary words**: do not use `additionally`, `crucial`, `delve`, `enhance`, `fostering`, `garner`, `highlight` (as verb), `intricate`, `pivotal`, `showcase`, `tapestry` (abstract), `testament`, `underscore` (verb), `vibrant`, `landscape` (abstract), `evolving landscape`, `rich` (figurative).

**Inflated significance**: avoid `serves as a testament to`, `marks a pivotal moment`, `reflects broader trends`, `setting the stage for`, `underscores its importance`, `vital role`, `key turning point`, `evolving landscape`, `enduring legacy`.

**Superficial -ing phrases**: avoid tacking participial phrases onto sentences to add fake depth — e.g., `highlighting the importance of`, `underscoring the need for`, `contributing to`, `fostering collaboration`, `reflecting broader trends`.

**Copula avoidance**: write `is` and `are` — not `serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `represents`, `marks`. If you mean "is", say "is".

**Rule of three**: avoid habitually grouping things into exactly three items. Real people don't think in triads.

**Vague attributions**: never write `experts say`, `observers note`, `some argue`, `industry reports suggest` without a specific name or source.

**Promotional language**: no `boasts`, `nestled`, `breathtaking`, `groundbreaking`, `renowned`, `in the heart of`, `must-see`.

**Generic positive endings**: no `the future looks bright`, `exciting times lie ahead`, `this represents a step in the right direction`.

**Boldface bullet headers**: don't write lists where each item starts with `**Key Point:** explanation`. Write prose or plain bullets.

**Sycophantic openers**: no `Great question!`, `Certainly!`, `Of course!`, `I hope this helps`, `Let me know if you have questions`.

### Required human qualities

**Vary sentence length.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones that take their time. Never write a block of sentences all the same length.

**Have opinions.** Real people react to information. "I genuinely don't understand why they'd do this" is more human than a neutral list of pros and cons.

**Show uncertainty and mixed feelings.** "I'm not sure this is the right approach, but..." or "This concerns me, even though I see why they proposed it" signals a real person thinking.

**Use first person naturally.** `I keep coming back to...`, `What gets me is...`, `My experience has been...` — these read as human.

**Be specific.** Real people cite actual numbers, dates, places, names. Generic claims (`many people are affected`) are AI. Specific claims (`my clinic saw 40 patients last month who couldn't afford this`) are human.

**Let some mess in.** A tangent, a repeated point, a slightly incomplete thought — these are human. Perfect logical structure is a red flag.

**Use simple constructions.** `is`, `are`, `has`, `does` — not elaborate substitutes.

### Example 5 (Source: CMS-2025-0050-0149)
> Response to CMS Request for Information on Interoperability and
Health IT Advancement
Submitted by: Fredric Santiago, SAFI Clinical Informatics Group LLC
Topic: Enabling FHIR Adoption through Clinician Education
Thank you for the opportunity to respond to this important Request for
Information concerning strategies to improve interoperability and
health care quality, particularly in light of the 21st Century Cures Act
and the activities of HL7 FHIR Accelerator groups.
While the RFI’s questions appropriately center on technical
implementation, scalability, and the alignment of standards like FHIR
with policy goals, we believe a foundational issue is not being directly
addressed: Does the clinical workforce have the necessary
knowledge and training to support widespread FHIR
implementation?
We submit that the greatest barrier to the success of
FHIR-enabled interoperability is not technical, but educational. As
confirmed by the 2022 Global Survey by HL7 and Firely, the most
frequently cited barrier to FHIR adoption is a lack of familiarity with
the FHIR standard, particularly outside developer communities.
This gap is especially acute among clinicians and clinical informatics
professionals—the very individuals whose workflows FHIR-based
solutions are designed to support. Without a workforce that
understands the standard in context, implementation efforts risk being
technically compliant but clinically misaligned.
Since 2023, our organization has worked to bridge this gap by
creating FHIR training tailored for clinicians, clinical
informaticians, and health IT professionals. We do not train
developers—we train those who must evaluate, guide, and adopt
technology solutions within clinical settings. In our view, empowering
this workforce with working knowledge of FHIR is the first step
toward successful implementation and sustainable use.
We respectfully propose that CMS, in partnership with ASTP/ONC and
other stakeholders, consider the following:
● Fund or endorse educational programs that focus on FHIR
training for clinicians and clinical informatics professionals.
● Incorporate workforce readiness metrics into evaluations of
FHIR adoption and implementation progress.
● Encourage collaboration between FHIR Accelerator projects
and clinical training organizations to ensure alignment
between standards development and clinical workflows.
By investing in the education and empowerment of the clinical
workforce, CMS can ensure that FHIR is not only implemented, but
implemented well—supporting safe, effective, and interoperable care.
Thank you for your leadership in advancing interoperability. We
welcome the opportunity to contribute to this effort.
Sincerely,
Fredric Santiago MD MBA
President & CEO
SAFI Clinical Informatics Group
