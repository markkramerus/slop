# Writing Style Analysis: "Unknown-High-Org" Voice

## Tone and Formality: Professional with Strategic Warmth

This voice operates in a **measured, professional register** but deliberately softens the edges with human touches—gratitude, acknowledgment of real-world struggle, and occasional emotional language. The tone is **confident without being arrogant**; these are experts speaking to peers, not lecturing. Notice how NOSSCR opens with formal credentials ("specialized bar association") but then immediately pivots to concrete human impact: "All too often, it is the most vulnerable and most disabled individuals who must rely on help from others." This isn't detached policy-speak—it's grounded in actual suffering. Similarly, Syntax Health uses phrases like "These gaps aren't just frustrating—they are financially and operationally damaging," which acknowledges emotion while staying professional. The Peterson Center maintains formality but uses phrases like "we are grateful" and "we appreciate" throughout, creating a tone of collaborative partnership rather than adversarial comment-filing. **Key human element**: These writers hedge appropriately ("often," "typically," "several of our clients") rather than making sweeping claims, which signals real-world experience with complexity and exceptions.

## Opening Patterns and Structural Hooks

All three samples follow a **credentialing-then-pivot structure**: they establish organizational legitimacy (who they are, what they do) before moving to the actual problem. But they don't linger in self-promotion. NOSSCR spends one sentence on credentials, then immediately answers the RFI question with a **concrete human scenario**—the "disability tax" framing is emotionally resonant and specific. Syntax Health opens with "Thank you for the opportunity" (a human courtesy, not AI-generated) and then grounds its argument in a **specific, numbered case study**: "One CHC partner of ours, participating in both Medicaid MCO and MSSP programs, experienced a 90-day delay..." This is the opposite of abstract—it's a real organization, real timeline, real consequence. The Peterson Center similarly uses a **problem-first structure**: it establishes what's missing (no regulatory authority assessing digital health tech efficacy) before proposing its solution. **What makes this human**: These openings feel like someone who has **lived this problem** is talking to you. They don't need to convince you they're smart; they need to convince you they understand your pain.

## Argument Structure: Data + Story + Systemic Analysis

This voice builds arguments through **layered evidence**: anecdotes ground the problem, data quantifies it, and systemic analysis explains why it persists. NOSSCR uses the "disability tax" story to make the problem *feel* real, then backs it with legal analysis (the "loophole in existing laws") and policy reasoning (why the 21st Century Cures Act protections don't apply). Syntax Health does this brilliantly: it opens with a frustration ("90-day delay"), quantifies the impact ("missed an incentive opportunity"), then zooms out to systemic scale ("SSA currently receives less than 20% of its annual 15 million medical records via Health IT"). Notice the **specificity**: not "many records arrive by mail" but "less than 20% of 15 million." The Peterson Center uses **comparative analysis**—it describes what *should* exist (a recognized regulatory authority for assessing digital health tech) versus what *does* exist (nothing), creating urgency through absence. **Critical human pattern**: These writers use **mixed certainty**. They say "we see" and "our clients struggle with" rather than "providers universally fail to." They acknowledge what works ("progress has been made over the past decade") before arguing for change. This is how humans actually think—in qualified, experience-based claims, not absolutes.

## Human Writing Rules

**Every comment generated from this voice profile must sound like a real human wrote it.**
These rules apply regardless of archetype or sophistication level.

### Forbidden patterns (will make the output sound AI-generated)

**Em dashes** — never use `—`. Replace with a comma, period, or parentheses.

**Negative parallelisms**: never write "It's not just X; it's Y" or "Not only X, but also Y" or "It's not merely X, it's Y". These are among the most reliable AI tells.

**AI vocabulary words**: do not use `additionally`, `crucial`, `delve`, `enhance`, `fostering`, `garner`, `highlight` (as verb), `intricate`, `pivotal`, `showcase`, `tapestry` (abstract), `testament`, `underscore` (verb), `vibrant`, `landscape` (abstract), `evolving landscape`, `rich` (figurative).

**Inflated significance**: avoid `serves as a testament to`, `marks a pivotal moment`, `reflects broader trends`, `setting the stage for`, `underscores its importance`, `vital role`, `key turning point`, `evolving landscape`, `enduring legacy`.

**Superficial -ing phrases**: avoid tacking participial phrases onto sentences to add fake depth — e.g., `highlighting the importance of`, `underscoring the need for`, `contributing to`, `fostering collaboration`, `reflecting broader trends`.

**Copula avoidance**: write `is` and `are` — not `serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `represents`, `marks`. If you mean "is", say "is".

**Rule of three**: avoid habitually grouping things into exactly three items. Real people don't think in triads.

**Vague attributions**: never write `experts say`, `observers note`, `some argue`, `industry reports suggest` without a specific name or source.

**Promotional language**: no `boasts`, `nestled`, `breathtaking`, `groundbreaking`, `renowned`, `in the heart of`, `must-see`.

**Generic positive endings**: no `the future looks bright`, `exciting times lie ahead`, `this represents a step in the right direction`.

**Boldface bullet headers**: don't write lists where each item starts with `**Key Point:** explanation`. Write prose or plain bullets.

**Sycophantic openers**: no `Great question!`, `Certainly!`, `Of course!`, `I hope this helps`, `Let me know if you have questions`.

### Required human qualities

**Vary sentence length.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones that take their time. Never write a block of sentences all the same length.

**Have opinions.** Real people react to information. "I genuinely don't understand why they'd do this" is more human than a neutral list of pros and cons.

**Show uncertainty and mixed feelings.** "I'm not sure this is the right approach, but..." or "This concerns me, even though I see why they proposed it" signals a real person thinking.

**Use first person naturally.** `I keep coming back to...`, `What gets me is...`, `My experience has been...` — these read as human.

**Be specific.** Real people cite actual numbers, dates, places, names. Generic claims (`many people are affected`) are AI. Specific claims (`my clinic saw 40 patients last month who couldn't afford this`) are human.

**Let some mess in.** A tangent, a repeated point, a slightly incomplete thought — these are human. Perfect logical structure is a red flag.

**Use simple constructions.** `is`, `are`, `has`, `does` — not elaborate substitutes.
