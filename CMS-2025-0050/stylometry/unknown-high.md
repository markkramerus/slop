# Writing Style Analysis: "Unknown-High" Voice

## Tone and Formality

This voice operates in a **highly professional, institutional register** that is neither cold nor emotionally driven—it's measured, authoritative, and collaborative. The tone is respectful but confident, with speakers positioning themselves as knowledgeable stakeholders rather than supplicants. Notably, there's an absence of urgency or alarm; even when identifying problems (multiple sign-ons, information barriers), the voice remains solution-oriented rather than frustrated. The Colorado comment exemplifies this: "Colorado appreciates the opportunity..." and "Colorado believes..." establish a measured, official stance. The AHRQ comment similarly opens with institutional gratitude and positions the agency as a knowledge-holder: "AHRQ's DHR has been advancing patient-centered healthcare through digital health technologies throughout its 20-year history." This is **confident without being arrogant**—it's the voice of someone speaking from institutional authority and experience.

**What makes it human:** This voice uses hedging and qualified claims rather than sweeping certainties. Notice "we should not accept the status quo" rather than "the status quo is unacceptable," and "we recognize that a single API sign-on will not be feasible for the foreseeable future" rather than "single sign-ons are impossible." These phrasings show deliberation and acknowledgment of complexity—hallmarks of human institutional thinking. The voice also naturally uses "we" and "our" when speaking on behalf of organizations, which feels authentic to how government and research agencies actually communicate.

## Opening Patterns and Structural Hooks

These comments **begin with formal acknowledgment and institutional positioning** rather than a hook designed to grab attention. Both samples open by thanking the opportunity to comment and immediately establishing credibility through organizational affiliation and scope of expertise. The Colorado comment spends considerable space (the first full paragraph) explaining which agencies contributed to the response—this is not efficient writing, but it's *human institutional writing*. It signals: "This is a coordinated response from multiple stakeholders, and we're being transparent about who's involved."

The AHRQ comment uses a slightly different but equally institutional approach: "Background" section that explains the organization's 20-year history and then signals that recommendations will follow "organized according to the section headers in the RFI." This is **scaffolding for the reader**—a human tendency to preview structure, especially in long formal documents. Neither comment uses a narrative hook, emotional appeal, or rhetorical flourish. Instead, they establish **legitimacy through transparency and organizational context**.

**What makes it human:** Real institutional voices preview their structure explicitly ("For ease of reference, our responses are grouped by topic..."). AI tends to either dive straight into content or use more subtle signposting. This voice also includes the mundane detail of contact information and specific office names—the kind of administrative specificity that humans include because it's part of their actual job, not because it's strategically necessary.

## Argument Structure: Layered, Evidence-Based, Values-Driven

This voice builds arguments through **multiple reinforcing layers**: (1) stating a principle or goal, (2) explaining why it matters, (3) providing specific examples or use cases, and (4) recommending concrete actions. The Colorado comment on digital health products exemplifies this:

- **Principle:** "The ultimate goal of digital health technologies should be to facilitate a healthy population with improved health outcomes."
- **Why it matters:** Three patient-specific objectives are then enumerated (awareness, access, knowledge).
- **Specific examples:** Bullet points detail what DHPs should include—"integrated applications in real-time," "simple screens," accessibility features.
- **Concrete recommendations:** "Colorado recommends that CMS and ASTP/ONC consider proven successes such as the Renewable Energy Credits (REC) framework..."

The AHRQ comment uses a similar structure but leans more heavily on **evidence and existing research products**. Rather than stating principles, it says "Based on knowledge gained from the AHRQ CDSiC, patients expect..." and then cites specific reports and publications. This is **data-driven argumentation**, but it's grounded in the organization's actual work, not abstract statistics.

**What makes it human:** This voice uses **anaphora and parallel structure** naturally—"Patients must be aware... Patients must have easy access... Patients must know how to..." ## Human Writing Rules

**Every comment generated from this voice profile must sound like a real human wrote it.**
These rules apply regardless of archetype or sophistication level.

### Forbidden patterns (will make the output sound AI-generated)

**Em dashes** — never use `—`. Replace with a comma, period, or parentheses.

**Negative parallelisms**: never write "It's not just X; it's Y" or "Not only X, but also Y" or "It's not merely X, it's Y". These are among the most reliable AI tells.

**AI vocabulary words**: do not use `additionally`, `crucial`, `delve`, `enhance`, `fostering`, `garner`, `highlight` (as verb), `intricate`, `pivotal`, `showcase`, `tapestry` (abstract), `testament`, `underscore` (verb), `vibrant`, `landscape` (abstract), `evolving landscape`, `rich` (figurative).

**Inflated significance**: avoid `serves as a testament to`, `marks a pivotal moment`, `reflects broader trends`, `setting the stage for`, `underscores its importance`, `vital role`, `key turning point`, `evolving landscape`, `enduring legacy`.

**Superficial -ing phrases**: avoid tacking participial phrases onto sentences to add fake depth — e.g., `highlighting the importance of`, `underscoring the need for`, `contributing to`, `fostering collaboration`, `reflecting broader trends`.

**Copula avoidance**: write `is` and `are` — not `serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `represents`, `marks`. If you mean "is", say "is".

**Rule of three**: avoid habitually grouping things into exactly three items. Real people don't think in triads.

**Vague attributions**: never write `experts say`, `observers note`, `some argue`, `industry reports suggest` without a specific name or source.

**Promotional language**: no `boasts`, `nestled`, `breathtaking`, `groundbreaking`, `renowned`, `in the heart of`, `must-see`.

**Generic positive endings**: no `the future looks bright`, `exciting times lie ahead`, `this represents a step in the right direction`.

**Boldface bullet headers**: don't write lists where each item starts with `**Key Point:** explanation`. Write prose or plain bullets.

**Sycophantic openers**: no `Great question!`, `Certainly!`, `Of course!`, `I hope this helps`, `Let me know if you have questions`.

### Required human qualities

**Vary sentence length.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones that take their time. Never write a block of sentences all the same length.

**Have opinions.** Real people react to information. "I genuinely don't understand why they'd do this" is more human than a neutral list of pros and cons.

**Show uncertainty and mixed feelings.** "I'm not sure this is the right approach, but..." or "This concerns me, even though I see why they proposed it" signals a real person thinking.

**Use first person naturally.** `I keep coming back to...`, `What gets me is...`, `My experience has been...` — these read as human.

**Be specific.** Real people cite actual numbers, dates, places, names. Generic claims (`many people are affected`) are AI. Specific claims (`my clinic saw 40 patients last month who couldn't afford this`) are human.

**Let some mess in.** A tangent, a repeated point, a slightly incomplete thought — these are human. Perfect logical structure is a red flag.

**Use simple constructions.** `is`, `are`, `has`, `does` — not elaborate substitutes.
