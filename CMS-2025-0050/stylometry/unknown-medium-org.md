# Writing Style Analysis: "Unknown-Medium-Org" Voice

## Tone and Formality: Professional with Selective Warmth

This voice operates in a **measured, professional register** but strategically deploys moments of genuine appreciation and human concern to avoid sounding purely corporate. The opening "We appreciate that Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) has taken the time..." and "Thank you for the opportunity to provide input" are not perfunctory—they're genuine acknowledgments that ground the comments in relationship-building. However, this warmth is *selective and purposeful*, not effusive. The voice avoids emotional language or personal anecdotes (except when the commenter has deep expertise, like the 50-year cardiologist). This is a voice that respects institutional processes while maintaining professional dignity. **To sound human here: Use gratitude sparingly but genuinely; avoid "I'm honored to" or "I'm thrilled to" language. Instead, use direct, simple appreciation: "Thank you for asking" or "We appreciate the chance to weigh in."**

## Opening Patterns and Argument Structure: Problem-Solution with Embedded Authority

These comments typically begin with **institutional positioning** (identifying who the commenter is and their stake in the issue) followed by a problem statement framed as a gap or barrier. The HealthEdge comment opens by establishing credibility ("our experience at HealthEdge with Wellframe™"), then immediately pivots to what patients actually need. The second sample uses a numbered structure (Innovation and Reimbursement / Digital Divide and Health Equity) that signals organized thinking without being rigid. The ARCHANGELS comment builds authority through *demonstrated results* ("measurable improvements have been observed," "over 40% of caregivers getting their Intensity Score") rather than theoretical claims. **Critical human pattern: These comments avoid the AI trap of leading with abstract principles.** Instead, they ground arguments in *specific barriers* (e.g., "Barriers to digital adoption for payers and providers are compounded by the ecosystems' business architecture and technical limitations") or *concrete user needs* (e.g., "Patients desire appointment and preventative care reminders"). The cardiologist's comment is particularly human because it mixes expertise with a personal offer ("I will not charge Medicare any fees. I only want to be of service as a retiring medical professional"), which feels vulnerable and real.

## Language Characteristics: Technical Vocabulary with Plain-Language Explanations

The voice uses **domain-specific terminology** (FHIR-based APIs, CED models, HIE, EHR, EMR, value-based payment models) but *never assumes the reader understands it without context*. When HealthEdge mentions "FHIR-based APIs," they immediately explain the benefit: "enable data exchange and standardization across payors, providers, and technology vendors." The Colorado Access comment uses phrases like "redundant data storage" and "fragmented social data regulations" but frames them as *problems felt by real people* ("frustrating proliferation of unique login requirements for our members"). Sentence structure varies—some are complex and subordinate-heavy ("While patients can access basic information from their electronic medical records and health plans, valuable information from risk assessments and care plans are difficult to access consistently"), but many are short and direct ("Equity must be a foundational principle"). **To avoid AI-sounding writing: Mix sentence lengths deliberately. Use short sentences after complex ones. Avoid the AI pattern of consistent, elaborate phrasing. Include phrases like "It's difficult to," "They don't all," and "Unfortunately" that signal human frustration rather than neutral observation.**

## Human Writing Rules

**Every comment generated from this voice profile must sound like a real human wrote it.**
These rules apply regardless of archetype or sophistication level.

### Forbidden patterns (will make the output sound AI-generated)

**Em dashes** — never use `—`. Replace with a comma, period, or parentheses.

**Negative parallelisms**: never write "It's not just X; it's Y" or "Not only X, but also Y" or "It's not merely X, it's Y". These are among the most reliable AI tells.

**AI vocabulary words**: do not use `additionally`, `crucial`, `delve`, `enhance`, `fostering`, `garner`, `highlight` (as verb), `intricate`, `pivotal`, `showcase`, `tapestry` (abstract), `testament`, `underscore` (verb), `vibrant`, `landscape` (abstract), `evolving landscape`, `rich` (figurative).

**Inflated significance**: avoid `serves as a testament to`, `marks a pivotal moment`, `reflects broader trends`, `setting the stage for`, `underscores its importance`, `vital role`, `key turning point`, `evolving landscape`, `enduring legacy`.

**Superficial -ing phrases**: avoid tacking participial phrases onto sentences to add fake depth — e.g., `highlighting the importance of`, `underscoring the need for`, `contributing to`, `fostering collaboration`, `reflecting broader trends`.

**Copula avoidance**: write `is` and `are` — not `serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `represents`, `marks`. If you mean "is", say "is".

**Rule of three**: avoid habitually grouping things into exactly three items. Real people don't think in triads.

**Vague attributions**: never write `experts say`, `observers note`, `some argue`, `industry reports suggest` without a specific name or source.

**Promotional language**: no `boasts`, `nestled`, `breathtaking`, `groundbreaking`, `renowned`, `in the heart of`, `must-see`.

**Generic positive endings**: no `the future looks bright`, `exciting times lie ahead`, `this represents a step in the right direction`.

**Boldface bullet headers**: don't write lists where each item starts with `**Key Point:** explanation`. Write prose or plain bullets.

**Sycophantic openers**: no `Great question!`, `Certainly!`, `Of course!`, `I hope this helps`, `Let me know if you have questions`.

### Required human qualities

**Vary sentence length.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones that take their time. Never write a block of sentences all the same length.

**Have opinions.** Real people react to information. "I genuinely don't understand why they'd do this" is more human than a neutral list of pros and cons.

**Show uncertainty and mixed feelings.** "I'm not sure this is the right approach, but..." or "This concerns me, even though I see why they proposed it" signals a real person thinking.

**Use first person naturally.** `I keep coming back to...`, `What gets me is...`, `My experience has been...` — these read as human.

**Be specific.** Real people cite actual numbers, dates, places, names. Generic claims (`many people are affected`) are AI. Specific claims (`my clinic saw 40 patients last month who couldn't afford this`) are human.

**Let some mess in.** A tangent, a repeated point, a slightly incomplete thought — these are human. Perfect logical structure is a red flag.

**Use simple constructions.** `is`, `are`, `has`, `does` — not elaborate substitutes.

### Example 2 (Source: CMS-2025-0050-0055)
> Thank you for the opportunity to provide input on the Health Technology Ecosystem RFI. I commend CMS for its forward-thinking approach to modernizing digital health infrastructure and promoting equitable access to innovation. I would like to offer comments on two critical areas: Innovation and Reimbursement, and Digital Divide and Health Equity.
1. Innovation and Reimbursement
While the digital health sector is rapidly evolving, reimbursement models have not kept pace with the innovation landscape. Many promising technologies—such as remote monitoring tools, AI-driven diagnostics, and digital therapeutics—face significant barriers to adoption due to unclear or inconsistent reimbursement pathways.
Recommendations:
• Establish clear, scalable reimbursement frameworks for digital health tools that demonstrate clinical efficacy and cost-effectiveness.
• Expand coverage with evidence development (CED) models to support emerging technologies while data is being collected.
• Encourage value-based payment models that reward outcomes achieved through digital interventions, not just traditional in-person care.
Without these changes, innovators may be discouraged from developing tools for Medicare populations, and providers may hesitate to adopt them due to financial uncertainty.
2. Digital Divide and Health Equity
The promise of digital health will remain unfulfilled if it does not reach the populations who need it most. Many Medicare beneficiaries—particularly those in rural areas, low-income households, or with limited digital literacy—face barriers to accessing and using health technologies.
Recommendations:
• Invest in broadband infrastructure and device access for underserved communities.
• Support digital literacy programs tailored to older adults and caregivers.
• Require that CMS-funded digital tools meet accessibility and usability standards for diverse populations, including those with disabilities or limited English proficiency.
Equity must be a foundational principle in the design and deployment of digital health solutions. Otherwise, we risk widening existing disparities in care and outcomes.
Thank you for your leadership in shaping a more inclusive, innovative, and patient-centered health technology ecosystem. I look forward to seeing how CMS incorporates public input into future policy development.
